Knowledge is formed through governed interpretation, not through ingestion alone. A candidate Knowledge item must retain its supporting sources, authorship or derivation, scope, review state and intended use. Organization-private Knowledge remains inside the Workplace boundary unless a purpose-bound sharing path is authorized. Shared or licensed Knowledge remains subject to its source and licence conditions even when projected inside a Product.


```text
stored content
≠ accepted Knowledge
≠ formal business fact
```

## Chapter Purpose

CH13 defined how Workplace discovers information, preserves provenance, assembles minimum sufficient context, and supplies that context to authorized consumers.

This chapter defines the next layer:

> How does Workplace consume governed Knowledge through the logical Brain without confusing shared Knowledge with private organizational memory, unverified information, AI-generated candidates, Product content, or professional judgment?

Professional organizations need reusable knowledge.

They need to know:

- what a jurisdiction requires;
- which source supports a rule;
- which version applies;
- what exceptions exist;
- which templates are approved;
- what evidence standard is relevant;
- how a status should be interpreted;
- whether a practice is official, local, historical, experimental, or disputed;
- whether AI may use the knowledge;
- whether Human Review remains required.

Without a shared Knowledge layer, each Product and each organization would rebuild the same rules, checklists, terminology, mappings, and professional explanations.

But a centralized knowledge system can also become dangerous if it:

- absorbs confidential organizational memory;
- converts unverified information into apparent truth;
- hides source and version;
- treats AI summaries as canonical;
- overrides professional judgment;
- forces all organizations into one practice;
- becomes the owner of every context;
- makes Product output depend on one opaque service;
- learns from client work without authorization.

The architecture therefore needs both reuse and restraint.

The central proposition is:

```text
Knowledge Consumption
=
Authorized Knowledge Reference
+ Scope
+ Version
+ Validation
+ Source Authority
+ Purpose
+ Consumer Context
+ Usage Boundary
+ Human Review
+ Trace
```

Mo Brain is defined as:

> the logical knowledge and context plane through which governed Knowledge can be discovered, related, retrieved, and consumed across Workplaces and Products without becoming the owner of private organizational memory, formal business facts, professional judgment, or every source representation.

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Brain ≠ one universal database

Brain ≠ all organizational memory

Knowledge ≠ Information

Knowledge Candidate ≠ Canonical Knowledge

Private Knowledge ≠ Shared Knowledge

Knowledge retrieval ≠ professional conclusion

Knowledge rule ≠ Policy decision

Knowledge guidance ≠ Workflow authority

Knowledge template ≠ Document

Knowledge example ≠ Evidence

Knowledge confidence ≠ legal certainty

Knowledge popularity ≠ authority

Shared Knowledge ≠ mandatory organizational practice

AI-generated explanation ≠ governed Knowledge

Knowledge use ≠ protected action
```

Book 02 remains authoritative for Knowledge objects, sources, references, versions, validation, access, and Core contracts.

Book 03 remains authoritative for governed Execution and protected-action boundaries.

This chapter defines how Workplace consumes those foundations.

It does not redefine them.

## 1. Knowledge Is Governed Reusable Understanding

For Workplace architecture, Knowledge means:

> a governed reference, rule, guide, checklist, precedent, source excerpt, template guide, glossary, mapping, or other structured understanding that is identifiable by source, scope, version, status, validation, and permitted use.

Knowledge is reusable because it can support more than one immediate context.

It is governed because reuse creates risk.

A professional organization may use Knowledge to support:

- jurisdiction guidance;
- classification;
- intake;
- quotation preparation;
- document requirements;
- evidence review;
- deadline explanation;
- provider evaluation;
- workflow guidance;
- Communication drafting;
- Human Review;
- Product explanation;
- AI grounding.

Knowledge supports professional work.

It does not decide, approve, execute, or mutate formal state.

The core relationship is:

```text
Information
→ candidate understanding
→ professional validation
→ governed Knowledge
→ authorized consumption
→ human or governed service use
```

The fact that a statement is useful does not make it Knowledge.

The fact that a statement appears repeatedly does not make it correct.

The fact that AI can summarize it does not make it authoritative.

---

## 2. Knowledge Consumption Is Not Knowledge Ownership

A Workplace may consume shared Knowledge.

That does not mean the Workplace owns the shared Knowledge asset.

A Product may display a Knowledge explanation.

That does not mean the Product owns the Knowledge lifecycle.

An AI Agent may retrieve a Knowledge reference.

That does not mean the Agent may update or validate it.

Knowledge ownership remains with the relevant Knowledge governance authority or owning domain.

Consumption means:

```text
discover
→ authorize
→ retrieve
→ interpret within scope
→ apply for a defined purpose
→ preserve source and version
→ review where required
→ trace use
```

Consumption does not mean:

```text
copy into Product
→ remove source
→ edit locally
→ treat as current forever
→ publish as organization truth
```

A consuming system must preserve the distinction between:

- source Knowledge;
- local projection;
- organization annotation;
- derived explanation;
- AI summary;
- professional conclusion.

## 3. Brain Is a Logical Knowledge and Context Plane

Mo Brain is a logical architecture responsibility.

It is not automatically:

- one repository;
- one database;
- one vector store;
- one service;
- one model;
- one knowledge graph;
- one Product;
- one search engine.

The Brain may be implemented through several components over time.

Its logical responsibilities may include:

- Knowledge discovery;
- Knowledge reference resolution;
- scope-aware retrieval;
- version-aware retrieval;
- relationship navigation;
- source and citation trace;
- validation status exposure;
- conflict exposure;
- contextual assembly support;
- AI grounding support;
- private/shared boundary enforcement;
- Knowledge-use trace.

The key rule is:

```text
Logical unity
does not require
physical centralization.
```

Knowledge may remain distributed across:

- Core Knowledge services;
- shared repositories;
- organization-private stores;
- Local Vault;
- Product-specific reference stores;
- approved external sources.

Brain provides a coherent consumption plane.

It must not erase ownership or boundary.

## 4. Brain Is Not a Universal Memory System

Organizational memory may include:

- historical decisions;
- client-specific instructions;
- internal lessons;
- relationship history;
- private provider experience;
- organization preferences;
- team corrections;
- internal drafts;
- personal working notes.

Some of these may later become governed private Knowledge.

Most should not automatically become shared Brain assets.

The distinction is:

```text
Shared Brain
→ reusable governed Knowledge authorized for broader consumption

Organization Memory
→ organization-controlled historical and operational context

Private Knowledge
→ organization- or client-scoped governed understanding

Personal Memory
→ user-scoped context under organization policy
```

Brain may reference private Knowledge when the Workplace authorizes it.

It does not absorb ownership of that Knowledge.

The system must avoid the pattern:

```text
user work
→ automatic central learning
→ global reuse
```

without explicit authorization, transformation, validation, and governance.

## 5. Shared Knowledge and Private Knowledge Must Remain Distinct

Shared Knowledge is intended for authorized reuse across more than one organization or Product context.

Private Knowledge is controlled within a narrower boundary.

Private Knowledge may be scoped to:

- organization;
- client;
- Matter;
- team;
- user;
- partner relationship;
- private network.

Examples of shared Knowledge:

- validated jurisdiction filing requirements;
- controlled terminology;
- approved classification mappings;
- public procedural guidance;
- common workflow guidance;
- shared professional checklists.

Examples of private Knowledge:

- organization pricing logic;
- client-specific instruction patterns;
- private provider assessments;
- internal response strategy;
- unpublished templates;
- organization-specific review standards;
- private case lessons.

A Knowledge item may move from private to shared only through a governed promotion process.

It must not become shared because:

- it was indexed;
- AI found it useful;
- several users viewed it;
- it produced a successful outcome;
- a Product stored a copy.

## 6. Knowledge Scope Determines Where Meaning Applies

Knowledge is not universally applicable.

A Knowledge asset may be scoped by:

- domain;
- jurisdiction;
- object;
- service;
- workflow;
- Product;
- Agent;
- client;
- Matter;
- organization;
- time period;
- language;
- user role;
- risk level.

Examples:

```text
A filing requirement
→ jurisdiction + service + effective date

A classification note
→ jurisdiction + class + goods/service context

A provider practice note
→ organization + provider relationship

A client instruction rule
→ client + engagement + validity period

A template guide
→ organization + service + Product + version
```

Consumption must preserve scope.

A useful rule from one jurisdiction must not silently apply to another.

A private organization practice must not appear as a global rule.

A Product-specific explanation must not redefine Core Knowledge.

The question is:

```text
Applicable where?
```

not merely:

```text
Relevant to the words in the query?
```

## 7. Knowledge Status and Validation Must Remain Visible

Knowledge may have different lifecycle and validation states.

Conceptually, it may be:

- Draft;
- Imported;
- Source Recorded;
- Pending Review;
- Reviewed;
- Validated;
- Official;
- Experimental;
- Deprecated;
- Expired;
- Conflicting;
- Rejected;
- Archived.

Not every state permits the same use.

Examples:

- Draft Knowledge may support internal research;
- Reviewed Knowledge may support drafting;
- Validated Knowledge may support Product guidance;
- Official Knowledge may support high-confidence reference;
- Experimental Knowledge may support controlled testing;
- Conflicting Knowledge may require comparison and escalation;
- Deprecated Knowledge may support historical reconstruction only.

A Product or Agent must not hide these states behind one generic “knowledge result.”

The correct model is:

```text
Knowledge content
+ status
+ validation
+ scope
+ version
+ usage limit
```

## 8. Candidate Before Canonical

The Brain boundary depends on the candidate-before-canonical rule.

Candidates may arise from:

- acquired information;
- AI extraction;
- Distillery;
- historical outcomes;
- organization suggestions;
- expert notes;
- source changes;
- Product feedback;
- observed workflow patterns.

A Knowledge Candidate may contain a useful proposition.

It is not yet approved Knowledge.

The normal route is:

```text
Information or experience
→ Knowledge Candidate
→ source review
→ professional validation
→ governance review
→ testing where needed
→ approved / experimental / rejected
```

A candidate should preserve:

- source;
- creator;
- scope;
- proposed meaning;
- uncertainty;
- affected Knowledge;
- validation need;
- privacy boundary.

Candidate content must not appear in a Product as canonical without a visible status and permitted experimental use.

## 9. Distillery Produces Candidates, Not Truth

Mo Distillery converts structured Information and experience into candidates such as:

- Knowledge Candidates;
- rule candidates;
- Capability Candidates;
- Skill Candidates;
- workflow fragments;
- mappings;
- checklists;
- summaries.

Distillery may use:

- extraction;
- clustering;
- comparison;
- pattern detection;
- AI analysis;
- historical outcomes;
- human annotation.

Its output is valuable because it accelerates professional governance.

Its output is risky if it bypasses governance.

The boundary is:

```text
Distillery
→ proposes

Professional validation
→ evaluates

Knowledge governance
→ registers or rejects

Brain
→ serves approved or explicitly experimental Knowledge
```

Distillery does not own professional truth.

## 10. Knowledge Source Must Remain Reachable

A Knowledge asset should remain traceable to its source where professional risk requires it.

The consuming user may not always need the full source.

But the system should preserve enough reference to support:

- review;
- verification;
- dispute;
- update;
- historical explanation;
- source replacement;
- licensing or usage compliance.

The source may be:

- official law;
- office guideline;
- public register;
- official form;
- foreign associate advice;
- internal expert memorandum;
- historical case;
- approved template;
- verified operational practice.

The Brain should not turn sources into anonymous fragments.

The rule is:

```text
Knowledge may be summarized.

Its source identity must not disappear.
```

## 11. Source Authority and Knowledge Validation Are Different

Source authority describes the origin.

Knowledge validation describes the governed evaluation of the Knowledge representation.

An official source may still be:

- incorrectly extracted;
- mistranslated;
- outdated;
- applied outside scope.

An internal expert note may be highly useful while remaining non-official.

A foreign associate’s advice may be authoritative for local practical guidance but limited to the stated question.

Therefore:

```text
Source authority
≠
Knowledge validation
```

A consuming system may need both:

- source authority;
- validation status;
- reviewer;
- validation basis;
- scope;
- limitations;
- effective time.

This prevents “official source” from becoming a blanket trust label.

## 12. Knowledge Version Is Part of Meaning

Professional Knowledge changes.

Examples include:

- fees;
- deadlines;
- filing requirements;
- document formalities;
- classification practice;
- evidence standards;
- provider procedures;
- product guidance;
- internal review standards.

A Knowledge reference without a version may be unsafe.

A consuming context may need:

- current version;
- historically applicable version;
- superseded version;
- proposed next version;
- effective date;
- expiry date;
- change reason.

The system must distinguish:

```text
latest version

currently effective version

version applicable to historical action
```

These may not be the same.

A changed Knowledge version may invalidate:

- Product explanation;
- AI Context;
- prepared draft;
- review;
- quote;
- workflow guidance;
- saved checklist.

## 13. Knowledge Relationships Support Understanding

Knowledge items rarely stand alone.

Relationships may include:

- depends on;
- supersedes;
- contradicts;
- supports;
- explains;
- applies to;
- exception to;
- derived from;
- cites;
- validated by;
- used by.

Examples:

```text
POA requirement
→ applies to assignment service

Notarization exception
→ exception to general POA rule

New fee schedule
→ supersedes old fee schedule

Foreign associate advice
→ supports private practice note

Evidence checklist
→ depends on jurisdiction requirement
```

The Brain may expose these relationships.

It must not infer authority merely from connectedness.

A highly connected Knowledge item may be popular.

It is not necessarily correct.

## 14. Knowledge Retrieval Is Governed Retrieval

Knowledge retrieval is not generic search.

It should consider:

- organization;
- actor;
- Product;
- purpose;
- subject;
- jurisdiction;
- service;
- object;
- validation status;
- effective date;
- source authority;
- language;
- AI permission;
- privacy boundary;
- professional risk.

A governed retrieval result should distinguish:

- authoritative match;
- validated match;
- private match;
- experimental match;
- outdated match;
- conflicting match;
- candidate match;
- missing Knowledge.

The result should not flatten all matches into one ranked list.

The Brain may rank for relevance.

It must also expose governance state.

The user or Agent may still lack permission to access or use it.

Examples:

- private provider assessments;
- client-specific Knowledge;
- internal legal strategy;
- restricted templates;
- confidential network practice;

Knowledge access depends on:

- Identity;
- Organization;
- Role;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- Knowledge scope;
- object relationship;
- Product entitlement;
- Agent Contract;
- data boundary.

The Brain does not grant permission.

It consumes Permission and Policy decisions.

```text
Product access
≠
all Knowledge access
```

## 17. Knowledge Consumption Must Be Purpose-Bound

The same Knowledge may be used differently for:

- internal orientation;
- AI drafting;
- client explanation;
- quotation;
- workflow guidance;
- professional review;
- formal filing preparation;
- Product help;
- network routing.

A Knowledge item may permit:

- internal reference;
- AI grounding;
- Product display;
- draft generation;
- recommendation support;
- human-reviewed external use.

It may prohibit:

- autonomous external advice;
- direct filing;
- unrestricted publication;
- cross-client reuse;
- network disclosure.

The consumption decision must ask:

```text
Use for what?
```

not only:

```text
Access by whom?
```

## 18. Knowledge Supports Judgment but Does Not Replace It

Knowledge can improve professional judgment by:

- making rules visible;
- surfacing exceptions;
- comparing sources;
- explaining terminology;
- providing checklists;
- preserving prior validated understanding;
- identifying gaps.

But Knowledge does not decide:

- final legal strategy;
- evidence sufficiency;
- commercial risk;
- whether to file;
- whether to oppose;
- whether to accept a provider;
- whether to send a Communication;
- whether to approve a protected action.

Professional judgment depends on:

- facts;
- client objective;
- uncertainty;
- timing;
- responsibility;
- risk;
- current Knowledge;
- review;
- human accountability.

The constitutional rule is:

```text
Knowledge supports.

Humans judge.

Owning Services change and record formal business facts.
```

## 19. Knowledge Rule Is Not Policy Decision

A Knowledge rule may say:

```text
A Power of Attorney is generally required.
```

A Policy decision may say:

```text
This Product must not proceed
until an approved POA is present.
```

The first expresses governed understanding.

The second governs behavior in context.

Knowledge and Policy may relate.

They must not be merged.

A Knowledge update does not automatically change operational Policy.

A Policy decision does not prove the Knowledge proposition is universally true.

---

## 20. Knowledge Guidance Is Not Workflow Authority

Knowledge may explain:

- recommended steps;
- common sequence;
- required documents;
- review points;
- possible exceptions.

A Workflow governs coordinated execution under controlled state and contracts.

A checklist is not automatically a Workflow.

A process guide is not automatically an executable state machine.

A Knowledge asset may be referenced by a Workflow.

It does not control Task creation, transitions, review, Communication, or external action.

The distinction is:

```text
Knowledge says what may matter.

Workflow coordinates governed work.

Owning Service changes formal state.
```

---

## 21. Knowledge Template Is Not a Document

Knowledge may include:

- template guide;
- clause library;
- drafting instruction;
- approved example;
- structure;
- checklist.

A template is not automatically a Document.

A completed output becomes a governed Document only through the relevant Document authority and lifecycle.

A template may be outdated, scoped, organization-private, or experimental.

The system should preserve:

```text
template source

template version

scope

approved use

required review

generated output reference
```

This prevents a template from being treated as a signed or formal record.

---

## 22. Knowledge Example Is Not Evidence

Examples and precedents may support understanding.

They may include:

- historical filings;
- prior arguments;
- anonymized decisions;
- sample evidence;
- case summaries.

They are not automatically Evidence for the current Matter.

Evidence requires:

- relevance;
- source;
- authenticity;
- purpose;
- object relationship;
- evidentiary registration;
- review.

The Brain may help locate examples.

It must not present them as current Evidence merely because they are similar.

---

## 23. Knowledge Confidence Is Not Legal Certainty

Knowledge systems may expose confidence.

Confidence may reflect:

- source quality;
- agreement among sources;
- review status;
- recency;
- extraction quality;
- validation level.

It does not mean:

- guaranteed legal outcome;
- complete fact pattern;
- no exceptions;
- professional approval;
- official acceptance;
- certainty of execution success.

A high-confidence Knowledge item may still require Human Review.

A low-confidence item may still be useful if uncertainty is explicit.

The system should avoid false precision.

---

## 24. Knowledge Popularity Is Not Authority

A Knowledge asset may be frequently viewed or used.

That may indicate utility.

It does not establish authority.

Popular Knowledge may be:

- generic;
- easy to understand;
- outdated;
- overbroad;
- organization-specific;
- wrong but repeated.

Usage metrics may support:

- prioritization;
- review;
- gap detection;
- improvement.

They should not promote Knowledge automatically.

The rule is:

```text
Frequently used
≠
professionally validated
```

## 25. Shared Knowledge Does Not Mandate One Practice

Organizations may differ in:

- risk tolerance;
- client positioning;
- service model;
- pricing;
- review standards;
- provider preferences;
- internal workflow;
- drafting style.

Shared Knowledge may describe:

- common requirements;
- known options;
- source-backed rules;
- validated alternatives;
- risk factors.

It should not force all Workplaces into one operational practice unless a true shared governance rule requires it.

An organization may add:

- private annotation;
- stricter review;
- preferred approach;
- internal exception;
- local template;
- client-specific rule.

The architecture is:

```text
Shared Knowledge
+ Organization Context
+ Private Knowledge
+ Client Instruction
+ Professional Judgment
```

not:

```text
Shared Knowledge
→ one mandatory global behavior
```

## 26. Organization Annotation Must Not Modify Shared Knowledge

A Workplace may annotate shared Knowledge with:

- internal interpretation;
- preferred provider;
- private checklist;
- practical warning;
- client note;
- historical experience.

The annotation belongs to the organization or narrower scope.

It must not alter the shared Knowledge asset.

The model is:

```text
Shared Knowledge reference
        +
Organization-private annotation
        =
Workplace-specific consumption view
```

Other organizations should not see the annotation unless it is deliberately promoted and approved.

This supports autonomy without fragmenting the shared Knowledge identity.

## 27. Private Knowledge Promotion Requires Transformation

An organization may choose to contribute private learning to the shared ecosystem.

The raw private Knowledge should not be copied directly.

Promotion may require:

- client-data removal;
- anonymization;
- source verification;
- scope clarification;
- intellectual-property review;
- professional validation;
- conflict review;
- versioning;
- organization approval;
- governance approval.

The path is:

```text
Private Knowledge
→ contribution candidate
→ privacy and rights review
→ source and scope validation
→ shared Knowledge candidate
→ governance
→ approved / experimental / rejected
```

A successful outcome alone does not justify promotion.

## 28. Client-Specific Knowledge Must Not Become General Practice Automatically

Client instructions may be highly valuable.

Examples:

- preferred jurisdictions;
- acceptable risk;
- brand naming rules;
- evidence practices;
- budget constraints;
- communication style.

These are authoritative for that client context.

They are not necessarily reusable across clients.

The system must distinguish:

```text
client instruction

client preference

client-specific Knowledge

organization learning

shared Knowledge
```

A client instruction may inform a private organization lesson.

That lesson may later produce a generalized candidate.

The raw instruction must remain protected.

## 29. AI-Generated Explanations Remain Derived Outputs

AI may use Knowledge to produce:

- explanations;
- summaries;
- comparisons;
- draft answers;
- checklists;
- examples;
- risk notes;
- suggested next questions.

These outputs are not the Knowledge asset itself.

They may:

- omit exceptions;
- combine versions;
- misstate scope;
- lose source hierarchy;

AI output should preserve:

- Knowledge references;
- source scope;
- Agent identity;
- output mode;
- uncertainty;
- missing context;
- Human Review requirement.

The Brain supplies governed grounding.

It does not certify every generated sentence.

A local model does not automatically receive unrestricted private Knowledge.

## 31. Knowledge Gaps Must Be Visible

A Knowledge gap may mean:

- no relevant Knowledge exists;
- the current version is missing;
- sources conflict;
- validation expired;
- the relevant jurisdiction is unsupported;
- the Knowledge is inaccessible;
- the scope does not match;
- the required private Knowledge is absent.

A gap may trigger:

- information research;
- source acquisition;
- expert consultation;
- Knowledge Candidate creation;
- review task;
- Product warning;
- AI limitation;
- safe refusal.

The Brain should not fill a gap with generic adjacent Knowledge without marking the mismatch.

---

## 32. Conflicting Knowledge Must Remain Comparable

Knowledge may conflict because:

- sources disagree;
- jurisdictions differ;
- effective dates differ;
- official and practical guidance differ;
- professional interpretations differ;
- one item is outdated;
- organization policy is stricter.

The Brain may support comparison.

It should preserve:

- each Knowledge identity;
- source;
- scope;
- version;
- validation;
- conflict relationship;
- reviewer notes.

The result may be:

- one item supersedes another;
- both apply in different scopes;
- one is rejected;
- conflict remains unresolved;
- professional review is required.

A single merged statement may hide important uncertainty.

---

## 33. Knowledge Consumption Must Preserve Historical Use

When Knowledge influences professional work, the system may need to preserve:

- Knowledge reference;
- version;
- source;
- validation state;
- purpose;
- consuming actor or Agent;
- context;
- output;
- review;
- time.

Later changes should not erase which version was used.

Historical trace supports:

- review;
- audit;
- explanation;
- error analysis;
- improvement;
- dispute handling.

The trace does not mean the Brain owns the resulting Task, Document, Communication, Matter, or decision.

## 34. Knowledge Update May Require Downstream Reassessment

A Knowledge change may affect active work.

Examples:

- filing fee changed;
- opposition deadline changed;
- POA requirement changed;
- classification rule changed;
- evidence standard changed;
- provider procedure changed.

Potential responses include:

- notify relevant users;
- mark saved context stale;
- reassemble AI Context;
- identify affected drafts;
- require new review;
- update Product guidance;
- create a candidate work signal;
- block a protected action.

The Knowledge layer should not directly mutate every downstream object.

It should provide traceable change signals.

Execution and Owning Services govern formal consequences.

## 35. Brain Must Not Become an Execution Engine

Brain may:

- retrieve;
- relate;
- compare;
- explain;
- identify gaps;
- prepare grounded context.

Brain must not by itself:

- create active Tasks;
- approve Communications;
- send messages;
- file applications;
- select providers;
- commit prices;
- change Matter state;
- record payment;
- publish content;
- mutate official records.

The handoff is:

```text
Knowledge
→ grounded understanding
→ candidate or prepared action
→ Human Review
→ governed Execution
→ Owning Service
```

This preserves the architecture of Part III.

## 36. Brain Must Not Become the Owner of Product Content

Products may use Knowledge to create:

- help text;
- guided explanations;
- recommendations;
- forms;
- checklists;
- client-facing content.

Product content has its own:

- audience;
- tone;
- version;
- localization;
- approval;
- Artifact or Content lifecycle.

The Brain may supply sources and governed meaning.

It does not own every Product sentence.

A Product must preserve which Knowledge informed the content.

Product content must not silently become canonical Knowledge.

## 37. Brain Must Support Local and Distributed Knowledge

Some Knowledge may remain in Local Vault or a private environment.

Examples:

- organization case library;
- private templates;
- internal provider notes;
- local historical datasets;
- client-specific guidance.

Brain may access this through:

- stable reference;
- local retrieval;
- metadata;
- authorized extract;
- safe summary;
- private context assembly.

The logical plane can include distributed sources without centralizing raw content.

The principle is:

```text
Bring governed retrieval to the Knowledge
where appropriate,
rather than copying all Knowledge into one place.
```

## 38. Knowledge Portability Must Preserve Boundaries

An organization should be able to preserve or export its own private Knowledge, subject to:

- client rights;
- source licensing;
- shared Knowledge rights;
- legal obligations;
- retention;
- audit;
- confidential third-party information.

A portability package may preserve:

- Knowledge identities;
- sources;
- versions;
- private annotations;
- scope;
- validation;
- relationships;
- usage history where appropriate.

It must not convert shared Knowledge into organization-owned content merely because it was consumed.

Shared references and private assets should remain distinguishable.

---

## 39. Knowledge Consumption Should Be Explainable

A user should be able to understand:

- which Knowledge was used;
- why it was selected;
- which version applied;
- whether it was shared or private;
- what source supported it;
- whether it was validated;
- which conflict or limitation remained;
- whether AI generated the explanation;
- whether Human Review was required.

Explainability need not expose:

- hidden prompts;
- model internals;
- restricted private content;
- proprietary ranking logic;
- secrets.

A professional explanation may say:

```text
This recommendation used:
- validated jurisdiction rule version 3;
- the client’s current private instruction;
- organization checklist version 2.

The official fee source changed yesterday.
The provider practice remains unverified.
Human review is required.
```

This is sufficient to support judgment.

## 42. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Universal Brain centralization

All shared, private, personal, and client Knowledge is absorbed into one platform-owned store.

### Memory laundering

Organizational history becomes shared Knowledge without consent or validation.

### Candidate promotion by convenience

AI or Distillery output appears canonical because it is useful.

### Product-owned Knowledge

Each Product copies and edits its own version of shared rules.

### Source disappearance

Knowledge becomes detached from its source and version.

### Scope leakage

A rule is applied outside its jurisdiction, service, client, or effective time.

### Validation flattening

Draft, validated, deprecated, and conflicting Knowledge appear identical.

### Policy collapse

Knowledge guidance is treated as operational Policy.

### Workflow collapse

A checklist is executed as if it were an authorized Workflow.

### Template formalization error

A template or example is treated as a formal Document or Evidence.

### Popularity authority

Frequently used Knowledge is automatically treated as correct.

### Mandatory global practice

Shared Knowledge overrides organization-specific professional judgment.

### Cross-client contamination

Client-specific Knowledge is reused across unrelated clients.

### AI grounding overclaim

A grounded AI response is presented as a final professional conclusion.

### Knowledge-execution collapse

Brain directly creates, approves, sends, files, pays, or mutates state.

### Historical rewriting

Current Knowledge replaces the version used in past work.

### Local Knowledge opacity

Private local Knowledge enters context without scope, source, or restrictions.

These failures may produce efficient systems.

They do not conform to MarkOrbit architecture.

## 45. Chapter Conclusion

Workplace needs shared Knowledge.

Without it, every organization, Product, and Agent would repeatedly reconstruct the same professional understanding.

But shared Knowledge is safe only when its boundary is clear.

The Brain must help Workplace answer:

```text
What Knowledge applies?

Where did it come from?

Which version is effective?

Is it validated?

Is it shared or private?

May this user or Agent access it?

May it be used for this purpose?

What conflicts or gaps remain?

Does Human Review still apply?
```

The correct chain is:

```text
Information
→ Knowledge Candidate
→ professional validation
→ governance
→ governed Knowledge
→ authorized retrieval
→ Workplace-specific consumption
→ explanation, candidate, or draft
→ human judgment
→ governed Execution where required
```

The Brain is therefore neither a universal memory nor an autonomous professional mind.

It is the logical plane that makes governed Knowledge available while preserving:

- source;
- scope;
- version;
- validation;
- privacy;
- organization autonomy;
- professional responsibility;
- Product independence;
- Execution authority.

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Brain knows references and relationships.

Workplace supplies organization context.

Private Knowledge remains private.

AI explains and assists.

Humans judge.

Execution governs action.

Owning Services own formal facts.
```

CH15 now moves from understanding to function:

> Once Workplace knows what Knowledge applies, how does it discover the right Capability and select a concrete, replaceable, testable Skill without confusing either with permission, Product access, Agent identity, or execution authority?
