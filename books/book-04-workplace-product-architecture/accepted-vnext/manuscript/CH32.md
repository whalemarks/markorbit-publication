# B04-CH-32 — Formalization, Outcome and Feedback

**Status:** vNext Candidate 04 — Editorial Consolidation Acceptance Candidate  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part V — Outcomes, Artifacts and Delivery

## Chapter Purpose

CH28–CH31 established:

- reusable Assets;
- Content, Artifact, and Document boundaries;
- Artifact lifecycle and provenance;
- Render, Edit, Delivery, and Publish.

This chapter closes Part V by explaining how prepared and approved outputs become formal records or verified outcomes, and how feedback returns without silently rewriting Knowledge, trust, Product state, or professional authority.

The central question is:

> How does an approved Artifact become input to a formal governed record or domain fact, or produce a verified delivered outcome, and how should usage, professional results, commercial results, failures, and feedback return to the organization and wider ecosystem?

The central proposition is:

```text
Formalization and Outcome
=
Approved Artifact or Prepared Action
+ Typed Intended Consequence
+ Current Identity, Permission, and Policy
+ Human Review and Approval
+ Governed Execution
+ Owning Service Acceptance
+ External Confirmation Where Required
+ Typed Outcome
+ Scoped Feedback
+ Historical Trace
```

The canonical loop is:

```text
Preparation
→ Review
→ Approval
→ Formalization or External Outcome
→ Confirmation
→ Usage and Result
→ Feedback
→ Organization Learning
→ Governed Ecosystem Contribution
```

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Artifact completion ≠ formalization

Formalization request ≠ formal record

Owning Service acceptance ≠ external confirmation

Delivery ≠ recipient acceptance

Publish ≠ business outcome

Usage ≠ agreement

Feedback ≠ truth

Outcome ≠ causation

Private learning ≠ shared Knowledge

Successful collaboration ≠ universal trust

One result ≠ universal capability evidence
```

## 1. Formalization Gives Output Domain Meaning

Formalization means:

> an authorized output is accepted by the appropriate Owning Service as a formal object, decision, communication, or business fact under that domain’s contract.

Formalization is not a visual or storage operation.

It is a governed semantic transition.

---

## 2. Formalization Requires an Owning Domain

Possible formalization targets include:

- Document;
- Communication;
- Opportunity;
- Order;
- Matter;
- Task;
- Routing decision;
- payment record;
- filing record;
- official status record;
- provider engagement record.

Each target has a different Owning Service.

Artifact architecture does not create a universal formalization authority.

---

## 3. Artifact May Be Input, Not Final Formal Object

A Quote Proposal Artifact may inform an Order.

A Provider Comparison Artifact may inform Routing.

A Filing Package Artifact may inform a filing operation.

The Artifact can remain a source and explanation package while the formal domain object has its own lifecycle.

---

## 4. Downstream Consequence Must Be Typed

A downstream request should identify both its operation family and intended consequence.

Formalization operations may include:

- create a formal record;
- update a formal record;
- record a decision;
- attach an approved output;
- supersede a formal record;
- cancel or close under the owning domain contract.

Execution and outcome operations may include:

- submit;
- send;
- deliver;
- publish.

These operations may participate in one Product journey, but they remain distinct. An approval must be represented as a typed approval decision under CH19, not hidden inside a generic formalization verb.

A generic `save as official` action is insufficient.

---

## 5. Formalization or Outcome Request Is a Prepared Action

The request should preserve:

- organization;
- actor;
- Product;
- target domain;
- target references;
- Artifact version;
- intended consequence;
- Review;
- approval;
- source;
- idempotency;
- validity;
- return route.

CH18 governs the Prepared Action boundary.

---

## 6. Current Permission and Policy Must Be Re-Evaluated

The Artifact may have been prepared under earlier conditions.

Before formalization, Execution must verify current:

- identity;
- role;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- target state;
- Review;
- approval;
- expiry;
- conflict;
- duplicate operation.

Preparation does not carry timeless authority.

---

## 7. Owning Service May Reject an Approved Output

Human approval does not force the Owning Service to accept invalid input.

The service may reject because of:

- contract mismatch;
- missing reference;
- invalid state;
- duplicate request;
- expired approval;
- unsupported operation;
- policy conflict.

Approval and service acceptance remain separate.

---

## 8. Formalization Result Must Be Explicit

Possible results include:

- accepted and recorded;
- accepted for processing;
- rejected;
- incomplete;
- conflict;
- duplicate;
- pending external action;
- failed safely.

The Product should not show formal success before the Owning Service confirms it.

---

## 9. External Outcome May Follow Formalization

An internal filing request may be accepted by an Owning Service.

The external office may later:

- receive;
- reject;
- assign number;
- request correction;
- confirm filing.

Internal formal result and external outcome remain distinct.

---

## 10. Outcome Is a Typed Result

Outcome may refer to:

- Product outcome;
- Review outcome;
- Execution outcome;
- formal business outcome;
- Delivery outcome;
- Publish outcome;
- external professional outcome;
- commercial outcome;
- learning outcome.

The system should identify which type is being discussed.

---

## 11. Product Outcome Is Not Formal Outcome

A Product may complete:

- Guide;
- comparison;
- validation;
- Artifact preparation;
- intake journey.

This is useful.

It does not necessarily change formal state.

---

## 12. Formal Outcome Is Not External Outcome

A Communication may be recorded as sent.

The recipient may not receive it.

A filing may be transmitted.

The authority may not acknowledge it.

External confirmation may arrive later.

---

## 13. External Outcome Is Not Business Outcome

A registration may be granted.

The client may still be dissatisfied with delay or cost.

A post may publish successfully.

It may produce no lead.

A provider may deliver on time.

The work may not solve the client problem.

---

## 14. Business Outcome Is Not Professional Truth

Revenue, conversion, or engagement may be commercially positive.

They do not prove:

- legal correctness;
- professional quality;
- client interest;
- ethical appropriateness.

Metrics must not reward commercially successful but professionally unsafe behavior.

---

## 15. Outcome Authority Must Be Visible

An outcome may be:

- Product-reported;
- user-reported;
- provider-reported;
- Owning Service-confirmed;
- external-system-confirmed;
- official-source-confirmed;
- inferred;
- disputed;
- stale.

The authority level controls downstream reliance.

---

## 16. Outcome Freshness Must Be Visible

An official status confirmed last month may no longer be current.

A public URL may disappear.

Provider availability may change.

Outcome records may require refresh or effective-time context.

---

## 17. Outcome May Be Partial

Examples include:

- some recipients received Delivery;
- some jurisdictions were filed;
- some classes were accepted;
- one provider deliverable was complete;
- one publication channel succeeded.

Partial outcomes should not become global success or failure.

---

## 18. Outcome May Be Unknown

The system may know:

- submission was sent;
- no confirmation received;
- user has not reported result.

Unknown is a valid state.

It is safer than invented certainty.

---

## 19. Outcome May Be Disputed

Participants may disagree about:

- completion;
- quality;
- instruction;
- fee;
- timeliness;
- responsibility;
- official effect.

The system should preserve the dispute and supporting context rather than choose a universal truth automatically.

---

## 20. Usage Is an Observation

Usage may include:

- viewed;
- opened;
- downloaded;
- copied;
- reused;
- shared;
- clicked;
- replied;
- accepted;
- formalized.

Each event has different meaning.

Usage should not be overinterpreted.

---

## 21. Engagement Is Not Acceptance

A client opening a proposal does not accept it.

A user clicking a recommendation does not create an Opportunity.

A recipient downloading a Document does not agree to its terms.

Typed decisions remain necessary.

---

## 22. Feedback Is a Governed Observation About Experience or Result

Feedback may come from:

- user;
- reviewer;
- client;
- provider;
- Product analytics;
- official outcome;
- incident;
- support team;
- quality review.

Feedback may concern:

- accuracy;
- clarity;
- usability;
- timeliness;
- cost;
- result;
- trust;
- recommendation;
- Skill;
- Template;
- Product journey.

---

## 23. Feedback Is Not Truth

Feedback may be:

- subjective;
- incomplete;
- biased;
- context-specific;
- mistaken;
- commercially motivated;
- disputed.

It should be preserved with source and scope.

---

## 24. Feedback Must Be Attributed

The system should know:

- who or what produced feedback;
- organization;
- relationship;
- subject;
- time;
- purpose;
- evidence;
- visibility;
- consent.

Anonymous aggregation may be useful later.

Raw attribution remains important for governance.

---

## 25. Feedback Scope Must Be Explicit

A client may dislike one report layout.

That does not prove the Template is globally poor.

One filing failure may involve one jurisdiction and unusual facts.

Feedback should identify the scope to which it applies.

---

## 26. Outcome Attribution Must Be Cautious

A successful result may arise from:

- professional skill;
- client cooperation;
- provider performance;
- official-office behavior;
- prior rights;
- timing;
- luck;
- several interacting factors.

The system should not assign causal credit solely because one Product or Agent participated.

---

## 27. Correlation Is Not Causation

A recommendation may precede a successful sale.

It may not have caused the sale.

Analytics should distinguish:

- sequence;
- association;
- user-reported influence;
- validated causation.

---

## 28. Feedback First Returns to the Organization

The default feedback destination is the Organization Orbit that produced or consumed the work.

It may update:

- private memory;
- preference;
- client context;
- provider relationship;
- Product practice;
- Template choice;
- internal training;
- risk signal.

Private learning should not require ecosystem publication.

---

## 29. Personal Feedback and Organization Feedback Differ

A user preference may affect Personal AI.

An organization quality decision may affect shared templates or policy.

One person’s preference should not silently become organizational standard.

---

## 30. Feedback May Create a Knowledge Candidate

Repeated, source-linked professional lessons may support:

- Knowledge Candidate;
- rule candidate;
- exception candidate;
- checklist improvement.

Professional validation and Knowledge governance determine promotion.

---

## 31. Feedback May Create a Capability Evidence Candidate

A completed collaboration may support evidence about:

- jurisdiction capability;
- response reliability;
- delivery quality;
- service scope.

One outcome remains a candidate.

CH34 and CH35 develop network use.

---

## 32. Feedback May Create a Skill Quality Signal

A renderer, extraction Skill, or Agent may show:

- accuracy;
- failure;
- latency;
- language limitations;
- data risk;
- review correction rate.

Skill governance may use these signals.

The signal does not rewrite the Skill record automatically.

---

## 33. Feedback May Improve Product Design

Product teams may learn from:

- abandonment;
- repeated missing context;
- Review corrections;
- handoff failure;
- confusing status;
- low-value recommendations;
- successful outcomes.

Product analytics should remain separate from professional audit.

---

## 34. Feedback May Improve Workflow Candidates

Repeated coordination patterns may suggest:

- new Workflow fragment;
- review gate;
- escalation;
- checklist;
- exception route.

The candidate must not become executable Workflow without governance and testing.

---

## 35. Feedback May Improve Asset and Template Governance

Feedback may identify:

- stale clause;
- wrong logo;
- poor layout;
- rights issue;
- unclear translation;
- channel mismatch.

The organization may revise or retire the Asset.

---

## 36. Feedback May Affect Trust Privately

An organization may update its private assessment of a provider or partner.

This private Trust remains organization-controlled.

It does not become public reputation by default.

---

## 37. Shared Feedback Requires Additional Governance

Before feedback enters an ecosystem-level pool, the system may need:

- organization consent;
- client confidentiality review;
- anonymization;
- dispute process;
- source validation;
- aggregation threshold;
- scope limitation.

Shared learning must not expose private relationships.

---

## 38. Negative Feedback Requires Fairness

Negative feedback may arise from:

- unclear instruction;
- client delay;
- official-office error;
- platform failure;
- provider action;
- Product defect.

The system should preserve context and allow correction or response.

---

## 39. Positive Feedback Requires Scope

A successful routine filing does not prove litigation capability.

A strong response time does not prove legal quality.

Positive evidence should remain scoped.

---

## 40. Feedback Can Expire

Provider teams change.

Products improve.

Rules change.

Templates are revised.

Old feedback may remain historically relevant but lose current predictive value.

---

## 41. Feedback Should Not Create Hidden Punishment

A participant should not be silently excluded from the network based on unexplained or unreviewed feedback.

Eligibility and Routing decisions should remain explainable.

---

## 42. Outcome Feedback May Trigger New Attention

An outcome may create:

- renewal signal;
- follow-up recommendation;
- client report;
- provider review;
- incident Task suggestion;
- maintenance candidate;
- Knowledge gap.

The signal remains candidate work until formalized.

---

## 43. Feedback Loop Must Preserve Original Outcome

Learning should not rewrite the historical record.

A later conclusion may say:

```text
The Template caused confusion.
```

The original Delivery and response remain unchanged.

New interpretation is layered onto history.

---

## 44. Outcome and Feedback Retention Differ

Formal outcomes may require long retention.

Raw analytics may require shorter retention.

Client feedback may be restricted.

Public outcome may remain observable.

Retention should follow purpose and classification.

---

## 45. The Organization May Export Its Outcome History

Portability may include:

- Artifact references;
- Delivery results;
- formal outcomes;
- private provider history;
- feedback;
- review trace;
- learning records.

Export remains subject to client, provider, and legal restrictions.

---

## 46. Ecosystem Learning Must Be Opt-In and Governed

The organization may choose to contribute:

- anonymized outcomes;
- verified capability evidence;
- Template improvements;
- Knowledge candidates;
- Product feedback.

Participation should not be the price of using the platform.

---

Owning Service Acceptance

                  │

                  │
                  ▼
Typed Outcome
  │
  ├── authority
  ├── freshness
  ├── scope
  ├── partial / unknown / disputed

        │
        ▼

        │
        ├── personal learning
        ├── organization learning
        ├── Product improvement

        ├── Capability evidence candidate

## 49. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Save-as-official

A Product output becomes formal without Owning Service acceptance.

### Internal-external collapse

Transmission is shown as official acknowledgement.

### Engagement-as-consent

Clicks and downloads become client instruction.

### Feedback-as-truth

One review creates universal ranking.

### Product-credit inflation

The Product claims causation for every successful result.

### Private-learning extraction

Organization outcomes automatically feed the shared ecosystem.

### Negative-feedback punishment

Unreviewed complaints silently exclude providers.

### Historical rewrite

Later learning changes the original outcome record.

These designs may improve metrics.

They weaken professional accountability and trust.

A conforming formalization and feedback architecture must preserve:

governed transition into

Policy, Review, approval,

Owning Service acceptance

Product, formal, Delivery,

voluntary, governed, and explainable.

A received Artifact, acknowledgement, decision, certificate, invoice or Return is evidence requiring provenance and acceptance. It may be visible immediately while remaining unaccepted as a formal business fact.

```text
received
≠ verified
≠ accepted
≠ reconciled
≠ formal-state mutation
```

The applicable authority path must verify identity, scope, source, version, completeness and unresolved conditions. Only the relevant Owning Service may record the resulting Customer, Matter, Order, Payment, filing or official-status change. Cross-Product or cross-Workplace Return remains subject to originating acceptance.

## 52. Chapter Conclusion

Part V has established the full outcome chain:

```text
Assets and Knowledge
→ Content
→ Artifact
→ Human Review
→ Render
→ Optional Edit
→ Delivery / Publish / Formalization
→ Outcome
→ Feedback
```

The chain creates value only when its boundaries remain visible.

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Artifacts carry prepared business outcomes.

Execution governs protected transitions.

Owning Services change and record formal business facts.

External systems confirm external results.

Usage provides observations.

Feedback supports scoped learning.

Organizations retain control of their history.

The ecosystem learns only through governance.
```
