A Product is a reusable governed definition and experience. It composes authorized capabilities, contracts and projections for a coherent user purpose. It does not acquire Workplace sovereignty or formal-state authority merely because it is the visible operating surface.

## Product Installation as Governed Workplace Participation

A Product Installation is the Workplace-scoped governed participation relationship through which a Product is entitled, activated, configured and authorized for use. It records the installation identity, Workplace, edition or plan, authorized users, permitted record scope, capabilities, configuration, purpose, Handoff and Return contracts, audit requirements, suspension and revocation state.

```text
entitlement
≠ activation
≠ authorized user
≠ authorized record scope
≠ formal-state authority
```

Disabling or revoking an installation stops future access, projection and Handoff within that installation. It does not erase authoritative records, completed work, audit history or legal retention duties.

## Chapter Purpose

Part III defined how Workplace consumes information, Knowledge, Intelligence, Capabilities, Skills, Assistants, Guides, AI Agents, recommendations, Human Review, and governed Execution.

Part IV now turns to the Products through which users receive direct value.

The first question is constitutional:

> Which Product principles allow Lite, MarkReg, MGSN interfaces, organization-specific applications, and future Products to remain focused and independently evolving while still preserving shared semantics, organizational continuity, governed Execution, and human accountability?

A Product is where architecture becomes a usable journey.

It is where the user:

- sees a need;
- understands an option;
- provides or confirms information;
- receives guidance;
- prepares work;
- reviews an output;
- requests a governed action;
- receives an outcome.

Products need freedom.

They need freedom to:

- choose their audience;
- define their primary problem;
- design their own interaction model;
- set their own release cadence;
- develop Product-specific logic;
- validate new user loops;
- replace implementation;
- operate embedded or standalone.

But Product freedom cannot become architectural sovereignty.

A Product must not independently redefine:

- Organization;
- Workplace;
- Customer;
- Matter;
- Task;
- Review;
- Communication;
- Opportunity;
- Routing;
- Capability;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- Artifact;
- protected-action authority.

The central proposition of this chapter is:

```text
Conforming Product
=
Clear User and Problem
+ Focused Product Loop
+ Direct User Value
+ Authorized Workplace Context
+ Shared Semantic Conformance
+ Governed Capability Consumption
+ Explicit Preparation and Handoff
+ Human Accountability
+ Outcome and Feedback
+ Independent Evolution
```

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Product ≠ Workplace

Product ≠ Core

Product ≠ Execution

Product ≠ Owning Service

Product ≠ organization identity

Product experience ≠ semantic authority

Product state ≠ formal business state

Product completion ≠ professional completion

Feature ≠ Capability

Embedding ≠ absorption

Shared foundations ≠ shared monolith

Product independence ≠ architectural isolation

Cross-Product continuity ≠ one universal Product

User convenience ≠ governance bypass

Product growth ≠ ecosystem value automatically
```

Book 02 remains authoritative for shared semantics, Core Objects, Capability, Permission, Policy, Events, and Owning Services.

Book 03 remains authoritative for Workflow, Task, Review, approval, protected-action coordination, failure, retry, and Execution.

Parts I–III of Book 04 remain authoritative for Workplace, organization context, Local Vault, Knowledge consumption, AI roles, recommendations, Prepared Action, and human responsibility.

This chapter establishes the Product principles that later Part IV chapters must preserve.

It does not define final Product requirements.

## 1. Product Is the Direct User-Value Unit

A Product is:

> a focused composition of user journeys, interaction patterns, domain context, capabilities, prepared outputs, and governed handoffs designed to create direct value for a defined user and problem.

A Product answers:

```text
Who is the user?

What problem are they trying to solve?

Which journey should they experience?

What must the Product understand?

What must the user decide?

What can the Product prepare?

Which formal action must be handed off?

What outcome should return?
```

Examples include:

- Lite;
- MarkReg;
- MGSN interfaces;
- organization-specific applications;
- future brand-professional Products.

The Product is the direct user-value unit because users do not experience architecture as a list of services.

They experience a coherent journey.

---

## 2. Product Is Not Merely a Screen Collection

A Product is not defined by:

- number of pages;
- menu structure;
- component library;
- dashboard count;
- database tables;
- API endpoints.

A screen collection may still lack:

- a clear user;
- a clear problem;
- a complete value loop;
- outcome;
- responsibility boundary.

The Product must be understood through behavior and value.

The key question is not:

```text
Which pages exist?
```

It is:

```text
Which user problem becomes meaningfully easier,
safer, faster, or more valuable?
```

---

## 3. Product Must Have a Primary User

A Product may serve several participants.

It should still identify its primary user or primary operating perspective.

Possible primary users include:

- independent trademark professional;
- small professional team;
- agency case manager;
- international trademark specialist;
- enterprise brand manager;
- external provider coordinator;
- client participant.

Without a primary user, the Product may become an accumulation of stakeholder requests.

Secondary users may have dedicated surfaces.

They should not erase the primary Product proposition.

---

## 4. Product Must Have a Primary Problem

A Product should solve a recognizable problem.

Examples:

```text
Lite:
What deserves my attention today,
and what useful action can I prepare next?

MarkReg:
How can an international trademark service
move from need to governed lifecycle completion?

MGSN interface:
Which external capability may satisfy this need,
and how can the organization prepare trusted collaboration?
```

A Product may solve related problems.

It should not become the default home of every new idea.

A clear primary problem protects focus.

---

## 5. Product Must Have a Primary Value Loop

A Product loop describes how the user repeatedly obtains value.

Examples include:

```text
Lite:
Today
→ Recommendation
→ Prepared Action
→ Confirmation
→ Handoff
→ Outcome Feedback
```

```text
MarkReg:
Need
→ Recommendation
→ Intake
→ Validation
→ Filing Preparation
→ Governed Execution
→ Lifecycle Outcome
```

```text
MGSN:
Capability Need
→ Candidate Discovery
→ Evidence and Trust Evaluation
→ Human Selection
→ Collaboration Preparation
→ Outcome Feedback
```

The Product may contain supporting loops.

The primary loop should remain visible.

---

## 6. Product Loop Must End in User-Meaningful Outcome

A Product loop is incomplete when it ends with:

- data entry;
- AI output;
- recommendation;
- draft generation;
- internal status;
- API success.

The loop should produce a user-meaningful outcome such as:

- missed work prevented;
- professional decision improved;
- service need clarified;
- filing prepared;
- collaboration selected;
- client Communication delivered;
- formal result recorded;
- reusable Artifact produced;
- organizational learning captured.

Technical completion is not enough.

---

## 7. Product Must Pass the Five Value Tests

Every Product should demonstrate contribution to one or more MarkOrbit value tests:

```text
1. Reduce Missed Work

2. Improve Professional Judgment

3. Accelerate Service Conversion

4. Improve Delivery Quality

5. Accumulate Organizational Capability
```

A Product does not need to optimize all five equally.

It should identify which value it primarily creates.

Features that do not support a meaningful value loop should be questioned.

---

## 8. Product Value Must Be Measured Beyond Engagement

Engagement may be useful.

It is not sufficient.

Useful Product measures may include:

- deadlines prevented from being missed;
- time from need to prepared action;
- reduction in incomplete intake;
- review correction rate;
- service conversion quality;
- delivery success;
- client response;
- Artifact reuse;
- organizational learning;
- provider collaboration outcome.

Clicks, session length, and generated content may not reflect professional value.

A Product should not optimize for attention at the expense of professional outcomes.

---

## 9. Product Must Preserve Client and Professional Interest

A Product may support commercial conversion.

It must not manipulate professional decisions to maximize:

- platform revenue;
- service purchase;
- provider commission;
- engagement;
- data capture.

Professional and client interests must remain visible.

Where a recommendation is commercial or sponsored by organizational preference, its nature should not be disguised as neutral professional necessity.

Product value must remain compatible with professional responsibility.

---

## 10. Product Owns the User Journey

A Product may own:

- navigation;
- interaction model;
- Product-specific forms;
- Product-specific views;
- Product-specific recommendation presentation;
- journey progress;
- Product-level drafts;
- Product-specific business logic;
- Product analytics;
- Product help and explanation.

This is meaningful ownership.

It allows Products to innovate independently.

But journey ownership is not semantic or formal-state ownership.

---

## 11. Product Does Not Own the Organization

A Product may consume:

- organization identity;
- branding;
- users;
- roles;
- clients;
- pricing;
- private Knowledge;
- partner relationships;
- preferences.

It must not create a competing representation of the organization as its own sovereign account.

The organization operates through Workplace.

Products serve that organization.

The rule is:

```text
Workplace preserves the organization.

Product serves a focused journey.
```

---

## 12. Product Does Not Own the Client Relationship

A Product may display and use client context.

It does not become the owner of:

- client identity;
- engagement;
- relationship history;
- communication preference;
- commercial ownership;
- organization responsibility.

Client continuity should survive Product replacement.

A Product-specific client profile may exist as a projection or extension.

It must not fragment the organization’s client relationship.

---

## 13. Product Does Not Own Shared Semantics

A Product must consume the accepted meaning of:

- Task;
- Matter;
- Opportunity;
- Communication;
- Review;
- Event;
- Routing;
- Document;
- Evidence;
- Capability.

It may present these concepts differently.

It may not redefine them for UI convenience.

The rule is:

```text
Product experience may vary.

Shared semantic meaning may not drift.
```

---

## 14. Product Does Not Own Governed Execution

A Product may:

- capture intent;
- prepare input;
- preview Workflow;
- show Task state;
- present Review;
- request a protected action.

It must not invent its own alternative semantics for:

- Workflow state;
- Task completion;
- approval;
- retry;
- failure;
- external Communication send;
- filing;
- payment.

Execution remains authoritative for governed coordination.

---

## 15. Product Does Not Own Formal Business Facts

A Product may display and prepare changes to formal objects.

The relevant Owning Service remains authoritative for mutation.

The constitutional chain is:

```text
Product captures intent and prepares.

Execution validates and coordinates.

Owning Service changes formal state.
```

A Product must not show formal success before the Owning Service confirms it.

---

## 16. Product-Specific State Must Be Explicit

Products need local state.

Examples include:

- current Guide step;
- unsaved form state;
- local draft;
- dismissed recommendation;
- Product filter;
- display preference;
- onboarding progress;
- selected tab.

This state is legitimate.

It must remain distinguishable from:

- Task status;
- Matter status;
- Review decision;
- Communication send state;
- Opportunity qualification;
- Routing decision;
- official filing state.

The distinction is:

```text
Product interaction state
≠
formal business state
```

---

## 17. Product Completion Must Have Precise Meaning

A Product may display completion such as:

- intake complete;
- draft prepared;
- Guide complete;
- review submitted;
- request handed off.

Each label should describe the exact layer completed.

“Complete” must not imply:

- professional review completed;
- filing completed;
- payment completed;
- client instruction received;
- external outcome confirmed.

Precise language prevents authority drift.

---

## 18. Product Must Use Candidate Before Canonical

Products frequently create:

- extracted fields;
- recommendations;
- opportunity candidates;
- classification suggestions;
- provider matches;
- document drafts;
- AI summaries.

These remain candidates until validated through the appropriate authority.

A Product must not promote them because:

- the model is confident;
- the UI needs a value;
- the user did not object;
- similar results were accepted before.

Candidate status must remain visible where material.

---

## 19. Product Must Separate Recommend, Prepare, Confirm, and Execute

The shared interaction pattern is:

```text
Observe
→ Explain
→ Recommend
→ Prepare
→ Confirm
→ Execute
→ Learn
```

A Product may compress low-risk steps.

It must preserve semantic distinction.

In particular:

```text
Recommendation ≠ decision

Preparation ≠ approval

Confirmation ≠ formal mutation

Execution request ≠ completion
```

Protected actions must remain governed.

---

## 20. Product Must Preserve Human Accountability

A Product may simplify complex work.

It must not obscure:

- who made the professional judgment;
- who approved;
- what AI contributed;
- which version was reviewed;
- which service acted.

Human responsibility must remain explicit for consequential work.

A smoother interface is not a reason to erase accountability.

---

## 21. Product Must Expose AI Participation Where Material

AI may assist through:

- Assistant;
- Guide;
- Agent;
- Skill;
- recommendation;
- extraction;
- drafting;
- validation.

The Product should preserve material AI trace.

It need not overwhelm users with technical detail.

It should make clear where output:

- is AI-generated;
- remains uncertain;
- uses restricted context;
- requires review;
- has not been executed.

Persona continuity must not hide Agent identity from governance.

---

## 22. Product Must Consume Authorized Workplace Context

A Product may need:

- active organization;
- user and role;
- client;
- Matter;
- private Knowledge;
- business rules;
- Product entitlement;
- reviewer eligibility;
- data policy;
- partner history.

This context should be purpose-bound.

The Product must not request the entire Workplace context by default.

Authorized context enables coherence without creating unrestricted access.

---

## 23. Product Must Minimize Data Consumption

A Product should receive the minimum sufficient context.

It may use:

- references;
- Metadata;
- safe summaries;
- selected fields;
- temporary access;
- local processing.

Product convenience does not justify:

- full client-file replication;
- copying Local Vault;
- unrestricted organizational memory;
- broad AI context.

Data minimization is a Product architecture principle, not only a security feature.

---

## 24. Product Must Respect Local and Private Boundaries

A Product may operate:

- in shared infrastructure;
- locally;
- privately;
- embedded;
- hybrid.

It must respect Workplace data policy.

A shared Product may consume a local result without receiving the full local source.

A local Product does not receive automatic authority.

Location changes implementation.

It does not change the constitutional model.

---

## 25. Product Must Consume Capabilities Through Explicit Boundaries

A Product may consume:

- Knowledge retrieval;
- classification;
- document generation;
- provider matching;
- AI drafting;
- Artifact rendering;
- Delivery preparation.

The Product should identify:

- Capability;
- eligible Skill;
- input and output;
- data boundary;
- review requirement;
- failure behavior.

A Product feature is not automatically a new shared Capability.

---

## 26. Product Feature and Shared Capability Must Remain Distinct

Example:

```text
Shared Capability:
Classification Recommendation

MarkReg Feature:
Class Recommendation Journey

Lite Feature:
Quick Class Insight Card
```

Both Products may consume the same Capability differently.

The Product owns presentation and composition.

The Capability retains stable governed meaning.

---

## 27. Product Must Support Replaceable Implementation

A Product should not depend permanently on one:

- AI model;
- provider;
- parser;
- rendering engine;
- data source;
- Skill;
- deployment.

Implementation may change.

The user loop and shared contracts should remain stable where possible.

Replaceable implementation supports resilience and controlled evolution.

---

## 28. Product Loop First, Shared Platform Extraction Second

Shared responsibilities should not be extracted before the Product loop proves them.

The sequence is:

```text
Identify user problem

Define Product loop

Build and test focused behavior

Observe repeated responsibility

Stabilize meaning and contract

Extract shared capability where justified
```

Premature extraction can create:

- generic services with no validated consumer;
- overbroad schemas;
- false consistency;
- slow Product learning;
- architecture built around assumptions.

Product architecture should protect learning.

---

## 29. Reuse Must Be Earned by Repetition

Two similar functions do not automatically justify one shared service.

Shared extraction should consider:

- repeated use;
- stable meaning;
- stable ownership;
- compatible risk;
- compatible data boundary;
- multiple real consumers;
- replaceability value.

Sometimes duplicated early implementation is safer than premature abstraction.

Architectural consistency should be preserved even before technical reuse.

---

## 30. Product Independence Must Not Become Isolation

A Product should be able to evolve independently.

It should not:

- recreate organization identity;
- recreate Permission;
- recreate Task semantics;
- copy all Knowledge;
- bypass shared contracts;
- hide outcomes from Workplace.

Independence means controlled ownership of the Product journey.

It does not mean architectural disconnection.

CH21 develops this relationship in detail.

---

## 31. Shared Foundations Must Not Become a Shared Monolith

Products may consume shared:

- semantics;
- organization context;
- Execution;
- Knowledge;
- Capabilities;
- Artifact rules;
- network interfaces.

This does not require:

- one codebase;
- one release;
- one frontend;
- one database;
- one Product team;
- one deployment.

Logical sharing and technical centralization are different decisions.

---

## 32. Product May Be Standalone, Embedded, or Hybrid

A Product may operate:

- as a standalone application;
- embedded within Workplace;
- linked through a handoff;
- locally;
- as a hybrid experience.

The form may differ by organization and Product maturity.

The authority model must remain stable.

Embedding should not cause Workplace to absorb Product ownership.

Standalone operation should not cause Product to recreate Workplace.

CH22 develops embedding in detail.

---

## 33. Product Must Preserve Context at Entry

When a user enters a Product, it should know or request the appropriate:

- organization;
- user;
- role;
- client or Matter;
- purpose;
- Product entitlement;
- data boundary.

The Product must not silently inherit stale context from another Product or tab.

Entry context should be visible where confusion could cause professional risk.

---

## 34. Product Must Preserve Context at Exit

When a Product hands work to another Product, Workplace, or Execution, it should preserve:

- source Product;
- organization;
- subject references;
- user intent;
- selected options;
- source and version;
- Prepared Action;
- review requirement;
- outcome expectation.

Exit should not be an unstructured redirect.

CH27 develops cross-Product handoffs in detail.

---

## 35. Cross-Product Continuity Must Use References and Contracts

Products should not achieve continuity by copying full internal state into one another.

They should use:

- stable references;
- authorized context packages;
- typed handoffs;
- Event and result trace;
- shared semantic contracts.

This preserves Product independence and reduces hidden coupling.

---

## 36. Product Must Return Outcomes to Workplace

A Product loop should return relevant outcomes to the organization’s operating context.

Examples include:

- formal result;
- pending review;
- failed action;
- delivered Artifact;
- provider outcome;
- learning signal;
- next recommendation.

Workplace should not need to inspect each Product manually to know what happened.

Outcome visibility does not transfer ownership to Workplace.

---

## 37. Product Must Preserve Provenance and Explainability

A Product should preserve, where relevant:

- source;
- versions;
- Product origin;
- AI contribution;
- Skill;
- reviewer;
- approval;
- Owning Service;
- external result.

The user should be able to understand why a recommendation appeared and what happened after confirmation.

Product convenience must not erase professional trace.

---

## 38. Product Failure Must Be Visible and Layer-Specific

A Product may fail at:

- input;
- validation;
- Capability;
- Skill;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- Review;
- Execution;
- Owning Service;
- external system.

The Product should not present every failure as:

```text
Something went wrong.
```

It should indicate:

- what is blocked;
- whether formal state changed;
- whether retry is safe;
- which layer requires attention;
- whether the user must re-prepare.

Failure visibility is part of professional safety.

---

## 39. Product Must Avoid Optimistic Formal Success

Optimistic UI can improve speed for low-risk interaction state.

It must not claim formal success before authoritative confirmation.

The Product must not display:

- sent;
- filed;
- paid;
- appointed;
- approved;
- completed;

when only a local action or request occurred.

Formal success comes from the appropriate Owning Service and, where required, external confirmation.

---

## 40. Product Must Support Versioned Evolution

Products will change.

Versioned evolution should preserve:

- organizational continuity;
- formal references;
- historical outcomes;
- user understanding;
- migration;
- rollback where appropriate;
- coexistence where necessary.

A Product upgrade should not require destructive replacement of Workplace identity, client history, or formal records.

---

## 41. Product Replacement Must Not Replace the Organization

A Product may be deprecated or replaced.

The organization should retain:

- identity;
- clients;
- private Knowledge;
- relationships;
- Tasks and Matters;
- Artifacts;
- audit;
- network participation;
- outcomes.

The principle is:

```text
Product may change.

The Orbit remains.
```

This is a core benefit of Workplace.

---

## 42. Product Editions Must Not Create Authority Drift

A Product may offer editions or configurations for:

- individual professional;
- small team;
- agency;
- enterprise;
- provider;
- local/private deployment.

Edition variation may change:

- features;
- complexity;
- limits;
- support;
- configuration.

It must not create weaker constitutional rules.

A low-cost edition must not remove required Human Review.

A lightweight interface must not redefine Task or approval.

---

## 43. Product Governance Must Remain Proportionate

Not every Product action requires maximum governance.

Low-risk interaction may remain simple.

High-consequence action requires stronger controls.

Governance should be proportionate to:

- data sensitivity;
- professional consequence;
- external effect;
- reversibility;
- financial impact;
- legal risk.

Proportionate governance avoids both unsafe shortcuts and unnecessary friction.

---

## 44. Product Architecture Must Support Deliberate Scope

Product teams must be able to say:

```text
This belongs in the Product.

This belongs in Workplace.

This belongs in a shared Capability.

This belongs in Execution.

This belongs in an Owning Service.

This does not belong in the current scope.
```

Scope discipline is an architectural capability.

A Product that accepts every adjacent responsibility becomes a monolith.

The Product owns the focused journey.

It does not own every layer in the chain.

## 47. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Mega-Product architecture

Workplace, Lite, MarkReg, MGSN, Execution, and shared services collapse into one application.

### Screen-driven Product definition

Menus and pages exist without a coherent user-value loop.

### Feature accumulation

Every adjacent idea enters the Product without scope discipline.

### Product-owned organization

Organization identity and client relationships become tied to one application.

### Product-owned semantics

Lite and MarkReg define different meanings for Task, Review, or Communication.

### Product-owned Execution

The UI directly performs protected state mutation.

### Product-state laundering

Guide or form completion appears as formal professional completion.

### AI-first Product

Available models and Tools define the Product rather than user need.

### Engagement optimization

Clicks and generated content replace professional outcome.

### Premature platform extraction

Generic shared services are created before Product loops prove value.

### Shared monolith

Reuse requires one codebase, release, or database.

### Isolated Product silo

The Product recreates Workplace, Knowledge, Permission, and audit locally.

### Embedding absorption

An embedded Product loses its Product boundary and becomes arbitrary Workplace UI.

### Optimistic formal success

The Product claims sent, filed, paid, or completed before authority confirms it.

### Destructive Product replacement

Upgrading or replacing one Product destroys organizational continuity.

These designs may appear productive in the short term.

They do not conform to MarkOrbit architecture.

## 48. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming MarkOrbit Product must preserve the following lock:

```text
The Product identifies a primary user,
primary problem, and primary value loop.

The Product creates direct professional
or organizational value.

The Product owns its focused journey
and Product-specific interaction state.

The Product does not own
the Organization, Workplace,
shared semantics, governed Execution,
or formal business facts.

Product experience may vary,
but semantic and authority boundaries may not drift.

The Product consumes only authorized
and minimum sufficient Workplace context.

Candidate outputs remain candidates.

Recommend, prepare, confirm,
execute, and learn remain distinguishable.

Protected actions enter governed Execution.

Human accountability remains explicit.

Formal mutation remains with Owning Services.

Product features consume Capabilities
without redefining them.

Implementation remains replaceable.

Product loops are validated
before shared platform extraction.

Product independence does not become isolation.

Shared foundations do not require a monolith.

Standalone, embedded, local,
and hybrid forms preserve the same authority model.

Cross-Product continuity uses
stable references and typed handoffs.

Outcomes return to Workplace visibility
without transferring domain ownership.

Failure and formal success remain precise.

Products may evolve or be replaced
without replacing the organization.
```

A commercially successful Product that violates this lock does not conform architecturally.

## 50. Chapter Conclusion

Part IV begins with Product discipline.

Products are necessary because shared architecture does not create user value by itself.

Users need focused journeys.

They need Lite to help organize attention and prepare action.

They need MarkReg to support the international trademark lifecycle.

They need MGSN interfaces to discover and evaluate external capability.

They may need organization-specific applications and future Products.

Each Product should be free to:

- serve its own user;
- solve its own problem;
- define its own loop;
- choose its own experience;
- evolve at its own speed.

But each Product must remain within the constitutional architecture.

The Product does not become the organization.

It does not become Core.

It does not become Execution.

It does not become the Owning Service.

The complete principle is:

```text
Product finds and proves value.

Workplace preserves organizational continuity.

Core preserves shared meaning.

Execution preserves governed coordination.

Owning Services preserve formal authority.

Humans preserve professional responsibility.
```

The Product architecture can therefore be summarized as:

```text
Focused journey
+ shared foundations
+ authorized context
+ governed handoff
+ accountable outcome
```

CH21 now develops the tension at the center of Part IV:

> How can Products remain independently owned, designed, released, deployed, and replaced while still consuming stable shared semantics, governance, identity, Knowledge, Capabilities, Artifact rules, and organizational context?
