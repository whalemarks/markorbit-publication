# B04-CH-26 — Workplace Editions and Organization-Specific Applications

**Status:** Draft 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part IV — Product Architecture and Product Embedding

## Chapter Purpose

Chapters 20–25 established:

- Product architecture principles;
- Product independence and shared foundations;
- Product embedding and cross-Product context;
- Lite as a lightweight Workplace;
- MarkReg as the flagship trademark application;
- MGSN Gateway as the Workplace-side network interface.

This chapter addresses organizational variation.

The central question is:

> How can solo professionals, small teams, agencies, enterprises, service providers, local deployments, and organization-specific applications use different Workplace editions without creating constitutional authority drift, semantic forks, duplicated organizational truth, or incompatible Product ecosystems?

Organizations differ materially.

A sole trademark professional may need:

- one primary user;
- simple client context;
- personal AI preferences;
- local case search;
- Today and lightweight review.

A growing agency may need:

- sales and process roles;
- team review;
- service pricing;
- provider coordination;
- workload visibility;
- cross-Product continuity.

An enterprise legal department may need:

- portfolio governance;
- internal business-unit access;
- legal review;
- approval chains;
- data residency;
- integration with enterprise systems.

A foreign associate or specialist provider may need:

- service coverage;
- incoming instructions;
- conflict review;
- delivery;
- invoices;
- MGSN participation.

These organizations should not be forced into one identical interface or operating model.

They must still share the same constitutional architecture.

The central proposition is:

```text
Workplace Edition
=
Stable Workplace Constitution
+ Organization-Type Profile
+ Product Composition
+ Progressive Complexity
+ Role and Review Configuration
+ Data and Deployment Boundary
+ Integration Profile
+ Network Participation Profile
+ Support and Operational Controls
```

An organization-specific application means:

> a Product or application created for the focused operating needs of one organization or organization class while consuming shared Workplace context, Core semantics, governed Execution, shared Capabilities, and formal Owning Services through explicit contracts.

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Edition ≠ Product

Edition ≠ Organization

Edition ≠ legal entity type

Edition ≠ subscription plan only

Edition ≠ deployment topology

Edition ≠ Permission model

Edition ≠ weaker constitution

Organization-specific application ≠ private Core

Customization ≠ semantic fork

Configuration ≠ authority

Feature absence ≠ authority absence

Hidden feature ≠ disabled governance

Solo mode ≠ no accountability

Enterprise mode ≠ unlimited access

Local edition ≠ ungoverned edition

Provider edition ≠ MGSN ownership

White label ≠ identity substitution

Dedicated deployment ≠ dedicated semantics
```

Every edition must preserve:

- Organization as the real professional and commercial actor;
- Workplace as the organization operating boundary;
- Core as shared semantic authority;
- Execution as governed coordination;
- Products as focused journeys;
- MGSN as the connection layer;
- Owning Services as formal mutation authorities;
- human professionals as accountable reviewers and approvers;
- AI Agents as governed assistants.

The accepted chapter map treats editions and organization-specific applications here as architectural profiles.

It does not define final commercial packages, pricing plans, licensing, deployment SKUs, or implementation roadmaps.

---

## 1. Why Workplace Requires Editions

Workplace serves organizations with different:

- size;
- structure;
- risk;
- service scope;
- Product needs;
- data requirements;
- integration maturity;
- network participation;
- professional obligations.

One fixed interface would create two bad outcomes.

A minimal interface could fail complex organizations.

A universal enterprise interface could overwhelm independent professionals.

Editions allow the visible and operational complexity to vary while preserving one architectural model.

---

## 2. Edition Is a Configuration of Experience and Capability

A Workplace Edition may determine:

- default Products;
- available surfaces;
- role templates;
- review patterns;
- reporting depth;
- integration options;
- deployment modes;
- network interfaces;
- support controls;
- data policies.

Edition expresses a packaged operating profile.

It does not create a separate constitutional system.

---

## 3. Edition Is Not a Product

A Product solves a focused user problem through a Product loop.

An Edition composes which Products and Workplace capabilities are available for an organization profile.

Example:

```text
Professional Edition
→ may include Lite
→ may launch MarkReg
→ may expose limited MGSN Gateway
```

Lite remains Lite.

MarkReg remains MarkReg.

The Edition does not absorb their Product ownership.

---

## 4. Edition Is Not the Organization

The organization remains the real actor.

Edition does not define:

- legal identity;
- professional qualification;
- client authority;
- contractual responsibility;
- regulatory status.

Two law firms may use the same Edition and remain entirely independent Orbits.

One organization may change Edition without becoming a different organization.

---

## 5. Edition Is Not Legal Entity Type

A sole practice, company, department, branch, or law firm may use similar Product compositions.

Software Edition must not infer legal status.

Legal and professional authority requires independent representation and verification.

A label such as `Enterprise Edition` does not prove enterprise authority.

---

## 6. Edition Is Not a Subscription Plan Only

Commercial packaging may align with Editions.

But the architectural meaning is broader.

A subscription plan may define:

- price;
- user limit;
- storage;
- support;
- usage allowance.

An Edition may additionally define:

- operating assumptions;
- Product composition;
- governance defaults;
- role complexity;
- deployment profile.

Commercial packaging must not redefine architecture.

---

## 7. Edition Is Not Deployment Topology

An Edition may support several deployments.

For example:

```text
Agency Edition
→ shared cloud
→ dedicated cloud
→ hybrid
→ private deployment
```

Deployment answers where and how components run.

Edition answers which operating profile is presented.

The two may be related.

They are not identical.

---

## 8. Edition Is Not a Separate Permission Model

All Editions use the same fundamental distinctions:

- Identity;
- Role;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- Capability;
- Human Review;
- Approval;
- Execution authority.

A lightweight Edition may expose fewer configuration screens.

It must not implement weaker Permission semantics.

---

## 9. Edition Is Not a Weaker Constitution

A low-complexity Edition must still preserve:

```text
Candidate Before Canonical

Human Review where required

Permission and Policy

Prepared Action before protected execution

Execution before formal mutation

Owning Service authority

source and provenance

data-boundary controls
```

Governance may be simplified through defaults.

It cannot be removed.

---

## 10. Edition Must Follow Progressive Complexity

The Edition model should expose only the complexity required by the organization.

Progressive complexity may mean:

- simple defaults first;
- advanced controls on demand;
- role templates;
- guided setup;
- limited administrative surfaces;
- gradual Product enablement.

This reduces cognitive burden without weakening underlying boundaries.

---

## 11. Edition Should Be Composable

A Workplace Edition may be composed from profiles such as:

```text
Organization Profile

Product Profile

Role and Review Profile

Data Boundary Profile

Deployment Profile

Integration Profile

Network Participation Profile

Operational Support Profile
```

Composition supports variation without creating one Edition for every combination.

---

## 12. Organization Profile Describes Operating Shape

An Organization Profile may describe:

- solo professional;
- small team;
- agency;
- enterprise department;
- service provider;
- specialist organization;
- distributed team;
- branch network.

This profile influences defaults.

It does not become a new Core organization type unless shared semantics require it.

---

## 13. Product Profile Defines Enabled Journeys

A Product Profile may include:

- Lite;
- MarkReg;
- MGSN Gateway;
- organization-specific applications;
- future Products.

Product enablement may be:

- full;
- limited;
- read-only;
- trial;
- local;
- unavailable.

Product access does not grant access to every subject or action inside the Product.

---

## 14. Role and Review Profile Defines Operational Defaults

A Role and Review Profile may provide default patterns such as:

```text
Solo Responsible Professional

Preparer + Reviewer

Sales + Process + Finance + Administrator

Business User + Legal Reviewer + Approver

Instructing Organization + Performing Provider
```

These are defaults.

Actual Permission and reviewer eligibility remain contextual.

---

A Data Boundary Profile may determine defaults for:

- Local Vault;
- raw-file synchronization;
- Metadata synchronization;
- AI processing;
- data residency;
- retention;
- export;
- backup;
- external sharing.

The profile constrains Product behavior.

It does not replace operation-specific Policy evaluation.

---

## 16. Deployment Profile Defines Technical Placement

A Deployment Profile may describe:

- shared cloud;
- dedicated cloud;
- private cloud;
- on-premises;
- local-first;
- hybrid;
- disconnected preparation mode.

Technical placement must preserve:

- identity;
- authority;
- source;
- version;
- synchronization rules;
- current-state validation.

---

## 17. Integration Profile Defines External-System Connections

An Integration Profile may include:

- email;
- calendar;
- document storage;
- finance system;
- CRM;
- identity provider;
- local database;
- official trademark data;
- browser extension;
- local agent;
- enterprise data platform.

Integration availability does not transfer ownership.

External systems must enter through explicit mapping and authority boundaries.

---

## 18. Network Participation Profile Defines MGSN Exposure

A Network Participation Profile may control:

- private partner use;
- trusted extended network access;
- limited public discovery;
- network identity fields;
- Capability visibility;
- evidence sharing;
- outcome feedback;
- provider-side participation.

Network participation remains voluntary and organization-controlled.

---

## 19. Operational Support Profile Defines Administration Depth

An Operational Support Profile may provide:

- onboarding;
- configuration assistance;
- audit support;
- migration tools;
- organization administration;
- service-level support;
- incident response;
- compliance controls.

Support depth may vary by Edition.

Professional authority does not come from the support tier.

---

## 20. A Solo Professional Edition Should Minimize Administration

A solo professional typically does not need:

- complex department hierarchies;
- broad user provisioning;
- large approval matrices;
- enterprise reporting.

The Edition may default to:

- one organization;
- one responsible professional;
- Lite as the primary shell;
- simple client context;
- personal and organization memory distinction;
- local case support;
- MarkReg handoff;
- limited MGSN Gateway.

The constitutional decisions remain visible even if performed by one human.

---

## 21. Solo Does Not Mean Personal Account Only

A solo professional still operates through an organization context.

They may have:

- legal or trade identity;
- clients;
- professional qualifications;
- pricing;
- private providers;
- brand;
- records;
- obligations.

The Edition must not collapse the organization into a consumer-profile account.

---

## 22. Solo Does Not Mean No Review

The same human may prepare, review, approve, and request execution where permitted.

The system should preserve each decision separately.

This supports:

- audit;
- historical explanation;
- future team growth;
- professional accountability.

One person does not require one undifferentiated action.

---

## 23. Small-Team Edition Should Add Collaboration Gradually

A small-team Edition may add:

- invitations;
- shared clients;
- assignment;
- review queue;
- organization templates;
- shared Knowledge;
- provider visibility;
- team Today;
- basic workload coordination.

It should avoid introducing enterprise administration before it creates value.

---

## 24. Small-Team Edition Still Requires Role Boundaries

A small team may include:

- business development;
- case manager;
- professional reviewer;
- finance;
- owner.

The same person may hold several roles.

Permission and Review remain action-specific.

Team size is not an authority rule.

---

## 25. Agency Edition Should Support Commercial and Professional Separation

An agency may require:

- lead and client continuity;
- service catalog;
- quotation;
- Order;
- Matter;
- sales ownership;
- process responsibility;
- provider coordination;
- finance visibility;
- management reporting.

The Edition may expose more Workplace surfaces.

It must not combine commercial conversion and professional approval into one authority.

---

## 26. Agency Edition Should Preserve Client Ownership

An agency’s clients, contacts, pricing, and history remain organization-controlled.

Product use and MGSN participation do not transfer:

- client ownership;
- relationship goodwill;
- private provider list;
- private commercial terms.

The Edition should make this boundary operational.

---

## 27. Agency Edition Should Support Team Review and Escalation

Agency work may require:

- junior preparation;
- senior review;
- jurisdiction specialist review;
- finance approval;
- management escalation;
- external provider review.

The Edition may provide richer queues and dashboards.

Execution and Review services remain authoritative.

---

## 28. Enterprise Edition Should Support Internal Organizational Complexity

An enterprise may include:

- legal department;
- brand team;
- business units;
- finance;
- procurement;
- regional offices;
- external counsel;
- executive approvers.

The Edition may need:

- scoped internal access;
- portfolio views;
- cost centers;
- internal request journeys;
- approval chains;
- legal review;
- external-provider coordination;
- enterprise integrations.

This does not justify universal access to all brand and Matter data.

---

## 29. Enterprise Edition Must Preserve Purpose-Limited Access

Enterprise membership alone should not grant access to:

- all trademarks;
- all business units;
- all Evidence;
- all external counsel;
- all financial data;
- all private strategy.

Context should remain limited by:

- role;
- business unit;
- region;
- Matter;
- purpose;
- data classification;
- Policy.

---

## 30. Enterprise Edition Must Not Become Central Surveillance

Management may need aggregated insight into:

- portfolio risk;
- deadline exposure;
- service cost;
- provider performance;
- review backlog;
- lifecycle progress.

Aggregation should minimize unnecessary individual monitoring.

The Edition must not turn professional trace into indiscriminate employee surveillance.

---

## 31. Enterprise Edition Must Preserve Business-Unit Autonomy Where Required

One enterprise may contain semi-independent units.

They may share:

- organization identity;
- selected Products;
- shared providers;
- portfolio standards.

They may retain separate:

- clients or brands;
- budgets;
- private strategy;
- reviewers;
- data boundaries.

The Workplace model should support scoped internal Orbits without inventing competing Core semantics.

---

## 32. Service Provider Edition Has a Different Primary Journey

A provider-facing Edition may focus on:

- service profile;
- Capability evidence;
- incoming requests;
- conflict checks;
- quotation;
- instruction;
- delivery;
- status reporting;
- invoice;
- outcome feedback.

It remains an Organization Workplace.

It is not simply a seller account in a marketplace.

---

## 33. Provider Edition Does Not Make the Provider Network-Owned

The provider retains:

- organization identity;
- professional responsibility;
- service catalog;
- pricing;
- relationship ownership;
- client and instructing-organization context;
- private Knowledge.

MGSN connects the provider.

It does not absorb the provider into platform inventory.

---

## 34. Provider Edition Must Separate Incoming Interest from Engagement

A network inquiry may be:

- exploratory;
- quotation request;
- conflict check;
- capability question;
- formal instruction candidate.

It does not become an active engagement automatically.

The provider may accept, decline, request information, or identify conflict.

---

## 35. Provider Edition Must Support Delivery Authority

The provider may deliver:

- quotation;
- advice;
- filing receipt;
- response draft;
- certificate;
- invoice;
- status report.

Delivery should preserve:

- responsible professional;
- organization;
- Matter or request;
- version;
- source;
- review;
- external confirmation.

Provider upload alone does not prove acceptance by the instructing organization.

---

## 36. Local-First Edition Supports Private Operation

A local-first Edition may prioritize:

- Local Vault;
- local database;
- private AI;
- local case retrieval;
- local document preparation;
- limited synchronization;
- explicit export.

This supports organizations with strong privacy or infrastructure needs.

Local-first does not mean isolated from shared semantics.

---

## 37. Local Edition Does Not Create Local Authority Automatically

A local component may hold data.

It is authoritative only where the architecture and contract assign ownership.

A local copy of:

- Task;
- Matter;
- Communication;
- provider status;
- official filing status;

does not automatically become authoritative formal business state.

Synchronization and reconciliation remain governed.

---

## 38. Private Deployment Does Not Create Private Semantics

A dedicated or private deployment may use organization-specific infrastructure.

It must still preserve shared meanings for:

- Task;
- Review;
- Communication;
- Opportunity;
- Routing;
- Capability;
- Artifact;
- Event.

Dedicated infrastructure cannot redefine constitutional concepts.

---

## 39. Hybrid Edition Must Define Which Boundary Owns What

A hybrid model may combine:

- shared Workplace services;
- local Vault;
- local AI;
- cloud Products;
- dedicated execution connector;
- enterprise system of record.

The Edition must identify:

- source of truth;
- cache;
- local working copy;
- synchronized projection;
- formal mutation route;
- conflict behavior;
- offline limitation.

Hybrid convenience must not create ambiguous authority.

---

## 40. Disconnected Preparation Mode Is Not a Full Edition Authority

A disconnected environment may support:

- local reading;
- local drafting;
- Artifact preparation;
- cached Knowledge;
- offline Guide state.

It should not claim current:

- Permission;
- Policy;
- reviewer eligibility;
- provider availability;
- official status;
- execution success.

---

## 41. Organization-Specific Application Solves a Focused Need

An organization-specific application may support:

- local source-file management;
- internal case analysis;
- custom quotation;
- internal intake;
- specialized reporting;
- organization-specific portfolio view;
- private provider management;
- custom business Artifact generation.

It is a Product or application.

It is not a second Workplace or private Core.

---

## 42. Organization-Specific Application Must Have a Product Boundary

The application should define:

- user;
- problem;
- Product loop;
- input;
- output;
- Product-owned state;
- shared dependencies;
- handoff;
- outcome.

A custom application without a boundary tends to absorb every adjacent responsibility.

---

## 43. Customization Must Not Redefine Shared Semantics

An organization may use different labels.

For example:

```text
Case
Project
Instruction
Job
Service File
```

The application must map these terms carefully to:

- Matter;
- Task;
- Order;
- Workflow;
- Product state;
- organization-specific object.

A custom label does not change the canonical meaning.

---

## 44. Organization-Specific Fields Must Remain Namespaced

Custom applications may need fields such as:

- internal sales code;
- local department;
- custom client tier;
- special reviewer group;
- organization-specific fee note.

These fields should remain organization- or Product-specific.

Repeated use does not make them Core automatically.

---

## 45. Organization-Specific Rules Belong in Organization Context

Custom rules may include:

- pricing;
- reviewer assignment;
- provider preference;
- document naming;
- risk threshold;
- communication style;
- escalation;
- approval threshold.

They belong in organization context or Product configuration.

They must not redefine shared status or permission semantics.

---

## 46. Organization-Specific Workflow Does Not Redefine Workflow

An organization may configure:

- review order;
- internal approval;
- provider step;
- document checklist;
- escalation.

Execution still owns Workflow progression and Task lifecycle.

A custom workflow is a governed Workflow application or configuration.

It is not a Product-maintained status sequence.

---

## 47. Custom Integration Requires an Anti-Corruption Boundary

Legacy or enterprise systems may use ambiguous concepts.

Examples:

- `case`;
- `project`;
- `customer`;
- `owner`;
- `status`;
- `completion`.

An adapter should determine actual meaning before mapping.

Field similarity is not semantic equivalence.

The anti-corruption boundary protects MarkOrbit concepts from external drift.

---

An organization-specific application may consume:

- Knowledge;
- AI drafting;
- document extraction;
- classification;
- provider matching;
- rendering;

- local search.

It should use explicit Capability and Skill boundaries.

Custom need does not justify unrestricted Tool access.

---

## 49. Custom AI Must Still Be a Governed Agent

An organization may configure:

- private Assistant;
- local Agent;

- Agent Contract;

## 50. White Label Changes Presentation, Not Acting Identity

An Edition or Product may use:

- organization brand;
- domain;
- colors;
- templates;
- sender identity;
- client-facing interface.

White labeling must preserve the real acting organization.

It must not:

- hide the responsible legal entity;
- impersonate another organization;
- misstate professional qualification;
- obscure external provider responsibility.

Presentation may vary.

Identity truth must remain accurate.

---

## 51. Dedicated Instance Does Not Mean Isolated Governance

An organization may receive a dedicated instance.

It may have:

- custom domain;
- dedicated storage;
- separate deployment;

The instance still conforms to shared constitutional rules.

Technical isolation does not authorize semantic divergence.

## 57. Edition Change Must Not Retroactively Rewrite History

A new Edition or configuration may change future behavior.

It should not rewrite:

- historical review;
- old Product access;
- prior network visibility;
- original data location;
- previous decision context.

Historical records should remain interpretable under the configuration that applied at the time.

---

## 58. Feature Absence Must Not Hide Required Governance

An Edition may omit a visible approval dashboard.

If an action requires approval, the system must still provide a compliant route.

The absence of a UI feature does not make the governance requirement disappear.

The action may be:

- unavailable;
- routed externally;
- handled through another Product;
- restricted.

---

## 59. Hidden Feature Is Not Disabled Authority

A Product or Edition may hide a feature from navigation.

If the underlying API or deep link remains accessible, entitlement and Permission must still apply.

Security and governance cannot rely on hidden menus.

---

## 60. Edition Must Support Safe Degradation

When an Edition lacks:

- integration;
- shared service;
- current connectivity;
- advanced Product;
- local connector;

it may degrade to:

- read-only;
- manual preparation;
- export;
- typed handoff;
- external review;
- unavailable protected action.

It must not fabricate success or weaken controls.

## 61. Edition Metrics Must Reflect Organization Value

Edition evaluation may consider:

- adoption;
- active Products;
- workflow completion;
- missed-work reduction;
- review quality;
- integration reliability;
- local-data success;
- collaboration quality;

## 62. Edition Proliferation Must Be Controlled

Too many Editions can create:

- confusion;
- incompatible defaults;
- support burden;
- hidden semantic differences;
- migration complexity.

The architecture should prefer composable profiles over a unique Edition for every organization.

A new Edition should represent a meaningful repeated operating pattern.

---

## 63. Organization-Specific Application Proliferation Must Also Be Controlled

Custom applications can produce value.

They can also create:

- duplicate clients;
- duplicate Tasks;
- incompatible status;
- hidden data stores;
- unregistered Agents;
- unsupported integrations;
- isolated Artifacts.

Every custom application should pass Product-boundary and conformance review.

---

## 64. Conformance Must Be Shared Across Editions

Every Edition and custom application should be testable for:

- Organization context;
- shared references;
- Permission and Policy;
- candidate handling;
- Review and approval;
- Prepared Action;
- Execution handoff;
- formal success reporting;
- AI governance;
- data boundary;
- cross-Product continuity.

A Product can have fewer features.

It cannot have fewer constitutional rules for the same consequence.

Required controls must still exist or the action remains unavailable.

### One-size-fits-all Workplace

### Enterprise-unlimited-access

### White-label impersonation

Brand customization hides the real responsible organization.

### Configuration-as-approval

### Hidden-menu security

### Edition proliferation

Every organization receives a unique incompatible product variant.

### Custom-app proliferation

Local applications become isolated silos with unregistered AI and duplicate formal state.

These designs may offer customization.

They do not preserve a coherent operating system.

## 70. Chapter Conclusion

Workplace must support organizational difference without producing architectural fragmentation.

A sole professional, small team, agency, enterprise, and provider organization should not be forced into one visible operating model.

They should still share one constitutional model.

The correct relationship is:

```text
Organization remains the Orbit.

Edition shapes the operating experience.

Products provide focused journeys.

Organization-specific applications solve local needs.

Workplace preserves continuity.

Core preserves shared meaning.

Execution preserves governed coordination.

Owning Services preserve formal authority.

Humans preserve professional accountability.
```

The key test is simple:

```text
Can the organization change Edition,
add or remove Products,
adopt a private deployment,
or build a custom application
without changing what Task, Review,
Permission, Communication,
Execution, or formal ownership mean?
```

If the answer is yes, variation is healthy.

If the answer is no, the Edition has become a semantic fork.

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Different organizations may operate differently.

Different Editions may look different.

Different Products may evolve differently.

Different deployments may run differently.

The authority architecture remains the same.
```

CH27 now closes Part IV:

> How do context, work, Review, Prepared Actions, Artifacts, formal results, failures, and outcomes continue across independent Products without copying ownership, losing provenance, duplicating work, or breaking the organization’s lifecycle?
