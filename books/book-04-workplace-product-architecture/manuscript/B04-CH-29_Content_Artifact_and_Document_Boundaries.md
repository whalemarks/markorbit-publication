# B04-CH-29 — Content, Artifact and Document Boundaries

**Status:** Draft 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part V — Outcomes, Artifacts and Delivery

## Chapter Purpose

CH28 defined organization-controlled reusable Assets.

This chapter defines the outputs produced by humans, AI Agents, Products, Skills, templates, and governed professional work.

The central question is:

> How should MarkOrbit distinguish semantic Content, governed business Artifacts, formal Documents, file representations, Communications, and Evidence so that a generated output is not mistaken for an approved, signed, delivered, published, or legally effective record?

The accepted working baseline establishes:

```text
Content
≠ Artifact

Artifact
≠ Document

Artifact
≠ file

Document
≠ Evidence

Draft
≠ approved output
```

The central proposition is:

```text
Content
→ expresses meaning

Artifact
→ packages a usable business outcome

Document
→ represents a formal professional record under Core authority

File
→ provides a technical representation

Communication
→ carries a message between participants

Evidence
→ supports a factual or professional assertion
```

These concepts may overlap in one user journey.

They must not collapse into one generic file or content object.

---

## 1. Content Is Semantic Expression

Content is:

> authored, generated, selected, or assembled semantic expression intended to communicate, explain, persuade, instruct, describe, or structure meaning.

Content may include:

- text;
- script;
- headline;
- description;
- explanation;
- caption;
- narrative;
- scene plan;
- message body;
- structured copy;
- visual concept;
- audio script.

Content may exist before a file is created.

It may remain internal.

It may later become part of an Artifact, Communication, Document, or Publication.

---

## 2. Content Does Not Require Formal Identity

Early Content may exist as:

- note;
- fragment;
- generated suggestion;
- draft paragraph;
- selected clause;
- prompt output;
- outline.

It does not need to become a Core Object merely because it exists.

The architecture should avoid formalizing every intermediate expression.

---

## 3. Content May Be Human, AI, or Mixed

Content may be:

- written by a human;
- generated by an AI Agent;
- extracted from a source;
- translated;
- summarized;
- assembled from templates;
- jointly edited.

Authorship and transformation should remain visible where consequence requires it.

AI participation does not determine whether Content is approved.

---

## 4. Content Status Is Contextual

Content may be:

- exploratory;
- draft;
- reviewed;
- approved for a defined use;
- rejected;
- superseded;
- published.

These are conceptual states.

Book 04 does not create one universal Content status model.

The Product or governing service should preserve the meaning required by the use case.

---

## 5. Artifact Is a Business Outcome

Artifact is:

> a usable, reviewable, deliverable, publishable, or further-processable business outcome produced from Content, data, Assets, Knowledge, Product context, and professional work.

Examples include:

- Quote Proposal;
- Trademark Recommendation Page;
- Filing Checklist;
- Client Report;
- Office Action Summary;
- Video Project;
- Graphic Post;
- Publish Package;
- Application Package;
- Provider Comparison;
- Registration Report;
- Portfolio Review.

Artifact is broader than a file.

It may be structured and not yet rendered.

---

## 6. Artifact Is a Cross-Product Concept

Lite, MarkReg, MGSN interfaces, and organization-specific applications may all produce Artifacts.

The concept provides continuity across Products.

It does not require one universal Artifact table at the current architecture stage.

Product-specific objects may implement the concept first.

---

## 7. Artifact Has an Intended Use

An Artifact should identify why it exists.

Possible intended uses include:

- internal decision;
- professional review;
- client Delivery;
- external Communication;
- public Publish;
- filing preparation;
- provider instruction;
- training;
- reuse as Asset;
- formalization into a Document.

Intended use influences review, rights, rendering, and authority.

---

## 8. Artifact May Exist Before Rendering

A structured Quote Proposal may contain:

- client;
- services;
- fees;
- assumptions;
- terms;
- approval;
- layout references.

It may later render to PDF or HTML.

The structured Artifact is not identical to the PDF.

---

## 9. Artifact May Have Several Representations

One Artifact may produce:

- PDF;
- DOCX;
- HTML page;
- PNG;
- MP4;
- audio;
- email preview;
- printed copy.

Representations should reference the same Artifact version where they express the same approved outcome.

---

## 10. Artifact Is Not Automatically Formal

A polished Artifact may still be:

- unreviewed;
- unapproved;
- unsigned;
- undelivered;
- unpublished;
- not filed;
- not accepted.

Visual completeness must not be confused with formal effect.

---

## 11. Document Is a Formal Professional Boundary

Document is a Core-recognized professional object.

A Document may represent:

- signed authorization;
- formal agreement;
- official notice;
- filed response;
- certificate;
- declaration;
- approved report;
- formal record.

The Document boundary preserves formal identity, status, ownership, and service authority.

Artifact architecture must not redefine it.

---

## 12. Artifact and Document May Overlap

An Artifact may become or produce a Document.

Examples:

```text
POA preview Artifact
→ client-reviewed version
→ signed POA Document

Office Action response Artifact
→ approved filing version
→ filed response Document

Quote Proposal Artifact
→ accepted and formally recorded agreement Document
```

The transformation requires explicit formalization.

---

## 13. Not Every Artifact Becomes a Document

A social post, provider comparison, internal video, or recommendation page may remain an Artifact.

Creating a Document for every Artifact would:

- over-formalize;
- create unnecessary lifecycle complexity;
- blur Product and Core boundaries.

Formalization should occur only when professional meaning requires it.

---

## 14. Not Every Document Is an Artifact

An official notice received from an authority may be a Document.

It may not be a Product-produced business outcome.

A legacy signed agreement may enter the system directly as a Document.

Artifact and Document overlap but neither contains the other completely.

---

## 15. File Is a Technical Representation

A file may contain:

- Content;
- an Artifact representation;
- a Document;
- Evidence;
- an Asset;
- several of these at once.

File format does not determine semantic role.

A PDF can be:

- draft Artifact;
- signed Document;
- Evidence exhibit;
- reusable template Asset;
- public publication.

Meaning comes from context and governance.

---

## 16. File Name Is Not Identity

A file named `final.pdf` may not be final.

A file named `signed.pdf` may not contain a valid signature.

A file named `certificate.pdf` may be an unofficial scan.

The system should rely on governed identity and source, not file naming.

---

## 17. Communication Is a Message Boundary

Communication represents a message or exchange among participants.

It may include Content and attach Artifacts or Documents.

Examples include:

- client email;
- provider instruction;
- internal review request;
- official report;
- message reply.

Communication authority, sender, recipients, send state, and thread remain governed separately.

---

## 18. Message Draft Is Not Sent Communication

A Message Draft may be:

- Content;
- Product state;
- an Artifact;
- a Communication preparation.

It becomes a sent Communication only through governed Communication execution and recording.

---

## 19. Attachment Does Not Determine Artifact Ownership

Attaching a PDF to an email does not transfer:

- Artifact ownership;
- Document authority;
- rights;
- source;
- lifecycle.

The Communication should reference the attached item and version.

The underlying object remains governed by its owning boundary.

---

## 20. Evidence Is an Assertion-Support Boundary

Evidence may support:

- use;
- ownership;
- identity;
- deadline;
- transaction;
- public recognition;
- confusion;
- damages;
- official status.

Evidence requires:

- source;
- integrity;
- relevance;
- Matter context;
- handling restrictions.

A useful Artifact is not automatically Evidence.

---

## 21. Document May Become Evidence

A Document may be used as Evidence.

Examples include:

- registration certificate;
- invoice;
- contract;
- official notice;
- filed response.

The Evidence role should reference the Document.

It should not erase the Document identity.

---

## 22. Artifact May Become Evidence Only Through Context

A generated report may support a decision.

It may be admitted or relied upon only after evaluating:

- sources;
- method;
- reviewer;
- integrity;
- professional relevance;
- applicable rules.

Artifact status alone does not establish evidentiary weight.

---

## 23. Official Certificate Requires Precise Distinctions

A registration certificate may involve:

```text
Official registration fact
→ recorded by the appropriate Owning Service

Certificate Document
→ formal certificate identity

Electronic or scanned file
→ technical representation

Delivered certificate package
→ Artifact or Delivery outcome

Certificate used in a dispute
→ Evidence role
```

These distinctions prevent one uploaded scan from being treated as the whole legal reality.

---

## 24. Physical Original and Digital Copy Must Be Distinct

A digital scan may show the content of a physical original.

It does not prove:

- current custody;
- authenticity;
- completeness;
- legal formalities;
- possession of the original.

Where physical-original status matters, the system should record it separately.

---

## 25. Content May Feed Several Artifacts

One approved explanation may support:

- client email;
- report;
- presentation;
- video script;
- knowledge contribution candidate.

Reuse should preserve the Content source and intended-use review.

Approval for one use does not automatically approve every derivative.

---

## 26. One Artifact May Contain Several Content Components

A proposal may contain:

- title;
- legal explanation;
- service description;
- fees;
- disclaimer;
- call to action;
- images;
- client-specific data.

Each component may come from a different source or Asset.

Artifact provenance should preserve this composition where needed.

---

## 27. Artifact May Reference Formal Facts Without Owning Them

A registration report may display:

- application number;
- registration number;
- owner;
- classes;
- official date.

The Artifact consumes those formal facts.

It does not become their Owning Service.

---

## 28. Artifact May Contain Recommendations Without Formalizing Them

A strategy report may contain:

- jurisdiction candidates;
- class recommendations;
- provider options;
- next actions.

The Artifact communicates those recommendations.

It does not convert them into:

- client instruction;
- Task;
- Opportunity;
- Routing decision;
- filing approval.

---

## 29. AI Output Is Content or Candidate Output First

An AI Agent may produce:

- draft text;
- extracted fields;
- summary;
- classification;
- recommendation;
- visual concept.

The output remains a candidate until the relevant Product and human process determine its role.

AI output should not become an Artifact or Document merely because it is generated.

---

## 30. Product Completion Does Not Determine Formality

A Product may mark an Artifact preparation journey complete.

That may mean:

- all sections filled;
- rendering ready;
- user stopped editing.

It does not prove:

- Human Review;
- approval;
- Delivery;
- Publish;
- formal Document status;
- external acceptance.

---

## 31. Review Applies to a Defined Target

Human Review may target:

- Content;
- Artifact;
- Document candidate;
- rendered representation;
- specific version;
- intended use.

The review record should identify exactly what was reviewed.

Reviewing Content does not automatically validate later layout or recipient data.

---

## 32. Approval Applies to a Defined Consequence

Approval may authorize:

- external Delivery;
- public Publish;
- formalization;
- filing;
- signing;
- provider instruction.

An approved Artifact for internal use may still be prohibited from public publication.

---

## 33. Formalization Is a Governed Transition

Formalization means:

> an approved output is accepted by the appropriate Owning Service as a formal object or business fact under its domain contract.

The transition may require:

- typed Prepared Action;
- current Permission and Policy;
- Human Review;
- approval;
- source validation;
- Owning Service acceptance.

CH32 develops this process.

---

## 34. Rendering Does Not Create Formality

Render may create a professional-looking PDF, page, image, audio file, or video.

The result remains tied to the Artifact and its governance state.

A rendered output is not automatically:

- signed;
- sent;
- filed;
- published;
- accepted;
- official.

---

## 35. Editing Does Not Create Approval

A human may correct:

- text;
- layout;
- timing;
- subtitles;
- colors;
- attachments.

Editing changes the output.

It does not itself prove professional approval or Delivery authority.

---

## 36. Delivery Does Not Create Document Status

Delivering an Artifact to a client may create a Delivery result.

It does not automatically convert the Artifact into a formal Document.

The Artifact may remain an informational report or proposal.

---

## 37. Publish Does Not Create Official Fact

A public post may state a filing occurred.

The publication does not create the filing fact.

Official and formal facts remain owned by the relevant services and external authorities.

---

## 38. Artifact Can Be Client-Facing Without Being Formal

Examples include:

- recommendation page;
- explanatory video;
- proposal;
- comparison report;
- checklist.

Client-facing presentation may require strong review.

It still need not become a Document.

---

## 39. Artifact Can Be Internal and High Consequence

An internal provider comparison or legal-risk report may influence significant decisions.

Internal does not mean low-risk.

Review and access should reflect consequence, not only audience.

---

## 40. Artifact Type Should Not Be Inferred Only from Format

A PDF format does not tell the system whether it is:

- proposal;
- certificate;
- report;
- checklist;
- official notice;
- Evidence;
- publication.

Artifact or Document type must be explicit.

---

## 41. Artifact Identity Should Survive Format Change

Converting HTML to PDF or PDF to image should not create a new business outcome automatically.

The new file may be another representation of the same Artifact version.

A substantive edit may create a new version.

---

## 42. Derivative Artifact Must Preserve Lineage

A client report may produce:

- public summary;
- presentation;
- video;
- translated version.

Each derivative should preserve:

- source Artifact;
- source version;
- transformation;
- omitted content;
- new review;
- new intended use.

---

## 43. Cross-Product Handoff Must Preserve Type

When Lite sends a Filing Checklist to MarkReg, the handoff should identify whether it is:

- Content;
- Artifact;
- Document;
- Evidence;
- Asset.

The receiving Product must not infer the type from a file alone.

---

## 44. Local and Cloud Representations Must Preserve Identity

A local PDF and cloud preview may represent the same Artifact.

Synchronization should preserve:

- Artifact reference;
- version;
- checksum or integrity context where required;
- transformation;
- data classification;
- source.

Locality should not create a second business identity.

---

## 45. Retention Rules Differ by Concept

Draft Content may be disposable.

An Artifact delivered to a client may require retention.

A formal Document may have legal or policy retention requirements.

Evidence may be subject to Matter preservation or legal hold.

One storage deletion rule cannot govern all concepts.

---

## 46. Deletion Must Respect References

Deleting a file must not silently delete:

- Artifact identity;
- Document record;
- Evidence relationship;
- Delivery trace;
- formal fact.

The system should distinguish removal of representation from removal of governed record.

---

## 47. Search Must Preserve Semantic Type

Search results should indicate whether a result is:

- Content;
- Artifact;
- Document;
- Asset;
- Evidence;
- Communication.

A unified search experience may aggregate results.

It should not erase their different authority.

---

## 48. User Interface Must Not Collapse Terms

A simple interface may use plain language.

It should still avoid misleading labels such as:

- `Document` for every file;
- `Published` for user-reported upload;
- `Final` for an unapproved Artifact;
- `Official` for provider-reported status.

Simplicity must preserve consequence.

---

## 49. Minimum Boundary Model

```text
Information, Knowledge, Data, and Assets
        │
        ▼
Content Preparation
        │
        ▼
Artifact Assembly
  │
  ├── intended use
  ├── source and provenance
  ├── version
  ├── rights
  ├── review
  └── approval
        │
        ├── Render / Edit
        ├── Delivery / Publish
        └── Formalization Request
                  │
                  ▼
             Owning Service
                  │
                  ▼
          Formal Document or Fact
```

Communication and Evidence may reference outputs at several stages.

Their own boundaries remain intact.

---

```text
Content ≠ Artifact
```

Expression is not yet a governed business outcome.

```text
Artifact ≠ Document
```

Formal professional identity requires explicit formalization.

```text
Artifact ≠ file
```

An Artifact may have several representations.

```text
Document ≠ Evidence
```

A Document may be used as Evidence under a separate role.

```text
Message Draft ≠ sent Communication
```

Communication authority remains governed.

```text
Rendered ≠ approved
```

Professional appearance does not create authority.

```text
Delivered ≠ formalized
```

Targeted transfer does not create a Core record.

```text
Published ≠ official
```


## 51. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Everything-is-a-file

Semantic and formal differences disappear.

### Everything-is-a-Document

Intermediate Product outputs become over-formalized.

### Artifact-is-Document

Product outcomes bypass Document Service authority.

### Render-is-final

A polished PDF is treated as approved.

### Attachment-is-copy

Communication attachments lose source and version.

### Certificate-scan-is-official-fact

A file replaces official status and formal record.

### AI-output-is-Artifact

Generated text receives business authority automatically.

### Product-complete-is-formal

Completing a Product journey changes protected state.

### Search-flattens-authority

Users cannot distinguish draft Content from formal Documents.

These designs may simplify implementation.

They create professional ambiguity.

---

## 52. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming outcome architecture must preserve the following lock:

```text
Content represents semantic expression.

Artifact represents a usable,
reviewable, deliverable,
publishable, or further-processable
business outcome.

Document remains a formal
Core-recognized professional boundary.

File remains a technical representation.

Communication remains a governed message.

Evidence remains an assertion-support role.

AI output begins as candidate work.

Review and approval identify
the exact target, version,
purpose, and consequence.

Render, Edit, Delivery,
Publish, and Formalization
remain separate transitions.

Cross-Product handoffs preserve
semantic type and authority.

No file format, filename,
interface label, or Product status
may create formal authority by convenience.
```

## 54. Chapter Conclusion

Professional systems fail when they treat every output as either text or file.

MarkOrbit requires a richer distinction:

```text
Content carries meaning.

Artifact carries usable business outcome.

Document carries formal professional identity.

File carries technical representation.

Communication carries message.

Evidence carries support for an assertion.
```

These concepts may work together.

They must remain distinguishable.

CH30 now defines the Artifact lifecycle itself:

> How does an Artifact move from preparation through versioning, provenance, Review, approval, representation, cross-Product use, retirement, and historical reconstruction without becoming a universal Core object prematurely?
