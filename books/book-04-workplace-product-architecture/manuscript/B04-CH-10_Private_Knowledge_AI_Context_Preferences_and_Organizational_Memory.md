# B04-CH-10 — Private Knowledge, AI Context, Preferences and Organizational Memory

**Status:** Release Candidate 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part II — Organization Context and Operating Environment

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter defines how an Organization Workplace preserves private professional knowledge, assembles authorized AI context, governs preferences, and accumulates organizational memory.

CH07 established organization identity and active Workplace Context.

CH08 established people, roles, permissions, responsibility, and accountability.

CH09 established clients, relationships, service context, pricing, preferences, and organization-specific business rules.

The next questions are:

```text
What does this organization know that is specifically its own?

Which parts of that knowledge may be used for the current purpose?

How may AI receive context without receiving the whole organization?

How are preferences distinguished from instructions, rules, and policies?

How does experience become organizational memory
without turning every message, draft, or model output into trusted knowledge?
```

The central proposition of this chapter is:

```text
Private Knowledge
→ governed organization- or client-specific professional understanding

Organizational Memory
→ traceable history of decisions, outcomes, relationships,
corrections, and operating experience

Preference
→ scoped and governed expression of how an organization,
client, team, or user tends or chooses to operate

AI Context
→ temporary, purpose-bound, permission-filtered assembly
of the information and knowledge needed for one AI-assisted operation
```

These concepts are related.

They are not interchangeable.

The chapter must preserve five constitutional boundaries:

```text
Private Knowledge ≠ Shared Knowledge

Organizational Memory ≠ Unfiltered Activity History

Preference ≠ Permission, Policy, or Professional Truth

AI Context ≠ Unlimited Organizational Memory Access

AI Output ≠ Organizational Knowledge or Memory by Default
```

Book 02 remains authoritative for Knowledge, Knowledge Source, Knowledge Asset, Knowledge Validation, Permission, Policy, AI Context contracts, and source-linked professional use.

Book 03 remains authoritative for governed Human Review, approval, Execution, protected-action controls, and downstream handoff.

Book 04 explains how an independent Workplace preserves and supplies organization-specific knowledge and memory without redefining those shared foundations.

## 1. Every Organization Develops Knowledge That Is Specifically Its Own

Professional organizations do not operate only from public laws, official guidance, and shared reference material.

They also accumulate knowledge through experience.

That knowledge may include:

- preferred filing strategies;
- internal review checklists;
- lessons from failed or corrected work;
- client-specific instructions;
- local associate practice notes;
- provider communication history;
- pricing rationale;
- service packaging experience;
- jurisdiction-specific observations;
- document-preparation patterns;
- evidence-quality judgments;
- deadline-control practices;
- escalation lessons;
- communication tone and client expectations;
- approved templates and arguments;
- internal quality standards.

This knowledge may be commercially valuable, professionally sensitive, confidential, or client-specific.

It cannot be treated as undifferentiated platform content.

At the same time, it cannot remain only in the memory of individual professionals, scattered emails, local files, private chats, and undocumented habits.

The purpose of Workplace is not to centralize all knowledge into one global pool.

It is to give the organization a governed boundary in which its distinctive knowledge can remain coherent, attributable, retrievable, and usable.

The architectural principle is:

```text
Shared professional foundations
support the organization.

Private organizational knowledge
preserves how this organization understands and operates.
```

---

## 2. Private Knowledge Is Governed Professional Understanding

Private Knowledge means:

> professional understanding governed within an Organization Workplace by the organization, or within an authorized client context, and not available for general ecosystem use by default.

Private Knowledge may be:

- organization-private;
- team-private;
- role-restricted;
- client-private;
- Matter-restricted;
- local-only;
- temporarily shared for a defined collaboration;
- approved for selected Products or AI Agents.

Private Knowledge is not defined only by storage location.

A file in cloud storage may still be private.

A local note may still be subject to organization governance.

A piece of information may be technically accessible but not authorized for the current purpose.

Therefore:

```text
Private
≠
local file only
```

and:

```text
Available
≠
authorized for use
```

Private Knowledge requires more than secrecy.

It requires:

- an owner or authority;
- a source;
- a scope;
- a validation state;
- a version;
- an access boundary;
- an intended use;
- retention or expiry expectations;
- provenance;
- correction and supersession rules.

A private note without those properties may still be useful information.

It should not automatically be treated as governed professional knowledge.

## 3. Private Knowledge and Shared Knowledge Must Remain Distinct

MarkOrbit may provide shared jurisdiction knowledge, classification knowledge, procedural guidance, source-linked professional rules, and validated reusable knowledge.

An Organization Workplace may consume that shared knowledge.

The organization may also hold private additions, interpretations, and operational experience.

For example:

```text
Shared Knowledge:
Official U.S. maintenance filing window

Organization-Private Knowledge:
Internal review checklist for Section 8 evidence

Client-Private Knowledge:
Client-approved specimen strategy and risk tolerance

Matter-Specific Context:
Evidence available for one registration
```

These layers may be assembled together for an authorized purpose.

They must not be collapsed into one knowledge class.

A private practice note does not become a universal jurisdiction rule.

A client instruction does not become shared organizational guidance.

A successful argument in one Matter does not automatically become canonical strategy.

A provider email does not become official law merely because it appears reliable.

The rule is:

```text
Shared Knowledge
→ reusable under shared governance

Private Knowledge
→ reusable only within its authorized organizational scope

Client Knowledge
→ usable only within the relevant client and purpose boundary

Matter Context
→ relevant to the specific professional situation
```

Promotion from private experience into shared knowledge requires a separate governed process.

Existence inside Workplace is not permission to publish, pool, train on, or share it.

## 4. Source Material, Information, Knowledge, and Memory Are Different

A Workplace may contain many forms of material.

They should not all be called knowledge.

### Source material

Source material may include:

- emails;
- official notices;
- files;
- call notes;
- imported records;
- client instructions;
- provider quotations;
- screenshots;
- communications;
- public webpages;
- Matter outcomes.

Source material records what was received, observed, or produced.

### Structured information

Structured information organizes facts or observations from sources.

Examples include:

- a deadline extracted from an official notice;
- a provider fee extracted from an email;
- a client preference extracted from a meeting note;
- a jurisdiction requirement mapped from an official source.

### Governed knowledge

Governed knowledge adds professional meaning, source authority, validation, scope, version, and usage rules.

### Organizational memory

Organizational memory preserves what the organization decided, experienced, learned, corrected, trusted, rejected, or relied upon over time.

The distinction is:

```text
Source Material
→ what was received or observed

Structured Information
→ what was extracted or organized

Private Knowledge
→ what the organization recognizes as usable understanding

Organizational Memory
→ what the organization needs to remember about its history,
decisions, relationships, outcomes, and learning
```

One source may support several forms of knowledge or memory.

The source itself should remain distinguishable from the interpretation derived from it.

## 5. Organizational Memory Is More Than Stored History

Organizational Memory means:

> the governed and retrievable record of experience that allows an organization to preserve continuity, explain past decisions, improve future judgment, and avoid repeating preventable failure.

It may include:

- why a provider was preferred or rejected;
- how a client asked to be served;
- which interpretation was approved at a particular time;
- why a quotation used a particular margin or exception;
- what failed in a previous filing preparation;
- which evidence problem caused delay;
- what correction a reviewer required;
- which template version was used;
- what outcome followed a strategic choice;
- what exception was authorized;
- which internal procedure changed after an incident.

Organizational Memory is not merely a larger database.

It does not mean preserving every click, draft, chat, temporary thought, and raw system event forever.

A complete activity log may support audit.

Audit history and organizational memory serve different purposes.

```text
Audit
→ proves what occurred and who acted

Organizational Memory
→ preserves the context and learning needed for future operation
```

Some audit records may contribute to memory.

Some memory may cite audit records.

They should not be treated as the same system responsibility.

## 6. Memory Must Preserve Decision Rationale, Not Only Final Outcome

A final result without rationale may be difficult to reuse.

For example:

```text
Provider A selected
```

is less useful than:

```text
Provider A selected for this filing because:
- existing trusted relationship;
- fixed package fee;
- strong communication history;
- client required filing within three days;
- Provider B lacked current availability.
```

Similarly:

```text
Goods/services list approved
```

does not explain:

- which business activities mattered;
- which jurisdiction practice applied;
- which items were removed;
- what risk was accepted;
- who reviewed;
- which version was approved.

Organizational Memory should preserve, where appropriate:

- decision subject;
- context;
- actor and responsible organization;
- source and evidence;
- alternatives considered;
- rationale;
- conditions and limitations;
- version;
- outcome;
- later correction or supersession.

This does not require recording unlimited internal deliberation.

It requires enough governed context to explain and safely reuse the decision.

The principle is:

```text
Remember the basis needed for future judgment,
not every hidden thought that occurred along the way.
```

## 7. Organizational Memory Has Several Categories

A useful architecture distinguishes several memory categories.

### Relationship memory

Relationship memory may include:

- client history;
- trusted contacts;
- communication expectations;
- provider reliability;
- partner preferences;
- conflict or sensitivity notes;
- collaboration outcomes.

### Decision memory

Decision memory may include:

- approved strategy;
- rejected option;
- pricing exception;
- reviewer rationale;
- policy override;
- selected provider;
- accepted risk.

### Process memory

Process memory may include:

- recurring operational bottlenecks;
- deadline-control lessons;
- review patterns;
- escalation routes;
- successful handoff practices;
- recurring failure causes.

### Knowledge memory

Knowledge memory may include:

- internal practice notes;
- validated interpretations;
- approved templates;
- checklists;
- local associate guidance;
- jurisdiction observations.

### Outcome memory

Outcome memory may include:

- filing acceptance;
- rejection or correction;
- client response;
- provider performance;
- delivery result;
- conversion result;
- later legal or commercial consequence.

### Preference memory

Preference memory may include:

- approved communication tone;
- preferred provider order;
- standard delivery format;
- client-specific scheduling preference;
- preferred Product or Workflow entry point.

These categories may overlap.

They exist to preserve meaning, not to require separate databases.

## 8. Preference Is a Scoped Choice, Not a Universal Rule

A Preference means:

> a scoped expression of how an organization, team, client, user, or relationship usually wishes to operate when higher authority does not require another result.

Examples include:

- preferred email tone;
- preferred foreign associates;
- preferred report format;
- preferred currency display;
- preferred reminder cadence;
- preferred review order;
- preferred language;
- preferred template;
- preferred Product entry point;
- preferred method of client Delivery.

A Preference may guide behavior.

It does not automatically create authority.

It does not override:

- law;
- official requirements;
- client instruction;
- engagement terms;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- professional judgment;
- current Workflow conditions;
- Human Review;
- formal approval.

The hierarchy may be expressed as:

```text
Applicable law and official requirement
        ↓
Contract, engagement, and explicit client instruction
        ↓
Permission, Policy, and professional responsibility
        ↓
Approved organization business rule
        ↓
Scoped Preference
        ↓
Inferred convenience or default
```

The exact hierarchy may vary by context.

The architectural requirement is that a lower-level Preference must not silently override a higher-authority rule or instruction.

## 9. Explicit and Inferred Preferences Must Be Distinguished

Preferences may arise in different ways.

### Explicit preference

An explicit preference is intentionally recorded or confirmed.

Examples:

- the client requests bilingual reports;
- the organization approves one provider as first preference;
- the user selects a weekly digest;
- the team approves a standard email tone.

### Observed preference

An observed preference is inferred from repeated behavior.

Examples:

- the user often chooses one report format;
- the organization frequently selects one provider;
- the client usually responds through WhatsApp.

### Suggested preference

A suggested preference is proposed by AI, Product logic, or analytics.

Examples:

- suggest using one template because it has been selected repeatedly;
- suggest a shorter report format for one client;
- suggest a preferred provider based on past outcomes.

These states must remain visible.

```text
Observed
≠
confirmed

Suggested
≠
approved

Repeated behavior
≠
permanent instruction
```

An inferred preference should be easy to correct, reject, expire, or limit.

It should not become an invisible rule that controls future professional work.

---

## 10. Preference Scope Must Be Explicit

A preference may apply to:

- one user;
- one role;
- one team;
- one organization;
- one client;
- one service;
- one jurisdiction;
- one Product;
- one Matter;
- one communication channel;
- one period of time.

For example:

```text
Use concise English email style
```

may mean:

- one user’s personal drafting preference;
- the organization’s general client Communication style;
- one client’s preference;
- one Product’s default;
- one foreign associate’s communication expectation.

Those scopes are not equivalent.

A Workplace should preserve:

- preference owner;
- applicable scope;
- source;
- confirmation state;
- effective date;
- expiry or review date;
- conflicts;
- priority;
- whether AI may use it;
- whether external output may reflect it.

Without scope, personalization becomes unpredictable.

## 11. AI Context Is a Temporary Authorized Projection

AI Context means:

> the temporary, purpose-bound, permission-filtered and policy-filtered projection of data, knowledge, memory, preferences, instructions, and current work context supplied to an AI-assisted operation.

AI Context is assembled for a task.

It is not the organization itself.

It is not a permanent copy of the Workplace.

It is not equivalent to the organization’s full memory.

A useful relationship is:

```text
Workplace Context
        ↓
Purpose and Product request
        ↓
Identity, Permission, Policy, and data-boundary evaluation
        ↓
Authorized context assembly
        ↓
AI Context
        ↓
AI-assisted output
```

AI Context may include:

- active organization;
- active user and role;
- client and relationship context;
- current Product purpose;
- relevant Core Object references;
- selected shared knowledge;
- selected private knowledge;
- approved preferences;
- current instructions;
- source references;
- known limitations;
- review requirements;
- prohibited actions.

It should include only what is necessary for the authorized purpose.

The principle is:

```text
AI receives a governed projection of context,
not unrestricted access to organizational memory.
```

---

## 12. AI Context Must Be Assembled, Not Assumed

An AI system may appear to know an organization because it has interacted with users before.

That appearance must not replace explicit context assembly.

The system should not assume that:

- a previous client remains the active client;
- a prior user role remains current;
- a previously used template remains approved;
- a provider preference applies to every jurisdiction;
- one organization’s rule applies to another;
- old pricing remains effective;
- a prior review authorizes a new output;
- all accessible memory may be reused.

AI Context should be assembled from current authorized references.

Relevant factors include:

```text
Who is acting?

For which organization?

For which client or purpose?

Inside which Product or Workflow context?

Which knowledge is authorized?

Which version is current?

Which private fields must be omitted?

Which output mode is allowed?

Which review is required?
```

Context assembly is therefore an architectural operation, not an informal prompt-building convenience.

## 13. AI Context Must Be Purpose-Bound and Minimized

The same organization may use AI for different purposes.

Examples include:

- summarizing an internal memo;
- preparing a client email;
- analyzing an Office Action;
- recommending a provider;
- preparing content;
- reviewing evidence;
- creating a management brief.

Each purpose requires different context.

An email-drafting assistant may need:

- recipient;
- approved facts;
- communication tone;
- relevant Matter context;
- attachment references.

It may not need:

- all client Matters;
- all provider evaluations;
- unrelated pricing;
- full employee records;
- all organizational knowledge.

A provider-routing assistant may need:

- jurisdiction;
- service need;
- relevant capability evidence;
- existing relationship history;
- approved commercial constraints.

It may not need confidential evidence from the underlying Matter.

The rule is:

```text
Minimum sufficient authorized context
is preferable to maximum available context.
```

This protects privacy, reduces confusion, improves auditability, and limits accidental cross-context use.

---

## 14. AI Context Must Preserve Source, Version, and Limitations

AI-assisted professional output becomes unsafe when the system cannot explain what context was used.

Where relevant, AI Context should preserve:

- source references;
- knowledge versions;
- authority or validation status;
- client and Matter scope;
- effective dates;
- missing context;
- conflicting context;
- omitted restricted fields;
- applicable preferences;
- applicable instructions;
- confidence and uncertainty;
- requested and allowed data scope;
- Human Review requirement.

A generated answer should not imply that all relevant information was available when context was incomplete.

Examples of necessary disclosure include:

```text
Current official source not available.

Provider fee information is based on an email dated 2025-11-03.

Client pricing instruction was not found.

Only metadata and safe summaries were available.

The recommendation does not include restricted evidence content.
```

The purpose is not to expose hidden prompts or internal model reasoning.

It is to make professional reliance and limitation visible.

## 15. Private Knowledge Access Does Not Follow from Product Access

A user may have access to a Product without having access to all private knowledge that the Product can technically consume.

An AI Agent may be available in a Workplace without being authorized to access every client or memory category.

An administrator may configure the Workplace without being entitled to read privileged professional content.

Therefore:

```text
Product entitlement
≠
private knowledge access
```

and:

```text
Agent capability
≠
data permission
```

Access may depend on:

- organization membership;
- role;
- client relationship;
- Matter assignment;
- professional responsibility;
- purpose;
- Product context;
- knowledge classification;
- consent or engagement condition;
- local-data rule;
- risk;
- review or approval.

Workplace supplies this context.

Permission and Policy remain authoritative for the decision.

## 16. Client Knowledge Requires Stronger Separation

Client-private knowledge may include:

- legal instructions;
- business plans;
- unreleased brands;
- dispute strategy;
- evidence;
- market expansion plans;
- settlement positions;
- internal contacts;
- payment arrangements;
- risk tolerance;
- privileged analysis.

This information should not become general organization memory merely because the organization handled the work.

The organization may need to remember that a decision occurred.

It may not be entitled to reuse the confidential content for unrelated clients or public knowledge.

A useful distinction is:

```text
Professional lesson
→ may be abstracted and validated for broader internal use

Client-confidential fact
→ remains within the client and purpose boundary
```

For example, an organization may learn that a certain review checkpoint is valuable without copying one client’s evidence into a general training set.

Abstraction must not become disguised disclosure.

Client separation should apply to:

- retrieval;
- AI Context assembly;
- search;
- recommendations;
- memory summaries;
- analytics;
- Product handoff;
- cross-Workplace collaboration.

## 17. Individual Knowledge and Organizational Knowledge Are Different

Professionals carry personal experience.

Organizations carry institutional knowledge.

These overlap but are not identical.

An individual may have:

- personal drafting preferences;
- private notes;
- professional experience gained before joining;
- knowledge acquired through work;
- personal contact history;
- qualified professional judgment.

The organization may have:

- approved templates;
- client history;
- internal procedures;
- service standards;
- provider evaluations;
- Matter records;
- organizational learning;
- licensed or purchased knowledge.

Workplace should not assume that everything known by an individual belongs to the organization.

Nor should it allow the organization’s private knowledge to leave automatically when an individual departs.

The boundary depends on:

- law;
- contract;
- professional duty;
- confidentiality;
- authorship;
- organization policy;
- client rights;
- data source;
- purpose.

Architecturally, knowledge should preserve source and authority rather than relying on possession alone.

## 18. Personalization Must Not Become Identity Capture

Lite and other Products may offer personalized assistants, Today views, recommended actions, drafting styles, and remembered preferences.

Personalization can improve value.

It creates risk when the system confuses a person’s profile with the organization’s truth.

A personal assistant may remember:

- preferred tone;
- recurring work patterns;
- preferred summaries;
- common Products;
- personal reminder style.

It should not automatically carry:

- all client knowledge;
- all organization pricing;
- all provider evaluations;
- professional authority;
- historical access after role change;
- organization memory into another Workplace.

The architectural rule is:

```text
Persona and Personal Assistant
may shape the user experience.

They do not own organizational memory
or preserve access beyond current authority.
```

When the user changes organization, role, or access scope, AI personalization must be re-evaluated.

Convenience must not preserve unauthorized context.

## 19. AI Output Does Not Become Memory or Knowledge Automatically

AI may produce:

- summaries;
- extracted facts;
- draft rules;
- recommendations;
- provider comparisons;
- client preference suggestions;
- case lessons;
- template revisions;
- knowledge candidates;
- memory candidates.

These may be useful.

They remain generated or derived outputs until governed validation occurs.

The minimum distinction is:

```text
AI Summary
≠
verified record

AI-Inferred Preference
≠
confirmed preference

AI-Extracted Rule
≠
validated knowledge

AI Case Lesson
≠
approved organizational memory

AI Recommendation
≠
organizational decision
```

AI may prepare a Memory Candidate that cites sources and proposes a lesson.

A human or authorized governance process must determine whether it is:

- accurate;
- appropriately scoped;
- non-confidential;
- reusable;
- current;
- worth retaining;
- safe for AI grounding.

This is Candidate Before Canonical applied to organizational learning.

## 20. Memory Promotion Must Be Explicit

Not every operational event should become retained organizational memory.

A useful promotion path is:

```text
Source or Outcome
        ↓
Observation
        ↓
Memory Candidate
        ↓
Human or governed validation
        ↓
Classified Organizational Memory
        ↓
Authorized future retrieval and use
```

Promotion may consider:

- professional relevance;
- recurrence;
- source reliability;
- confidentiality;
- client separation;
- accuracy;
- outcome evidence;
- risk;
- usefulness;
- retention need;
- whether a shared Knowledge Candidate should also be created.

Memory promotion should preserve:

- source;
- creator or extracting Agent;
- reviewer;
- classification;
- scope;
- version;
- effective date;
- limitations;
- approved use.

A Product should not silently convert user behavior into permanent organizational rules.

## 21. Corrections and Negative Outcomes Are Valuable Memory

Organizations often retain successful templates and positive outcomes.

They may fail to preserve corrections, rejections, near misses, and operational failure.

That creates repeated risk.

Useful organizational memory may include:

- a missed deadline cause;
- a provider communication failure;
- a rejected document format;
- a pricing mistake;
- an incorrect assumption;
- a review correction;
- a client complaint;
- a failed handoff;
- an AI hallucination;
- a rule that became outdated;
- a context-sharing mistake.

The purpose is not to create a permanent blame system.

The purpose is to preserve actionable learning.

Memory should distinguish:

```text
personnel judgment
from
process lesson
```

and:

```text
one exceptional incident
from
a validated recurring pattern
```

Negative history should not become an unexplained universal penalty or public score.

It should remain contextual, evidence-based, reviewable, and correctable.

## 22. Memory Must Be Versioned and Historically Explainable

Knowledge, preferences, client instructions, provider relationships, and organization practices change.

A current preference must not rewrite the meaning of a historical decision.

Examples include:

- a client changed its preferred communication channel;
- an organization replaced a foreign associate;
- a pricing policy changed;
- a jurisdiction rule changed;
- a template was superseded;
- a user lost review authority;
- a private note was corrected;
- a memory was found to be inaccurate.

Historical work should remain explainable through the context that applied at the time.

A conforming model should preserve, where necessary:

- effective date;
- superseded date;
- prior version;
- change reason;
- changed by;
- reviewed by;
- affected scope;
- downstream reliance;
- correction or revocation status.

The principle is:

```text
Current memory guides current operation.

Historical memory explains historical action.
```

## 23. Forgetting, Expiry, and Revocation Are Part of Memory Governance

An organizational memory system that only accumulates becomes unsafe.

Some information should expire, be archived, restricted, corrected, or deleted.

Reasons may include:

- client instruction;
- retention policy;
- legal requirement;
- privilege or confidentiality;
- outdated knowledge;
- withdrawn consent;
- incorrect extraction;
- employment departure;
- relationship termination;
- source deletion obligation;
- excessive sensitivity;
- lack of continuing purpose.

Memory governance should support distinctions such as:

```text
active

restricted

superseded

expired

revoked

archived

deleted where legally and technically permitted
```

Deletion of a source does not always mean deletion of every audit reference.

Retention of an audit reference does not always authorize retention of full content.

Those details belong to data-governance and Local Vault specifications.

The architectural requirement is that forgetting and restriction are designed responsibilities, not exceptional manual cleanup.

## 24. Cross-Product Memory Requires Continuity Without Unlimited Sharing

Lite, MarkReg, MGSN interfaces, and future Products may all contribute to or consume organizational memory.

Examples include:

```text
Lite
→ records a confirmed client follow-up preference

MarkReg
→ records an approved filing strategy and outcome

MGSN interface
→ records a provider-collaboration outcome

Workplace
→ makes relevant memory available for future authorized work
```

This continuity is valuable.

It must not become unrestricted cross-Product access.

Each handoff should consider:

- Product purpose;
- organization identity;
- client scope;
- role and Permission;
- private classification;
- effective version;
- minimum necessary context;
- AI use permission;
- audit and provenance.

The correct relationship is:

```text
One organizational memory boundary
supports several Products.

Each Product receives only the authorized projection
required for its journey.
```

Product independence remains compatible with organizational continuity.

## 25. Cross-Workplace Knowledge Sharing Requires Deliberate Promotion

Independent Workplaces may collaborate.

They may exchange:

- instructions;
- Documents;
- provider guidance;
- templates;
- service requirements;
- selected capability evidence;
- outcome summaries;
- professional knowledge.

Sharing does not convert private knowledge into network knowledge automatically.

Cross-Workplace sharing should define:

- sender and recipient;
- purpose;
- authority;
- permitted content;
- permitted reuse;
- expiry or retention;
- confidentiality;
- attribution;
- onward-sharing restriction;
- correction path.

A local associate’s advice may be shared for one Matter.

It should not automatically become public MGSN knowledge.

A collaboration outcome may contribute to trust evidence.

It should not automatically expose private communications or client facts.

The Orbit principle remains:

```text
Connection enables governed exchange.

Connection does not erase ownership, confidentiality,
or purpose limitation.
```

## 26. The Brain Boundary Must Remain Clear

MarkOrbit may use the concept of a shared Brain or knowledge/context plane.

Workplace private knowledge and organizational memory must not be collapsed into an unrestricted central Brain.

A useful distinction is:

```text
Shared Brain / Knowledge Plane
→ governed shared knowledge and reusable context infrastructure

Workplace Private Knowledge
→ organization-controlled professional understanding

Organizational Memory
→ organization-controlled history and learning

AI Context
→ temporary authorized projection assembled for one operation
```

The shared Brain may help retrieve or connect authorized knowledge.

It does not become the owner of every organization’s memory.

Workplace may use shared knowledge infrastructure.

It retains control of private scope and promotion.

The deeper relationship between validated shared knowledge and the Brain is developed in CH14.

This chapter establishes the organizational boundary that CH14 must respect.

## 27. Local Vault Supports Private Context but Does Not Define It

Some private knowledge and memory may remain local or inside a private technical boundary.

Local Vault may support:

- local-only files;
- private databases;
- encrypted organization context;
- controlled AI retrieval;
- metadata-only synchronization;
- selected-value synchronization;
- revocable local access.

But private knowledge is not defined by Local Vault technology.

Workplace determines the organizational meaning, authority, scope, and policy.

Local Vault provides a possible technical boundary.

The relationship is:

```text
Workplace
```

## 29. Required Distinctions

This chapter establishes the following distinctions:

```text
Private Knowledge ≠ Shared Knowledge
```

Organization- or client-controlled knowledge does not become ecosystem knowledge automatically.

```text
Source Material ≠ Governed Knowledge
```

A file, email, or note may support knowledge without being validated knowledge by itself.

```text
Organizational Memory ≠ Full Activity Log
```

Memory preserves continuity and learning; audit preserves occurrence and accountability.

```text
Preference ≠ Instruction
```

A preference guides when higher authority does not require another result.

```text
Preference ≠ Permission or Policy
```

A preferred action may still be prohibited or review-required.

```text
Observed Preference ≠ Confirmed Preference
```

Repeated behavior does not become permanent authority silently.

```text
AI Context ≠ Workplace Context
```

AI receives a limited projection, not the whole organizational environment.

```text
AI Context ≠ Memory Store
```

Context is assembled for an operation and should not become an uncontrolled permanent copy.

```text
AI Output ≠ Knowledge or Memory
```

Generated outputs remain candidates until validated.

```text
Product Access ≠ Private Knowledge Access
```

Products and Agents require purpose-specific authorization.

```text
Personalization ≠ Continuing Authority
```

Role or organization changes must change accessible context.

```text
Cross-Product Continuity ≠ Unrestricted Sharing
```

Products receive only the context required for their authorized journeys.

## 30. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Central knowledge absorption

The platform treats all organization and client knowledge as a shared AI asset.

### Memory dumping

Every message, draft, click, and AI output is preserved as permanent organizational memory without classification or purpose.

### Context flooding

AI receives all available organization data instead of the minimum authorized context.

### Cross-client contamination

One client’s facts, strategy, evidence, or preferences influence another client’s work without authority.

### Preference inflation

Observed behavior becomes an invisible permanent rule.

### Prompt authority

Prompt text or model memory is treated as the source of professional truth.

### Candidate promotion without review

AI summaries, extracted rules, inferred preferences, and case lessons become trusted knowledge automatically.

### Historical rewriting

Current preferences or knowledge versions silently reinterpret past decisions.

### Product-specific memory silos

Lite, MarkReg, and MGSN each create incompatible versions of organizational memory.

### Personalization leakage

A user retains client or organization context after moving to another role or Workplace.

### Permanent accumulation

Outdated, withdrawn, incorrect, or over-sensitive memory cannot be expired, restricted, corrected, or deleted.

### Shared Brain overreach

A common knowledge infrastructure becomes the owner of all private organizational memory.

These failures may make AI appear more capable in the short term.

They weaken trust, confidentiality, explainability, and organizational autonomy.

## 31. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming Workplace should preserve the following rule:

```text
Private knowledge remains controlled by its organization,
client scope, purpose, authority, and governance.

Shared knowledge and private knowledge remain distinguishable.

Organizational memory preserves relevant decisions,
outcomes, corrections, relationships, and rationale
without becoming an unlimited activity archive.

Preferences remain scoped, sourced, versioned,
correctable, and subordinate to higher authority.

AI Context is assembled for a defined purpose
from the minimum authorized context.

AI Context preserves source, version, limitation,
data scope, and review requirements where relevant.

Product access does not grant private knowledge access.

AI outputs, inferred preferences, and memory candidates
remain candidates until validated.

Cross-Product continuity does not become unrestricted sharing.

Cross-Workplace exchange remains purpose-limited.

Local or private technical storage remains governed.

Humans remain professionally accountable.

Execution governs coordinated and consequential work.

Owning Services change and record formal business facts.
```

Organizations may adopt different memory practices, personalization models, knowledge taxonomies, and technical storage choices.

They may not remove source, authority, privacy, context, review, and accountability boundaries.

## 33. Chapter Conclusion

An organization becomes more capable when it can preserve what it has learned without losing control of what is private.

Workplace provides that boundary.

It allows the organization to distinguish public or shared professional knowledge from private practice knowledge, client-confidential context, confirmed preferences, operational history, and institutional memory.

It also prevents AI convenience from becoming unlimited memory access.

The architecture can be summarized as:

```text
Source and Experience
→ evidence of what occurred

Private Knowledge
→ governed organization- or client-specific understanding

Organizational Memory
→ traceable continuity of decisions, outcomes, and learning

Preference
→ scoped choice subordinate to higher authority

Authorized Context Assembly
→ selection of the minimum necessary current context

AI Context
→ temporary governed projection for one AI-assisted operation

Human Review and Execution
→ accountable use in consequential work

Outcome
→ possible new memory or knowledge candidate
```

A conforming Workplace must preserve:

- organizational control over private knowledge;
- client and Matter separation;
- distinction between source, information, knowledge, and memory;
- source-linked and versioned professional use;
- scoped and correctable preferences;
- minimum necessary AI Context;
- explicit AI participation and limitation;
- Candidate Before Canonical;
- correction, expiry, restriction, and forgetting;
- continuity across Products without unlimited sharing;
- deliberate promotion across Workplaces or into shared knowledge;
- human professional accountability.

This allows MarkOrbit to become more intelligent without centralizing every organization’s memory, more personalized without preserving unauthorized access, and more capable over time without allowing generated output or repeated behavior to become professional truth silently.

With private knowledge, AI Context, preferences, and organizational memory established, Part II can move to the operating surfaces through which work, review, Communication, and organizational attention become visible without transferring Execution authority to the interface.
