# B04-CH-04 — Position Between Core, Execution, Products and Network

**Status:** Release Candidate 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part I — The Workplace as an Independent Orbit

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter defines the position of **Workplace** between MarkOrbit Core, governed Execution, Products, MGSN, Owning Services, human professionals, and AI Agents.

The architectural problem is not whether these parts exist.

The problem is whether their responsibilities remain distinct.

When boundaries are unclear:

- Core becomes overly procedural;
- Execution becomes a Product shell;
- Workplace becomes a Workflow engine;
- Products become owners of organizational truth;
- MGSN becomes a central marketplace;
- AI Agents become informal authorities;
- Owning Services lose control of formal business state.

Book 04 must prevent that collapse.

The constitutional spine is:

```text
Industry and Professional Philosophy
                ↓
MarkOrbit Operating System
                ↓
MarkOrbit Core
                ↓
MarkOrbit Execution System
                ↓
Organization Workplace / Independent Orbit
                ↓
Products and Operating Models
                ↓
Mark Global Service Network
                ↓
Orbital Ecosystem Learning and Evolution
```

This sequence does not mean that every layer is a software service or that work always moves through the layers in one linear order.

It means that each layer answers a different architectural question.

```text
Core:
What does the shared professional model mean?

Execution:
How does governed work proceed?

Workplace:
Which organization is operating,
under which identity, authority, context, and boundaries?

Product:
How does a user experience and complete a domain journey?

MGSN:
How do independent Workplaces discover, evaluate,
and collaborate with one another?

Owning Service:
Which authority may change formal business facts?

Human professional:
Who remains accountable for professional judgment?

AI Agent:
Which governed runtime role may assist,
under which identity, permission, and context?
```

The central proposition of this chapter is:

```text
Core defines shared meaning.

Execution governs coordinated work.

Workplace provides authorized organization context.

Products compose focused user journeys.

MGSN connects independent organizational Orbits.

Owning Services change and record formal business facts.

Humans remain professionally accountable.

AI assists under explicit governance.
```

## 1. Why Positioning Matters

Architecture often fails through responsibility drift.

A Product needs a new field, so the Product defines a new object.

A Workplace needs a faster approval flow, so the Workplace creates its own Task states.

A network needs provider status, so the network invents a parallel Capability model.

An AI Agent needs to complete an action, so the Agent writes directly to formal records.

Each decision may appear efficient in isolation.

Together, they fragment the Operating System.

MarkOrbit must distinguish between:

```text
where meaning is defined;

where work is coordinated;

where organizational context is supplied;

where user experience is composed;

where organizations connect;

where formal state changes;

where professional responsibility remains.
```

This determines:

- who is authoritative for truth;
- who may mutate state;
- where governance applies;
- where Product variation is allowed;
- where organizational autonomy is preserved;
- where AI may assist;
- where Human Review is required;
- how cross-Product consistency is maintained.

The most important distinction is:

```text
Use is not ownership.

Visibility is not authority.

Invocation is not definition.
```

Workplace may display Tasks.

Products may initiate Workflows.

MGSN may produce recommendations.

AI Agents may prepare Artifacts.

None of those facts transfers architectural authority.

## 2. Core Defines Shared Professional Meaning

MarkOrbit Core is the shared semantic foundation.

It defines concepts that must remain consistent across Products, Workplaces, Execution patterns, and network interactions.

Core may define:

- domains;
- Core Objects;
- Core Services;
- Service ownership;
- canonical statuses;
- transition rules;
- Workflow Contract components;
- Common Contracts;
- permission and policy boundaries;
- Human Review boundaries;
- Event and audit rules;
- AI governance boundaries;
- validation rules.

Core answers questions such as:

```text
What is a Matter?

What is a Task?

What is a Communication?

What is a Document?

What is a Review?

What is an Event?

Which Service is authoritative for this Object?

Which status values are valid?

Which transitions are permitted?
```

Core must remain stable enough to support multiple organizations and Products.

That stability is why organization-specific behavior must not be pushed into Core casually.

One organization may require two review steps before sending a client Communication.

Another may require one.

A third may require escalation for a particular client or jurisdiction.

Those are important operating differences.

They do not automatically justify new universal Core semantics.

The rule is:

```text
Core defines shared meaning.

Core does not contain every organization’s operating preference.
```

Workplace, Products, Execution, and MGSN may consume Core definitions.

They must not silently redefine them.

## 3. Execution Coordinates Governed Work

Valid semantics do not automatically produce safe operation.

A Task may exist but not be ready for completion.

A Communication may exist but not be approved to send.

A Workflow may be available but not permitted to advance.

An AI draft may be useful but is not Human Review.

Execution coordinates the movement from valid structure to governed action.

It may govern:

- Workflow progression;
- Task lifecycle;
- review and approval gates;
- Communication boundaries;
- permission and policy checks;
- protected-action controls;
- Human–AI handoff;
- safe failure;
- retry;
- idempotency;
- audit;
- trace;
- escalation;
- completion.

Execution answers:

```text
When does work begin?

What must happen next?

What must pause?

Who must review?

Which condition blocks progress?

When may a protected action proceed?

How is failure recorded?

How is the result traced?
```

Execution does not own the organization.

It does not own:

- organizational identity;
- client relationships;
- private knowledge;
- pricing;
- Product composition;
- partner networks;
- Workplace branding.

It uses organizational context supplied through Workplace.

The distinction is:

```text
Workplace supplies authorized context.

Execution applies governed coordination.
```

If Execution absorbed Workplace, it would become an organization-management platform.

If Workplace absorbed Execution, each Workplace edition or Product surface could invent its own Workflow, Task, Review, and protected-action behavior.

Both outcomes would break the architecture.

## 4. Workplace Supplies Authorized Organizational Context

Workplace is the unified professional operating environment of an organization.

It carries the context that shared semantics and governed Execution require to become legitimate organizational work.

That context includes:

- organization identity;
- users;
- roles;
- permissions;
- accountability;
- clients;
- relationships;
- private knowledge;
- business rules;
- pricing;
- preferences;
- service catalog;
- partner relationships;
- AI context;
- Product access;
- work and review visibility;
- organizational memory.

Workplace answers:

```text
Which organization is operating?

Which user is acting?

Which role do they hold?

Which client context applies?

Which private knowledge may be used?

Which pricing and service rules apply?

Which Product has access?

Which reviewer is authorized?

Which partner relationships are trusted?

Which records remain local or private?

Which organization remains accountable?
```

Workplace does not define shared semantics.

It does not define Task lifecycle.

It does not own every Product feature.

It does not automatically own formal business state.

It supplies the organizational setting in which those things are consumed.

The concise distinction is:

```text
Core defines the shared professional model.

Execution governs movement through work.

Workplace establishes whose organization is operating.
```

## 5. Products Compose User Experience and Domain Journeys

Products turn architecture into usable professional experiences.

A Product may define:

- user journeys;
- navigation;
- interaction patterns;
- domain composition;
- screen behavior;
- Workflow entry points;
- recommendations;
- forms;
- reports;
- Artifact preparation;
- Product-specific views;
- Product-specific business logic that does not belong in Core.

Examples include:

- MarkOrbit Lite;
- MarkReg;
- MGSN interfaces;
- organization-specific applications;
- future professional Products.

A Product answers:

```text
What is the user trying to accomplish?

What should they see?

What should they enter?

What should be recommended?

What should be prepared?

Which governed action should be handed off?

What outcome should the user receive?
```

Products may be independent, embeddable, and separately evolving.

But a Product must not become the owner of organizational truth.

A Product may use organization identity.

It does not own the organization.

A Product may use client context.

It does not own the client relationship.

A Product may initiate a Workflow.

It does not redefine Workflow semantics.

A Product may display Tasks.

It does not own the Task lifecycle.

A Product may prepare an Artifact.

It does not approve the Artifact automatically.

The rule is:

```text
Product composes the experience.

Product does not absorb system authority.
```

## 6. MGSN Connects Independent Workplaces

MGSN connects independent organizational Orbits.

Its primary nodes are:

```text
Organization / Workplace / Orbit
```

MGSN may support:

- private partner networks;
- trusted extended networks;
- limited public discovery;
- Capability discovery;
- jurisdiction coverage;
- provider evidence;
- trust signals;
- routing;
- collaboration preparation;
- outcome feedback.

MGSN answers:

```text
Which capability is needed?

Which Workplaces may provide it?

What evidence supports the match?

What trust relationship already exists?

Which candidates should be considered?

How should collaboration be prepared?
```

MGSN does not own the organizations it connects.

It is not authoritative for:

- client relationships;
- private organizational knowledge;
- provider appointment;
- organization pricing;
- final professional judgment;
- formal business-state mutation.

The correct line is:

```text
Need
→ candidate discovery
→ evidence and explanation
→ human selection
→ governed collaboration
```

not:

```text
Need
→ automatic platform appointment
```

MGSN connects Workplaces.

It does not replace them.

## 7. Owning Services Change Formal Business Facts

Formal business facts require clear mutation authority.

That authority belongs to the proper Owning Service.

An Owning Service is authoritative for changing formal state within its domain.

Examples may include:

- creating or updating a formal Matter;
- changing a Task status;
- recording an approved Communication send;
- recording payment state;
- confirming an official filing Event;
- recording provider instruction;
- updating a formal Opportunity;
- recording an approved outcome.

The key principle is:

```text
Only the proper Owning Service changes formal business facts.
```

Workplace may collect context.

A Product may capture user intent.

Execution may coordinate the process.

An AI Agent may prepare a proposal.

MGSN may recommend a provider.

A human professional may approve a decision.

But the formal mutation still belongs to the Owning Service.

This distinction is essential in AI-assisted systems.

Generation is not mutation.

Recommendation is not recordal.

Approval is not execution.

The correct line is:

```text
AI prepares.

Humans review and decide.

Execution governs coordinated work.

Owning Services record formal facts.
```

## 8. Human Professionals Remain Accountable

MarkOrbit is a professional Operating System.

Professional responsibility cannot be assigned to software for convenience.

Human professionals remain accountable for:

- professional judgment;
- legal conclusions;
- strategic decisions;
- client advice;
- review;
- approval;
- protected actions;
- provider selection;
- filing instructions;
- payment decisions;
- official recordal;
- external commitments.

The system may support that accountability by:

- surfacing evidence;
- explaining recommendations;
- preparing drafts;
- checking completeness;
- identifying risks;
- preserving trace;
- enforcing gates;
- recording decisions.

But support does not transfer responsibility.

```text
System capability
≠
professional authority
```

and:

```text
AI confidence
≠
professional judgment
```

Human accountability is not a temporary limitation to be removed later.

It is a constitutional property of MarkOrbit.

## 9. AI Agents Assist Under Governance

An AI Agent is a governed runtime role.

It operates under:

- task scope;
- capability limits;

- summarize;
- classify;
- compare;
- draft;
- extract;
- recommend;
- prepare;
- check;
- explain;
- assemble context;
- support Human Review.

An AI Agent does not own formal business state.

It does not automatically:

- approve;
- send;
- file;
- pay;
- appoint;
- instruct;
- record official outcomes;

An AI Agent may appear in Workplace.

But placement does not change authority.

```text
Placement is not ownership.

Capability is not permission.

Output is not approval.
```

## 10. The Responsibility and Authority Model

The constitutional responsibility model is:

```text
Core
→ defines shared semantics and contracts

Execution
→ governs coordination and handoff

Workplace
→ supplies authorized organizational context

Product
→ composes user experience and domain journey

MGSN
→ connects independent Workplaces

Owning Service
→ changes formal business facts

Human professional
→ remains professionally accountable

AI Agent
→ assists under identity, permission, context, and governance
```

This model is deliberately distributed.

No one layer becomes the entire system.

### Responsibility and Non-Responsibility Matrix

| Concept | Primary responsibility | May consume or expose | Must not own |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core | Shared semantics, contracts, canonical status values, ownership rules, governance primitives | Products, Workplace, Execution, and MGSN may consume Core definitions | Organization-specific preferences, Product UX, execution sequencing, provider appointment |
| Execution | Governed coordination, Workflow progression, Task lifecycle, review gates, protected-action controls, trace | Workplace context, Core contracts, Product entry points, Human Review | Organization identity, private knowledge, Product UX, client ownership |
| Workplace | Authorized organizational context, identity, users, roles, clients, private knowledge, rules, preferences, Product access | Core, Execution, shared capabilities, Products, and MGSN | Shared Core semantics, Workflow meaning, Task lifecycle, universal Product behavior |
| Product | User journey, interaction, domain composition, recommendations, forms, Product-specific views | Workplace context, Core, Execution, and shared capabilities | Organizational truth, formal mutation authority, network ownership |
| MGSN | Capability discovery, trust-supported routing, network participation, collaboration preparation | Workplace profiles, capability evidence, outcome feedback | Automatic provider appointment, client ownership, final professional judgment |
| Owning Service | Formal business-state mutation within its domain | Approved decisions, Execution results, authorized context | Arbitrary cross-domain mutation or substitute professional judgment |
| Human professional | Professional judgment, review, approval, accountability | Evidence, AI support, recommendations, Execution context | Shared semantic definitions or automated execution mechanics |
| AI Agent | Governed assistance, preparation, explanation, recommendation | Skills, context, policies, approved tools | Formal business state, Human Review, protected-action authority |

This matrix is an authority map.

It is not an implementation topology.

Several responsibilities may exist in one codebase.

One responsibility may later be implemented by several services.

The technical form may vary.

The authority boundaries must remain.

## 11. Three Core Distinctions

### Visibility Does Not Equal Ownership

A layer may display an object without owning its meaning or lifecycle.

Workplace may display a Task.

Execution remains authoritative for Task progression.

A Product may display a Matter.

The relevant Owning Service remains authoritative for formal Matter state.

MGSN may display provider candidates.

The participating organization retains selection authority.

```text
Visible in a surface
≠
owned by the surface
```

This distinction prevents user-facing layers from accumulating authority merely because they are where people interact.

### Invocation Does Not Equal Definition

A Product may initiate a Workflow.

It does not define Workflow semantics.

Workplace may invoke a Skill.

It does not define the Skill.

MGSN may initiate collaboration preparation.

It does not define formal provider appointment.

An AI Agent may call a capability.

It does not own the capability definition.

```text
Invokes
≠
defines
```

Invocation is operational.

Definition is architectural and governed.

### Context Does Not Equal Mutation Authority

Workplace may carry rich context:

- client identity;
- pricing;
- private knowledge;
- reviewer preferences;
- risk rules;
- Product access;
- partner history.

But rich context does not authorize formal mutation.

The correct line is:

```text
Workplace supplies context.

Product captures intent.

AI may prepare.

Execution governs coordinated work.

Humans review.

Owning Services mutate formal state.
```

This preserves the difference between knowing enough to recommend, being permitted to act, being responsible for the result, and being authoritative for the formal fact.

## 12. The Cross-Layer Handoff Model

The architecture becomes clearer when viewed as a handoff model.

Consider a professional need.

### Workplace supplies context

The organization, user, client, private knowledge, pricing rules, permissions, and partner history are known.

### Product composes the journey

The user receives a relevant flow, recommendation, form, or review surface.

### AI and shared capabilities prepare

The system may analyze, compare, draft, classify, or recommend.

### Execution coordinates

Tasks, reviews, approvals, failures, retries, and protected-action gates are governed.

### Human professional decides

Professional judgment and approval remain accountable.

### Owning Service records

The formal business fact is changed by the proper authority.

### MGSN connects when external capability is needed

Candidates may be discovered, explained, evaluated, and selected.

### Outcome returns

The outcome becomes visible to Workplace, Product, audit, and governed learning.

The flow can be summarized as:

```text
Workplace context
→ Product journey
→ AI and capability preparation
→ Execution
→ Human Review
→ Owning Service
→ Outcome
→ Workplace and ecosystem feedback
```

This is not a mandatory technical pipeline.

It is an authority-preserving interaction model.

## 13. End-to-End Example: International Trademark Filing

An international trademark filing illustrates the responsibility boundaries.

### Workplace

Workplace supplies:

- organization identity;
- client relationship;
- client preferences;
- pricing rules;
- authorized staff;
- preferred foreign associates;
- private templates;
- local-data restrictions.

### MarkReg

MarkReg composes the Product journey:

- country selection;
- class and goods/services interaction;
- intake;
- quotation;
- document collection;
- filing preparation;
- progress visibility.

### AI and shared capabilities

AI and shared capabilities may provide:

- country recommendations;
- class suggestions;
- goods/services preparation;
- risk explanations;
- draft Communications;
- completeness checks.

These outputs remain candidates until reviewed.

### Execution

Execution coordinates:

- intake;
- Task creation;
- document review;
- fee confirmation;
- Human Review;
- protected-action gates;
- failure and retry;
- status follow-up.

### Human professional

The human professional reviews:

- legal and commercial recommendations;
- goods/services strategy;
- the final filing package;
- provider selection;
- filing instruction.

### MGSN

When an external associate is needed, MGSN may support:

- jurisdiction coverage;
- provider discovery;
- capability evidence;
- trust history;
- candidate explanation.

The organization retains selection authority.

### Owning Services

The proper Owning Services record:

- the formal Order;
- the Matter;
- payment state;
- provider instruction;
- official filing outcome.

No single layer owns the entire process.

That is the architectural purpose of the model.

## 14. Product and Workplace Variation Must Not Create Authority Drift

Products need freedom to innovate.

Lite may use a Today Feed.

MarkReg may use a lifecycle journey.

An enterprise Workplace may use portfolio and team views.

That variation is desirable.

But Product variation must not create semantic drift.

A Task must not mean one thing in Lite and another in MarkReg.

Human Review must not disappear because one interface is simplified.

A Communication draft must not become equivalent to an approved send.

A provider recommendation must not become an appointment.

The rule is:

```text
Product experience may vary.

Shared semantics and authority boundaries may not drift.
```

The same applies to Workplace editions.

A solo-professional Workplace, small-team Workplace, agency Workplace, enterprise Workplace, local Workplace, and embedded Workplace may expose different capabilities and complexity.

But editions must not create different constitutional authority models.

```text
Edition variation
≠
authority variation
```

## 17. Chapter Conclusion

Workplace sits between shared foundations and concrete organizational use.

It is the organizational operating layer that allows Core, governed Execution, Products, capabilities, and network participation to become coherent for a particular professional organization.

The constitutional model is:

```text
Core
→ defines shared meaning

Execution
→ governs coordinated work

Workplace
→ supplies organization identity, authority, and context

Products
→ compose user journeys

MGSN
→ connects independent Workplaces

Owning Services
→ change formal business facts

Human professionals
→ remain accountable

AI Agents
→ assist under governance
```

The essential lesson is that interaction does not erase authority boundaries.

A Product may invoke Execution.

A Workplace may expose Tasks.

MGSN may recommend providers.

An AI Agent may prepare a draft.

A human may approve.

An Owning Service may record the result.

Each part contributes.

No part becomes the whole system.

This separation allows MarkOrbit to remain shared without becoming centralized, Product-driven without becoming fragmented, intelligent without making AI the authority, and connected without turning the network into the owner.
