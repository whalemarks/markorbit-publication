# B04-CH-37 — Governance, Privacy and Professional Responsibility Across Orbits

**Status:** Draft 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part VI — Network Participation and Orbital Ecosystem Evolution

## Chapter Purpose

CH33–CH36 established network layers, Capability Evidence, Routing, Trust feedback, and decentralized learning.

This chapter defines the cross-Orbit governance boundary.

The central question is:

> When identity, client context, data, Knowledge, Artifacts, work, AI, professional responsibility, and outcomes cross from one Workplace to another, which organization authorizes, controls, reviews, receives, retains, and remains accountable?

Cross-organization collaboration is not simply a larger cross-Product handoff.

It involves:

- separate organizations;
- separate clients or responsibilities;
- separate Permission and Policy;
- separate professional authority;
- separate data boundaries;
- separate records;
- separate legal and commercial obligations.

The central proposition is:

```text
Governed Cross-Orbit Collaboration
=
Distinct Organization Identities
+ Explicit Relationship and Purpose
+ Minimum Necessary Exchange
+ Sender Authorization
+ Receiver Eligibility and Re-Evaluation
+ Staged Disclosure
+ Defined Professional Responsibility
+ Independent Review and Approval
+ Governed Communication and Execution
+ Retention, Revocation, Incident, and Audit
```

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Cross-Product ≠ cross-Organization

Sender permission ≠ receiver permission

Sender approval ≠ receiver approval

Data delivery ≠ data ownership transfer

Provider access ≠ client relationship ownership

Collaboration ≠ joint professional authority

Network identity ≠ legal authority

Shared Artifact ≠ unrestricted reuse

AI access in one Orbit ≠ AI access in another

Audit trace ≠ surveillance
```

---

## 1. Every Orbit Remains an Independent Authority Boundary

Each participating organization retains its own:

- identity;
- people;
- roles;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- clients;
- Knowledge;
- professional responsibility;
- records;
- commercial terms;
- retention.

Connection does not merge the Workplaces.

---

## 2. Cross-Orbit Collaboration Requires a Relationship Basis

The basis may include:

- client instruction;
- provider engagement;
- referral;
- network invitation;
- service agreement;
- affiliated-office arrangement;
- joint project;
- legal obligation.

The relationship basis should be explicit.

---

## 3. Purpose Must Be Defined Before Exchange

Possible purposes include:

- conflict check;
- quotation;
- filing;
- legal advice;
- translation;
- evidence collection;
- review;
- Delivery;
- status reporting.

Purpose constrains what may be shared and how it may be used.

---

## 4. Sender Authorization Is Required

The sending organization should evaluate:

- client authority;
- user Permission;
- Policy;
- confidentiality;
- data classification;
- intended recipient;
- purpose;
- minimum data;
- retention expectation.

Ability to access data does not authorize sharing it.

---

## 5. Receiver Eligibility Is Independently Required

The receiving organization should evaluate:

- identity;
- role;
- service scope;
- conflict;
- Permission;
- Policy;
- data handling;
- professional responsibility;
- subcontracting;
- AI use.

Sender approval cannot establish receiver eligibility.

---

## 6. Authority Is Not Transitive

If Organization A may use client data, it cannot automatically authorize Organization B, C, or a subcontractor.

Each onward transfer requires a valid basis.

The rule is:

```text
Authorized in Orbit A
does not mean
authorized in Orbit B.
```

---

## 7. Network Identity Is Not Full Organization Identity

A network profile may show:

- organization;
- Capability;
- qualification;
- contact;
- evidence.

It does not prove:

- authority for a specific Matter;
- engagement;
- client permission;
- current conflict clearance;
- acting professional.

---

## 8. Client Ownership Must Remain Explicit

The instructing organization may retain:

- client relationship;
- scope definition;
- billing;
- client Communication;
- strategic responsibility.

The performing organization may have its own professional duties.

MGSN does not own the client.

---

## 9. Professional Responsibility Must Be Allocated

The collaboration should identify responsibility for:

- advice;
- local law;
- filing accuracy;
- translation;
- deadline;
- client instruction;
- provider instruction;
- review;
- official reporting;
- escalation.

Unclear responsibility creates hidden risk.

---

## 10. Responsibility May Be Shared but Not Vague

Several professionals may contribute.

The system should still identify:

- who prepared;
- who reviewed;
- who approved;
- who submitted;
- who reported;
- who remains accountable for each scope.

---

## 11. Performing Provider Is Not Automatically Subordinate

An external provider is an independent professional organization.

The engagement may define instruction and deliverables.

It should not erase the provider’s professional obligations or judgment.

---

## 12. Data Exchange Must Be Minimal

Early exchange may use:

- Capability summary;
- anonymized Matter type;
- jurisdiction;
- deadline range;
- limited identity for conflict.

Full client files should follow only when required and authorized.

---

## 13. Data Exchange Should Expand by Stage

The staged model remains:

```text
Discovery

Conflict Check

Quotation

Selection

Instruction

Active Collaboration

Delivery

Outcome and Retention
```

Each stage has different data needs.

---

## 14. Exchange Package Must Preserve Source and Restrictions

A package should identify:

- sender;
- receiver;
- purpose;
- subject;
- source;
- classification;
- permitted use;
- onward-sharing limit;
- retention;
- AI restriction;
- expiry;
- audit reference.

---

## 15. Receiver Must Re-Evaluate the Package

The receiver should not assume:

- sender classification is sufficient;
- all data is necessary;
- onward use is allowed;
- AI use is allowed;
- retention is unlimited.

Receiver Policy applies independently.

---

## 16. Data Custody and Data Authority Differ

A receiver may hold a copy.

The sender or client may retain authority over:

- purpose;
- confidentiality;
- return;
- deletion;
- onward sharing.

Technical custody does not create unrestricted ownership.

---

## 17. Shared Reference and Shared Copy Differ

Where possible, collaboration may use:

- reference;
- secure access;
- view-only representation;
- redacted package;
- time-limited link.

A full copy may be necessary for some work.

The choice should be deliberate.

---

## 18. Artifact Sharing Requires Intended-Use Review

An Artifact approved for internal use may not be approved for provider sharing.

Sharing should verify:

- version;
- client restriction;
- rights;
- redaction;
- professional context;
- recipient;
- onward use.

---

## 19. Document Sharing Preserves Formal Identity

A formal Document shared across Orbits remains the same Document or an authorized copy.

The receiver should not convert it into an editable Artifact without preserving source and status.

---

## 20. Evidence Sharing Requires Integrity and Matter Context

Evidence may require:

- chain of custody;
- original reference;
- source;
- completeness;
- redaction;
- legal hold;
- access limits.

Ordinary file sharing may be insufficient.

---

## 21. Knowledge Sharing Requires Scope

An organization may share:

- public Knowledge;
- approved guidance;
- organization-specific note;
- Matter-specific advice.

The receiver should understand the scope and authority.

Private advice does not become shared canonical Knowledge automatically.

---

## 22. AI Use Must Be Declared Where Material

The receiving provider may use AI for:

- translation;
- summarization;
- drafting;
- extraction;
- research.

The collaboration should respect:

- client restriction;
- organization Policy;
- data location;
- model retention;
- Human Review;
- prohibited uses.

---

## 23. AI Authorization Does Not Cross Automatically

Organization A may allow a particular Agent.

Organization B may prohibit it.

The receiver cannot rely on the sender’s Agent approval.

---

## 24. External AI Provider Is Another Boundary

Using a cloud AI provider may create an additional data recipient.

The organization must evaluate whether that transfer is authorized.

---

## 25. Communication Must Preserve Acting Identity

Cross-Orbit Communication should identify:

- sender organization;
- responsible person;
- recipient organization;
- purpose;
- Matter;
- authority;
- attachments;
- thread.

Network interface convenience must not obscure the acting entity.

---

## 26. Provider Instruction Is a Protected Action

Instruction may:

- disclose data;
- create work;
- create commercial obligation;
- affect deadlines;
- establish representation.

It should pass Human Review, approval, Communication, Routing, and Execution gates where required.

---

## 27. Receiver Acceptance Is Separate

The receiver may:

- accept;
- decline;
- request clarification;
- identify conflict;
- narrow scope;
- modify terms.

A sent instruction does not prove engagement.

---

## 28. Each Orbit May Require Independent Review

The sender may review the instruction.

The receiver may review acceptance and service approach.

A client may review a strategy.

These Reviews have different purposes and authorities.

---

## 29. Sender Approval Does Not Replace Receiver Approval

The instructing organization may approve disclosure and fee.

The provider may still require internal professional acceptance.

Both decisions should remain visible.

---

## 30. Cross-Orbit Execution Must Preserve Both Sides

Execution trace may include:

- sender actor;
- receiver actor;
- organization references;
- Routing decision;
- Communication;
- provider acceptance;
- deliverable;
- formal outcome.

One organization’s internal Task need not be exposed fully to the other.

---

## 31. Formal Records Remain in Appropriate Domains

Each organization may record:

- Order;
- Matter;
- Task;
- Communication;
- invoice;
- provider engagement;
- outcome.

The records may reference one collaboration.

They need not be one shared database object.

---

## 32. Shared Collaboration Reference Can Connect Records

A logical collaboration reference may help correlate:

- instructing Matter;
- provider Matter;
- Communications;
- Deliveries;
- invoices;
- outcomes.

Correlation does not merge ownership.

---

## 33. Retention Must Be Agreed or Governed

The receiver may need to retain:

- instruction;
- Documents;
- Evidence;
- work product;
- invoice;
- official result.

Retention may be governed by:

- contract;
- professional obligation;
- law;
- client instruction;
- Policy.

Deletion cannot always occur immediately on request.

---

## 34. Return and Deletion Requests Need Typed Outcomes

A sender may request:

- return;
- delete;
- revoke access;
- stop onward sharing;
- correct data.

The receiver may respond:

- completed;
- partly completed;
- legally retained;
- impossible for external copies;
- disputed.

The trace should remain.

---

## 35. Revocation Affects Future Use

Revocation may stop:

- access;
- synchronization;
- Agent use;
- onward sharing;
- new work.

It may not erase:

- completed professional records;
- audit;
- legal retention;
- external filings;
- recipient downloads.

---

## 36. Incident Response Must Cross Orbits

A data or professional incident may require:

- containment;
- notification;
- evidence preservation;
- client Communication;
- provider Communication;
- correction;
- escalation;
- legal review.

Responsibilities should be defined before an incident where possible.

---

## 37. Incident Disclosure Must Be Purpose-Limited

Participants need enough information to act.

Incident handling does not authorize public disclosure of confidential Matter details.

---

## 38. Cross-Orbit Audit Must Be Proportionate

Audit should preserve:

- authorization;
- exchange;
- acceptance;
- review;
- formal action;
- outcome;
- incident.

It should not expose unrelated internal operations or employee surveillance data.

---

## 39. Audit Views May Differ by Orbit

The sender and receiver may see different authorized representations of one collaboration trace.

Shared correlation does not require shared full logs.

---

## 40. Privacy Rights and Professional Obligations May Conflict

A deletion request may conflict with:

- legal hold;
- professional retention;
- defense of claim;
- official filing record.

The system should support governed resolution rather than promise automatic deletion.

---

## 41. Cross-Border Context Requires Additional Review

Cross-border collaboration may involve:

- data transfer;
- professional regulation;
- local secrecy;
- client consent;
- official requirements;
- provider qualification.

Book 04 defines the architecture boundary.

Specific legal requirements belong to jurisdictional specifications and legal review.

---

## 42. Payment and Commercial Data Need Separate Scope

A provider may need:

- agreed fee;
- invoice entity;
- currency;
- payment status.

It may not need the instructing organization’s full client pricing or margin.

Commercial minimization protects autonomy.

---

## 43. Client Communication Authority Must Be Explicit

The provider may communicate:

- only through instructing organization;
- directly with client;
- jointly;
- for limited procedural issues.

The authority should not be inferred from access.

---

## 44. Subcontracting Requires Governance

A provider may wish to involve another organization.

Onward transfer may require:

- disclosure;
- consent;
- qualification;
- data review;
- updated responsibility;
- new network handoff.

Provider appointment does not create unlimited subcontracting authority.

---

## 45. Collaboration Termination Must Preserve Continuity

Ending a relationship may require:

- transfer of files;
- deadline report;
- unfinished-work status;
- revocation;
- invoice;
- client notice;
- replacement provider;
- historical trace.

Termination should not orphan the Matter.

---

## 46. Dispute Does Not Suspend All Safety Obligations

Even during fee or quality dispute, participants may need to:

- protect deadlines;
- preserve Evidence;
- return client property;
- maintain confidentiality;
- cooperate in transition.

---

## 47. Minimum Cross-Orbit Governance Model

```text
Organization A
  │
  ├── client authority
  ├── sender Permission and Policy
  ├── minimum exchange package
  └── Human Review / approval
        │
        ▼
MGSN / Governed Communication / Execution
        │
        ▼
Organization B
  │
  ├── identity and eligibility
  ├── receiver Permission and Policy
  ├── conflict and data review
  ├── professional acceptance
  └── defined responsibility
        │
        ▼
Collaboration, Delivery, and Outcome
        │
        ▼
Separate Formal Records
+ Shared Correlation
+ Scoped Audit
+ Retention / Revocation / Feedback
```

---

## 48. Required Distinctions

```text
Cross-Product ≠ cross-Orbit
```

Independent organizations add separate authority boundaries.

```text
Sender permission ≠ receiver permission
```

Both sides evaluate.

```text
Sender approval ≠ receiver approval
```

Professional acceptance remains independent.

```text
Delivery ≠ ownership transfer
```

Purpose and rights remain.

```text
Provider access ≠ client ownership
```

Relationship responsibility stays explicit.

```text
Collaboration ≠ merged authority
```

Each organization remains accountable.

```text
AI approval in one Orbit ≠ AI approval in another
```

Agent governance is local.

```text
Shared correlation ≠ shared database
```

Formal records remain domain-owned.

---

## 49. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Transitive permission

One organization’s access authorizes every receiver.

### Full-package discovery

Candidates receive complete client files before selection.

### Provider-client capture

Network access transfers client ownership.

### Shared-workspace merger

Two Workplaces become one ambiguous authority boundary.

### AI onward transfer

A provider sends client data to an unapproved model.

### Audit overexposure

One organization can inspect the other’s full internal operations.

### Deletion overpromise

The system erases required professional records.

### Subcontracting chain

Data moves through unknown providers.

### Termination orphaning

Deadlines and work disappear when collaboration ends.

These designs may make collaboration easier initially.

They create serious professional and privacy risk.

## 50. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming cross-Orbit architecture must preserve:

```text
Every participating organization
remains an independent Orbit
with its own identity, Permission,
Policy, data, records,
and professional responsibility.

Cross-Orbit collaboration has
an explicit relationship basis,
purpose, scope, and responsibility map.

Sender authorization and
receiver eligibility are independent.

Authority is not transitive.

Information exposure expands by stage
and remains minimum necessary.

Exchange packages preserve
source, classification, restrictions,
retention, AI use, and onward-sharing limits.

Artifact, Document, Evidence,
Knowledge, Communication,
and formal records retain
their own boundaries.

AI authorization is evaluated
inside each Orbit and for each provider.

Instruction, acceptance, Review,
approval, Execution, and formal recording
remain distinct.

Retention, revocation, deletion,
incident, subcontracting, termination,
and dispute are governed explicitly.

Shared correlation supports continuity
without merging formal ownership.

Organizations retain client relationships,
commercial autonomy,
and professional accountability.
```

## 52. Chapter Conclusion

Connection across Orbits is valuable because organizations remain independent.

The governance model is therefore:

```text
Separate identities
→ explicit purpose
→ minimum exchange
→ independent authorization
→ defined responsibility
→ governed collaboration
→ separate formal records
→ shared trace and outcome
```

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Organizations connect.

Authority does not merge.

Data does not become ownerless.

Professional responsibility remains visible.

AI remains bounded.

Relationships remain organization-owned.

The network remains connective rather than controlling.
```
