# B04-CH-35 — Collaboration Outcomes and Trust Feedback

**Status:** Release Candidate 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part VI — Network Participation and Orbital Ecosystem Evolution

## Chapter Purpose

CH33 defined network layers.

CH34 defined Capability Evidence, Routing, and human selection.

This chapter defines the learning that follows real collaboration.

The central question is:

> How should delivery quality, professional outcome, Communication, timeliness, cost, problem handling, and dispute contribute to private and network Trust without becoming one universal public score?

Trust in professional services is not popularity.

It is accumulated confidence based on:

- relationship history;
- Capability Evidence;
- professional behavior;
- confidentiality;
- delivery;
- transparency;
- correction;
- outcome;
- context;
- human judgment.

The central proposition is:

```text
Trust Feedback
=
Collaboration Context
+ Defined Responsibility
+ Delivery and Outcome Evidence
+ Communication and Reliability
+ Commercial Transparency
+ Problem and Correction History
+ Source and Attribution
+ Scope and Time
+ Private Organization Judgment
+ Governed Network Contribution
```

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Collaboration completion ≠ successful outcome

Delivery ≠ acceptance

Official outcome ≠ provider quality alone

Client satisfaction ≠ legal correctness

One result ≠ universal trust

Private trust ≠ network trust

Feedback ≠ public rating

Complaint ≠ proven failure

Popularity ≠ reliability

Trust ≠ one permanent score
```

---

## 1. Collaboration Must Have a Defined Context

Trust feedback requires understanding:

- instructing organization;
- performing organization;
- responsible professionals;
- client;
- Matter;
- service;
- scope;
- deadline;
- commercial terms;
- data boundary;
- expected deliverable.

Without context, outcome comparison becomes misleading.

---

## 2. Responsibilities Must Be Explicit

A collaboration may divide responsibility among:

- instructing organization;
- local provider;
- client;
- translator;
- reviewer;
- filing connector;
- official authority.

Trust assessment should not assign every outcome to one participant.

---

## 3. Collaboration Completion Is a Process Result

A collaboration may be marked complete when:

- deliverables supplied;
- instruction closed;
- Matter transferred;
- final report sent.

Completion does not by itself establish quality or success.

---

## 4. Delivery Outcome Must Be Typed

Delivery may be:

- complete;
- partial;
- late;
- rejected;
- corrected;
- pending external confirmation;
- unavailable;
- disputed.

The feedback should reference the actual deliverable and version.

---

## 5. Professional Outcome Must Be Distinguished

Professional outcome may include:

- correct filing;
- accepted response;
- registration;
- opposition result;
- useful advice;
- accurate translation;
- compliant recordal.

Some outcomes depend on external authorities and facts beyond the provider’s control.

---

## 6. Official Outcome Does Not Equal Provider Quality Alone

An application may be refused despite high-quality work.

An application may register despite weak advice.

Quality review should consider:

- reasoning;
- process;
- completeness;
- communication;
- risk handling;
- professional standard.

---

## 7. Client Satisfaction Is One Dimension

Client satisfaction may reflect:

- clarity;
- speed;
- cost;
- expectations;
- result;
- relationship.

A satisfied client does not prove legal correctness.

A dissatisfied client does not prove provider failure.

---

## 8. Communication Reliability Is a Trust Dimension

Relevant observations may include:

- response time;
- clarity;
- proactive updates;
- acknowledgement;
- deadline communication;
- language;
- escalation;
- transparency.

Communication quality is contextual to agreed expectations.

---

## 9. Timeliness Is a Trust Dimension

Timeliness may consider:

- agreed deadline;
- official deadline;
- provider response;
- client delay;
- dependency;
- extension;
- emergency.

A late result should preserve cause and impact.

---

## 10. Commercial Transparency Is a Trust Dimension

Feedback may consider:

- fee clarity;
- official-fee separation;
- disbursement;
- scope change;
- invoice accuracy;
- unexpected cost;
- currency;
- tax;
- discount commitment.

Low price is not the only commercial quality.

---

## 11. Confidentiality Is a Trust Dimension

The organization may evaluate whether the provider:

- limited access;
- protected client information;
- used approved channels;
- respected subcontracting limits;
- avoided unauthorized disclosure;
- handled incidents responsibly.

Confidentiality feedback may be highly restricted.

---

## 12. Problem Handling Is a Trust Dimension

Professional trust often depends on what happens when work goes wrong.

Relevant behavior includes:

- disclosure;
- response speed;
- correction;
- responsibility;
- mitigation;
- cooperation;
- transparency;
- learning.

A perfect-history requirement may hide problems rather than improve trust.

---

## 13. Correction History Must Be Preserved

A provider may correct:

- wrong filing data;
- invoice;
- translation;
- report;
- status;
- deadline.

The final corrected outcome should not erase the original issue.

The correction may improve or reduce trust depending on context.

---

## 14. Dispute Must Be a First-Class State

A dispute may concern:

- scope;
- fee;
- delay;
- instruction;
- quality;
- responsibility;
- official effect.

The system should preserve positions and evidence.

It should not publish one side’s allegation as fact.

---

## 15. Complaint Is Not Proven Failure

A complaint is a feedback event.

It may be:

- substantiated;
- partly substantiated;
- unresolved;
- withdrawn;
- mistaken;
- malicious.

Network-level use requires governance.

---

## 16. Positive Feedback Is Not Universal Proof

A provider may be excellent for routine filing and unsuitable for litigation.

Positive feedback must preserve service and jurisdiction scope.

---

## 17. One Matter Is a Weak Universal Signal

One outcome may be affected by unusual facts.

Trust becomes stronger through:

- repeated evidence;
- relevant scope;
- current team;
- transparent context;
- consistent performance.

---

## 18. Trust Is Relational

Organization A may trust Provider X.

Organization B may have no basis to do so.

The platform should not replace relationship-specific judgment with universal reputation.

---

## 19. Private Trust Is the Default

The instructing organization may record private assessments of:

- reliability;
- quality;
- communication;
- fee;
- fit;
- preferred contact;
- risk.

These remain organization-controlled.

---

## 20. Private Trust May Be Person-Specific

An organization may trust:

- one partner;
- one case manager;
- one office;
- one team.

The provider organization as a whole may change.

Trust should preserve the responsible level.

---

## 21. Network Trust Is Governed Shared Context

Network Trust may use:

- verified identity;
- consented Capability Evidence;
- anonymized outcomes;
- validated collaboration signals;
- dispute status;
- freshness.

It must not expose private notes automatically.

---

## 22. Private and Network Trust May Differ

A private partner may be strongly trusted despite limited public evidence.

A publicly verified provider may have no private relationship history.

The organization-specific view may present both.

---

## 23. Trust Should Be Multidimensional

Possible dimensions include:

```text
Relationship Strength

Capability Evidence

Qualification Confidence

Communication Reliability

Delivery Reliability

Professional Quality

Commercial Transparency

Confidentiality

Problem Handling

Current Availability
```

One number may hide critical differences.

---

## 24. Composite Trust Score Is Optional and Limited

If a composite is used, it should:

- explain dimensions;
- show missing data;
- preserve private vs shared source;
- show freshness;
- avoid false precision;
- permit human override.

The score must not become appointment authority.

---

## 25. Trust Decays and Changes

Trust may change because:

- people leave;
- office changes;
- service focus changes;
- repeated outcomes improve confidence;
- unresolved disputes reduce confidence;
- evidence becomes stale.

Trust should have time context.

---

## 26. Trust Feedback Requires Source

Feedback may come from:

- instructing professional;
- client;
- Product analytics;
- official outcome;
- reviewer;
- provider self-report;
- support investigation.

The source influences weight and visibility.

---

## 27. Trust Feedback Requires Evidence Where Consequential

High-impact negative or positive network conclusions may require:

- Matter reference;
- deliverable;
- Communication trace;
- official outcome;
- review;
- dispute process.

Unsubstantiated opinion should remain limited.

---

## 28. Feedback Should Be Structured Enough for Scope

A feedback record may identify:

- service;
- jurisdiction;
- role;
- time;
- expected deliverable;
- observed behavior;
- outcome;
- confidence;
- visibility.

Structure supports contextual use.

---

## 29. Free-Text Feedback Requires Privacy Review

Free text may contain:

- client name;
- confidential facts;
- accusations;
- personal data;
- legal strategy;
- commercial terms.

It should not become public or AI training data automatically.

---

## 30. Provider Should Have Correction Rights

Where network-level information affects a provider, the provider should be able to:

- see permitted summary;
- identify factual error;
- provide context;
- submit correction;
- mark dispute.

This does not require revealing another organization’s private notes.

---

## 31. Network Governance Must Handle Abuse

Potential abuse includes:

- retaliatory ratings;
- competitor manipulation;
- paid praise;
- duplicate feedback;
- fabricated Matter;
- coercion;
- review bombing.

Trust systems require identity, evidence, and moderation.

---

## 32. Feedback Visibility Must Be Controlled

Possible scopes include:

- personal;
- team;
- organization;
- trusted network;
- selected candidates;
- governed aggregate;
- public.

Public visibility should be the exception, not the default.

---

## 33. Aggregation Must Protect Confidentiality

Aggregated network signals may require:

- minimum sample size;
- anonymization;
- scope;
- delay;
- removal of outliers;
- consent;
- legal review.

Aggregation does not automatically eliminate privacy risk.

---

## 34. Outcome Evidence May Update Capability Candidates

A validated collaboration may support:

- service Capability;
- jurisdiction experience;
- response reliability;
- delivery quality;
- problem-handling evidence.

It remains scoped and time-bound.

---

## 35. Outcome Feedback May Update Routing Quality

The system may compare:

- recommended candidate;
- selected candidate;
- actual outcome;
- human override;
- failure.

This can improve future recommendations.

It must not eliminate human choice.

---

## 36. Outcome Feedback May Update Private Preferences

An organization may adjust:

- preferred provider;
- backup provider;
- price expectation;
- communication route;
- review level;
- service scope.

These preferences remain private.

---

## 37. Outcome Feedback May Create Escalation

A serious issue may require:

- internal incident;
- provider suspension candidate;
- legal review;
- client notification;
- data incident response;
- network governance review.

A low score alone should not trigger irreversible punishment.

---

## 38. Suspension and Revocation Require Governance

Network participation may be limited because of:

- expired qualification;
- identity issue;
- serious confidentiality breach;
- repeated verified failure;
- fraud;
- unresolved risk.

The decision should preserve evidence, scope, authority, and review.

---

## 39. Trust Feedback Must Not Replace Professional Due Diligence

Even a highly trusted provider may require:

- current conflict check;
- current qualification;
- current quote;
- current availability;
- Matter-specific review.

Trust supports decision.

It does not eliminate due diligence.

---

## 40. Trust Feedback Must Not Replace Contracting

A strong rating does not define:

- scope;
- fee;
- liability;
- confidentiality;
- deadline;
- governing terms.

Engagement remains explicit.

---

## 41. Collaboration Learning Returns to Both Orbits

Each organization may learn from:

- Communication;
- delivery;
- professional process;
- technical integration;
- client response;
- outcome.

The learning may differ because responsibilities differ.

---

## 42. Shared Learning Does Not Require Shared Raw History

MGSN may learn from governed aggregates or contributed evidence.

It does not need every private Communication, invoice, Document, or client record.

---

## 43. Historical Trust Must Be Explainable

A user should be able to understand:

```text
Why is this provider preferred?

Which services support that view?

Which evidence is private?

Which evidence is shared?

How current is it?

Are disputes unresolved?
```

## 46. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Star-rating reduction

Complex professional trust becomes one public number.

### Official-result attribution

All success or failure is assigned to the provider.

### Private-note publication

Confidential organization assessments become public.

### Complaint punishment

One unverified complaint creates automatic exclusion.

### Positive-universalization

One success proves all Capabilities.

### Trust-staleness

Old team performance appears current.

### Feedback-surveillance

Every Communication becomes employee or provider scoring.

### Contracting-by-rating

High trust bypasses scope and terms.

These designs may resemble consumer marketplaces.

They do not fit professional Orbits.

## 47. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming collaboration and Trust architecture must preserve:

```text
Every Trust signal remains linked
to collaboration context,
responsibility, service, jurisdiction,
source, time, and evidence.

Completion, Delivery, professional outcome,
official outcome, client satisfaction,
commercial outcome, and provider quality
remain distinct.

Trust remains relational,
contextual, multidimensional,
time-aware, and contestable.

Private Trust remains
organization-controlled by default.

Network Trust uses only
governed shared evidence and aggregates.

Complaints, disputes, corrections,
positive outcomes, and failures
retain context and review.

Providers receive appropriate
correction and dispute mechanisms
without access to private notes.

Trust supports but does not replace
eligibility, due diligence,
human selection, contracting,
or professional judgment.

Shared learning does not require
shared raw collaboration history.
```

## 49. Chapter Conclusion

Trust is not a score attached to provider inventory.

It is a contextual conclusion formed through real professional relationships.

The correct loop is:

```text
Collaboration
→ delivery and professional outcome
→ contextual feedback
→ private Trust
→ governed evidence contribution
→ improved future selection
```

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Outcomes create evidence.

Evidence informs Trust.

Trust remains contextual.

Organizations retain judgment.

Providers remain contestable and independent.

The network learns without owning the relationship.
```

> How can Knowledge, Capability, Skills, Products, Workflows, Artifacts, Routing, and governance improve across the MarkOrbit ecosystem without requiring every organization to share raw data, adopt one practice, or converge on one centralized operating model?
