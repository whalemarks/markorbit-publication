# B04-CH-34 — Capability Evidence, Routing and Human Selection

**Status:** Release Candidate 1  
**Chapter Map:** B04-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part VI — Network Participation and Orbital Ecosystem Evolution

## Chapter Purpose

CH33 defined the progression from private networks to trusted extended networks and limited public discovery.

This chapter defines how candidates are evaluated and selected.

The central question is:

> How should Capability claims become scoped evidence, how should Routing compare eligible candidates, and how should human selection remain authoritative before provider appointment or instruction?

The central proposition is:

```text
Governed Provider Selection
=
Capability Need
+ Candidate Identity
+ Scoped Capability Evidence
+ Verification State
+ Eligibility Gates
+ Contextual Trust
+ Availability and Conflict
+ Commercial Context
+ Explainable Routing
+ Human Selection
+ Approval and Instruction Handoff
```

The constitutional chain is:

```text
Capability claim
→ evidence
→ validation
→ governed profile
→ contextual eligibility
→ candidate comparison
→ Routing recommendation
→ human selection
→ Routing decision
→ appointment preparation
→ approval where required
→ governed provider instruction
→ provider acceptance
```

The constitutional distinctions are:

```text
Claim ≠ evidence

Evidence ≠ validation

Validation ≠ universal capability

Capability ≠ qualification

Qualification ≠ availability

Eligibility ≠ ranking

Ranking ≠ Routing decision

Routing recommendation ≠ human selection

Human selection ≠ appointment

Appointment ≠ instruction

Outcome evidence ≠ universal score
```

## 1. Capability Evidence Supports a Defined Claim

Capability Evidence is:

> source-linked information supporting the proposition that an organization, team, or professional may perform a defined Capability under a defined scope and time.

The evidence must answer:

```text
Capability for what?

In which jurisdiction?

For which service?

By which organization or professional?

Under which conditions?

Based on what source?

As of when?
```

---

## 2. Capability Claim Is the Starting Point

A provider may claim:

- trademark filing;
- opposition;
- litigation;
- translation;
- evidence collection;
- recordal;
- portfolio strategy;
- local representation.

A claim enables discovery.

It does not establish reliability.

---

## 3. Evidence Types Must Be Distinguishable

Evidence may include:

- official qualification;
- professional registration;
- organization identity;
- jurisdiction coverage;
- prior collaboration;
- completed Matter;
- verified outcome;
- work sample;
- peer referral;
- training;
- language capability;
- availability history;
- quality review.

Different evidence supports different aspects.

---

## 4. Evidence Source Must Be Visible

The source may be:

- provider self-declaration;
- uploaded record;
- official register;
- private organization history;
- trusted referral;
- network validation;
- public source;
- external authority;
- Product outcome.

The source influences confidence.

---

## 5. Evidence Must Be Scoped

Evidence should be scoped by:

- Capability;
- service;
- jurisdiction;
- language;
- complexity;
- organization;
- team or professional;
- client type;
- time.

A successful filing in one jurisdiction does not prove global opposition capability.

---

## 6. Evidence Freshness Matters

Evidence may become stale because:

- license expired;
- team changed;
- office closed;
- provider stopped a service;
- jurisdiction rules changed;
- availability changed;
- technology changed.

Historical evidence may remain useful.

Current eligibility still requires current context.

---

## 7. Evidence Quality and Evidence Quantity Differ

Many weak self-declarations do not equal one strong official verification.

The system should not rank by count alone.

Evidence quality depends on:

- source authority;
- directness;
- recency;
- scope;
- integrity;
- review;
- dispute status.

---

## 8. Verification Is a Process, Not a Badge Alone

Verification may mean:

- identity checked;
- document checked;
- official register checked;
- qualification current;
- outcome validated;
- relationship confirmed.

The system should explain what was verified.

A generic badge can overstate authority.

---

## 9. Verification May Expire

A verified qualification may need renewal.

A verified office address may change.

The profile should preserve verification date and current status.

---

## 10. Capability and Qualification Must Remain Distinct

Qualification may permit professional practice.

Capability may reflect actual ability and experience.

A qualified professional may lack experience in a specialized service.

A capable support provider may not have authority to provide legal advice.

Routing must respect both.

---

## 11. Capability and Capacity Must Remain Distinct

A provider may have the ability but not current capacity.

Capacity may include:

- workload;
- deadline;
- team availability;
- geographic coverage;
- language support.

A high-capability unavailable provider is not an eligible current option.

---

## 12. Capability and Trust Must Remain Distinct

Evidence may indicate ability.

Trust may reflect relationship, confidentiality, communication, and consistency.

A new provider may have strong evidence but no private trust history.

Both dimensions matter.

---

## 13. Capability Profile Must Be Governed

A governed profile may include:

- claims;
- evidence;
- verification;
- scope;
- restrictions;
- current status;
- disputes;
- version;
- responsible organization.

The profile is not a marketing page only.

---

## 14. Private Evidence and Shared Evidence Must Be Distinct

Private Evidence may include:

- internal quality notes;
- client-specific results;
- private fee history;
- confidential mistakes.

Shared Evidence may include:

- public qualification;
- consented anonymized outcomes;
- verified capability statements.

Private Evidence should not become network-public automatically.

---

## 15. Outcome Evidence Requires Attribution Review

A successful result may reflect:

- provider work;
- instructing organization;
- client cooperation;
- official-office conditions;
- prior facts.

Outcome Evidence should not assign sole credit without basis.

---

## 16. Negative Evidence Requires Context

A failure may arise from:

- provider error;
- incomplete instruction;
- client delay;
- official rejection;
- platform failure;
- scope dispute.

Negative Evidence should preserve context and dispute state.

---

## 17. Evidence May Be Disputed

A provider may challenge:

- identity error;
- incorrect outcome attribution;
- stale qualification;
- unfair feedback;
- wrong scope.

The system should support correction and review.

---

## 18. Eligibility Precedes Ranking

Before ranking, Routing should evaluate whether a candidate is eligible under:

- jurisdiction;
- service;
- qualification;
- conflict;
- availability;
- data boundary;
- client restriction;
- organization Policy;
- provider status;
- evidence minimum.

Ineligible candidates should not be promoted by score.

---

## 19. Eligibility Is Contextual

A provider may be eligible for one Matter and ineligible for another.

Eligibility is not a permanent universal label.

---

## 20. Conflict Is a Distinct Gate

Conflict status may be:

- not checked;
- check requested;
- clear for defined scope;
- conflict found;
- uncertain;
- expired.

No conflict is not the same as capability.

---

## 21. Availability Is a Distinct Gate

Availability may be:

- confirmed;
- limited;
- pending;
- unavailable;
- unknown.

A historical response time does not prove present availability.

---

## 22. Data Handling May Be an Eligibility Gate

A Matter may require:

- local processing;
- no AI;
- specific jurisdiction;
- confidentiality terms;
- limited subcontracting;
- no public cloud.

A capable provider may still be ineligible under the data boundary.

---

## 23. Client Restriction May Be an Eligibility Gate

A client may prohibit:

- certain providers;
- certain countries;
- subcontracting;
- public discovery;
- prior adverse parties.

The organization should preserve the instruction.

---

## 24. Routing Is a Governed Decision Context

Routing means:

> the governed evaluation and selection context through which a need is directed toward an eligible target under explicit criteria, evidence, human decision, and trace.

Routing is not merely sorting a list.

---

## 25. Routing Request Must Be Typed

The request should identify:

- organization;
- Capability Need;
- subject;
- service;
- jurisdiction;
- timing;
- data boundary;
- decision-maker;
- review;
- expected result.

---

## 26. Candidate Set Must Be Preserved

The Routing context should preserve:

- candidates considered;
- candidates excluded;
- exclusion reasons;
- evidence available;
- missing evidence;
- ranking basis;
- private relationship context;
- time.

This supports explanation and review.

---

## 27. Ranking Is a Recommendation Mechanism

Ranking may consider:

- Capability fit;
- qualification;
- evidence strength;
- private trust;
- response reliability;
- delivery quality;
- availability;
- price;
- language;
- data handling;
- client preference.

Ranking does not make the decision.

---

## 28. Composite Scores Must Be Explainable

If a composite score is used, the user should understand:

- dimensions;
- weights or importance;
- missing data;
- uncertainty;
- organization preference;
- overrides;
- freshness.

A hidden score is unsuitable for professional selection.

---

## 29. Price Is One Dimension

Price may include:

- official fee;
- professional fee;
- disbursement;
- tax;
- urgency;
- currency;
- private rate;
- indicative quote.

The lowest price does not establish best fit.

---

## 30. Private Relationship May Influence Ranking

An existing trusted provider may be preferred.

That preference should remain visible.

It should not override conflict, qualification, or client prohibition.

---

## 31. Evidence Gaps Must Be Visible

A candidate may rank well but lack:

- current availability;
- current fee;
- specialized outcome evidence;
- conflict confirmation;
- data-handling information.

The user should see the gap.

---

## 32. Routing Recommendation Must Include Alternatives

A recommendation should identify:

- recommended candidate;
- alternatives;
- rationale;
- evidence;
- tradeoffs;
- missing context;
- risk;
- validity.

This prevents false certainty.

---

## 33. Human Selection Is the Authoritative Choice

The participating organization selects the provider or target.

The selector must have:

- organizational authority;
- relevant context;
- Permission;
- Policy compliance;
- professional responsibility where required.

AI and platform ranking support the decision.

They do not replace it.

---

## 34. Human Selection Must Be Typed

Actions may include:

- shortlist;
- request more evidence;
- request quote;
- request conflict check;
- reject;
- defer;
- select for Routing;
- approve appointment;
- approve instruction.

One `Select` button should not collapse these consequences.

---

## 35. Human Override Must Be Recorded

A human may choose a candidate other than the top recommendation.

The system may preserve:

- reason;
- client instruction;
- private relationship;
- strategic consideration;
- price;
- risk acceptance;
- evidence unavailable to the system.

Override is not automatically error.

---

## 36. Human Selection Does Not Grant Permission

Selecting a provider does not:

- create professional qualification;
- override Policy;
- grant data access;
- approve fee;
- authorize Communication;
- create engagement.

Subsequent gates remain.

---

## 37. Routing Decision Is a Formal Record Where Required

Routing Service may record:

- need;
- candidate set;
- evaluation;
- selected target;
- decision-maker;
- review;
- override;
- time;
- conditions.

The Product or AI must not fabricate this formal decision.

---

## 38. Routing Decision Does Not Appoint Provider

Appointment may require:

- conflict clearance;
- engagement terms;
- client authority;
- fee approval;
- provider acceptance;
- formal service record.

Routing identifies the target.

Appointment establishes the relationship for the service.

---

## 39. Appointment Does Not Send Instruction

Provider instruction may disclose data and trigger work.

It requires Communication and Execution governance.

The sequence remains:

```text
Human selection
→ Routing decision
→ appointment preparation
→ approval
→ provider instruction
→ provider acceptance
```

---

## 40. Provider Acceptance Is Independent

A provider may:

- accept;
- decline;
- narrow scope;
- request information;
- change price;
- identify conflict.

The instructing organization’s selection does not bind the provider.

---

## 41. Routing Decision May Expire

The decision may become stale if:

- availability changes;
- conflict appears;
- fee changes;
- deadline passes;
- client withdraws;
- provider status changes.

Execution should revalidate material conditions.

---

## 42. Multi-Provider Selection Must Be Explicit

A Matter may need:

- local counsel;
- translator;
- investigator;
- filing provider;
- reviewer.

Each role and responsibility should be explicit.

One provider score should not govern all roles.

---

## 43. AI May Assist Evidence and Routing

AI may:

- extract claims;
- summarize evidence;
- identify gaps;
- compare candidates;
- explain ranking;
- prepare questions;
- draft decision rationale.

AI must not invent evidence or make the final appointment.

---

## 44. AI Confidence Is Not Capability Confidence

A model may be confident in its summary while the underlying evidence is weak.

The system should display source strength separately from AI certainty.

---

## 45. Routing Analytics Must Not Become Hidden Authority

Analytics may improve:

- matching;
- response estimates;
- evidence quality;
- candidate discovery.

Historical success rates must not silently become mandatory allocation.

---

## 46. Safe Failure Is Required

Routing may return:

- no eligible candidate;
- insufficient evidence;
- conflict unresolved;
- availability unknown;
- Policy denied;
- data boundary unsupported.

The system should not weaken requirements to force a result.

---


Self-description does not prove ability.

```text

```

Evidence requires interpretation and scope.

```text
Capability ≠ qualification
```

Ability and legal authority differ.

```text
Capability ≠ availability
```

Current capacity matters.

```text
Eligibility ≠ ranking
```

Only eligible candidates should be compared.

```text
Ranking ≠ decision
```

Recommendation supports human judgment.

```text
Selection ≠ appointment
```

Commercial and professional gates remain.

```text
Appointment ≠ instruction
```

Protected Communication remains.

```text
Outcome evidence ≠ universal rating
```

Scope and attribution remain.

## 49. Failure Modes This Chapter Prevents

### Profile-as-proof

Self-declared capability is treated as verified.

### Badge overclaim

A generic verification badge hides what was checked.

### Evidence-count ranking

Many weak signals outrank strong evidence.

### Price-first routing

Lowest fee overrides professional fit.

### Ineligible top candidate

Ranking occurs before qualification or conflict.

### Automatic appointment

The system selects and engages the top result.

### Human-selection laundering

A user click bypasses approval and instruction governance.

### Outcome universalization

One success becomes global capability score.

These designs may make routing fast.

They do not make it professionally accountable.

## 50. Minimum Conformance Rule

A conforming Capability Evidence and Routing architecture must preserve:

```text
Capability claims remain distinct
from source-linked evidence.

Evidence remains scoped by
service, jurisdiction, organization,
team, professional, complexity,
time, and purpose.

Verification explains
what was checked and when.

Qualification, Capability,
capacity, trust, availability,
conflict, and eligibility
remain distinct.

Eligibility gates precede ranking.

Candidate sets, exclusions,
evidence gaps, dimensions,
alternatives, and tradeoffs
remain explainable.

Price remains one dimension.

AI and analytics may assist
but do not make the authoritative choice.

A qualified human acting for
the participating organization selects.

Routing Service records
the formal decision where required.

Selection, Routing decision,
appointment, instruction,
and provider acceptance
remain separate.

No eligible candidate remains
a valid safe result.
```

## 52. Chapter Conclusion

Professional routing is not a race to produce one answer.

It is a governed decision process:

```text
Claim
→ evidence
→ validation
→ eligibility
→ comparison
→ explanation
→ human selection
→ formal Routing
→ governed engagement
```

The constitutional outcome is:

```text
Capability creates relevance.

Evidence supports confidence.

Eligibility protects the Matter.

Routing structures the decision.

Humans select.

Organizations remain responsible.

Execution governs instruction.

Providers remain independent.
```

CH35 now addresses what happens after collaboration:

> How should delivery quality, professional outcome, Communication, timeliness, cost, problem handling, and dispute contribute to private and network Trust without becoming one universal public score?
