# B05-CH-10 — Country Bundles and Portfolio Strategy

**Status:** Part II Draft  
**Chapter Map:** B05-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part II — Need, Strategy and Recommendation

## Chapter Purpose

CH09 defined jurisdiction and filing-route recommendations as separate, explainable candidates.

The next Product problem is composition.

Users rarely decide one jurisdiction in isolation.

They ask:

- Which countries should we file now?
- Which countries can wait?
- Which route is more efficient?
- Which combination fits the budget?
- Which markets create the highest risk?
- Which rights already exist?
- Which applications should be consolidated, repeated, or avoided?
- Which strategy creates the strongest long-term portfolio?

A country bundle can simplify comparison.

It can also hide material differences.

The central proposition is:

```text
Portfolio Strategy
=
Individual Jurisdiction Candidates
+ Existing Portfolio
+ Business Scenarios
+ Filing-Route Dependencies
+ Timing and Priority
+ Budget and Resource Constraints
+ Risk and Enforcement Priorities
+ Staging and Review
+ Visible Omissions
+ Versioned Decision Rationale
```

The Product may prepare **Portfolio Strategy Scenarios** and **Country Bundle Candidates**.

```text
Bundle Candidate
≠ One Legal Right
≠ One Filing
≠ One Official Fee
≠ One Deadline
≠ One Provider Engagement
≠ One Outcome
```

---

## 1. A Bundle Is a Decision Interface

A bundle is a Product presentation that groups several jurisdiction and route candidates for comparison or staged action.

It may help the user see:

- total estimated cost;
- immediate coverage;
- deferred coverage;
- route relationships;
- common document needs;
- timing;
- provider needs;
- risk concentration;
- portfolio gaps.

The bundle does not merge the underlying jurisdictions into one legal object.

---

## 2. Every Jurisdiction Retains Independent Consequences

Within one bundle, jurisdictions may differ in:

- applicant eligibility;
- mark format;
- class structure;
- goods/services requirements;
- priority;
- documents;
- official fees;
- professional fees;
- provider requirements;
- examination;
- opposition;
- maintenance;
- renewal;
- enforceability;
- official outcome.

The Product should allow the user to open each jurisdiction card and see those differences.

---

## 3. Bundle Names Must Describe a Strategy

Useful bundle names may include:

- Core Launch Markets;
- Manufacturing and Sales Protection;
- Regional Expansion Wave;
- Marketplace Enforcement Priorities;
- Minimum Viable Coverage;
- Recommended Two-Year Expansion;
- Priority-Preservation Filings;
- Existing Portfolio Gap Closure.

Weak bundle name:

```text
Package A
```

A strategy name helps the user understand why the jurisdictions were grouped.

---

## 4. Required, Recommended, Optional, and Deferred Items Must Remain Visible

A bundle may contain:

```text
Required now
Strongly recommended now
Optional now
Deferred to later wave
Not included
Professional review required
```

The Product should not flatten the list into “included” and “not included”.

The reason for each category should remain available.

---

## 5. Omission Is Part of the Recommendation

A useful strategy explains:

- which jurisdictions are omitted;
- why they are omitted;
- which risk remains;
- when they should be reconsidered;
- which facts would change the decision.

Example:

```text
Deferred:
Country X

Reason:
No launch or manufacturing activity expected during the next 18 months.

Reconsider if:
a distributor is appointed or local marketplace sales begin.
```

A bundle that hides omissions may create false confidence.

---

## 6. Portfolio Strategy Begins with Existing Coverage

MarkReg should identify:

- active applications;
- active registrations;
- expiring rights;
- cancelled or refused rights;
- limited specifications;
- ownership inconsistencies;
- duplicate filings;
- related marks;
- missing local-language versions;
- missing classes;
- geographic gaps.

New filing should be compared with existing coverage.

The Product should not recommend a complete new bundle simply because the user started a new journey.

---

## 7. Portfolio Gaps Must Be Explainable

A gap may arise from:

- no right in a relevant jurisdiction;
- wrong applicant;
- wrong mark;
- insufficient class coverage;
- insufficient item coverage;
- lapsed registration;
- pending assignment;
- route dependency;
- weak enforceability;
- missing local-language protection;
- new business activity.

The Product should state the gap type.

“Not covered” is often too broad.

---

## 8. Duplicate Rights Are Not Always Waste

Duplicate or overlapping rights may support:

- different mark variants;
- different owners;
- different priority chains;
- defensive coverage;
- local enforcement;
- recovery from route dependency;
- licensing;
- portfolio restructuring.

They may also create:

- unnecessary cost;
- inconsistent ownership;
- conflicting specifications;
- confusing renewal duties.

MarkReg should flag duplication and explain possible reasons.

It should not automatically delete or reject overlapping candidates.

---

## 9. Portfolio Strategy May Be Scenario-Based

Common scenarios include:

```text
Scenario 1:
Protect current revenue markets

Scenario 2:
Protect manufacturing and supply chain

Scenario 3:
Support next launch wave

Scenario 4:
Reduce copying and marketplace risk

Scenario 5:
Build investment-ready portfolio

Scenario 6:
Use minimum budget for maximum immediate coverage
```

The same jurisdiction may rank differently across scenarios.

The Product should allow comparison without pretending that one scenario is universally correct.

---

## 10. Staging Converts a Large Strategy into Waves

A portfolio plan may be staged as:

```text
Wave 1:
urgent and required jurisdictions

Wave 2:
near-term growth jurisdictions

Wave 3:
defensive or lower-confidence jurisdictions

Maintenance wave:
renewals, recordals, and clean-up
```

Each wave should preserve:

- trigger;
- target date;
- dependency;
- estimated cost;
- unresolved facts;
- responsible actor;
- Review requirement.

A wave is a planning construct.

It is not an official deadline or Execution state.

---

## 11. Priority May Constrain Staging

A staged plan must identify when delay may affect:

- priority;
- route eligibility;
- launch;
- disclosure;
- contractual obligations;
- enforcement position.

A lower-priority jurisdiction may still require early action if a priority window is expiring.

The Product should not stage solely by commercial ranking.

---

## 12. Filing Routes May Couple Some Bundle Items

A bundle may include:

- one international filing with several designations;
- direct filings in selected jurisdictions;
- a regional filing;
- later territorial extension;
- a mixed route.

The Product should show:

- which items are procedurally linked;
- which remain independent;
- which depend on a basic right;
- which can be changed separately;
- which provider or document requirements are shared;
- which later actions remain jurisdiction-specific.

---

## 13. Route Efficiency Must Not Hide Dependency Risk

An international or regional route may reduce some operational effort.

It may also create:

- central dependency;
- scope limitations;
- mark-alignment constraints;
- applicant-alignment constraints;
- later transformation or conversion complexity;
- different recordal consequences.

Efficiency is one dimension.

It is not the full recommendation.

---

## 14. Budget Allocation Should Be Transparent

MarkReg may show:

- immediate budget;
- total estimated lifecycle budget;
- official fees;
- professional fees;
- provider costs;
- taxes;
- translation;
- document formalities;
- search;
- later-stage costs;
- contingency;
- currency assumptions.

The Product should distinguish:

```text
Budget cap
Estimated current-stage cost
Estimated later-stage cost
Unpriced risk
```

A low first-stage cost may not represent the long-term portfolio cost.

---

## 15. Cost Reduction Options Must Show the Trade-Off

Possible cost-reduction strategies include:

- defer lower-priority jurisdictions;
- reduce filing units;
- reduce optional classes;
- narrow goods/services;
- use a different route;
- stage searches;
- use existing provider relationships;
- combine document preparation.

Each option should show what protection, flexibility, evidence, or speed may be lost.

The Product should not frame every reduction as equivalent savings.

---

## 16. Risk Weighting Should Be Contextual

Portfolio risk may include:

- loss of priority;
- earlier rights;
- copying;
- first-to-file exposure;
- manufacturing vulnerability;
- marketplace enforcement gap;
- applicant inconsistency;
- non-use;
- route dependency;
- provider complexity;
- cost volatility;
- deadline uncertainty.

A portfolio may intentionally accept one risk to reduce another.

The decision should remain visible.

---

## 17. Enforcement-Oriented Strategy Differs from Registration-Only Strategy

A registration strategy may focus on:

- filing eligibility;
- registrability;
- scope;
- cost;
- timing.

An enforcement-oriented strategy may also focus on:

- customs;
- marketplace mechanisms;
- evidence;
- local representation;
- litigation readiness;
- language versions;
- product categories;
- known counterfeit channels.

The Product should ask which outcome the organization values.

It should not promise enforcement merely because a registration is included.

---

## 18. Manufacturing Strategy Should Not Be Treated as Sales Strategy

Manufacturing-related filings may respond to:

- supplier risk;
- local bad-faith applications;
- customs;
- tooling;
- contract requirements;
- counterfeit leakage;
- ownership disputes.

Sales-related filings may respond to:

- customer recognition;
- local distribution;
- online enforcement;
- licensing;
- expansion.

One bundle may include both.

The reasons should remain separate.

---

## 19. Local-Language and Transliteration Strategy May Create Additional Filing Units

A geographic bundle may require consideration of:

- original-language mark;
- local script;
- transliteration;
- translation;
- nickname;
- abbreviated mark;
- local consumer usage.

These are not automatically extra items in every bundle.

CH11 defines the filing-unit analysis.

CH10 should at least show when the bundle assumes additional mark variants.

---

## 20. Class and Goods/Services Scope Must Be Comparable

A country bundle may appear cheaper because some jurisdictions contain fewer classes or narrower items.

The comparison should identify:

- class count;
- item count;
- scope differences;
- local grouping;
- excluded items;
- amendment assumptions;
- official-fee consequences.

Equivalent pricing requires equivalent scope.

Where equivalence is impossible, the difference should be explicit.

---

## 21. Provider Strategy Is Part of Portfolio Feasibility

A portfolio plan may depend on:

- current private partners;
- local counsel;
- direct connectors;
- central coordination;
- translation;
- evidence specialists;
- conflict clearance;
- capacity.

MarkReg may identify the capability need.

It should not silently appoint providers or assume availability.

---

## 22. Internal Workload May Constrain the Plan

The organization may need capacity for:

- applicant verification;
- specification Review;
- client approval;
- document collection;
- translation;
- payment;
- provider coordination;
- deadline monitoring;
- office action response.

A portfolio strategy that exceeds operational capacity may create more risk than a staged plan.

Workload should be visible where material.

---

## 23. Portfolio Strategy Should Preserve Relationship Ownership

If external providers are involved, the bundle should not expose:

- client identity;
- confidential market plans;
- private pricing;
- complete portfolio;
- strategic rationale;

unless the purpose and authorization require it.

MGSN and provider collaboration remain private-first and purpose-limited.

---

## 24. Bundle Comparison Should Support More Than Total Cost

A useful comparison may include:

| Dimension | Scenario A | Scenario B | Scenario C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Immediate markets | high | medium | high |
| Manufacturing coverage | low | high | high |
| Priority preservation | high | high | high |
| Estimated initial cost | low | medium | high |
| Long-term coverage | medium | medium | high |
| Operational complexity | low | medium | high |
| Unresolved risk | medium | medium | low |

The dimensions should be adapted to the Need Brief.

---

## 25. Bundle Selection Is Not Commercial Acceptance

A user may select a scenario for further review.

That selection does not automatically:

- accept a quotation;
- create an Order;
- authorize payment;
- create a Matter;
- approve applicant identity;
- approve specification;
- instruct a provider;
- authorize filing.

The Product should record the selection as a Product decision state.

Part III defines the commercial and formal handoff.

---

## 26. Material Changes Require Recalculation

A portfolio strategy may need recalculation when:

- market plan changes;
- budget changes;
- search results arrive;
- mark changes;
- applicant changes;
- priority expires;
- official fees change;
- provider availability changes;
- existing rights are discovered;
- a jurisdiction becomes unavailable;
- launch timing changes.

A previously selected bundle should not remain “approved” after a material change without re-evaluation.

---

## 27. Bundle Versions Must Preserve Rationale

A versioned bundle should preserve:

- included jurisdictions;
- excluded jurisdictions;
- route;
- mark filing units;
- class and scope assumptions;
- cost assumptions;
- timing assumptions;
- priority context;
- risk rationale;
- provider assumptions;
- source versions;
- user selections;
- professional Review;
- change history.

The Product should support comparison between versions.

---

## 28. Portfolio Strategy May Produce No-Filing Recommendations

A valid strategy may recommend:

- monitor first;
- complete assignment first;
- clarify ownership;
- search first;
- wait for final mark;
- consolidate existing rights;
- renew existing registration;
- limit filing;
- take no action now.

MarkReg should not measure success only by the number of filings generated.

---

## 29. Portfolio Learning Is Organization-Specific

Over time, the organization may learn:

- which markets consistently matter;
- which providers perform well;
- which classes are repeatedly required;
- which filing routes create friction;
- which cost assumptions are inaccurate;
- which bundles convert;
- which risks materialize.

This learning remains private by default.

It may improve future bundle preparation without becoming universal truth automatically.

---

## 30. Country Bundle Candidate Structure

A useful candidate may contain:

```text
Bundle identifier and version
Strategy name
Need Brief reference
Scenario objective
Included jurisdictions
Recommendation category per jurisdiction
Route per jurisdiction
Wave or sequence
Existing-right references
Mark-unit assumptions
Class and goods/services assumptions
Priority context
Cost assumptions
Timing assumptions
Provider capability needs
Risk and enforcement rationale
Omissions and residual risk
Sources and checked dates
Required Review
User selection state
```

This is a Product composition.

It is not one formal legal object.

---

## 31. Failure Modes to Reject

```text
One package price with hidden jurisdiction scope
All countries treated as legally equivalent
Omitted countries not shown
Existing rights ignored
Bundle selected shown as Order accepted
Route dependency hidden
Priority conflict ignored
Different class scope compared as equivalent
Provider availability assumed
Manufacturing and sales reasons merged
Global coverage language used for partial coverage
Cheapest bundle shown as best bundle
Later-stage costs omitted
Bundle version overwritten without rationale
No-filing option excluded
```

---

## 32. Minimum Portfolio Strategy Lock

```text
Bundles simplify decisions.

They do not merge legal rights.

Every jurisdiction retains its own
route, scope, cost, deadline,
provider, examination, maintenance,
and outcome.

Existing rights are considered first.

Scenarios and waves remain explainable.

Omissions and residual risk remain visible.

Budget reductions show trade-offs.

Bundle selection remains a Product decision.

Commercial acceptance, formal objects,
Review, approval, and filing remain later gates.
```

---

## 33. Transition to Mark Identity and Filing Units

A portfolio strategy cannot be priced, reviewed, or filed correctly until the Product understands what is being protected.

One commercial brand may require:

- a word mark;
- one or more logos;
- a local-language version;
- a transliteration;
- a slogan;
- a packaging or shape mark;
- several materially different variants.

CH11 defines how MarkReg turns brand context into explicit filing-unit candidates without assuming that one brand equals one application.
