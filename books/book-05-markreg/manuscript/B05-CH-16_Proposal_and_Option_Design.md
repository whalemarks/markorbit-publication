# B05-CH-16 — Proposal and Option Design

**Status:** Part III Draft  
**Chapter Map:** B05-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part III — Commercial Journey and Formal Intake

## Chapter Purpose

Part II ends with a connected set of versioned Product candidates: Need Brief, jurisdiction strategy, filing-unit candidates, applicant and authority context, classification candidates, specification candidates, and risk assessments.

Part III must turn those candidates into understandable service options without pretending that a recommendation, proposal, quote, acceptance, instruction, Order, Matter, payment, approval, or filing is the same thing.

The central proposition is:

```text
Proposal
=
Defined Objective
+ Referenced Candidate Inputs
+ One or More Service Options
+ Included Scope
+ Excluded Scope
+ Assumptions
+ Dependencies
+ Cost and Timing Model
+ Risk and Uncertainty
+ Decision Rights
+ Expiry and Version
+ Next Step
```

```text
Proposal
≠ Recommendation
≠ Quote
≠ Contract
≠ Commercial Instruction
≠ Filing Approval
≠ Filing Package
≠ Official Outcome
```

---

## 1. A Recommendation Must Become a Decision Surface

Part II may recommend countries, routes, filing units, classes, wording, searches, and risk responses.

A decision-maker still needs to understand:

- what is being proposed;
- which parts are required;
- which parts are optional;
- what may be deferred;
- what each option costs;
- what each option does not cover;
- which assumptions remain;
- what changes if the user chooses differently.

A list of recommendations is not yet a usable commercial decision surface.

---

## 2. Proposal Design Begins with a Defined Objective

The proposal should identify the objective it is trying to serve.

Examples include:

- protect a launch market before a fixed date;
- secure core word marks globally;
- reduce immediate budget while preserving priority options;
- respond to a known copying risk;
- consolidate an inherited portfolio;
- prepare phased international expansion;
- renew and regularize existing rights.

The objective should reference the current Need Brief version.

A material objective change may require a new proposal.

---

## 3. A Proposal References Candidate Inputs

A proposal should reference rather than silently copy:

- Need Brief;
- jurisdiction strategy;
- route recommendation;
- filing-unit candidates;
- applicant and authority assumptions;
- classification candidate set;
- specification candidate package;
- search or risk assessment;
- provider assumptions;
- fee-source versions.

The proposal should show which versions it used.

A later change to mark, applicant, jurisdiction, class, or scope may invalidate part or all of the proposal.

---

## 4. One Proposal May Contain Several Options

A strong proposal may contain options such as:

```text
Option A — Minimum Required Protection
Option B — Recommended Launch Protection
Option C — Expanded Portfolio Coverage
Option D — Staged Filing Plan
Option E — Search-First Plan
Option F — Urgent Filing with Residual Risk
```

Options should represent meaningful strategic alternatives, not artificial packaging.

---

## 5. Decision Categories Must Remain Visible

Each element should retain a category:

```text
Required
Strongly Recommended
Optional
Deferred
Not Recommended
Insufficient Information
Professional Review Required
```

A bundle label must not hide why an element appears.

The Product should expose:

- reason;
- supporting facts;
- assumptions;
- consequence of omission;
- consequence of delay;
- affected filing unit and jurisdiction.

---

## 6. Minimum and Expanded Scope Are Different Decisions

A minimum option may reduce current spend while increasing:

- uncovered-market risk;
- later filing cost;
- priority loss;
- local-language vulnerability;
- class gaps;
- enforcement difficulty.

An expanded option may improve coverage while increasing cost, complexity, and maintenance obligations.

The Product should explain both benefit and burden.

---

## 7. Staged Options Preserve Time and Budget Logic

A staged plan may divide work into waves:

```text
Wave 1 — urgent or required jurisdictions
Wave 2 — priority-dependent jurisdictions
Wave 3 — planned market expansion
Wave 4 — defensive or monitoring-triggered filings
```

Each wave should identify trigger, target date, dependency, scope, estimated cost, and risk of waiting.

A staged plan is not one filing.

---

## 8. Route Alternatives Need Separate Consequence Models

The same country set may support direct national, regional, or international routes.

The proposal should compare:

- eligibility;
- filing-unit compatibility;
- dependency on a base application or registration;
- language;
- representative requirement;
- cost timing;
- central dependency exposure;
- examination path;
- later recordal and renewal consequences.

A lower initial price does not automatically mean a better route.

---

## 9. Search Options Must Remain Separate from Filing Options

A proposal may include:

```text
No Search
Knockout Search
Register Search
Similarity Search
Common-Law or Market Search
Full Clearance Review
Specialist Opinion
```

Search scope, source, coverage, timing, limitation, and effect should remain visible.

Search should not be embedded as an unexplained fee line.

---

## 10. Scope Must Be Atomic Enough to Change

A proposal should be decomposable by:

- jurisdiction;
- route;
- filing unit;
- class;
- goods/services scope;
- search service;
- professional service;
- provider service;
- document requirement;
- later-stage service.

This supports targeted revision and prevents a small change from replacing an opaque whole package.

---

## 11. Included and Excluded Scope Must Be Explicit

Included scope may cover strategy, filing preparation, review cycles, filing, reporting, registration-stage service, stated classes, and stated item counts.

Common exclusions include:

- search;
- substantive office action;
- opposition or appeal;
- evidence collection;
- notarization or legalization;
- translation beyond stated scope;
- excess-item fees;
- publication or registration fees;
- certificate delivery;
- renewal or recordal.

An exclusion is part of the decision, not a footnote.

---

## 12. Later-Stage Services Need Honest Treatment

Some jurisdictions charge later official fees or require later professional work.

The proposal may classify them as:

```text
Included now
Expected later
Possible later
Unknown until office action or outcome
Not included
```

The Product should not present an initial filing price as lifecycle total unless the stated scope truly covers it.

---

## 13. Assumptions and Dependencies Are First-Class

Assumptions may concern applicant type, class count, item count, route eligibility, priority, exchange rate, official fee schedule, document availability, provider, and ordinary processing.

Dependencies may include search completion, applicant verification, signed POA, priority document, provider availability, conflict clearance, payment, and filing approval.

A proposal may be commercially acceptable while dependencies remain unresolved.

---

## 14. Proposal Versions Must Be Immutable Once Issued

A proposal version should preserve:

- identifier;
- issue date;
- issuer;
- input references;
- included scope;
- exclusions;
- price basis;
- validity period;
- assumptions;
- decision history.

A revision creates a new version.

An accepted proposal must not be silently rewritten.

---

## 15. Validity Is More Than a Sales Expiry

A proposal may expire because official fees, exchange rates, provider rates, priority deadlines, strategy assumptions, applicant status, or source Knowledge changed.

Expiry protects against reliance on outdated terms.

---

## 16. Decision Rights Must Be Clear

The Product should identify who may:

- view;
- comment;
- compare;
- select an option;
- accept commercial terms;
- instruct work;
- approve filing;
- authorize payment;
- sign documents.

Account access does not imply all rights.

---

## 17. Selection Is Not Acceptance

```text
Option Selection
≠ Quote Acceptance
≠ Commercial Instruction
≠ Order Formation
≠ Filing Approval
```

The Product should label the effect of each action.

---

## 18. Comparison Must Preserve Material Differences

Comparison should expose scope, coverage, route, filing unit, classes, items, search, included services, exclusions, timing, dependencies, fees, uncertainty, residual risk, and later obligations.

A single “recommended” badge should not suppress differences.

---

## 19. AI May Compose but Not Bind

AI may assemble options, summarize differences, identify missing terms, explain cost drivers, generate phasing, detect inconsistent scope, and prepare revisions.

AI should not invent discounts, waive fees, bind the organization, promise outcomes, accept terms, or select for the client.

---

## 20. Failure Modes to Reject

```text
One opaque package for several legal rights
Required and optional scope mixed together
Search hidden inside filing price
Later official fees omitted without explanation
Proposal rewritten after acceptance
Discount invented by AI
Option selection treated as instruction
Validity period absent
Assumptions buried in free text
Lifecycle total claimed from filing-stage price
```

---

## 21. Minimum Proposal Lock

```text
A proposal is a versioned decision Artifact.

It references the candidate strategy,
filing units, applicant context,
classification, specification, and risk inputs.

It may contain several meaningful options.

Required, recommended, optional,
deferred, included, excluded,
expected-later, and uncertain scope remain visible.

A selected option is not yet
a quote acceptance, commercial instruction,
Order, Matter, filing approval,
submission, or official outcome.

AI may compose and explain.

Authorized humans decide and bind.
```

---

## 22. Handoff to Pricing and Quote Formation

The output is a versioned Proposal Candidate containing one or more service options.

The next chapter determines how each option expresses official fees, professional fees, provider costs, currency, tax, later-stage charges, and uncertainty without creating false precision.
