# B05-CH-09 — Jurisdiction Recommendation and Filing Routes

**Status:** Part II Draft  
**Chapter Map:** B05-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part II — Need, Strategy and Recommendation

## Chapter Purpose

CH08 established the Need Brief as the Product boundary between an ordinary-language objective and a structured recommendation journey.

The next Product question is geographic and procedural:

> Which jurisdictions and filing routes should MarkReg recommend, with what priority, assumptions, sources, dependencies, costs, timing, and uncertainty?

A country picker is not a jurisdiction strategy.

A map, list, or checkbox can record a choice.

It does not explain whether the choice responds to:

- present sales;
- planned expansion;
- manufacturing;
- licensing;
- enforcement exposure;
- investment;
- online distribution;
- existing rights;
- priority;
- route eligibility;
- cost;
- timing;
- provider requirements.

The central proposition is:

```text
Jurisdiction Recommendation
=
Understood Business Need
+ Geographic Relevance
+ Existing Rights
+ Route Eligibility
+ Route Dependencies
+ Timing and Priority Context
+ Cost and Provider Context
+ Risk and Enforcement Context
+ Source-Backed Jurisdiction Knowledge
+ Explainable Ranking
+ Human Review
```

The output is a versioned **Jurisdiction Strategy Candidate**.

```text
Jurisdiction Strategy Candidate
≠ Client Decision
≠ Legal Conclusion
≠ Filing Instruction
≠ Official Eligibility Decision
≠ Provider Appointment
```

---

## 1. Jurisdiction Recommendation Is Not a Country List

The same country may matter for different reasons.

Example:

```text
Jurisdiction A:
current sales market

Jurisdiction B:
manufacturing location

Jurisdiction C:
planned investment market

Jurisdiction D:
high copying risk

Jurisdiction E:
existing portfolio gap
```

MarkReg should preserve the reason for relevance.

A country without a reason is difficult to prioritize, explain, review, or later reconsider.

---

## 2. Geographic Relevance Begins with the Need Brief

The recommendation should consume:

- business objective;
- current and planned markets;
- manufacturing and sourcing;
- distribution;
- licensing;
- e-commerce;
- investment;
- known copying;
- competitors;
- existing rights;
- timing;
- budget;
- enforcement priorities.

The Product should not infer that the organization needs protection everywhere it can technically file.

---

## 3. Jurisdiction Categories Must Be Explainable

MarkReg may classify candidates as:

```text
Required
Strongly Recommended
Optional
Deferred
Not Currently Recommended
Insufficient Information
Professional Review Required
```

Each category should have a reason.

Example:

```text
Strongly Recommended:
The jurisdiction is a planned launch market,
the mark is not currently protected there,
and a material distributor agreement is expected.
```

A label without an explanation is not a professional recommendation.

---

## 4. “Required” Must Be Used Carefully

“Required” may mean:

- necessary to satisfy a contractual condition;
- necessary to support a defined market-entry plan;
- necessary to preserve a specific priority strategy;
- necessary because an existing process depends on filing;
- necessary under an organization’s approved policy.

It should not be used merely because the Product ranks a country highly.

The source of the requirement should be visible.

---

## 5. “Not Currently Recommended” Is Not “Never”

A jurisdiction may be not currently recommended because:

- no commercial activity is planned;
- adequate rights already exist;
- the mark will change;
- applicant ownership is unresolved;
- the budget is better allocated elsewhere;
- route eligibility is unclear;
- the market is deferred;
- filing would create avoidable dependency;
- reliable current information is unavailable.

The Product should state which facts would change the recommendation.

---

## 6. Filing Route Is Part of the Recommendation

A jurisdiction may be reached through:

- direct national filing;
- regional filing;
- international registration or designation;
- later territorial extension;
- conversion or transformation where legally available;
- other jurisdiction-specific mechanisms.

Book 05 does not encode route law itself.

MarkReg consumes versioned jurisdiction Knowledge and route configuration.

The Product must compare routes rather than treating the jurisdiction and route as the same decision.

---

## 7. Route Comparison Requires Common Dimensions

A route comparison may consider:

- eligibility;
- dependency on another application or registration;
- applicant alignment;
- mark alignment;
- goods/services alignment;
- priority;
- language;
- provider requirements;
- official fee structure;
- examination structure;
- vulnerability to central dependency;
- amendment flexibility;
- future expansion;
- timing;
- maintenance;
- recordal complexity;
- recovery or conversion options;
- source freshness.

The dimensions should remain explicit.

---

## 8. Route Eligibility Is a Sourced Candidate

MarkReg may prepare an eligibility assessment from:

- applicant nationality;
- applicant domicile;
- real and effective establishment;
- existing filing;
- existing registration;
- filing date;
- mark identity;
- goods/services relationship;
- membership in an applicable system;
- current official rules.

The Product should state:

```text
Eligibility appears satisfied
Eligibility appears not satisfied
Eligibility requires professional verification
Eligibility cannot be determined from current information
```

Only the applicable authority or formal process determines final acceptance.

---

## 9. Route Dependency Must Be Visible

Some filing routes may depend on:

- a basic application;
- a basic registration;
- ownership continuity;
- mark identity;
- goods/services scope;
- filing timing;
- later maintenance;
- another official proceeding.

A route may be cheaper or operationally simpler while creating dependency risk.

The Product should not hide that dependency inside a low total price.

---

## 10. Applicant Alignment May Change the Route

If existing rights are owned by one entity and the proposed application by another, route eligibility or continuity may change.

MarkReg should compare:

- current owner;
- proposed applicant;
- group relationship;
- assignment status;
- priority claimant;
- route-specific applicant requirements;
- pending recordals.

A user’s preferred entity does not automatically become a valid route applicant.

---

## 11. Mark Alignment May Change the Route

The proposed mark may differ from an earlier mark in:

- wording;
- stylization;
- color;
- language;
- transliteration;
- composition;
- filing unit;
- image version.

MarkReg should not assume that brand relationship equals route identity.

A route recommendation may require professional assessment of whether the proposed mark can rely on the relevant earlier right.

---

## 12. Goods and Services Alignment May Change the Route

The desired scope may be:

- narrower;
- broader;
- differently grouped;
- translated differently;
- subject to local limitations;
- inconsistent with an earlier application.

The route comparison should identify whether the intended scope fits within the available basis or requires a separate filing strategy.

---

## 13. Priority Must Be Treated as a Time-Bound Dependency

Priority context may affect:

- route sequence;
- filing deadline;
- applicant identity;
- mark identity;
- scope;
- document needs;
- urgency.

The Product should distinguish:

```text
User-stated priority claim
Potential priority candidate
Calculated priority deadline
Verified priority deadline
Priority claim prepared
Priority claim accepted
```

A business launch date is not a priority deadline.

A calculated date is not official acceptance.

---

## 14. Existing Rights Must Be Considered Before Recommending New Filing

MarkReg should check for:

- current applications;
- registrations;
- designations;
- pending renewals;
- assignments;
- limitations;
- cancellations;
- prior refusals;
- duplicate filings;
- related marks;
- earlier portfolio strategies.

A new filing may still be useful.

The Product should explain whether it:

- fills a gap;
- replaces a weak filing;
- protects a new mark;
- adds a new class;
- changes the owner;
- creates redundancy;
- creates avoidable duplication.

---

## 15. Market Presence Is Not the Only Geographic Signal

A jurisdiction may matter because of:

- manufacturing;
- contract manufacturing;
- export transit;
- distributor obligations;
- customs risk;
- counterfeit concentration;
- digital availability;
- app distribution;
- licensing;
- franchise plans;
- capital-market plans;
- competitor activity.

The recommendation should preserve the signal type and strength.

---

## 16. Online Availability Must Be Interpreted Carefully

A website may be accessible globally.

That fact alone does not create the same commercial relevance in every jurisdiction.

MarkReg may consider:

- targeted shipping;
- local currency;
- local advertising;
- local marketplace listings;
- local customer volume;
- language;
- distributor presence;
- app-store availability;
- contractual plans.

Global accessibility should not automatically produce a global filing recommendation.

---

## 17. Manufacturing and Sales May Create Different Priorities

A manufacturing location may matter for:

- supply-chain continuity;
- counterfeit risk;
- customs strategy;
- local contract requirements;
- ownership disputes;
- enforcement.

A sales market may matter for:

- customer recognition;
- distribution;
- marketplace enforcement;
- licensing;
- expansion.

The Product should not collapse the two reasons into one generic “market”.

---

## 18. Enforcement Objectives Should Be Explicit

The user may seek rights primarily to:

- support registration;
- deter copying;
- enable platform complaints;
- support customs action;
- strengthen licensing;
- preserve investment value;
- satisfy a distributor;
- improve negotiation position.

MarkReg should show whether the proposed jurisdiction and route support the stated objective, subject to professional review.

It should not promise enforcement success.

---

## 19. Cost Comparison Must Preserve Scope

A route may appear cheaper because it differs in:

- number of jurisdictions;
- number of classes;
- item count;
- provider involvement;
- document requirements;
- later-stage fees;
- renewal structure;
- currency;
- tax;
- translation;
- examination response risk.

The Product should compare equivalent scope where possible.

If the scope differs, the difference should be visible.

---

## 20. Timing Comparison Must Separate Estimates and Official Time

MarkReg may display:

- estimated preparation time;
- estimated filing time;
- expected acknowledgement time;
- historical examination range;
- official procedural period;
- provider service target;
- uncertainty.

The Product should identify the source and checked date.

Historical averages are not official commitments.

---

## 21. Provider Requirement Is a Route Attribute

Some routes may require or practically benefit from:

- local representative;
- specialist counsel;
- translation provider;
- search provider;
- evidence specialist;
- notarization or legalization support.

Provider need is not the same as provider selection.

CH25–CH26 will define the later discovery and appointment journey.

CH09 may only identify the capability requirement.

---

## 22. Recommendation Should Use Eligibility Before Ranking

The sequence should be:

```text
Identify potentially relevant jurisdictions
→ test basic eligibility and blocking conditions
→ identify route candidates
→ compare route dimensions
→ rank eligible options
→ explain exclusions and uncertainty
```

A route should not rank highly if it is not currently eligible.

---

## 23. Ranking Must Remain Multidimensional

Possible dimensions include:

- business importance;
- timing;
- risk;
- existing coverage;
- route dependency;
- cost;
- provider complexity;
- enforceability objective;
- portfolio fit;
- uncertainty.

The Product should not reduce the result to one unexplained score.

A weighted model may support ranking.

The weights and material factors should remain inspectable.

---

## 24. The Recommendation Should Be Scenario-Aware

The same Need Brief may support several scenarios:

```text
Scenario A:
launch-first protection

Scenario B:
minimum budget protection

Scenario C:
manufacturing and sales protection

Scenario D:
regional expansion

Scenario E:
priority-preservation sequence
```

A scenario is a decision aid.

It is not a bundle or instruction by itself.

CH10 develops scenario composition further.

---

## 25. Jurisdiction Knowledge Must Be Versioned

Relevant Knowledge may include:

- current filing routes;
- eligibility;
- representation requirements;
- class-fee logic;
- item limits;
- document rules;
- language;
- priority;
- examination;
- maintenance;
- source links;
- checked date.

The recommendation should preserve the rule version or checked date used.

A recommendation based on outdated route information should not appear current.

---

## 26. Source Hierarchy Should Be Visible

The Product may use:

```text
Official source
Validated jurisdiction Knowledge
Organization-approved professional note
Provider-supplied information
Historical case data
AI inference
User statement
```

These sources do not have equal authority.

A provider quotation may be useful for cost.

It is not automatically authoritative for official law or status.

---

## 27. Uncertainty Must Be Part of the Output

Uncertainty may arise from:

- incomplete business plans;
- pending corporate changes;
- unclear applicant eligibility;
- missing priority records;
- outdated fee data;
- changing office practice;
- unavailable official source;
- unresolved mark identity;
- uncertain goods/services scope.

The Product should show which uncertainty affects:

- ranking;
- eligibility;
- cost;
- timing;
- professional Review;
- later filing readiness.

---

## 28. Human Review Applies to Consequential Recommendations

Professional Review may be required when:

- route dependency is material;
- priority may be lost;
- applicant alignment is unclear;
- a jurisdiction is high value or high risk;
- the user requests a legal conclusion;
- existing rights conflict;
- a route may limit scope;
- source freshness is insufficient;
- a recommendation is outside configured confidence.

MarkReg may prepare the comparison.

An eligible human determines the professional recommendation where required.

---

## 29. The Jurisdiction Strategy Candidate Should Be Versioned

A useful candidate may contain:

```text
Need Brief reference
Jurisdiction candidate
Reason for relevance
Recommendation category
Route candidates
Eligibility assessment
Dependencies
Applicant and mark alignment
Scope alignment
Priority context
Cost assumptions
Timing assumptions
Provider capability need
Risk and enforcement context
Sources and checked dates
Uncertainty
Required Review
Alternative scenarios
Version
```

This is a Product result.

It is not a new mandatory Core Object.

---

## 30. User Selection Is Not Filing Instruction

The user may select:

- a jurisdiction;
- a route;
- a scenario;
- a budget level;
- a staged sequence.

Selection records preference.

It does not prove:

- formal eligibility;
- commercial acceptance;
- applicant correctness;
- filing readiness;
- professional approval;
- payment;
- execution authority.

Later chapters preserve those gates.

---

## 31. Recommendation Changes Must Be Traceable

A jurisdiction recommendation may change because:

- the user changes the market plan;
- search results arrive;
- applicant ownership changes;
- a priority deadline passes;
- a route becomes unavailable;
- official fees change;
- a provider becomes unavailable;
- new rights are discovered;
- budget changes.

The Product should preserve why the recommendation changed.

It should not silently overwrite the earlier strategy.

---

## 32. Failure Modes to Reject

```text
Alphabetical country list treated as strategy
User address treated as the only geographic signal
Cheapest route ranked first without eligibility
Regional or international route shown as consequence-free
Existing rights ignored
Applicant mismatch hidden
Mark mismatch hidden
Scope mismatch hidden
Priority date shown without source
Historical timing shown as official promise
Global website accessibility treated as global market need
Provider quote treated as legal authority
One unexplained score used for all recommendations
User selection shown as filing instruction
Outdated jurisdiction Knowledge shown as current
```

---

## 33. Minimum Jurisdiction Recommendation Lock

```text
Jurisdictions are recommended for stated reasons.

Routes are compared separately from countries.

Eligibility is evaluated before ranking.

Dependencies remain visible.

Existing rights, applicant, mark, scope,
priority, cost, timing, providers,
and enforcement objectives are considered.

Sources and checked dates remain visible.

Uncertainty remains part of the result.

The output is a Jurisdiction Strategy Candidate.

Human Review and client decision remain distinct.

No recommendation becomes filing authority by itself.
```

---

## 34. Transition to Country Bundles and Portfolio Strategy

A user may understand individual jurisdiction recommendations and still face a practical question:

```text
Which combination should we choose now,
which should we defer,
and how should the sequence fit the portfolio?
```

CH10 defines how MarkReg composes individual jurisdiction candidates into scenarios, waves, and country bundles without hiding the independent legal and commercial consequences of each jurisdiction.
