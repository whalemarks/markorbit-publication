# B05-CH-14 — Goods and Services Guidance

**Status:** Part II Draft  
**Chapter Map:** B05-TOC-V0.1  
**Part:** Part II — Need, Strategy and Recommendation

## Chapter Purpose

CH13 defined class candidates.

A class number provides only a broad category.

The actual filing scope depends on the goods and services wording.

The next Product question is:

> How should MarkReg translate the organization’s real business activity into item-level wording that is understandable, jurisdiction-aware, versioned, reviewable, and suitable for later filing preparation?

The central proposition is:

```text
Goods and Services Guidance
=
Business-Language Activity
+ Classification Context
+ Official and Validated Wording Sources
+ Jurisdiction Rules
+ Filing-Unit Context
+ Current and Intended Use
+ Required and Recommended Items
+ Scope Trade-Offs
+ Cost and Item Limits
+ Translation and Local Adaptation
+ Human Review
```

The output is a versioned **Specification Candidate Package**.

```text
Business description
≠ filing wording
Class heading
≠ complete scope
Accepted wording
≠ appropriate wording
Candidate specification
≠ approved specification
```

---

## 1. Goods and Services Wording Defines Practical Scope

The Product should not communicate that choosing a class completes the scope decision.

Within one class, wording may be:

- broad;
- narrow;
- specific;
- ambiguous;
- duplicated;
- unacceptable;
- locally restricted;
- commercially irrelevant.

The wording determines what the application seeks to cover.

---

## 2. Business Language and Filing Language Serve Different Purposes

A business may say:

```text
“We sell smart home products.”
```

A filing specification may need to distinguish:

- sensors;
- controllers;
- downloadable software;
- cloud software;
- installation;
- repair;
- retail services;
- related products.

MarkReg should preserve the original business description and show how it maps to candidate wording.

---

## 3. The Product Should Begin with Activities, Not a Giant List

A long official list may contain thousands of items.

The user should not be expected to search it without context.

The preferred sequence is:

```text
Understand the business
→ identify relevant activities
→ locate supported wording
→ compare scope alternatives
→ ask targeted questions
→ prepare candidate specification
```

---

## 4. Item Sources Must Be Visible

Candidate wording may come from:

- official classification database;
- jurisdiction-specific accepted-item database;
- office manual;
- validated organization template;
- prior accepted filing;
- provider recommendation;
- professional drafting;
- AI-generated draft;
- user text.

These sources do not have equal authority.

The Product should identify the source and checked date.

---

## 5. Accepted Wording Is Not Automatically Appropriate

An item may be accepted by an office but still be:

- irrelevant;
- too narrow;
- too broad for the actual plan;
- duplicative;
- commercially unhelpful;
- inconsistent with the applicant’s use;
- inconsistent with the filing unit;
- costly because of item limits.

MarkReg should recommend wording based on the Need Brief, not only database acceptance.

---

## 6. Required and Recommended Items Must Be Distinct

The Product may classify items as:

```text
Must cover
Strongly recommended
Optional expansion
Defensive
Jurisdiction alternative
Excluded
Uncertain
Professional drafting required
```

Each item should have a rationale.

---

## 7. “Must Cover” Requires a Business Basis

An item may be must-cover because it represents:

- core revenue product;
- launch product;
- contractual scope;
- key enforcement target;
- essential software or service;
- approved portfolio policy.

It should not be labelled must-cover merely because it is common in the industry.

---

## 8. Recommended Items Should Show the Relationship

A recommended item may cover:

- accessory;
- component;
- complementary service;
- after-sales service;
- software layer;
- future product;
- adjacent market.

The Product should state whether the recommendation is based on:

- current activity;
- approved launch;
- credible expansion;
- defensive strategy;
- existing portfolio;
- professional judgment.

---

## 9. Optional Expansion Items Need Consequence Analysis

An optional item may increase:

- scope;
- filing cost;
- Review time;
- non-use exposure;
- examination complexity;
- evidence burden;
- maintenance burden.

The Product should not present optional expansion as free protection.

---

## 10. Broad and Narrow Wording Must Be Compared

Example:

```text
Broad concept:
computer software

Narrower concept:
downloadable software for managing inventory

Specific commercial item:
downloadable mobile application for warehouse inventory tracking
```

The Product should explain:

- what broader wording may include;
- what narrower wording may exclude;
- whether the office accepts the wording;
- whether the user actually uses or intends to use it;
- whether local practice requires specificity.

---

## 11. Class Headings Require Caution

A class heading may be:

- acceptable in one context;
- insufficiently specific in another;
- interpreted differently;
- broader than intended;
- not aligned with actual use.

MarkReg should not automatically insert a full class heading.

The Product should preserve jurisdiction-specific guidance and professional Review.

---

## 12. Item Count and Fee Rules Must Be Visible

Some jurisdictions or routes may calculate fees by:

- class;
- item;
- subclass;
- excess item;
- filing route;
- later designation.

The Product should show:

```text
Included item count
Additional item count
Estimated fee effect
Items removed for cost
Residual scope consequence
```

Cost optimization should remain reviewable.

---

## 13. Similar-Group or Subclass Coverage May Affect Item Selection

Local groupings may influence:

- similarity analysis;
- examination;
- opposition;
- filing strategy;
- item distribution.

MarkReg should identify:

- jurisdiction;
- group;
- source;
- practical consequence;
- uncertainty.

It should not treat local groups as universal international classification.

---

## 14. Duplicated Items Should Be Detected

Duplication may occur through:

- synonyms;
- overlapping broad and narrow items;
- repeated imported lists;
- translation variants;
- prior templates;
- user additions.

The Product may flag duplicates.

It should not delete them silently when the scope consequence is uncertain.

---

## 15. Conflicting Items Should Be Flagged

Examples:

- goods and service descriptions inconsistent with each other;
- item incompatible with applicant business;
- item contradicts route or filing-unit strategy;
- broad item and exclusion conflict;
- translation changes the meaning;
- local wording belongs in another class.

The Product should preserve the conflict for Review.

---

## 16. Translation Is a Professional Scope Operation

Translation may affect:

- meaning;
- class;
- breadth;
- accepted terminology;
- local consumer understanding;
- official examination.

The Product should preserve:

```text
Source-language item
Translated item
Translator or model
Translation version
Back-translation where useful
Professional Review
```

Machine translation should remain a draft.

---

## 17. Local Adaptation Is Not Mere Translation

A jurisdiction may require:

- different specificity;
- local official terminology;
- item splitting;
- item consolidation;
- subclass mapping;
- exclusion;
- limitation;
- special description.

The Product should distinguish:

```text
Translation
from
jurisdiction adaptation
```

Both may require separate Review.

---

## 18. One Global Master Specification May Have Local Variants

MarkReg may maintain:

- master business scope;
- global candidate specification;
- jurisdiction-specific version;
- filed version;
- amended version;
- registered version.

These versions should remain linked.

The local filed scope should not overwrite the global planning scope.

---

## 19. Current Use and Intended Use Must Be Visible

Relevant status may include:

```text
Currently used
Launch-ready
Approved near-term use
Credible expansion
Defensive only
Speculative
No longer used
Unknown
```

The Product should not show all items as equally supported.

Use requirements and consequences may vary by jurisdiction.

---

## 20. Evidence Context May Support Item Selection

Relevant evidence may include:

- catalogue;
- product image;
- website;
- invoice;
- app listing;
- service agreement;
- packaging;
- advertising;
- business plan.

Evidence may support the business mapping.

It does not automatically prove legal use in every jurisdiction or context.

---

## 21. AI May Draft and Compare Wording

AI may:

- extract products and services;
- map business language to official wording;
- identify synonyms;
- compare broad and narrow options;
- detect duplicates;
- suggest missing items;
- explain exclusions;
- prepare local-language drafts.

AI output remains a candidate.

It should preserve the source and reason.

---

## 22. Organization Templates May Accelerate Drafting

An organization may maintain validated templates for:

- industries;
- recurring clients;
- standard filing units;
- jurisdictions;
- common exclusions;
- preferred drafting style.

A template should be:

- versioned;
- source-linked;
- reviewed;
- purpose-limited;
- adaptable.

Template reuse does not eliminate current verification.

---

## 23. User Interaction Should Present Meaningful Choices

Instead of presenting hundreds of items, MarkReg may show:

```text
Core coverage
Recommended coverage
Expanded coverage
Local alternative
Excluded items
```

Each choice should explain:

- included activities;
- omitted activities;
- cost effect;
- scope effect;
- uncertainty;
- Review need.

---

## 24. User Editing Must Preserve Provenance

The user may:

- add item;
- remove item;
- narrow wording;
- choose alternative;
- provide business language.

The Product should preserve:

- original candidate;
- user edit;
- source;
- affected class;
- affected cost;
- Review status.

A user edit should not erase the earlier professional rationale.

---

## 25. Professional Review Must Apply to the Exact Version

Review should identify:

- filing unit;
- applicant assumption;
- jurisdiction;
- class;
- item list;
- language;
- version;
- source;
- exclusions;
- cost assumptions;
- intended use;
- unresolved issues.

A later wording change may invalidate the Review.

---

## 26. Specification Candidate Package Structure

A useful package may contain:

```text
Need Brief reference
Filing unit
Applicant assumption
Jurisdiction and route
Class
Business activity
Item identifier
Source-language wording
Jurisdiction wording
Translation
Source and checked date
Relevance category
Use status
Scope rationale
Cost effect
Local grouping
User edit
Exclusion
Uncertainty
Review status
Version
```

This remains Product preparation.

---

## 27. Candidate Specification Is Not Filing Ready

A candidate may still require:

- applicant confirmation;
- route confirmation;
- search result;
- professional Review;
- client approval;
- cost approval;
- provider input;
- local adaptation;
- formal Intake;
- filing package preparation.

The Product should not label a plausible item list as filing-ready automatically.

---

## 28. Amendments and Later Limitations Must Preserve Lineage

After filing, wording may change through:

- office requirement;
- voluntary limitation;
- response;
- settlement;
- registration limitation;
- renewal or recordal rule.

The Product should preserve:

```text
Originally proposed
Filed
Amended
Accepted
Registered
Current
```

Part V and Part VI address those later journeys.

---

## 29. Failure Modes to Reject

```text
Class number treated as complete scope
Full class heading inserted automatically
Officially accepted item treated as appropriate automatically
User forced to search an uncontrolled item list
All template items copied without verification
Item removed for cost without showing lost scope
Translation treated as local adaptation
Machine translation treated as approved wording
Global master overwritten by local variant
User edit erases provenance
AI draft shown as filing-ready
Review applied to an unspecified version
```

---

## 30. Minimum Goods and Services Lock

```text
Goods and services wording defines practical scope.

Business language is preserved.

Candidate wording is source-linked.

Required, recommended, optional,
excluded, and uncertain items
remain distinct.

Broad and narrow alternatives
show their trade-offs.

Cost and item limits remain visible.

Translation and local adaptation
remain separate.

User editing preserves provenance.

Human Review applies to the exact version.

The output is a Specification Candidate Package,
not an approved filing specification.
```

---

## 31. Transition to Search, Availability and Risk Assessment

Once MarkReg has identified:

- filing units;
- applicant context;
- jurisdictions and routes;
- classes;
- candidate goods and services;

it may determine whether and how search or risk assessment should occur.

CH15 defines the search modes, source boundaries, similarity analysis, uncertainty, and decision options that complete Part II.
