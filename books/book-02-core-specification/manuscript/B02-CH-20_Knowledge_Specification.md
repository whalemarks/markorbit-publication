# 1. Chapter Purpose

This chapter defines the Knowledge Specification of the MarkOrbit Core.

Chapter 18 defined Identity.

Chapter 19 defined Capability.

This chapter defines Knowledge.

Knowledge is the foundation that allows MarkOrbit to move from facts to understanding, from understanding to judgment, and from judgment to professional action.

MarkOrbit is not merely a data system.

It is not only a workflow system.

It is not only an AI interface.

It is a professional operating system for global brand and trademark services.

A professional operating system must know:

- what a jurisdiction requires;
- how trademark lifecycle works;
- what classification rules apply;
- what documents are needed;
- what evidence may support use;
- what deadline rules matter;
- what office action types mean;
- what agent capabilities are reliable;
- what policies govern action;
- what AI may rely on;
- what human review must confirm;
- what knowledge is authoritative, outdated, inferred or uncertain.

Without a governed Knowledge Specification, MarkOrbit would depend on scattered documents, personal memory, informal prompts and unverified AI responses.

This chapter defines Knowledge as a Core foundation.

---

# 2. Core Question

This chapter answers one question:

> How shall MarkOrbit represent, govern, validate, retrieve and apply professional knowledge across Core Domains, Core Services, AI Capabilities, Workflows and Products?

The answer is:

> MarkOrbit shall define Knowledge as a governed, source-linked, versioned, validated, retrievable and executable Core asset that supports professional understanding, judgment, service execution, AI grounding and product consumption.

Knowledge is not only text.

Knowledge is not only documents.

Knowledge is not only vector embeddings.

Knowledge is not only prompts.

Knowledge is not only human experience.

Knowledge is a governed Core asset.

# 4. Knowledge Definition

In the MarkOrbit Core, Knowledge means:

> a governed professional understanding asset that is source-linked, versioned, validated, retrievable and applicable to Core Domains, Objects, Services, AI Capabilities, Workflows and Products.

This definition contains six parts.

## 4.1 Governed

Knowledge must have ownership, authority, validation and usage rules.

## 4.2 Professional

Knowledge must support professional service work, not only generic information.

## 4.3 Source-Linked

Knowledge must trace back to its source where professional risk exists.

## 4.4 Versioned

Knowledge changes over time.

A rule, template or practice may have effective dates and superseded versions.

## 4.5 Retrievable

Knowledge must be accessible to Core Services, AI agents, Workflows and Products.

## 4.6 Applicable

Knowledge must be usable in execution, not only stored for reading.

# 6. Knowledge Principles

The Knowledge Specification is governed by the following principles.

## Principle 1 — Knowledge Before Response

AI and products should not generate professional responses without authorized knowledge where professional meaning is required.

## Principle 2 — Source Before Assertion

Important professional knowledge should have source, authority or validation.

## Principle 3 — Version Before Update

Knowledge changes should preserve history where downstream use depends on it.

## Principle 4 — Validation Before Professional Use

High-risk knowledge should be validated before use in client advice, filing, review or AI recommendation.

## Principle 5 — Retrieval Before Generation

AI should retrieve authorized knowledge before generating professional output.

## Principle 6 — Knowledge Is Not Prompt

Prompt text may use knowledge.

Prompt text does not define knowledge authority.

## Principle 7 — Knowledge Must Be Executable

Knowledge should be connected to services, workflows, agents and products.

## Principle 8 — Knowledge Gaps Must Be Visible

The system should recognize when required knowledge is missing, stale, conflicting or uncertain.

# 7. Knowledge Ontology

Knowledge belongs primarily to System Ontology.

It also touches Reality Ontology and Execution Ontology.

## 7.1 System Ontology

Knowledge is a Core system foundation because it enables understanding, judgment, AI grounding and professional service execution.

## 7.2 Reality Ontology

Some knowledge reflects external professional reality, such as trademark office rules, jurisdiction requirements, legal procedures and official forms.

## 7.3 Execution Ontology

Knowledge becomes executable when services, tasks, events, workflow contracts and AI agents use it.

## 7.4 Collaboration Ontology

Knowledge may include agent reliability, service provider capability, communication history and network practice.

The Knowledge Specification therefore supports multiple ontology layers.

Its primary architectural position is Foundation Domain.

# 14. Knowledge Rule

Knowledge Rule is:

> an executable or semi-executable rule derived from governed knowledge.

Examples:

```text
If jurisdiction is Thailand and service is assignment, POA may require notarization.
If U.S. registration is in maintenance window, Section 8 declaration may be required.
If goods/services exceed local limit, recommendation should split or reduce items.
If evidence relates to only one product, coverage may be limited.
If agent response delay exceeds threshold, follow-up task should be created.
```

Knowledge Rules may be:

- deterministic;
- policy-based;
- recommendation-based;
- risk-based;
- jurisdiction-specific;
- AI-assisted;
- human-reviewed.

Knowledge Rules should preserve source and version.

# 20. Knowledge Gap

Knowledge Gap is:

> a missing, stale, conflicting or insufficient knowledge condition that prevents safe understanding, judgment or execution.

Examples:

- no current POA requirement for a jurisdiction;
- conflicting foreign agent advice;
- outdated fee or deadline rule;
- missing evidence standard;
- unverified classification practice;
- no verified provider capability;
- AI cannot find source for recommendation.

Knowledge Gaps should be visible.

They may trigger:

- review task;
- knowledge update request;
- expert consultation;
- warning to product;
- AI refusal or limited output;
- fallback workflow.

# 29. Knowledge and AI Grounding

AI requires Knowledge grounding.

AI may use Knowledge to:

- summarize trademark status;
- recommend classes;
- draft document explanations;
- generate client guidance;
- analyze evidence;
- identify risks;
- suggest routing;
- discover opportunities;
- draft communications.

AI must follow these rules:

- use authorized knowledge sources;
- cite or reference knowledge where required;
- distinguish known facts from assumptions;
- warn when knowledge is missing or outdated;
- avoid professional conclusion beyond permission;
- trigger human review for high-risk output;
- record knowledge usage where audit is required.

AI output without Knowledge grounding is not professional Core output.

Review turns candidate knowledge into trusted knowledge or trusted application.

# 36. Knowledge AI Usage

AI may assist Knowledge, but under governance.

Permitted AI-assisted uses may include:

- source summarization;
- knowledge extraction;
- rule candidate generation;
- knowledge relationship suggestion;
- gap detection;
- translation assistance;
- classification rule explanation;
- evidence standard explanation;
- client-friendly explanation drafting.

Restricted AI uses include:

- creating authoritative legal rules without review;
- replacing official source validation;
- hiding uncertainty;
- using outdated or unverified knowledge for final advice;
- generating high-risk recommendations without human review;
- treating prompt memory as knowledge source.

AI-generated knowledge should be marked as candidate knowledge until reviewed.

# 41. Knowledge Review Questions

When reviewing a knowledge asset, rule, service or AI usage, reviewers shall ask:

1. What professional question does this knowledge answer?
2. What source supports it?
3. What authority level applies?
4. What jurisdiction or domain scope applies?
5. Is it validated, pending review, imported, AI-extracted, conflicting or deprecated?
6. What version applies?
7. What effective date applies?
8. What risk level applies?
9. Which Core Services may use it?
10. Which AI agents may access it?
11. Is human review required before professional use?
12. What products consume it?
13. What events should be emitted when it changes?
14. What happens if this knowledge is missing or stale?
15. Can Codex implement related services from approved specs?

# 42. Knowledge Anti-Patterns

## 42.1 Document Library Equals Knowledge

A folder of documents is treated as Knowledge without structure, source, validation or retrieval rules.

Correction:

```text
Documents may contain knowledge.
Knowledge requires source, authority, validation, version and application rules.
```

## 42.2 Prompt Equals Knowledge

Prompt instructions are treated as knowledge authority.

Correction:

```text
Prompts may consume knowledge.
They do not define knowledge authority.
```

## 42.3 AI Output Equals Knowledge

AI-generated text is accepted as professional knowledge without review.

Correction:

```text
AI output may become candidate knowledge.
Validated knowledge requires review or source authority.
```

## 42.4 Stale Knowledge Used Silently

Outdated rules remain in use without warning.

Correction:

```text
Knowledge requires version, effective date and validation status.
```

## 42.5 Knowledge Without Domain Link

Knowledge is stored but not connected to domains, objects or services.

Correction:

```text
Knowledge should be applicable to Core execution.
```

## 42.6 Knowledge Without Risk Level

High-risk legal or procedural guidance is treated like low-risk help text.

Correction:

```text
Knowledge Risk must affect review and AI usage.
```

## 42.7 Product-Local Knowledge

MarkReg, Lite and MGSN each define separate country rules.

Correction:

```text
Shared professional knowledge belongs to Core.
Products consume it through contracts.
```

# 43. Knowledge Rules

The following rules are established by this chapter.

## Rule 1 — Knowledge SHALL be governed Core asset

Professional knowledge must have source, authority, validation and usage rules where risk exists.

## Rule 2 — Knowledge SHALL NOT equal document storage

Documents may contain knowledge, but knowledge requires structure and governance.

## Rule 3 — Knowledge SHALL be versioned where professional use depends on it

Updates must preserve history when matters, advice, AI output or workflows depend on prior knowledge.

## Rule 4 — Knowledge SHALL define authority and validation

Official, reviewed, imported, AI-extracted and unverified knowledge must be distinguished.

## Rule 5 — Knowledge SHALL be retrievable by context

Knowledge must support retrieval by domain, jurisdiction, service type, object context, risk and consumer.

## Rule 6 — Knowledge SHALL support application and audit

Professional use of knowledge should be traceable where risk exists.

## Rule 7 — AI SHALL use authorized knowledge

AI professional output must be grounded in permitted knowledge sources.

## Rule 8 — Knowledge gaps SHALL be visible

Missing, stale, conflicting or insufficient knowledge should trigger warning, review or fallback.

## Rule 9 — Products SHALL consume Knowledge through contracts

Products shall not redefine professional knowledge locally.

## Rule 10 — MVP SHALL include Knowledge grounding before AI recommendations

AI without governed knowledge is not acceptable for professional Core execution.
