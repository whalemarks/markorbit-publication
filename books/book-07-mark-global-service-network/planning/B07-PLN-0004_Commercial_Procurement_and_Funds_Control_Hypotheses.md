# B07-PLN-0004 — Commercial, Procurement and Funds-Control Hypotheses

## Status

```text
Draft Hypotheses — Not Yet Accepted
```

## Purpose

MGSN cannot create a reliable global capability network without a sustainable commercial engine.

This record examines candidate commercial mechanisms without accepting a final legal, accounting, pricing or payment model.

## Commercial Thesis

The platform becomes valuable to both demand and supply when it can combine:

```text
qualified professional demand
+ global service coverage
+ repeat purchasing volume
+ negotiated procurement
+ standardized service scope
+ funds and fulfillment protection
+ reliable settlement
```

The platform should not be neutral about commercial quality.

It may actively negotiate:

- provider cost;
- included services;
- official-fee treatment;
- urgency fees;
- volume tiers;
- currency and tax handling;
- response expectations;
- correction and replacement obligations;
- payment timing;
- cancellation and refund conditions.

## Why Unified Procurement Matters

Without platform procurement, each user must repeatedly:

- find a provider;
- ask for a quotation;
- interpret inconsistent scopes;
- negotiate individually;
- assess unfamiliar payment risk;
- reconcile official and professional fees;
- handle non-performance alone.

Aggregated procurement can create:

- lower or more stable provider cost;
- standardized scope;
- predictable margins;
- faster quotation;
- provider volume confidence;
- stronger service-level commitments;
- lower failure and replacement cost;
- better data for future negotiation.

## Pricing Layers

Candidate pricing layers must remain distinct:

```text
Provider Cost
+ Official Fees and Third-Party Disbursements
+ Platform Service and Risk Cost
+ Optional User Organization Margin
= Customer-Facing Commercial Offer
```

Depending on the Product model, the user may receive:

- platform wholesale price;
- recommended resale price;
- fixed customer-facing package;
- configurable customer-facing price;
- commission or revenue-share offer;
- fully managed service price.

These models should not be mixed silently.

## Platform Procurement Models

### Model A — Platform wholesale network

The platform negotiates provider supply prices and sells network service to participating Organizations.

The Organization controls the customer price within accepted rules.

### Model B — Managed service package

The platform defines and sells a complete service package, procures the provider and assumes defined coordination responsibilities.

The user may act as reseller, referrer or client-facing professional depending on the agreement.

### Model C — Controlled marketplace transaction

The platform presents eligible options and controlled commercial terms, manages payment and fulfillment, and charges a platform fee.

This remains closed and governed rather than open bidding.

### Model D — Subscription plus transaction services

The Organization pays for network access and management capabilities, with additional transaction, verification, funds or collaboration fees.

### Model E — Strategic provider program

Selected providers receive preferred demand access in exchange for negotiated terms, service levels, evidence obligations and settlement rules.

These models may coexist by service category, but Product presentation must clearly identify which model applies.

## Funds-Control Objectives

Funds control should protect:

- customer or user funds;
- provider payment confidence;
- official-fee integrity;
- platform revenue;
- service completion;
- replacement and refund capacity;
- accounting and audit integrity;
- cross-border settlement traceability.

## Candidate Funds Lifecycle

```text
Commercial offer
→ customer or user acceptance
→ funds request
→ funds received or authorized
→ protected allocation by purpose
→ provider commitment
→ official-fee or milestone release
→ delivery evidence
→ acceptance, correction or dispute
→ provider settlement
→ platform revenue recognition
→ reconciliation and closure
```

These are hypotheses. Existing Book 02, Book 03 and formal finance-service ownership must be reviewed before creating new canonical states.

## Milestone Control

Possible milestone evidence includes:

- provider acceptance;
- conflict-clearance completion;
- formal document preparation;
- filing-ready package;
- official submission evidence;
- official receipt;
- report delivery;
- registration or later-stage deliverable;
- correction completion.

The correct milestone depends on service type.

Payment should not be released merely because a provider self-reports completion where consequential evidence is available.

## Official Fees

Official fees require special handling because they may need advance payment.

Potential controls include:

- separation from provider professional fees;
- defined permitted use;
- deadline and amount evidence;
- receipt or official acknowledgement;
- unused-balance return;
- currency and bank-charge treatment;
- prohibition on provider reuse for another Matter;
- emergency exception process.

## Non-Performance Controls

Candidate controls include:

- provider acceptance deadline;
- milestone deadline;
- acknowledgement requirement;
- evidence requirement;
- automatic escalation;
- replacement route;
- partial release;
- hold or freeze;
- refund decision;
- provider suspension;
- incident review.

## Provider Attractiveness

Strict control alone will not attract strong providers.

The platform must also offer:

- qualified demand;
- repeat volume;
- clear instructions;
- reliable settlement;
- reduced collection risk;
- lower business-development cost;
- standardized service scope;
- predictable dispute process;
- reputation and capability growth through validated outcomes;
- access to users who otherwise lack the provider's jurisdiction.

## Demand-Side Attractiveness

Demand-side users need:

- competitive and understandable pricing;
- service continuity;
- one interface;
- reduced provider search;
- payment safety;
- provider replacement;
- clear responsibility;
- consistent reporting;
- customer-facing flexibility where permitted;
- confidence that the platform will not expose the user's customer relationship unnecessarily.

## Routing Neutrality and Commercial Incentives

Platform economics may influence provider programs, but recommendation integrity must not be hidden.

Candidate rules:

- paid placement must not masquerade as professional fit;
- preferred procurement status should be disclosed where material;
- margin should not override eligibility, conflict or capability;
- lowest provider cost should not automatically win;
- platform-affiliated providers should not receive unexplained priority;
- commercial weights should be governed and reviewable;
- user or customer interests remain relevant to selection.

## Bypass and Non-Circumvention

Platform bypass can damage:

- procurement leverage;
- funds protection;
- provider accountability;
- auditability;
- platform economics;
- consistent service quality.

Candidate controls may include:

- staged contact disclosure;
- platform collaboration channels;
- provider and user agreements;
- non-circumvention terms;
- benefits tied to platform-managed transactions;
- loss of guarantee outside the platform;
- provider suspension for prohibited bypass;
- user education about risk.

Any non-circumvention policy requires legal and commercial review and must not be framed as unrestricted ownership of people or organizations.

## Legal and Regulatory Questions

Before implementation, specialist review is required for:

- payment services and custody;
- escrow or safeguarded-funds characterization;
- money transmission;
- cross-border settlement;
- sanctions and AML/KYC;
- tax and invoicing;
- revenue recognition;
- client-money rules;
- professional conduct;
- refund and consumer rules;
- foreign-exchange restrictions;
- trust-account obligations;
- insolvency treatment.

## Decision Status

The following are supported as Product direction:

```text
MGSN is not free infrastructure.
Platform procurement is a core value source.
Platform-controlled funds can reduce fulfillment and financial risk.
Commercial terms must be typed and purpose-specific.
Provider attractiveness and user protection must both be designed.
```

The specific legal and commercial model remains unresolved.
