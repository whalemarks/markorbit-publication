# B07-CH-24 — Incidents, Corrections, Escalation and Replacement

## 1. Managed service quality is proven when something goes wrong

A network is not reliable because every engagement is assumed to succeed. It is reliable because delay, missing evidence, incorrect scope, provider failure and disputed outcomes can be detected and handled without losing authority, continuity or accountability.

```text
Observation of deviation
→ Network Incident
→ Correction or Escalation
→ Continue, Replace, Cancel or Dispute
→ Governed Return
```

## 2. Network Incident

A Network Incident records a material deviation from the accepted engagement.

Typical incident types include:

- missed or threatened deadline;
- provider non-response;
- conflict discovered after allocation;
- scope mismatch;
- incorrect filing data;
- missing or invalid evidence;
- unauthorized provider substitution;
- unexpected official fee or requirement;
- confidentiality or data-access concern;
- professional misconduct concern;
- payment or settlement dispute;
- inability to complete the service.

An incident is not automatically proof of fault. It is a governed trigger for review.

## 3. Incident severity

Severity should reflect impact, urgency and reversibility.

```text
Informational
Operational concern
Material service risk
Deadline-critical incident
Professional or legal-risk incident
Security or protected-action incident
```

Severity affects notification, escalation timing, evidence requirements and who may decide the next step.

## 4. Correction Request

A Correction Request asks the responsible party to repair a defined deficiency.

It should specify:

- the deficient milestone, evidence or work product;
- required correction;
- source of the requirement;
- response deadline;
- responsible actor;
- effect on funds or release eligibility;
- consequence of non-correction;
- whether customer or Originating Workplace approval is required.

A correction must not silently expand the original service scope. Additional work may require a revised offer and new approval.

## 5. Escalation

Escalation is appropriate when:

- the provider does not respond;
- the incident threatens a legal deadline;
- evidence conflicts materially;
- correction fails repeatedly;
- responsibility is disputed;
- a provider may be restricted or suspended;
- funds or client interests are at risk;
- protected action may be required.

Escalation should identify the decision authority and preserve an audit trail.

## 6. Replacement Decision

Replacement is not a casual routing refresh. It changes the responsible execution provider after engagement.

A Replacement Decision should address:

- why replacement is necessary;
- whether the current provider can still cure;
- current deadline and urgency;
- work already completed;
- materials and evidence transferable;
- customer and Originating Workplace consent;
- additional cost or refund;
- conflict and qualification of the replacement provider;
- responsibility for transition loss;
- Return obligations of both providers.

```text
Replacement candidate
→ eligibility and conflict checks
→ commercial and timing review
→ authorization
→ new provider acceptance
→ controlled handoff
```

No silent substitution is permitted.

## 7. Continuity during replacement

The platform should preserve continuity through a structured transfer package containing only authorized information:

- accepted engagement scope;
- current procedural status;
- deadlines and sources;
- completed work;
- missing work;
- available evidence;
- unresolved issues;
- communication constraints;
- funds and settlement status.

The transfer package does not transfer ownership of the customer relationship or private Workplace history.

## 8. Cancellation and partial completion

Some incidents end in cancellation rather than correction or replacement.

The record should distinguish:

- not started;
- partially performed;
- provider cost committed;
- official fees incurred;
- reusable work product;
- refundable amount;
- customer consequence;
- required closing Return.

Cancellation must not erase the evidence needed to understand what happened.

## 9. Dispute Case

A Dispute Case should be opened when parties disagree materially about scope, performance, evidence, payment, refund, responsibility or replacement.

The process should provide:

- notice;
- evidence submission;
- neutral review where possible;
- conflict disclosure;
- interim safeguards;
- reasoned decision;
- appeal or governance review;
- correction of Trust consequences when appropriate.

## 10. Provider governance consequences

An incident may later contribute to:

- performance evidence;
- Trust assessment;
- procurement changes;
- restriction;
- suspension;
- retirement;
- required remediation.

But a single allegation should not automatically become a permanent adverse score. Due process and source quality remain necessary.

## 11. Product rule

MGSN must treat correction, escalation and replacement as first-class governed paths. Service continuity is protected not by hiding failure, but by making failure observable, reviewable and recoverable.
