# Chapter 7

# Operating Models

> *Shared infrastructure creates common capability.*
>
> *Operating models determine how organisations create value.*

---

## 7.1 One Architecture, Many Businesses

Every successful operating system supports many different ways of working.

Linux powers cloud platforms, mobile devices, embedded systems and supercomputers.

The Internet supports education, finance, entertainment and commerce.

Their strength lies not in enforcing a single business model, but in enabling many.

The same principle applies to professional brand services.

Every organisation serves different customers.

Possesses different expertise.

Operates in different markets.

Builds different competitive advantages.

An operating system should never require these organisations to become alike.

Instead, it should provide a common foundation upon which many different operating models can flourish.

Shared architecture.

Independent business.

That balance defines a healthy ecosystem.

---

## 7.2 Business Is a Choice

Infrastructure provides capability.

Business determines how that capability is used.

This distinction is fundamental.

Two organisations may build upon exactly the same operating system while creating entirely different businesses.

One may specialise in trademark prosecution.

Another may focus on portfolio management.

Another may build an AI-first customer experience.

Another may provide enterprise consulting.

The operating system remains unchanged.

Only the operating model changes.

Business innovation therefore occurs above the architectural layer rather than inside it.

---

## 7.3 Four Questions Every Organisation Must Answer

Although operating models differ, every professional organisation must answer the same four questions.

**Who serves the customer?**

Who owns the customer relationship?

Who is responsible for trust?

---

**Who operates the business?**

Who manages daily operation?

Who builds customer experience?

Who develops the organisation?

---

**Who delivers the service?**

Does the organisation deliver services directly?

Does it collaborate with specialists?

Does it coordinate multiple providers?

---

**How is value shared?**

How are responsibilities distributed?

How are commercial benefits allocated?

How does collaboration remain sustainable?

Every operating model represents a different answer to these four questions.

---

## 7.4 Direct Operation

Some organisations prefer to perform every professional activity themselves.

They acquire customers.

Deliver services.

Manage relationships.

Build expertise.

Develop their own teams.

This model offers maximum control.

It also requires the greatest investment.

Every capability must be developed internally.

For many organisations this approach is appropriate.

The operating system fully supports it.

Shared infrastructure strengthens independent operation without changing organisational autonomy.

---

## 7.5 Collaborative Operation

Other organisations choose collaboration.

They continue owning customer relationships while working with trusted professional partners.

Expertise flows between organisations.

Capabilities are combined.

Customers receive broader services than any single organisation could easily provide.

Importantly, collaboration does not weaken independence.

It strengthens capability.

The operating system provides common language and shared infrastructure.

Trust enables professional cooperation.

Together they allow collaboration to scale.

---

## 7.6 Managed Operation

Not every organisation wishes to build professional capability internally.

Some possess customers but not specialised trademark expertise.

Others operate digital platforms, industrial parks or enterprise communities.

Their strength lies in customer access rather than professional delivery.

In these situations, business operation and professional service delivery may be separated.

The organisation remains responsible for customer relationships.

Professional operations are entrusted to experienced service providers.

Customers experience one service.

Behind the scenes, responsibilities are shared.

This model allows organisations to extend professional services without becoming trademark specialists themselves.

---

## 7.7 Hybrid Operation

In practice, many organisations combine multiple operating models.

Routine services may be delivered internally.

Highly specialised work may be delegated.

International matters may involve trusted overseas partners.

Artificial Intelligence may automate standard activities while professionals focus on strategic judgement.

Different services.

Different customers.

Different operating models.

One shared operating system.

Flexibility is not an exception.

It is an architectural objective.

---

## 7.8 Operating Models Evolve

No operating model remains fixed forever.

Organisations grow.

Markets change.

Customer expectations evolve.

Artificial Intelligence creates new possibilities.

Capabilities that were once specialised gradually become routine.

New forms of collaboration emerge.

Operating models therefore evolve continuously.

The operating system provides stability throughout that evolution.

Architecture remains consistent while businesses adapt to changing opportunities.

---

## 7.9 Freedom Through Shared Infrastructure

A common concern surrounding shared infrastructure is that it reduces strategic freedom.

Experience suggests the opposite.

The less effort organisations spend rebuilding common capabilities, the more freedom they have to innovate.

Shared infrastructure lowers operational cost.

Operating models determine competitive differentiation.

Architecture therefore encourages diversity rather than uniformity.

Every organisation remains free to answer the four business questions in its own way.

The ecosystem grows stronger because its participants become increasingly specialised rather than increasingly similar.

---

Businesses require multidisciplinary expertise.
