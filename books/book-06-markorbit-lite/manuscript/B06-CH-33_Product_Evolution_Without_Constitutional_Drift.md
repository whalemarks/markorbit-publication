# B06-CH-33 — Product Evolution Without Constitutional Drift

## Chapter Role

This chapter closes Book 06 by defining how MarkOrbit Lite may expand in capability, edition, integration and commercial reach without losing the Product meaning accepted by the Product Charter and Product Baseline.

The central proposition is:

> Lite must be allowed to evolve rapidly at the capability and implementation layers while changing its Product constitution slowly, explicitly and only through governed decisions.

A Product that never evolves becomes irrelevant.

A Product that changes identity every time a new feature, customer segment, model or price appears becomes incoherent.

The goal is disciplined evolution.

---

## 1. What must remain stable

The enduring identity of Lite is:

```text
AI business operating system
for independent trademark professionals and small trademark agencies
```

Its four enduring loops are:

1. Customer and Service Growth;
2. Professional Work Products;
3. Professional Memory and Business Assets;
4. MarkOrbit Ecosystem Handoff.

Its architectural identity is:

```text
lightweight Workplace Product
individual-first but Organization-centered
```

Its constitutional boundaries include:

- Candidate is not formal business truth;
- Today is not an active Task system;
- Artifact is not Document or Evidence;
- Human Review is not user confirmation;
- Handoff is not destination acceptance;
- Return is not Lite-owned formal truth;
- MarkReg owns the formal trademark-service lifecycle;
- MGSN owns Trust, Routing and provider collaboration depth;
- Core, Execution and Owning Services retain their formal authority;
- AI does not autonomously perform consequential professional action;
- commercial plans do not define Product identity.

These rules should not change merely because implementation becomes more capable.

These changes may improve Product performance without changing what the Product is.

## 3. The layer model for evolution

A disciplined evolution process separates six layers.

### Layer 1 — Product Constitution

Defines:

- Product identity;
- intended users;
- value promise;
- four loops;
- architectural position;
- non-goals;
- enduring authority and safety rules.

Current authority:

```text
B06-PLN-0004 Product Charter v0.3
B06-PLN-0005 Owner Decision Matrix v0.3
```

### Layer 2 — Product Baseline

Defines:

- Product-local records;
- controlled IDs;
- journeys;
- conformance scenarios;
- Handoff contracts;
- MVP acceptance criteria.

Current authority:

```text
B06-SPEC-0001–0004 v0.1
```

### Layer 3 — Publication and Reader Projection

Defines:

- chapter structure;
- explanatory prose;
- examples;
- editorial projections;
- reader-facing terminology.

Current authority:

```text
B06-TOC-V0.1
B06-CH-00–B06-CH-33
```

### Layer 4 — Product Increment

Defines a bounded capability or journey improvement, such as:

- portfolio opportunity detection;
- customer reactivation;
- short-video Artifact production;
- MarkReg launch integration;
- Local/Private continuity;
- team memory promotion;
- entitlement fulfillment.

A Product Increment consumes the Constitution and Baseline.

It does not rewrite them silently.

### Layer 5 — Implementation Specification and ADR

Defines:

- services;
- APIs;
- storage;
- schemas;
- workflows;
- security design;
- model and provider choices;
- synchronization;
- observability;
- migration;
- deployment.

Implementation may use different internal structures from the publication records if it preserves their meanings and scenario behavior.

### Layer 6 — Commercial Plan and Experiment

Defines:

- price;
- plan;
- entitlement;
- quota;
- trial;
- support level;
- edition;
- experiment population;
- commercial pass/fail rules.

Commercial evidence may influence priorities, but it cannot silently change the Constitution.

Possible editions include:

### Lite Basic / Growth Starter

### Lite Portfolio

### Lite Local / Private

Focus:

- local data processing;

### Lite Professional / Advanced

Each edition must still preserve the same Product boundaries.

## 5. Edition does not grant authority

An advanced or expensive edition cannot grant authority that the user, Organization or destination does not possess.

```text
premium plan
≠ access permission
≠ professional approval
≠ applicant authority
≠ provider appointment
≠ protected-action authority
```

Edition differences may alter convenience, capacity and supported workflows.

They do not alter responsibility.

## 6. Product increments should begin with a problem

A new increment should not begin with:

> We have a new model, tool or data source. Where can we put it?

It should begin with:

- which user problem exists;
- which Product loop it belongs to;
- which journey is affected;
- which Product-local records are involved;
- which Owning Services participate;
- which conformance scenarios apply;
- what evidence would demonstrate value;
- what risks and costs arise.

For example:

```text
Problem:
Users spend too long preparing jurisdiction-specific client explanations.

Product loop:
Professional Work Products.

Records:
ML-W01–ML-W10.

Destinations:
Human Review and Communication.

Evidence:
use rate, edit rate, time saved, correction rate, response quality.

Risks:
source staleness, deterministic-field error, unreviewed claim, rights.
```

## 7. Capability expansion decision test

Before adding a capability, ask:

1. Which user and Organization problem does it solve?
2. Which of the four loops does it strengthen?
3. Does Lite own the proposed state?
4. Is a Product-local candidate or projection sufficient?
5. Which formal destination owns the consequence?
6. What source, privacy and rights rules apply?
7. Which Human decision remains visible?
8. Which Handoff and Return are required?
9. Which conformance scenarios test it?
10. What Product and commercial evidence will determine success?

A capability that cannot answer these questions is not ready for the Lite roadmap.

## 8. Historical V1 reconciliation as an evolution lesson

The early V1 model contains valuable discovery evidence, including:

- unified object thinking;
- private customer protection;
- platform/private separation;
- membership and commercial ideas;
- opportunity and follow-up workflows;
- audit requirements;
- Local-ready principles.

It also demonstrates common early-model risks:

- Role, permission, membership and qualification mixed together;
- a universal TrademarkAsset table;
- File, Document, Evidence and Artifact collapsed;
- Product-local candidates becoming formal Opportunity too early;
- one visibility switch controlling unrelated meanings;
- data model names becoming Product assumptions.

The current reconciliation classes remain:

```text
KEEP
REFRAME
PRODUCT-LOCAL
MOVE TO OWNING SERVICE
DEFER
REJECT
```

This pattern should continue for future historical documents and prototypes.

A prototype that works is evidence.

It is not automatically authority.

## 9. Tool and provider neutrality

Lite may use:

- multiple AI providers;
- local and cloud models;
- multiple Render engines;
- external TTS/video tools;
- third-party CRM or Communication adapters;
- public and commercial data providers;
- local helper applications.

The Product should preserve a provider-neutral contract where practical.

A tool should be replaceable when:

- quality changes;
- cost changes;
- privacy requirements differ;
- a region needs local processing;
- a provider becomes unavailable;
- a new implementation performs better.

```text
provider choice
≠ Product identity
```

## 10. AI evolution

AI capabilities will change faster than the Product constitution.

Future AI may improve:

- source extraction;
- multilingual reasoning;
- portfolio pattern recognition;
- Artifact generation;
- image and video production;
- response classification;
- memory retrieval;
- local-device processing;
- quality evaluation.

Greater capability does not eliminate the need for:

- source visibility;
- uncertainty;
- versioning;
- Human Review;
- final user confirmation;
- destination revalidation;
- audit;
- correction;
- failure and unknown states.

A more powerful model can increase both value and risk.

## 11. Automation maturity levels

Lite may mature through automation levels.

### Level 0 — Manual support

- user gathers context;
- Lite helps structure or draft;
- user performs external actions manually.

### Level 1 — Assisted preparation

- Lite assembles context;
- prepares Candidates and Artifacts;
- exports or prefills destinations.

### Level 2 — Governed Handoff

- typed Envelopes;
- destination revalidation;
- typed Returns;
- Human confirmation before consequential action.

### Level 3 — Policy-bounded execution support

- approved workflows;
- explicit actors and authority;
- Execution handles protected steps;
- Lite remains the Product surface and continuity layer.

### Level 4 — Narrow autonomous operation

Only possible for explicitly authorized, low-risk, reversible operations with:

- accepted policy;
- defined scope;
- observable behavior;
- rollback;
- audit;
- exception handling;
- owner approval.

This Book does not authorize Level 4 professional or External Protected Action.

## 12. Constitutional drift patterns

Drift often appears through reasonable local changes.

### Drift pattern 1 — A successful feature becomes the Product

Example:

```text
daily content performs well
→ Lite is redefined as a content subscription
```

### Drift pattern 2 — A commercial metric changes meaning

Example:

```text
many prospects are supplied
→ every prospect is called an intention customer
```

### Drift pattern 3 — Convenience becomes authority

Example:

```text
user clicked confirm
→ Product treats professional Review and formal authority as satisfied
```

### Drift pattern 4 — Integration becomes ownership

Example:

```text
Lite displays MarkReg state
→ Lite creates a parallel MarkReg lifecycle
```

### Drift pattern 5 — AI capability removes governance

Example:

```text
model accuracy improves
→ source, Review and confirmation controls are removed
```

### Drift pattern 6 — Local access becomes data centralization

Example:

```text
local helper can read a file
→ file is synchronized to platform by default
```

### Drift pattern 7 — One customer request becomes universal scope

Example:

```text
a mature agency asks for full CRM
→ Lite absorbs general Workplace responsibilities
```

## 13. Drift detection questions

A roadmap or PR review should ask:

- Does this change alter Product identity?
- Does it create a parallel formal object?
- Does it move authority into Lite?
- Does it weaken Human Review or confirmation?
- Does it turn a commercial entitlement into constitutional scope?
- Does it treat a provider or implementation as permanent architecture?
- Does it broaden data use beyond the original purpose?
- Does it preempt MarkReg, MGSN, Core, Execution or Workplace?
- Does it remove a failure or unknown state?
- Does it change an accepted controlled meaning?

A “yes” may require a Product Baseline change or Change Proposal rather than an ordinary implementation PR.

## 14. Change classes

Changes should be classified before approval.

### Class A — Editorial clarification

Examples:

- clearer wording;
- corrected link;
- added example;
- improved reader explanation.

Does not change controlled meaning.

### Class B — Implementation change

Examples:

- new model provider;
- schema optimization;
- new renderer;
- UI redesign;
- performance improvement.

Requires implementation review and conformance evidence.

### Class C — Product Increment

Examples:

- new journey variant;
- new work-product type;
- new Handoff adapter;
- new edition-specific capability.

Requires mapping to accepted records and scenarios.

### Class D — Product Baseline change

Examples:

- new Product-local record family;
- changed formalization boundary;
- changed Handoff contract;
- changed acceptance criteria;
- changed scenario meaning.

Requires Product Baseline versioning and owner acceptance.

### Class E — Constitutional change

Examples:

- changing target users;
- replacing the four loops;
- absorbing MarkReg or MGSN authority;
- authorizing autonomous professional action;
- redefining Lite as a content or lead product.

Requires explicit Charter revision and cross-Book review.

## 15. Change artifacts

Depending on class, a change may require:

- Product brief;
- journey map;
- ownership analysis;
- privacy and rights assessment;
- scenario additions;
- Handoff contract update;
- ADR;
- implementation specification;
- migration plan;
- Evaluation Run;
- commercial experiment;
- publication update;
- owner decision.

No single artifact replaces all others.

## 16. Versioning

Suggested versioning principles:

```text
Charter
→ changes rarely; explicit constitutional version

Product Baseline
→ semantic versions for controlled records and contracts

Chapter Map / Manuscript
→ publication versions and release stages

Product Increment
→ bounded increment IDs and release versions

Implementation
→ repository/service versions and ADRs

Commercial Plan
→ independently versioned plan definitions
```

A price change should not require a Charter version.

A new formal authority boundary should.

## 17. Migration and compatibility

Evolution must consider existing users and records.

A migration plan should address:

- old Candidate states;
- Artifact Versions;
- approvals and confirmations;
- entitlements;
- Personal and Organization Memory;
- Reusable Assets;
- formal references;
- Handoff/Return history;
- local data;
- audit;
- retired capability;
- plan changes.

Migration must not silently reinterpret past states.

For example:

```text
old “sent” field with uncertain evidence
→ must not automatically become confirmed Communication result
```

## 18. Product learning without silent policy change

Evaluation and user behavior may suggest improvements.

The Product may learn that:

- a Candidate type is frequently rejected;
- one content format is rarely used;
- a source is unreliable;
- users prefer weekly over daily supply;
- a Handoff package often lacks authority context;
- a plan is economically unsustainable.

These findings may trigger:

- ranking changes;
- better prompts;
- improved defaults;
- Product Increment proposals;
- plan changes;
- scenario additions.

They must not silently change:

- permissions;
- formal-state ownership;
- Review requirements;
- data-sharing scope;
- professional authority;
- constitutional Product identity.

## 19. The durable moat

Lite's durable value does not come from exclusive access to one general AI model.

It comes from the governed combination of:

```text
user and Organization context
+ authorized customer and trademark data
+ relevant professional observations
+ trustworthy Artifact history
+ real customer responses
+ governed service outcomes
+ scoped Feedback and Correction
+ Personal and Organization Memory
+ Reusable Assets
+ MarkReg and MGSN continuity
```

This capability compounds because later work can begin from a better, safer and more specific position.

A generic AI tool may generate fluent text.

Lite should understand:

- what this professional is trying to achieve;
- which Organization context applies;
- which customer and trademark facts are authorized;
- what happened before;
- which corrections matter;
- which Asset is safe to reuse;
- which destination owns the next formal step;
- what outcome returned;
- what should be done next.

## 20. Growth without absorption

Lite may become the most frequently used MarkOrbit surface without owning every underlying lifecycle.

This is a strength.

```text
Lite organizes daily value
Core preserves shared meaning
Execution governs formal work
Workplace preserves Organization context
MarkReg owns trademark-service lifecycle
MGSN owns capability Trust and Routing
Owning Services change formal truth
```

A useful Product is not defined by how many objects it owns.

It is defined by how well it helps the user move through the right responsibilities.

## 22. Closing proposition

MarkOrbit Lite should remain lightweight in entry friction, not shallow in responsibility.

Its long-term purpose is not to produce the largest amount of content, the longest list of prospects or the most automated external activity.

Its purpose is to help trademark professionals operate a better practice:

```text
notice relevant value earlier
→ prepare trustworthy professional work faster
→ make Human judgment visible
→ move real needs into governed services
→ preserve outcomes and corrections
→ build reusable personal and Organization capability
→ repeat with greater relevance and lower risk
```

That is the life of the Product.

Capabilities, plans, models and interfaces may change.

The constitutional promise should remain recognizable:

> Every day, Lite helps the professional understand what matters, prepare what is useful, take the right governed next step and compound the capability of the practice.
