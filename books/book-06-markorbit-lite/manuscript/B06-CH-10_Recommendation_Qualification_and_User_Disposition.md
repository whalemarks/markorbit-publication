# B06-CH-10 — Recommendation, Qualification and User Disposition

**Status:** Complete Draft 1  
**Chapter Map:** B06-TOC-V0.1 — Owner Accepted  
**Part:** Part II — The Daily Operating Model

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how MarkOrbit Lite presents Product-local candidates, supports professional inspection and records user disposition without confusing recommendation, decision, qualification, approval or formal state.

The central proposition is:

> A recommendation is a reasoned presentation of options and uncertainty. It becomes useful only when the responsible user can inspect, correct, defer, reject, suppress or accept it for preparation.

The controlled records are:

```text
ML-O07 — Recommendation Presentation
ML-O08 — Qualification Result
ML-S04 — User Disposition
```

## 1. Recommendation Is a Presentation, Not a Command

A candidate may be relevant but still require judgment.

The Product should not say only:

> Contact this client now.

It should explain:

- the candidate type;
- the subject;
- why it appears now;
- supporting sources;
- source freshness;
- confidence and missing information;
- possible client or professional value;
- foreseeable risks;
- alternative actions;
- required Review or verification;
- likely destination if the user proceeds.

```text
Recommendation
= candidate + reasoning + limitations + options
```

A recommendation does not grant authority.

## 2. `ML-O07 Recommendation Presentation`

A Recommendation Presentation is the user-facing expression of one or more candidates.

It may contain:

- headline and concise explanation;
- subject and relationship context;
- source references;
- timing;
- confidence;
- value and risk factors;
- missing information;
- suggested work product;
- possible next actions;
- reasons not to act;
- destination and consequence preview.

The presentation may be generated by AI, deterministic rules, templates or a combination.

Its semantics remain the same regardless of interface.

A chat response, dashboard card, email digest or mobile notification may all present a recommendation. None of these interfaces changes its authority.

## 3. Ranking Is Not the Same as Qualification

Ranking orders items for attention.

Qualification determines whether a candidate is sufficiently relevant and supported for a defined next stage.

A candidate may rank highly because:

- it is time-sensitive;
- the client impact is large;
- the source is fresh;
- a work product is nearly ready;
- a destination has returned a request.

It may still fail qualification because:

- the relationship is not verified;
- authority is missing;
- the service need is speculative;
- contact permission is unknown;
- professional Review is required;
- the destination cannot accept it.

```text
high rank
≠ qualified
```

## 4. Ranking Factors Must Remain Explainable

Possible factors include:

- urgency;
- client impact;
- professional risk;
- confidence;
- source freshness;
- relationship strength;
- user preference;
- readiness;
- destination response;
- expected reuse;
- expected commercial value.

The user should be able to understand why one item appears above another.

A single opaque score is insufficient when different factors conflict.

For example:

```text
commercial value: high
source confidence: low
client value: uncertain
professional risk: high
```

The correct presentation may be:

> Verify before acting.

not:

> Top opportunity.

## 5. Client Interest and Professional Risk Control the Ceiling

A Product may support commercial growth, but it cannot recommend unnecessary or unsuitable work merely because it may generate revenue.

A candidate should be downgraded, blocked or rejected when:

- the proposed service provides little client value;
- the cost is disproportionate;
- ownership or authority is unclear;
- the proposed scope exceeds the client’s business need;
- legal or factual support is weak;
- contact would violate a preference or policy;
- the professional cannot responsibly explain the recommendation.

```text
commercial attractiveness
cannot override
client interest + professional responsibility
```

This is not an optional ethical preference. It is a Product conformance rule.

## 6. The User Needs to Inspect the Underlying Basis

A user should be able to move from summary to basis.

The Product should expose, according to permission:

- relevant Observation;
- subject linkage;
- source date;
- relationship context;
- previous service activity;
- assumptions;
- conflicting evidence;
- missing information;
- proposed destination.

The user does not need every raw file in the first view. The Product should allow sufficient inspection before a consequential disposition.

## 7. `ML-S04 User Disposition`

User Disposition records what the user decides to do with a Product-local item.

Controlled dispositions include:

```text
inspect
accept_for_preparation
defer
reject
dismiss
suppress
correct
route
request_more_information
```

Each disposition should preserve:

- user;
- Organization;
- item and version;
- time;
- reason where material;
- scope;
- follow-up condition;
- effect on related records;
- whether a formal Handoff is requested.

## 8. Inspect Is a Real State

Opening an item should not be treated as acceptance.

A user may inspect because:

- the source seems suspicious;
- the candidate is unfamiliar;
- the client relationship needs checking;
- another participant should review it;
- the user wants to understand but not act.

Analytics may record inspection, but the Product must not convert it into:

- interest;
- consent;
- qualification;
- formal instruction.

```text
viewed
≠ accepted
```

## 9. Accept for Preparation Is Not Approval

When a user accepts a candidate for preparation, Lite may:

- assemble Structured Content;
- prepare an Artifact Draft;
- generate a Development Package;
- identify missing information;
- prepare a Review Package;
- prepare a Handoff Envelope.

It may not assume permission to:

- send a Communication;
- create a formal Opportunity;
- launch MarkReg;
- assign a Task;
- appoint a provider;
- publish content;
- perform an External Protected Action.

```text
accept_for_preparation
→ prepare
≠ execute
```

## 10. Defer Needs a Return Condition

A defer disposition should identify when or why the item should return.

Possible conditions include:

- a date;
- a source refresh;
- receipt of customer information;
- destination response;
- completion of another Matter action;
- user-defined review cycle.

A deferred item should not simply disappear into an unstructured backlog.

It should preserve:

- original basis;
- current freshness;
- return condition;
- expiry;
- whether the source must be revalidated.

## 11. Reject, Dismiss and Suppress Are Different

### Reject

The user determines that this candidate should not proceed.

Possible reasons:

- no client value;
- unsupported reasoning;
- wrong subject;
- wrong timing;
- wrong service;
- professional risk;
- duplicate candidate.

### Dismiss

The user removes the item from current attention without necessarily rejecting the underlying category forever.

### Suppress

The user or policy prevents similar items from appearing within a defined scope.

Suppression may apply to:

- a customer;
- a Contact;
- a service type;
- a channel;
- a source;
- a Product mode;
- an Organization policy.

Suppression must override growth ranking.

## 12. Correction Is Product Learning With Limits

A correction may state:

- this customer no longer owns the mark;
- this contact should not be used;
- the source was misread;
- the service was already completed;
- the proposed jurisdiction is irrelevant;
- the recommendation does not reflect the Organization’s practice;
- the relationship is personal, not Organization-owned.

The Product should record:

- affected record;
- corrected value or interpretation;
- source or user basis;
- scope;
- whether related candidates should be re-evaluated;
- whether a Memory Candidate should be created.

Correction does not allow Lite to rewrite external formal truth silently.

## 13. Route Is Not Delegation of Formal Responsibility

A user may route an item to:

- another participant for inspection;
- Human Review;
- MarkReg preparation;
- a Communication draft flow;
- a Capability Need flow;
- a Task/Execution request.

Routing within Lite indicates intended next handling.

Formal responsibility remains with the relevant destination and authority model.

```text
route requested
≠ Task assigned
≠ Review approved
≠ provider appointed
```

## 14. `ML-O08 Qualification Result`

Qualification records whether a candidate is sufficiently supported for a defined next step.

A Qualification Result should identify:

- candidate;
- purpose;
- reviewer or user;
- qualification criteria;
- supporting sources;
- missing information;
- result;
- allowed next destination;
- restrictions;
- expiry or requalification condition.

Possible results include:

```text
qualified_for_preparation
qualified_for_contact_preparation
qualified_for_review
qualified_for_handoff
not_qualified
more_information_required
blocked
expired
```

Qualification is purpose-specific.

A candidate may be qualified to prepare a customer explanation but not qualified for external contact.

It may be qualified for Human Review but not for MarkReg Handoff.

## 15. Qualification Is Not Formal Opportunity Creation

This distinction is essential.

A user may qualify a Service-Value Candidate because:

- the source is current;
- the customer relationship is valid;
- the service appears relevant;
- a customer-facing explanation can be prepared.

The formal Opportunity Service still decides whether to create or update an Opportunity.

```text
ML-O08 qualified_for_handoff
→ ML-H07 Opportunity Formalization Request
→ destination revalidation
→ formal Opportunity reference or rejection
```

Lite does not assign formal pipeline state to itself.

## 16. Qualification Is Not Customer Intention

A Prospect Candidate may be qualified for outreach preparation because:

- identity is sufficiently reliable;
- the service relevance is explainable;
- the contact channel is available;
- outreach is permitted under the active policy.

This does not mean the prospect intends to buy.

Only later evidence such as:

- a response;
- a stated need;
- a request for quotation;
- a meeting;
- an instruction;

may support stronger qualification.

Even then, formal Customer and Opportunity creation remain separate.

## 17. Recommendation Must Show Alternatives

Professional decisions often have more than one suitable path.

A recommendation may present:

- contact now;
- prepare but wait;
- request more information;
- verify the source;
- route to Review;
- hand off to MarkReg;
- take no action;
- suppress future recommendations.

A Product that always presents one preferred action may hide uncertainty or commercial bias.

Alternatives make assumptions and consequences visible.

## 18. Recommendation Quality Improves Through Corrections, Not Blind Optimization

The Product may learn from:

- user dispositions;
- customer responses;
- destination acceptance or rejection;
- work-product edits;
- professional Review;
- explicit corrections.

It should not infer universal rules such as:

- clicked items are good recommendations;
- accepted-for-preparation items should always be ranked higher;
- high-revenue outcomes should dominate;
- repeated use means Organization approval;
- no response means wrong service.

Learning remains scoped, reviewable and reversible.

## 19. A Recommendation Can Correctly End in No Action

A conforming Product recognizes that no action may be the correct professional result.

Examples:

- the registration is already maintained;
- the customer does not need the proposed jurisdiction;
- the source is unreliable;
- the contact has opted out;
- the cost is not justified;
- the candidate duplicates an active Matter;
- the professional cannot verify the claim.

Rejecting or blocking an unsuitable candidate is Product value.

## 20. Minimum Conforming Recommendation and Qualification Flow

A minimum conforming experience should demonstrate:

1. a sourced candidate;
2. an explainable Recommendation Presentation;
3. visible uncertainty and missing information;
4. multiple meaningful dispositions;
5. separate inspect and accept-for-preparation states;
6. defer with a return condition;
7. suppression that overrides ranking;
8. correction with scope;
9. purpose-specific Qualification Result;
10. no direct formal Opportunity creation;
11. no claim that a Prospect Candidate is purchase intent;
12. client-interest and risk precedence;
13. a safe no-action outcome.

## Chapter Conclusion

MarkOrbit Lite does not create value by making decisions for the professional.

It creates value by presenting the right candidate with enough reasoning, context and alternatives for responsible judgment.

```text
Recommendation
→ inspection
→ User Disposition
→ purpose-specific qualification
→ preparation or safe stop
```
