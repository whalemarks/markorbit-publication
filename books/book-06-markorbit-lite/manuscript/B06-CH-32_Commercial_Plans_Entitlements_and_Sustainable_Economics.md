# B06-CH-32 — Commercial Plans, Entitlements and Sustainable Economics

## Chapter Role

This chapter explains how MarkOrbit Lite may be packaged, priced and operated sustainably without allowing one commercial plan to redefine the Product.

The central proposition is:

> A commercial plan is a bounded promise about access, supply, support and limits. It is not the Product constitution, not a permission model and not proof that every included output creates professional value.

The controlled evaluation records are:

```text
ML-E05 — Commercial Experiment
ML-E06 — Entitlement / Fulfillment Observation
```

The RMB 99 monthly plan, recurring content, Prospect Candidate supply and production quotas remain hypotheses to be tested.

---

## 1. Product identity must remain above the plan

The enduring Product identity is:

```text
AI business operating system
for independent trademark professionals and small trademark agencies
```

Its four loops remain:

- Customer and Service Growth;
- Professional Work Products;
- Professional Memory and Business Assets;
- MarkOrbit Ecosystem Handoff.

A commercial plan selects and limits capabilities from these loops.

```text
Product Charter
→ what Lite is and why it exists

Product Edition
→ which population and operating context it serves

Commercial Plan
→ which capabilities, limits and support are included

Entitlement
→ what this subscriber may receive or use in a defined window

Fulfillment Observation
→ what was actually delivered or made available
```

A plan cannot silently change:

- Candidate into formal Opportunity;
- content into approved Artifact;
- user confirmation into Human Review;
- subscription payment into authority to access client data;
- Render credit into permission to publish;
- prospect quantity into permission to contact;
- premium pricing into a right to bypass governance.

## 2. Why an entry plan matters

An entry plan can reduce adoption friction for users who:

- operate independently or in small teams;
- are not ready to connect a full customer portfolio;
- need immediate, visible professional value;
- want to test MarkOrbit before deeper integration;
- cannot justify a large software commitment;
- need practical content, client-development or work-product support.

A low-price plan may serve as:

- an acquisition channel;
- a habit-forming layer;
- an introduction to Artifact production;
- a controlled source of Prospect Candidates;
- a gateway into MarkReg;
- a path to portfolio-connected or team editions.

It should not be treated as the maximum value of Lite.

## 3. The RMB 99 hypothesis

The reference hypothesis is:

```text
Lite Basic / Growth Starter
RMB 99 per month
```

This is not a universal or permanent price.

It may vary by:

- edition;
- market;
- billing channel;
- tax;
- promotion;
- Organization size;
- included formats;
- AI/Render/TTS allowance;
- support level;
- professional Review level;
- source or prospect acquisition cost.

The experiment question is not simply:

> Will users pay RMB 99?

It is:

> Can a bounded RMB 99 plan create recurring professional value, lead users into deeper Lite loops and remain economically sustainable without lowering Product controls?

## 4. Candidate plan composition

A conforming entry plan may combine:

1. recurring professional content or work-product supply;
2. customer and service opportunity prompts;
3. a bounded number of Prospect Candidates;
4. basic Development Packages;
5. selected Artifact and Render allowances;
6. manual or assisted Publish preparation;
7. MarkReg entry and Handoff support;
8. limited personal preferences and continuity;
9. an upgrade path into portfolio-connected or team editions.

The plan should state clearly which items are:

- included;
- limited;
- metered;
- subject to fair use;
- dependent on supplied data;
- dependent on Review;
- best effort;
- not guaranteed.

## 5. Entitlement is a time-bound promise

An entitlement should identify:

- subscriber and Organization;
- plan version;
- effective window;
- included capability;
- quantity or frequency;
- quality threshold;
- supported formats;
- required user inputs;
- expiry or carryover;
- replacement or service-credit rule;
- exclusions;
- evidence required for fulfillment.

Potential categories include:

- content supply entitlement;
- work-product quota;
- Render credit;
- TTS/video allowance;
- Prospect Candidate quota;
- portfolio opportunity scan;
- storage allowance;
- support allowance;
- Human Review allowance;
- MarkReg Handoff support.

These categories are commercial semantics, not accepted database tables.

## 6. Fulfillment must reflect usable value

`ML-E06 Entitlement / Fulfillment Observation` should distinguish:

```text
scheduled
prepared
available
used
partially fulfilled
fulfilled
failed
replaced
credited
expired
```

A promise is not fulfilled merely because a generation job ran.

Examples:

### Content

The following do not count as a usable content unit by themselves:

- a topic;
- a headline;
- raw notes;
- a generic paragraph;
- a script without the promised representation;
- an empty template;
- a failed Render.

### Video

If the plan promises a usable video, a script or storyboard alone is not fulfillment.

### Prospect Candidate

A duplicate, stale, invalid or suppressed candidate may require replacement under the plan's defined quality rule.

### Work product

A PDF with incorrect applicant, price or jurisdiction data is not fulfilled merely because a file exists.

### Human Review

Automated checking cannot be counted as professional Human Review when the entitlement promises a named review level.

## 7. Daily or recurring content as a plan option

Recurring content can create:

- immediate visible value;
- a reason to return;
- professional visibility;
- client-education material;
- reusable input for customer development;
- evidence of Artifact and Render capability.

A candidate formulation is:

```text
at least one usable content unit per covered day
selected from:
- illustrated professional post
- Moments / private-domain copy
- template-rendered short video
```

However, daily content creates risks:

- repetitive themes;
- generic language;
- source staleness;
- channel fatigue;
- low actual use;
- excessive Render/TTS cost;
- pressure to count weak output as fulfillment;
- product drift into a content factory.

The experiment should therefore measure:

- use, not only delivery;
- format preference;
- modification rate;
- audience fit;
- fatigue;
- source correction;
- cost per used item;
- whether content supports Growth or Work Product loops.

A weekly or event-driven supply may outperform a daily entitlement for some editions.

## 8. Prospect Candidate supply as a plan option

Commercial plans must not describe every candidate as an “intention customer.”

The value hierarchy remains:

```text
Level A — Existing Customer Opportunity
Level B — Historical Customer Reactivation
Level C — User-Owned Prospect Candidate
Level D — Platform-Supplied Prospect Candidate
```

Platform-supplied candidates should define:

- supported source classes;
- minimum quantity;
- freshness;
- duplicate rules;
- suppression rules;
- contactability standard;
- relevance threshold;
- invalid-candidate definition;
- replacement rule;
- exclusivity or non-exclusivity;
- region and industry;
- outreach limitations;
- complaint and opt-out handling.

The Product should prefer fewer explainable candidates over a large unverified list.

```text
quantity
≠ quality
≠ permission
≠ purchase intent
```

## 9. Artifact, Render and Publish allowances

Production allowances may include:

- customer explanation cards;
- quotation explainers;
- portfolio analysis;
- illustrated posts;
- PDF reports;
- template videos;
- client-development packages;
- MarkReg launch packages.

The plan should separate:

```text
Artifact preparation allowance
Render allowance
Human Review allowance
Edit/revision allowance
Delivery/Publish preparation allowance
```

This prevents an unlimited “content credit” from hiding materially different costs.

## 10. Cost architecture

The cost of Lite is not one AI token number.

A plan may incur:

### Variable generation cost

- language-model inference;
- image generation;
- Render;
- video;
- TTS;
- translation;
- data extraction.

### Data cost

- trademark data access;
- public-source acquisition;
- prospect data;
- identity resolution;
- freshness checking;
- suppression and duplicate processing.

### Human cost

- onboarding;
- support;
- professional Review;
- source correction;
- prospect validation;
- incident handling;
- commercial replacement.

### Platform cost

- storage;
- synchronization;
- audit;
- search;
- integration;
- observability;
- security;
- payment and billing.

### Destination cost

- MarkReg support;
- MGSN routing;
- Communication or Publish integration;
- formal service operations.

Unit economics must allocate these costs to plan, user cohort and usage behavior.

## 11. Why average cost is insufficient

Average cost can hide loss-making cohorts.

Examples include users who:

- generate many videos;
- request repeated revisions;
- consume heavy Human Review;
- store large files;
- use expensive prospect sources;
- create frequent failed Render jobs;
- need extensive onboarding;
- submit poor-quality inputs requiring support.

The plan should examine:

- median and high-percentile cost;
- cost by capability;
- cost per used work product;
- cost per qualified need;
- cost per governed Handoff;
- support cost per retained subscriber;
- gross margin after replacement and credits.

## 12. Sustainable economics does not mean minimum quality

A low-price plan cannot justify:

- fabricated or stale prospects;
- raw drafts counted as final outputs;
- misleading professional claims;
- removal of source visibility;
- bypass of Review;
- hidden reuse of client data;
- autonomous external action;
- false fulfillment.

The correct response to unsustainable cost is to change:

- frequency;
- format mix;
- quota;
- edition;
- automation method;
- support level;
- price;
- target customer;
- included capability.

It is not to weaken constitutional controls.

## 13. Quotas and fair-use boundaries

A quota should be expressed in a way users can understand.

Possible units include:

- usable content units;
- Artifact Versions;
- successful Render Results;
- video minutes;
- TTS minutes;
- Prospect Candidates meeting the plan standard;
- Human Review items;
- support cases;
- storage volume.

A quota should not count failed output as successful use unless the failure was caused by user-supplied invalid input and the rule is clearly disclosed.

Fair-use rules should identify:

- the abuse pattern;
- the Product or cost risk;
- warning and remediation;
- suspension or plan change;
- appeal or support path.

## 14. Replacement and service credits

Replacement rules matter particularly for:

- invalid Prospect Candidates;
- duplicate candidates;
- failed Render;
- incorrect deterministic fields;
- missed recurring supply;
- unavailable promised formats.

The plan should define whether the remedy is:

- replacement;
- retry;
- credit;
- carryover;
- extension;
- refund;
- support correction.

Unknown external outcomes should not trigger blind replacement before reconciliation.

## 15. Upgrade paths

An entry plan should lead naturally to deeper Product value.

Possible upgrade triggers include:

- connecting more customer and trademark records;
- moving from generic content to portfolio-specific work products;
- adding team roles and Organization Memory;
- increasing Review or Render allowances;
- using structured MarkReg journeys;
- requesting MGSN capability discovery;
- adding Local/Private continuity;
- expanding audit, reporting or integration.

Upgrade should correspond to genuine capability needs, not artificial feature obstruction.

## 16. Downgrade, cancellation and data continuity

A sustainable plan must also define exit.

The Product should clarify:

- which entitlements stop;
- which unused allowances expire or carry over;
- what happens to Personal and Organization Memory;
- how Artifacts remain accessible;
- which local data remains local;
- which formal records remain in Owning Services;
- how export or retention works;
- whether shared assets become unavailable;
- how reactivation works.

Cancellation should not erase formal MarkReg, Opportunity or Task records that Lite never owned.

## 17. Commercial experiment design

`ML-E05 Commercial Experiment` should state:

- hypothesis;
- target user;
- edition and plan;
- price;
- entitlement set;
- population;
- acquisition channel;
- time window;
- cost model;
- pass/fail rules;
- safety controls;
- exclusions;
- decision owner.

A useful experiment may compare:

- daily versus three-times-weekly content;
- content-led versus portfolio-opportunity-led onboarding;
- user-owned versus platform-supplied prospects;
- low-cost template video versus higher-cost custom video;
- self-service versus assisted Review;
- RMB 99 versus a higher price with greater professional value.

## 18. Commercial metrics

### Acquisition and conversion

- acquisition cost;
- activation rate;
- time to first useful result;
- trial-to-paid conversion;
- source of subscriber.

### Use and retention

- meaningful weekly use;
- renewal;
- downgrade;
- cancellation;
- reactivation;
- upgrade.

### Value

- relevant opportunity identified;
- work product used;
- customer action;
- qualified need;
- governed Handoff;
- perceived value.

### Fulfillment

- promised versus fulfilled units;
- failure;
- replacement;
- credit;
- user dispute;
- format availability.

### Economics

- variable cost;
- support and Review cost;
- gross margin;
- high-cost cohort;
- cost per retained subscriber;
- cost per qualified need;
- cost per MarkReg Handoff.

### Risk

- privacy finding;
- complaint;
- opt-out violation;
- incorrect prospect;
- professional correction;
- false fulfillment;
- unsafe action blocked.

## 19. Pass and fail conditions

A commercial experiment should not pass merely because:

- many users register;
- users download free content;
- a discounted plan converts temporarily;
- output quantity is high;
- gross margin appears positive before support and Review cost;
- revenue comes from unsafe outreach;
- churn has not yet occurred within a short test window.

A meaningful pass requires evidence that:

- users perceive recurring professional value;
- the plan leads into deeper Product loops;
- promised entitlements are honestly fulfilled;
- retention is credible;
- cost is sustainable;
- safety controls remain intact;
- support burden is manageable;
- the plan has a plausible upgrade or long-term role.

## 20. Interpreting an unsuccessful RMB 99 test

A failed RMB 99 experiment does not necessarily mean Lite lacks value.

Possible interpretations include:

- the price is too low for the promised support;
- daily content is not the right habit mechanism;
- the target user needs portfolio-connected value;
- prospect acquisition is too costly;
- video cost is too high;
- the plan combines too many unrelated benefits;
- users value a higher-priced professional edition;
- activation requires assisted onboarding.

The response should be to reframe the plan, not to rewrite the Product Charter around the cheapest deliverable.

## 21. Commercial plan governance

Commercial plan changes should require:

- versioned plan definition;
- entitlement compatibility analysis;
- user notice;
- migration rules;
- fulfillment impact review;
- cost and risk evidence;
- legal and billing review where applicable;
- no hidden change to Product meaning or authority.

A plan version may change monthly while the Product Charter remains stable.

## 22. Chapter conclusion

Commercial plans translate Lite's capabilities into understandable, bounded promises.

The correct hierarchy remains:

```text
Product identity
→ Product Edition
→ Commercial Plan
→ Entitlement Window
→ Fulfillment Observation
→ Product and economic evidence
```

RMB 99, recurring content, Prospect Candidate supply and Render quotas may become valuable commercial mechanisms.

They remain successful only when they support the deeper Lite loops, produce honest fulfillment, retain users and operate sustainably without weakening professional, privacy or authority controls.
