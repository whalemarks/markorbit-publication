# Chapter 10 — Resale Authorization Before Visibility

The most dangerous sentence in a trademark marketplace is often the simplest:

> This mark is for sale.

That statement may be true, partly true, outdated or entirely unsupported. The owner may have agreed to discuss a transfer but not public promotion. An intermediary may have authority for one country but not another. A prior authorization may have expired. The seller may control the company but not the recorded trademark owner. A customer may have asked for confidential demand testing rather than an open Listing.

Lite should therefore require Resale Authorization before external commercial visibility.

```text
Trademark Inventory
→ Resale Authorization
→ purpose-specific Listing or offer
```

Authorization is not a checkbox. It is an evidence-bearing commercial mandate.

## 1. Why consent must be structured

A message saying “you can help sell it” leaves critical questions unanswered:

- Which trademark or portfolio?
- Who grants the authority?
- Does the granting party have authority to do so?
- Is the appointment exclusive?
- Which countries and channels are permitted?
- May the owner identity be disclosed?
- May price be published?
- May the intermediary negotiate?
- May sub-agents or network partners participate?
- How is commission calculated?
- How long does the authority last?
- What happens to existing inquiries after termination?

A structured authorization protects the owner, intermediary, platform, buyer and downstream partner.

## 2. Authorization should identify the granting party and basis

The record should distinguish:

- recorded trademark owner;
- beneficial owner where relevant;
- authorized company representative;
- existing customer;
- appointed agent or broker;
- court-appointed or insolvency representative;
- portfolio manager;
- other claimed authority.

The system should preserve evidence of why the grantor may authorize commercialization.

Possible evidence includes:

- signed instruction;
- corporate authority document;
- owner confirmation;
- power of attorney;
- brokerage agreement;
- recorded assignment;
- verified communication from an authorized contact;
- platform approval event backed by authenticated identity.

```text
Person controls email account
≠ person may authorize sale
```

## 3. The scope must be explicit

Resale Authorization may define:

- covered marks;
- included jurisdictions;
- included classes or goods;
- whether related domains, logos or assets are included;
- permitted sales channels;
- permitted target buyers;
- public, network or selected visibility;
- white-label or co-broker participation;
- translation and localization rights;
- content and concept-generation rights;
- data-disclosure limits;
- authority to obtain due-diligence documents.

A narrow authorization should not be expanded silently.

## 4. Exclusivity is not binary

Exclusivity may be:

- fully exclusive;
- non-exclusive;
- exclusive by territory;
- exclusive by channel;
- exclusive by buyer segment;
- exclusive for named introduced buyers;
- exclusive only during a campaign period;
- first-right-to-negotiate;
- owner-direct-sale permitted;
- co-broker permitted with approval.

Lite should not reduce all of these to `exclusive: true/false`.

The system needs enough structure to detect conflicts between overlapping authorizations.

## 5. Price authority must be separated from promotion authority

An intermediary may be allowed to advertise the mark but not negotiate below a target price. Another may be permitted to collect offers but not bind the owner.

Authorization should distinguish:

```text
May disclose asking price
May present price-on-request
May receive offers
May negotiate within range
May propose discount
May accept offer
May sign agreement
```

The final steps may require direct owner approval or separate legal authority.

```text
Authority to market
≠ authority to bind
```

## 6. Commission and attribution must be known before promotion

Commercial participants should know:

- owner net expectation;
- public asking price;
- permitted markup or fee;
- sourcing commission;
- buyer-side service fee;
- co-broker split;
- platform fee;
- transfer-service fee;
- tax or withholding assumptions;
- payment trigger;
- treatment of repeat or related transactions.

Opaque economics create conflict and discourage serious promotion.

The platform should preserve which participant introduced:

- the owner;
- the asset;
- the buyer;
- the transaction route;
- the professional service need.

Attribution does not automatically determine legal entitlement, but it provides the evidence needed to apply the agreed commercial rules.

## 7. Owner identity disclosure should be purpose-specific

An owner may permit:

- no external identity disclosure during demand testing;
- disclosure only to qualified buyers;
- disclosure to selected network partners;
- full public disclosure;
- disclosure only after confidentiality agreement;
- disclosure through the originating Workplace.

The same applies to contact information, internal documents and price floors.

Minimum necessary disclosure should expand as the buyer becomes qualified and the transaction progresses.

## 8. Sub-agency and network participation require permission

A Workplace may want to project the Listing to other Workplaces or the MGSN-related ecosystem.

Authorization should specify whether the originating Workplace may:

- appoint co-brokers;
- share with selected Workplaces;
- publish to an authorized network;
- permit white-label promotion;
- allow buyer-side intermediaries;
- provide private documents downstream;
- create translated Marketing Packs;
- use platform ranking or paid promotion.

```text
Authorized to sell
≠ authorized to distribute owner information to the whole network
```

## 9. Authorization has a lifecycle

A practical lifecycle is:

```text
Draft
→ identity and authority review
→ owner confirmation
→ active
→ amended
→ suspended
→ expired
→ revoked
→ terminated
→ archived
```

Reasons for suspension may include:

- ownership conflict;
- legal-status change;
- missing document;
- owner non-response;
- exclusivity dispute;
- price withdrawal;
- active transaction;
- compliance concern;
- suspected fraud.

## 10. No Listing should remain active indefinitely

Authorization should have:

- start date;
- expiry or review date;
- update obligation;
- seller-response expectation;
- automatic pause conditions;
- revalidation method.

Even open-ended appointments should require periodic confirmation.

A stale Listing is not harmless. It wastes buyer attention and damages trust.

## 11. Amendments must be versioned

Material changes include:

- asking price;
- minimum price;
- exclusivity;
- permitted channels;
- identity disclosure;
- commission;
- included assets;
- term;
- negotiation authority;
- co-broker rights.

Lite should retain the prior version and record which Listings, Marketing Packs and inquiries were governed by it.

A new price should not retroactively change the commercial rules for a buyer introduced under an earlier agreement unless the parties agree.

## 12. Revocation does not erase history

When authorization is revoked, Lite should:

- stop new external promotion;
- pause affected Listings;
- prevent new derivative content;
- identify active inquiries;
- notify authorized participants where appropriate;
- preserve introduction and communication evidence;
- determine whether existing transaction protections survive;
- record the revocation reason;
- archive rather than delete the authorization.

The owner may withdraw future authority while existing contractual obligations remain unresolved.

## 13. The buyer should receive proportionate authority assurance

Public Listings do not need to expose the full authorization document.

Lite may show evidence states such as:

```text
Owner-direct supply
Authorization verified
Authorization recently confirmed
Network source verified
Authority review pending
Authorization expired
```

For a qualified transaction, stronger evidence may be disclosed under the applicable confidentiality and due-diligence process.

The platform should not display a “verified” badge unless it can explain what was verified, when and by whom.

## 14. Conflicting authorizations require investigation

Conflicts may include:

- two parties claiming exclusivity;
- different price floors;
- owner and intermediary giving inconsistent instructions;
- an expired agent continuing to promote;
- territory overlap;
- a recorded owner change;
- one authorization permitting public promotion and another forbidding it.

Lite should not resolve these conflicts by choosing the first record or the highest-revenue route.

Possible states include:

```text
Potential conflict
Promotion paused
Owner clarification required
Legal review required
Non-exclusive overlap accepted
Superseded authority
Fraud concern
```

## 15. Authorization evidence may be imperfect

Small owners and long-standing agency relationships may not begin with formal brokerage agreements.

Lite should support progressive evidence without treating weak evidence as strong evidence.

For example:

- authenticated owner confirmation may permit private testing;
- a signed instruction may permit selected-network promotion;
- a fuller agreement may be required for public Listing or negotiation authority;
- direct owner confirmation may be required before accepting an offer.

The exact thresholds are Product and jurisdiction policy decisions. The principle is that authority strength and permitted action should correspond.

## 16. AI may assist but cannot create authority

AI can:

- extract terms from documents;
- identify missing fields;
- compare overlapping authorizations;
- detect expiry;
- summarize price and commission rules;
- generate owner-confirmation requests;
- flag inconsistent identity information.

AI cannot:

- infer authorization from friendly correspondence alone;
- declare a signatory legally authorized;
- extend an expired mandate;
- resolve exclusivity disputes;
- authorize publication;
- accept an offer for the owner.

## 17. Example: non-exclusive network sale

An owner authorizes Workplace A to market a mark for six months.

Terms include:

- non-exclusive appointment;
- public price may be shown;
- owner identity disclosed only to qualified buyers;
- Workplace A may share with selected network partners;
- partners may not contact the owner directly;
- owner net price is fixed;
- Workplace A controls the partner commission pool;
- any discount requires owner approval;
- active introduced buyers remain protected for ninety days after expiry.

Lite should encode these terms so that:

- public users see the approved Listing;
- selected partners see attribution and commission rules;
- qualified buyers receive owner information only at the correct stage;
- expiry pauses new promotion;
- protected buyers remain linked to the prior authority period.

## 18. Fraud and impersonation risk

Trademark sales attract false owners, forged documents and unauthorized intermediaries.

Risk controls may include:

- identity verification;
- corporate-domain verification;
- official-record comparison;
- owner contact through an independent channel;
- document-signature checks;
- bank-account name comparison;
- unusual urgency or price alerts;
- duplicate authority claims;
- sanctions and compliance checks where applicable;
- escalation for high-value assets.

These controls do not guarantee authenticity. They improve evidence and response.

## 19. Failure modes

The authorization system fails when:

- permission is stored only in free text;
- grantor identity is not verified;
- exclusivity is simplified to one Boolean;
- marketing authority is confused with acceptance authority;
- public identity disclosure is assumed;
- co-broker participation is not covered;
- price and commission changes overwrite history;
- expired authority leaves Listings active;
- revocation deletes introduction evidence;
- badges obscure what was actually verified;
- AI creates certainty from incomplete documents;
- platform-owned inventory receives weaker verification than partner inventory.

## 20. Product and technical implications

Lite needs a Resale Authorization object with:

- granting party and represented owner;
- authority basis and evidence;
- covered Trademark Inventory;
- scope and included assets;
- exclusivity dimensions;
- territory, audience and channel policy;
- disclosure policy;
- pricing and negotiation permissions;
- commission and attribution rules;
- co-broker and network permissions;
- lifecycle and versioning;
- revalidation and expiry;
- revocation and survival terms;
- conflict handling;
- links to Listings, Marketing Packs, inquiries and Opportunities.

## 21. Commercial implications

Strong authorization improves:

- owner trust;
- partner willingness to promote;
- buyer confidence;
- commission clarity;
- transaction conversion;
- dispute resolution;
- inventory freshness.

It may slow initial supply onboarding. That is a deliberate trade-off against false liquidity.

## 22. The operating principle

```text
Authority before visibility
Scope before distribution
Evidence before verification claims
Version before amendment
Preservation before deletion
```

> Lite should never say a trademark is for sale unless it can explain who authorized that statement, what the authorization permits and whether it is still current.
