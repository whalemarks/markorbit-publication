# Chapter 07 — AI Visuals, Deterministic Rendering and Factual Control

AI image generation gives Lite the ability to turn a trademark into packaging, product, store, poster and campaign concepts within minutes. That speed is commercially powerful. It is also unreliable in exactly the places professional content must be reliable.

Image models may alter a logo, misspell a mark, invent certification symbols, distort product labels, change a color between images, create impossible packaging and imply that a concept business already exists.

The solution is not to reject AI visuals. It is to separate imaginative generation from controlled rendering.

```text
Governed facts and brand assets
→ AI-assisted source imagery
→ deterministic composition
→ factual and visual validation
→ reviewed output
→ versioned publication
```

AI should contribute visual possibility. Deterministic systems should protect identity, text, facts and repeatability.

## 1. Why a single image-generation step is insufficient

A one-step prompt may ask a model to create:

> A premium product poster for the FAIRYTALE trademark, including the exact logo, Class 11 products, a slogan, a price and a store environment.

The model may produce an attractive image, but several failures are likely:

- the mark is misspelled;
- the logo is redrawn inconsistently;
- product labels contain invented text;
- the goods shown do not match the specification;
- a registration symbol is inserted without authority;
- the price is fabricated;
- the store appears to be a real operating location;
- the same brand cannot be reproduced in the next image;
- small text is unreadable;
- the output cannot be adapted cleanly to another channel.

The image looks finished while remaining operationally uncontrolled.

## 2. Separate source imagery from controlled brand layers

The stronger pipeline divides the visual into layers.

### AI-assisted source imagery

AI may generate:

- product environments;
- lifestyle scenes;
- packaging forms;
- store interiors;
- models or contextual subjects;
- abstract backgrounds;
- textures;
- illustration directions;
- conceptual product forms.

These elements are useful because exact reproduction is not always required.

### Controlled brand assets

Deterministic rendering should place:

- approved trademark image;
- approved logo variation;
- exact brand name;
- verified legal or product text;
- factual labels;
- required concept disclaimer;
- approved typography;
- approved color tokens;
- call to action;
- QR code or URL;
- version and campaign metadata where needed.

The controlled layer should not depend on an image model’s ability to spell or preserve geometry.

## 3. The governed visual pipeline

A practical Lite visual pipeline is:

```text
Select source object and purpose
→ create visual brief
→ lock verified facts and approved brand assets
→ generate or select source imagery
→ inspect source imagery for rights and factual risk
→ compose with parameterized template
→ render deterministically
→ run automated checks
→ perform required human review
→ approve channel-specific output
→ publish and retain provenance
```

Each stage has a distinct responsibility.

## 4. The visual brief is more than a prompt

A Visual Brief should record:

- source Trademark Marketing Pack;
- intended audience;
- target channel and dimensions;
- commercial purpose;
- concept category;
- approved logo asset;
- protected elements;
- color and typography guidance;
- products that may be depicted;
- products that require expansion disclaimers;
- prohibited claims and symbols;
- required factual labels;
- required concept status;
- cultural and jurisdictional constraints;
- review level;
- output count and cost limit.

This creates a stable transformation contract even when the model, template or contributor changes.

## 5. Protect the mark as an asset

The trademark or logo should be handled through a controlled asset pipeline.

Possible protected assets include:

- original filed or registered mark image;
- owner-approved high-resolution logo;
- black-and-white version;
- approved negative or reversed version;
- approved simplified commercial display version;
- icon or monogram;
- vector or transparent source;
- clear-space and minimum-size rules.

The system should know which asset is:

- the official trademark representation;
- a continuity-preserving refinement;
- a commercial display variation;
- a new-mark concept.

```text
Visually similar
≠ legally identical
≠ owner-approved
```

An image model should not silently redraw the official mark inside every scene.

## 6. Deterministic templates create repeatability

Parameterized HTML and CSS templates can control:

- logo placement;
- text hierarchy;
- factual panels;
- labels and disclaimers;
- image crop;
- margins and safe areas;
- channel ratios;
- typography;
- page or frame sequence;
- call-to-action placement;
- localization.

A browser renderer such as Playwright can produce stable static outputs. Remotion or similar composition tools can produce motion graphics from the same structured data. FFmpeg can assemble, transcode and validate media outputs.

The tools themselves are not the governing model. The important principle is:

```text
same approved inputs
+ same template version
+ same rendering profile
= reproducible output
```

This allows corrections, re-rendering and multi-channel adaptation without asking an image model to recreate the entire design.

## 7. Exact text must remain outside uncontrolled imagery

Professional visuals often contain:

- brand name;
- goods or service description;
- jurisdiction;
- registration status;
- price or inquiry status;
- contact route;
- concept disclaimer;
- campaign headline.

These should be rendered from structured fields, not painted into the source image.

Where source imagery contains incidental text—such as signs, packaging or storefront lettering—the system should either:

- remove it;
- blur it;
- replace it during composition;
- verify it manually;
- reject the image.

Small invented text can still create misleading impressions even when the main title is correct.

## 8. Factual control applies to images, not only captions

An image can make a factual claim without using words.

Examples include:

- showing a physical store implies an operating location;
- showing a product range implies actual production;
- showing a celebrity-like spokesperson implies endorsement;
- showing an award badge implies recognition;
- showing a registration symbol implies a status or usage basis;
- showing a country flag may imply origin or official association;
- showing medical or safety outcomes implies performance;
- showing a packaging barcode implies a real market product.

Lite should therefore review visual assertions as part of the Claim Ledger.

A concept label may be required on the image itself, not only in surrounding text.

## 9. Concept status should survive reposting

A disclaimer in a web-page paragraph may disappear when an image is downloaded and reposted.

For concept visuals, Lite may use proportionate persistent labels such as:

- Concept visualization;
- Brand incubation concept;
- AI-assisted concept image;
- Product concept, not existing product;
- Store concept, not operating location.

The label should be readable without overwhelming the design. Internal, private-buyer and public channels may use different levels of detail.

The goal is not to stigmatize concept work. It is to prevent the concept from becoming false evidence after it leaves the original context.

## 10. Visual continuity requires controlled tokens

A coherent campaign needs more than repeated use of the same prompt.

Lite should preserve:

- approved logo asset;
- color tokens;
- typography roles;
- spacing system;
- icon and graphic language;
- photography or illustration direction;
- packaging geometry where relevant;
- product family rules;
- tone and motion principles;
- concept-disclosure style.

AI-generated imagery may vary, but the deterministic composition and brand tokens create recognizable continuity.

## 11. Reproducibility does not require visual sameness

A deterministic system should not make every brand look identical.

Templates can be modular and expressive. They may support:

- editorial layouts;
- luxury minimalism;
- youth-oriented campaigns;
- product-detail cards;
- marketplace listings;
- professional legal explainers;
- social carousels;
- cinematic video frames.

Reproducibility means the system can recreate the approved output and preserve exact elements. It does not mean reducing every visual to one platform style.

## 12. Multi-channel rendering should derive from one approved source

A visual campaign may need:

- square social post;
- vertical short-video frame;
- horizontal website hero;
- carousel sequence;
- sales card;
- printable one-page sheet;
- email image;
- private buyer deck.

Lite should generate these from a shared approved visual object and template family.

Each derivative should record:

- source Pack version;
- source image version;
- template version;
- channel profile;
- rendered text and language;
- review status;
- publication history.

A correction to a fact or logo should identify every affected derivative.

## 13. Automated validation can catch predictable errors

Automated checks may include:

- image dimensions and aspect ratio;
- file size;
- logo presence and expected asset hash;
- text overflow;
- minimum font size;
- safe-area violations;
- missing concept label;
- missing required disclaimer;
- contrast and readability;
- missing source metadata;
- unexpected template fields;
- duplicated output;
- prohibited words or symbols;
- expired Pack or Authorization;
- broken links or QR codes.

Computer vision may assist with detecting distorted logos or unwanted text, but uncertain findings should remain review candidates rather than final judgments.

## 14. Human review remains necessary for meaning

Automated validation cannot reliably determine:

- whether a visual implies a real business;
- whether cultural symbolism is inappropriate;
- whether the mark remains recognizable;
- whether the product concept fits the specification;
- whether the brand direction is commercially coherent;
- whether the scene creates trademark or advertising risk;
- whether the output feels credible rather than generic.

The review level should follow the output’s public reach, commercial value and professional consequence.

## 15. Model provenance and generation history matter

For each AI-assisted source image, Lite should retain proportionate provenance:

- model or service used;
- generation date;
- prompt or structured brief reference;
- source assets supplied;
- input rights and restrictions;
- edit history;
- contributor identity where relevant;
- review findings;
- approved uses;
- retention and deletion policy.

This helps answer later questions about rights, correction, consistency and reproducibility.

The platform should not expose confidential prompts or customer data unnecessarily, but it needs enough internal evidence to govern the asset.

## 16. External model providers create a data boundary

Sending a customer mark, confidential launch plan or private seller information to an external model is a separate processing decision.

The Product Installation should govern:

- whether external models may be used;
- which fields may be sent;
- whether customer consent is needed;
- retention and training terms;
- regional processing requirements;
- subprocessor disclosure;
- private-model or local-processing alternatives;
- incident response.

```text
AI feature enabled
≠ every Workplace object may be sent to every model
```

## 17. Rights review must cover inputs and outputs

Visual production may involve:

- customer logos;
- stock images;
- AI-generated people;
- product photographs;
- fonts;
- music;
- templates;
- third-party marks;
- reference images;
- contributor work.

Lite should track rights and permitted uses at the asset level.

A public campaign should not rely on an input that was authorized only for private concept review. A contributor’s accepted output should have a clear compensation and usage basis. Font and music licensing may differ across web, social, video and print.

## 18. Cost and latency must be managed without weakening control

AI imagery and video can create unpredictable cost.

Lite may govern cost through:

- draft-resolution generation;
- limited concept rounds;
- user-visible generation budgets;
- reuse of approved source imagery;
- deterministic adaptation instead of regeneration;
- model selection by task;
- caching and asset deduplication;
- escalation to premium generation only after direction approval;
- human review before expensive motion production.

The system should not hide significant generation cost behind an unlimited promise and then degrade quality or introduce dark-pattern restrictions.

## 19. Model drift and template drift must be separated

An image model may change its visual behavior. A template may also change.

Lite should distinguish:

```text
source-image generation version
≠ template version
≠ brand-token version
≠ Pack version
≠ published derivative version
```

This allows the system to reproduce an approved campaign even if a newer model produces different imagery.

For important assets, the approved source image itself should be retained rather than relying on prompt regeneration.

## 20. Example: a concept storefront

Suppose Lite creates a storefront visualization for an authorized trademark Listing.

The pipeline should:

1. verify the trademark and active Resale Authorization;
2. select the approved mark asset;
3. define the concept as a possible retail direction;
4. generate a storefront scene without final signage;
5. remove invented signs and incidental text;
6. place the approved logo deterministically;
7. add a discreet “concept storefront” label;
8. render social, buyer-deck and web variants;
9. perform visual and commercial review;
10. link all variants to the same Pack version.

The result can be persuasive without suggesting that the store already exists.

## 21. Failure modes

This pipeline fails when:

- the AI image becomes the only source file;
- exact logo and text are left to the model;
- a concept disclaimer exists only outside the downloadable asset;
- the model invents products beyond the stated commercial scope;
- a generated store, factory or product is presented as existing reality;
- templates overwrite factual labels;
- model or template changes cannot be traced;
- external providers receive private data without policy;
- rights restrictions are lost during reuse;
- paid reviewers approve visual quality without reviewing factual implications;
- corrected source facts do not trigger re-rendering.

## 22. Product and technical implications

Lite needs a governed media system with:

- Visual Briefs;
- protected brand-asset storage;
- AI source-image generation and import;
- rights and provenance metadata;
- parameterized templates;
- deterministic static and motion rendering;
- channel profiles;
- concept-label policies;
- automated preflight;
- review routing;
- version and dependency graphs;
- correction impact analysis;
- cost controls;
- retention and deletion controls;
- export and publication records.

This is not merely a creative feature. It is production infrastructure connecting imagination to accountable commercial communication.

## 23. Commercial implications

The pipeline supports:

- premium Marketing Packs;
- repeatable multi-channel production;
- paid designer and professional review;
- faster Listing launch;
- buyer-specific adaptations;
- agency white-label production;
- subscription retention;
- controlled media generation at scale.

The defensible value lies less in access to an image model and more in the system’s ability to preserve exact brand assets, facts, rights, versions and review evidence across repeated production.

## 24. The operating principle

```text
AI imagines
Templates compose
Rules protect
Reviewers interpret
Workplaces approve
Evidence preserves
```

> Lite should use AI to expand what a trademark can look like, while deterministic rendering protects what the trademark is and what the business may truthfully claim.
