# Chapter 12 — Public, Network, Private and Selected Visibility

Visibility is not a single switch.

A trademark owner may want to test demand without exposing identity. A Workplace may want to share an asset only with trusted partners. A buyer may need fuller information after qualification. A public Listing may show a brand concept but hide price. An exclusive representative may permit public promotion while restricting direct owner contact.

Lite therefore needs a visibility architecture, not a `public/private` Boolean.

```text
Private
Selected Workplaces
Authorized Network
Qualified Recipients
Public
```

Each mode changes audience, disclosure, routing, ranking, evidence and relationship risk.

## 1. Visibility follows purpose

Before choosing visibility, Lite should ask:

- What commercial objective is being pursued?
- Is the owner testing demand, seeking a specific buyer or launching a public sale?
- Which facts must be disclosed for the audience to act competently?
- Which facts should remain protected?
- Who may respond?
- Who owns and receives the resulting relationship?
- How long should the projection remain active?

Visibility without purpose becomes uncontrolled exposure.

## 2. Private visibility

Private visibility keeps the Inventory, Marketing Pack or Listing inside the originating Workplace or a defined internal group.

Private uses include:

- owner portfolio review;
- internal pricing discussion;
- Marketing Pack preparation;
- matching against private buyer Requests;
- rights and document review;
- seller-readiness assessment;
- campaign planning.

Private does not mean ungoverned. Internal members still need appropriate Membership, role and purpose.

## 3. Selected Workplace visibility

The originating Workplace may share with named Workplaces.

This mode is useful for:

- known co-brokers;
- trusted industry specialists;
- a buyer representative;
- regional partners;
- a white-label commercial partner;
- a defined campaign group.

The projection should specify:

- recipient Workplaces;
- permitted users or roles;
- visible fields;
- onward-sharing rights;
- owner-contact rules;
- attribution and commission;
- expiry;
- revocation;
- permitted actions.

```text
Selected access
≠ permission to republish
```

## 4. Authorized network visibility

Network visibility makes the asset discoverable to an approved participant pool rather than the general public.

Possible benefits include:

- higher trust than open publication;
- clearer attribution;
- partner qualification;
- controlled commission terms;
- lower owner-exposure risk;
- specialized industry reach;
- better feedback on demand.

The network itself does not become Relationship Owner or asset owner.

Participants should know whether they may:

- show the mark to clients;
- create localized materials;
- receive buyer inquiries;
- negotiate;
- contact the originating Workplace only;
- contact the owner under specified conditions;
- invite another intermediary.

## 5. Qualified-recipient visibility

Some information should become visible only after the recipient meets conditions.

Conditions may include:

- verified identity;
- credible buyer mandate;
- accepted confidentiality terms;
- budget or category fit;
- conflict check;
- originating Workplace approval;
- owner approval;
- transaction-stage threshold.

Qualified recipients may receive:

- owner identity;
- fuller legal documents;
- internal price range;
- transfer restrictions;
- detailed due-diligence materials;
- commercial history;
- direct communication route.

This is progressive disclosure, not concealment of material facts needed for the current decision.

## 6. Public visibility

Public visibility may support:

- broad discovery;
- search-engine indexing;
- social distribution;
- thematic catalogues;
- buyer-request attraction;
- public campaigns;
- seller lead generation.

Public visibility carries the highest leakage and staleness risk.

Before activation, Lite should require stronger checks for:

- authority;
- factual freshness;
- owner disclosure policy;
- price treatment;
- concept labels;
- inquiry routing;
- correction and withdrawal capability;
- advertising and privacy requirements;
- third-party asset rights.

## 7. Visibility should be field-specific

A Listing may be public while some fields remain restricted.

Fields may have separate visibility policies:

- mark image;
- jurisdiction;
- status;
- goods summary;
- exact specification;
- owner identity;
- seller identity;
- asking price;
- owner net price;
- commission;
- Authorization evidence;
- due-diligence documents;
- buyer identity;
- inquiry history.

The policy should be comprehensible. Excessive field-level complexity should be managed through reusable profiles.

## 8. Reusable visibility profiles

Lite may offer governed profiles such as:

### Private preparation

- internal facts and documents;
- no external discovery;
- no inquiry route.

### Confidential demand test

- mark and high-level category visible to selected recipients;
- owner identity hidden;
- price on request;
- inquiries routed to originating Workplace.

### Network brokerage

- verified network participants;
- commission and attribution visible;
- owner contact restricted;
- onward sharing controlled.

### Public Listing

- public facts and concepts;
- approved price presentation;
- clear inquiry route;
- persistent concept labels;
- regular revalidation.

### Qualified buyer room

- named recipient;
- confidentiality accepted;
- fuller documents;
- activity logged;
- expiry and download rules.

Profiles reduce user burden without removing explicit policy.

## 9. Visibility is not ownership or authority

```text
Can view
≠ can promote

Can promote
≠ can negotiate

Can negotiate
≠ can accept

Can download
≠ can reuse

Can contact
≠ owns relationship
```

The interface should expose allowed actions, not leave participants to infer them.

## 10. Onward sharing must be explicit

A network participant may want to send a Listing to a customer or another partner.

Possible policies include:

- no onward sharing;
- share link only;
- share with named customer;
- share through a tracked buyer projection;
- download approved sales card;
- create localized derivative after approval;
- invite another Workplace with attribution;
- public repost permitted.

Every route should preserve source and restrictions where practical.

## 11. Downloading creates additional risk

Once a file is downloaded, revocation becomes harder.

Lite may use:

- dynamic web views;
- expiring links;
- recipient watermarks;
- embedded source identifiers;
- visible concept labels;
- download logging;
- restricted document rooms;
- updated-version notices.

These controls cannot guarantee that information will not be copied. They improve accountability and correction.

## 12. Revocation should propagate

When visibility is revoked, Lite should:

- deactivate platform-hosted views;
- stop ranking and search indexing where controlled;
- notify recipients where appropriate;
- invalidate expiring links;
- prevent new derivatives;
- preserve prior access evidence;
- identify downloaded materials;
- handle active inquiries separately;
- record survival obligations.

Revocation cannot erase what recipients already learned. Governance should therefore minimize disclosure from the beginning.

## 13. Search indexing is a separate decision

Public does not always mean searchable by external engines.

A public link may be:

- unlisted;
- platform-searchable only;
- externally indexable;
- campaign-limited;
- geographically limited where feasible.

Search-engine caches and third-party reposts reduce reversibility. The owner should understand this consequence.

## 14. Social distribution needs source linkage

Social posts often outlive the Listing.

Every distributed asset should ideally retain:

- Listing or Pack reference;
- concept label;
- source Workplace;
- inquiry route;
- campaign or expiry information where practical;
- correction path.

Lite should track which posts were generated and published so withdrawn supply can be addressed.

## 15. Buyer Requests can reverse the visibility flow

Not all commerce should begin with public supply.

A buyer may publish or privately project a Trademark Request. Lite can match private Inventory without exposing it broadly.

```text
Private Inventory
+ qualified Request
→ selected recommendation
```

This demand-led route may create better liquidity with lower owner exposure.

## 16. Ranking scope must follow visibility scope

A private asset should not appear in public recommendations. A selected-Workplace Listing should not be used to train public marketplace ranking without authority.

Ranking should respect:

- audience eligibility;
- channel;
- represented Workplace;
- buyer Request;
- authority freshness;
- commercial restrictions;
- paid promotion disclosure;
- relationship policy.

## 17. Visibility and confidentiality are not identical

A qualified buyer may be allowed to see sensitive information under confidentiality terms. A public user may see less information without being bound by confidentiality.

The system should record:

- policy accepted;
- recipient identity;
- permitted use;
- retention period;
- onward disclosure;
- document access;
- deletion or return expectations;
- breach route.

The platform should not claim that a click-through agreement solves every legal confidentiality issue.

## 18. Platform operators also need minimum access

Operating the visibility system may require platform personnel or services to process content, moderation and security signals.

This does not create commercial use rights.

Operational access should remain:

- role-limited;
- purpose-limited;
- logged;
- separated from sales prospecting;
- subject to retention policy;
- governed for AI processing.

## 19. Example: confidential owner identity

An owner authorizes a public Listing but does not want identity disclosed until a buyer is qualified.

The public view may show:

- mark;
- jurisdiction and selected facts;
- approved brand concepts;
- asking price range;
- represented-by statement;
- inquiry form.

After identity and buyer intent are checked, a qualified view may show:

- owner identity;
- source documents;
- transfer scope;
- detailed price and conditions;
- due-diligence steps.

The buyer gains what is needed at each stage without the owner being exposed to uncontrolled direct contact.

## 20. Failure modes

The visibility system fails when:

- public/private is the only control;
- all Listing fields inherit one visibility state;
- selected recipients may republish silently;
- network access implies owner-contact rights;
- confidential documents are attached to public pages;
- downloads lose restrictions and source;
- revocation deletes evidence but leaves live derivatives;
- public pages are indexed without owner awareness;
- private inventory enters public ranking;
- platform operators reuse data for prospecting;
- minimum disclosure hides facts necessary for professional or transaction decisions.

## 21. Product and technical implications

Lite needs:

- audience and recipient objects;
- reusable visibility profiles;
- field-level policy where needed;
- permitted-action controls;
- progressive disclosure gates;
- qualified-recipient rooms;
- tracked links and derivatives;
- search-indexing policy;
- download and access logs;
- revocation propagation;
- ranking-scope enforcement;
- inquiry provenance;
- confidentiality and onward-sharing records;
- AI and operational-access boundaries.

## 22. Commercial implications

Layered visibility enables:

- confidential owner services;
- selected-network brokerage;
- private buyer matching;
- public marketplace discovery;
- premium buyer rooms;
- disclosed promotion;
- controlled co-broker participation.

The platform should not force public visibility as the price of using Lite.

## 23. The operating principle

```text
Reveal only what the current purpose requires,
to the recipients entitled to see it,
with the actions they are allowed to take,
for as long as the authority remains current.
```

> Liquidity does not require making every asset public. It requires making the right supply visible to the right demand under a route that remains trustworthy.
