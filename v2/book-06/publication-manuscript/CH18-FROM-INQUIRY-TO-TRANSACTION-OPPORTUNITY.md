# Chapter 18 — From Inquiry to Transaction Opportunity

An Inquiry becomes a Transaction Opportunity only when there is enough evidence that a specific commercial route may be viable.

Interest alone is not enough. A buyer may ask for a price out of curiosity. A seller may respond without confirming authority. A matching result may look attractive while the goods coverage, ownership chain or budget remains unresolved.

Lite should therefore require an explicit conversion from conversation to opportunity.

```text
Inquiry
→ qualification
→ seller and authority reconfirmation
→ commercial-fit assessment
→ Opportunity proposal
→ participant approval
→ Transaction Opportunity
```

## 1. Opportunity is not a label for every lead

```text
Inquiry ≠ Transaction Opportunity
Recommendation ≠ Transaction Opportunity
Offer ≠ Transaction Opportunity by itself
```

A Transaction Opportunity represents a governed hypothesis that named participants may be able to complete a defined transaction under identifiable conditions.

It should answer:

- which trademark or asset package is involved;
- who represents the buyer;
- who represents the seller;
- which Workplaces participate;
- what commercial route is proposed;
- which terms are known;
- which facts remain unverified;
- which approvals and services are required;
- what would cause the route to fail.

## 2. Conversion criteria

Before conversion, Lite should normally have evidence of:

- a sufficiently qualified buyer context;
- a specific candidate asset;
- current Listing or private-supply authority;
- seller willingness to proceed or reconfirm;
- a plausible price or negotiation range;
- compatible confidentiality and contact rules;
- an identifiable transaction route;
- no known blocking conflict;
- agreement on who coordinates the next stage.

Unknowns may remain, but they must be visible and assigned for resolution.

## 3. Opportunity anatomy

A Transaction Opportunity may record:

- parent Inquiry and Request;
- selected Trademark, Inventory, Authorization and Listing versions;
- buyer and seller represented identities;
- Buyer Relationship Owner;
- Seller Relationship Owner;
- sourcing, referral and co-broker roles;
- proposed price structure;
- commissions and service fees;
- proposed transaction model;
- due-diligence scope;
- required professionals;
- document and evidence gaps;
- deadlines and reservation status;
- confidentiality rules;
- risk register;
- next decision gate;
- probability as an operational estimate, not a fact.

## 4. Transaction models must be explicit

Possible models include:

```text
Direct owner sale
Referral
Co-brokerage
White-label transaction support
MarkReg-assisted transfer
Platform-coordinated but third-party contracted transaction
```

The model affects:

- who contracts with whom;
- who receives funds;
- who invoices service fees;
- who prepares agreements;
- who conducts due diligence;
- who files the official transfer;
- who owns the customer relationship;
- who bears failure and refund responsibilities.

Lite should not select a model silently from the payment route.

## 5. Roles are engagement-specific

The Opportunity may involve:

- buyer representative;
- seller representative;
- transaction coordinator;
- due-diligence professional;
- agreement drafter;
- payment service provider;
- local recordal agent;
- translator;
- platform operator.

```text
Transaction Coordinator
≠ Contracting Party
≠ Payment Custodian
≠ Professional Authority
```

Each role must be scoped to the Opportunity and later Engagement.

## 6. Commercial terms remain versioned proposals

Lite should distinguish:

- owner net expectation;
- asking price;
- buyer indication;
- counteroffer;
- permitted discount;
- service fees;
- official fees;
- taxes and payment costs;
- commission allocations;
- contingency or success fees;
- refund conditions.

A term history should show who proposed, approved, rejected or superseded each version.

```text
Displayed price ≠ accepted price
Accepted commercial terms ≠ signed agreement
Signed agreement ≠ completed transfer
```

## 7. Attribution and commission ledger

The Opportunity should preserve contribution and economic attribution:

- who sourced the asset;
- who introduced the buyer;
- who created the successful match;
- who qualified the Inquiry;
- who negotiated;
- who performs due diligence;
- who prepares and records the transfer;
- what commission rules existed before the work;
- what later amendments were approved.

This does not mean every contribution earns a fee. It means the basis for payment or non-payment is visible.

## 8. Opportunity lifecycle

A useful lifecycle is:

```text
Proposed
→ Qualification Complete
→ Seller Confirmed
→ Buyer Confirmed for Review
→ Due Diligence Pending
→ Terms Under Negotiation
→ Engagement Pending
→ Agreement Pending
→ Transaction Executing
→ Completed / Failed / Withdrawn / Expired
```

Some stages may occur in a different order, but state changes should be evidence-based.

## 9. Opportunity probability must remain contestable

AI may estimate conversion probability using:

- buyer responsiveness;
- seller responsiveness;
- price distance;
- authority freshness;
- document readiness;
- transaction complexity;
- historical route performance.

The estimate should explain its evidence and uncertainty.

It must not:

- change the Opportunity state;
- allocate resources automatically without policy;
- pressure users to disclose more information;
- reduce relationship protection;
- be presented as guaranteed revenue.

## 10. Reservation and exclusivity

The Opportunity may request a Reservation, but Reservation remains a separate governed event.

It should specify:

- buyer;
- asset;
- period;
- consideration or deposit, if any;
- whether other inquiries pause;
- conditions for extension;
- release rules;
- attribution protection;
- approving authority.

```text
Opportunity Active ≠ Asset Reserved
```

## 11. Opportunity does not automatically create Engagement

Before professional or transactional work begins, participants may need an Engagement covering:

- services and exclusions;
- fees;
- represented party;
- professional responsibility;
- data access;
- confidentiality;
- conflicts;
- review and acceptance;
- termination.

The Opportunity identifies a route. The Engagement authorizes accountable participation in that route.

## 12. Example

A buyer agency confirms interest in a private Listing at USD 8,000. The seller Workplace confirms the asset remains available but requires owner approval for any discount. The buyer wants a US assignment and a separate China filing review.

The Transaction Opportunity records:

- USD 8,000 asking price, not accepted price;
- buyer budget of USD 7,000–8,000;
- seller approval required for discount;
- source and buyer-side commission rules;
- MarkReg-assisted due diligence and recordal as a proposed route;
- China filing review as a separate service scope;
- no Reservation yet;
- ownership and authorization documents pending.

This is a viable Opportunity, not a completed deal.

## 13. Failure modes

The model fails when:

- every Inquiry is called an Opportunity;
- seller willingness is assumed from an old Listing;
- payment route determines contracting role silently;
- commissions are negotiated only after value is created;
- one opaque probability score replaces risk analysis;
- Reservation is inferred from negotiation;
- Opportunity state changes from AI sentiment;
- due diligence begins without scope or authorization;
- professional work begins before Engagement;
- failed Opportunities disappear and cannot inform future matching.

## 14. Product and technical implications

Lite needs:

- explicit Inquiry-to-Opportunity conversion;
- participant and represented-role model;
- transaction-model selection;
- term and proposal versioning;
- attribution and commission ledger;
- risk and Unknown register;
- Reservation requests;
- Engagement handoff;
- probability explanation;
- state and evidence events;
- closure and failure reasons;
- relationship-preserving history.

## 15. Commercial implications

A clean Opportunity object supports:

- accurate pipeline forecasting;
- fair commission allocation;
- qualified professional-service handoffs;
- partner trust;
- reduced duplicate negotiation;
- clearer customer communication;
- measurable transaction conversion.

The platform should earn from real coordination and services, not from inflating lead counts.

## 16. The operating principle

> A Transaction Opportunity should begin only when a specific buyer, a specific governed asset and a plausible accountable route have met—not merely when two people exchanged messages.
