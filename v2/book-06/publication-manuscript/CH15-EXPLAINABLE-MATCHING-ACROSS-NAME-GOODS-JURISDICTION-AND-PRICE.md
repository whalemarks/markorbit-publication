# Chapter 15 — Explainable Matching Across Name, Goods, Jurisdiction and Price

A trademark recommendation cannot be reduced to text similarity.

A name may look perfect but exist in the wrong jurisdiction. It may be registered in the right country but cover unusable goods. It may have strong brand potential but no valid sale authorization. It may fit the buyer’s budget but require expensive recordal, rebranding or new filings. It may be visually attractive while carrying a meaning that fails in the target market.

Lite should therefore treat matching as a governed multi-dimensional comparison rather than one opaque score.

```text
Qualified Request
+ permitted supply pools
+ candidate eligibility
+ multi-dimensional fit
+ explicit trade-offs
+ unresolved verification
= explained recommendation set
```

The purpose of matching is not to prove that a trademark is right. It is to identify a small number of candidates worth informed human attention.

## 1. Retrieval, eligibility, matching and recommendation are separate

The system should preserve four stages.

### Retrieval

Retrieval finds possible candidates from permitted sources.

Sources may include:

- originating Workplace Inventory;
- customer-authorized Inventory;
- selected partner Inventory;
- network Listings;
- public platform Listings;
- official trademark records where a commercial route is not yet established;
- candidate new-filing names.

### Eligibility

Eligibility checks whether the candidate may enter this matching route.

Possible requirements include:

- visibility permission;
- active Resale Authorization;
- valid Listing status;
- compatible jurisdiction;
- current legal-status evidence;
- price or inquiry route;
- no known exclusivity conflict;
- confidentiality compatibility;
- buyer qualification.

### Matching

Matching compares the eligible candidate against Request dimensions.

### Recommendation

Recommendation selects and explains candidates for a particular recipient and purpose.

```text
Retrieved
≠ Eligible
≠ Matched
≠ Recommended
```

A public trademark record may be retrieved for market intelligence but should not be presented as transaction-ready without a viable commercial route.

## 2. Name fit has several dimensions

Name matching may consider:

- exact string;
- normalized spelling;
- edit distance;
- prefix or suffix;
- syllable count;
- pronunciation;
- transliteration;
- semantic meaning;
- language associations;
- visual word shape;
- initials;
- abbreviation;
- coined-word character;
- memorability;
- negative meanings;
- cultural sensitivity.

These dimensions should not collapse into one “name similarity” number.

For example, two marks may be visually similar in Latin letters but pronounced differently. Another may have a different spelling but the same pronunciation. A Chinese-language buyer may care about transliteration potential more than literal English meaning.

The recommendation should state which name dimensions match and which do not.

## 3. Semantic fit can be useful and dangerous

Semantic models can identify trademarks associated with concepts such as:

- purity;
- speed;
- luxury;
- nature;
- technology;
- childhood;
- health;
- strength;
- elegance.

This can support brand exploration. It can also create false confidence.

A name associated with “medical trust” may be inappropriate for unregulated consumer goods. A name that sounds premium in one language may be awkward in another. A model may infer meaning from a coined word that the buyer does not perceive.

Lite should show semantic interpretation as an inference, with language and cultural context.

```text
Model association
≠ established public meaning
≠ legal conclusion
```

## 4. Goods fit should use actual commercial use and actual specification

Class number alone is too coarse.

Two Class 11 registrations may cover very different goods. A buyer seeking coffee machines may not benefit from a registration limited to lighting fixtures. A Class 25 registration for footwear may not satisfy a buyer focused on sportswear if the specification is narrow and the jurisdiction treats goods strictly.

Matching should compare:

- buyer’s actual planned goods and services;
- normalized goods concepts;
- official specification wording;
- class;
- jurisdictional interpretation;
- breadth and limitations;
- adjacent expansion needs;
- buyer tolerance for new filing.

The recommendation should state:

```text
Directly covered
Possibly related — professional review needed
Outside current scope
Unknown because specification is incomplete
```

It should not convert a class match into a goods-coverage conclusion.

## 5. Industry fit and legal scope are different

A mark may fit the buyer’s industry aesthetically while lacking relevant registration coverage.

Another mark may have useful legal scope but feel wrong for the intended audience.

Lite should show both:

```text
Commercial / industry fit
Legal-scope fit
```

For example:

- a playful name may fit children’s products but be registered only for adult clothing;
- a technical name may have broad electronics coverage but feel unsuitable for a lifestyle brand;
- a premium-sounding mark may have strong packaging potential but require new filing for the buyer’s core goods.

A good recommendation makes the trade-off explicit.

## 6. Jurisdiction fit is more than country equality

Jurisdiction matching may consider:

- exact country or region;
- application versus registration;
- national versus regional right;
- current status;
- renewal and maintenance position;
- ownership-record accuracy;
- transferability and recordal process;
- use requirements;
- language and script;
- whether the buyer needs one or several jurisdictions;
- whether a new filing route is feasible.

For a buyer seeking the United States and China, possible routes include:

- one name with existing rights in both;
- an existing right in one jurisdiction plus a new filing in the other;
- separate names by jurisdiction;
- a new name with clearance and filings in both;
- acquisition of a broader portfolio.

Matching should compare routes, not merely reject every candidate lacking two existing registrations.

## 7. Legal status is an evidence dimension

Possible candidate states include:

- registered and recently verified;
- registered but maintenance review needed;
- pending application;
- status conflict between sources;
- owner-record mismatch;
- cancellation or expiry risk;
- unknown current state;
- candidate new filing only.

Lite should show the source date and verification level.

```text
Database says registered
≠ transaction-safe legal conclusion
```

High-value recommendations should trigger refreshed verification before presentation or Inquiry.

## 8. Authorization and availability are separate from legal status

A valid registration may not be for sale. A seller may have authorized promotion but not negotiation. A Listing may be active but require seller reconfirmation.

Matching should consider:

- Inventory relationship;
- Resale Authorization status;
- Listing status;
- visibility scope;
- owner response history;
- reservation;
- price confirmation;
- negotiation authority;
- buyer restrictions;
- expiry.

The recommendation should state commercial readiness such as:

```text
Transaction-ready subject to due diligence
Seller reconfirmation required
Private inquiry route only
Authorization revalidation pending
Public record only — no established sale route
```

## 9. Price fit requires total-cost thinking

The asking price is only one component.

A buyer may also face:

- brokerage or platform fees;
- legal due diligence;
- transfer agreement costs;
- official recordal fees;
- tax or payment costs;
- translation and notarization;
- new filings in missing jurisdictions or classes;
- logo refinement;
- rebranding;
- evidence and maintenance work.

Lite should compare:

```text
Asset price
+ expected transaction cost
+ expected expansion cost
+ rebranding cost
+ uncertainty reserve
```

The total remains an estimate. It should not be presented as a binding quotation unless the responsible parties have issued one.

## 10. Speed fit also needs route decomposition

A registered mark may appear faster than a new filing, but the transaction may be delayed by:

- seller response;
- authority verification;
- agreement negotiation;
- payment arrangements;
- document legalization;
- recordal;
- unresolved ownership issues;
- additional filings.

A new-filing route may provide quick application submission but not immediate registration certainty.

The recommendation should distinguish:

- time to first commercial decision;
- time to agreement;
- time to operational use under agreed risk;
- time to official recordal;
- time to registration in expansion jurisdictions.

```text
Fast to start
≠ fast to complete
```

## 11. Visual fit should not become legal clearance

AI may compare logos, word shapes and visual styles. This can help brand selection.

It should not declare:

- non-infringement;
- registrability;
- no confusing similarity;
- continuity with an existing mark;
- safe use in a jurisdiction.

Visual matching is one commercial dimension. Legal clearance requires a different search, evidence set and professional authority.

## 12. Brand potential is a structured recommendation

Brand potential may consider:

- memorability;
- pronounceability;
- semantic flexibility;
- visual identity potential;
- product extension;
- domain and digital usability where checked;
- audience fit;
- story potential;
- channel fit;
- distinctiveness in a commercial sense.

It should be explained through observable reasons, not a mystical score.

For example:

> Strong visual identity potential because the mark is short, symmetrical and adaptable to an icon; weaker pronunciation consistency across English and Mandarin; product scope currently aligns with selected Class 11 goods but would need review for retail services.

## 13. Seller restrictions affect matching

A candidate may be excluded or downgraded because:

- owner will not sell below a minimum price;
- certain countries or channels are excluded;
- buyer identity must be disclosed before price;
- co-brokerage is not allowed;
- the Listing is reserved;
- the seller will not work with competitors;
- associated domains or logos are excluded;
- direct contact is prohibited;
- the authorization is close to expiry.

These restrictions are not defects if disclosed. They affect route suitability.

## 14. Matching should support hard constraints and preferences

The Request should distinguish:

### Hard constraints

Examples:

- must cover the United States;
- budget may not exceed a fixed amount;
- buyer refuses pending applications;
- specific wording must appear;
- public disclosure is prohibited.

### Strong preferences

Examples:

- short English word;
- premium feeling;
- existing Class 3 registration;
- seller willing to move quickly.

### Flexible preferences

Examples:

- color direction;
- exact syllable count;
- secondary goods;
- one of several name themes.

### Unknowns

Examples:

- second jurisdiction;
- associated assets;
- exact transfer timeline.

The recommendation should not violate a hard constraint merely because the overall score remains high.

## 15. No single score should hide trade-offs

A composite score can help sort candidates internally. It should not become the explanation.

A useful presentation may show dimensions such as:

```text
Name fit: strong
Industry fit: strong
Goods fit: partial
Jurisdiction fit: strong
Authorization: verified
Price fit: moderate
Speed fit: uncertain
Brand potential: strong
Professional checks: required
```

The system should explain critical weaknesses even when the aggregate ranking is high.

A candidate with a lower overall score may be preferable because it satisfies the buyer’s hard legal or budget constraint.

## 16. Pareto sets are often better than one winner

Many Requests have no single best candidate.

Lite may present a small set of non-dominated options:

- best exact-name fit;
- best legal-scope fit;
- best budget option;
- fastest viable route;
- strongest brand potential;
- best cross-jurisdiction strategy.

This supports real choice.

```text
Recommended set
≠ one objectively best trademark
```

## 17. Negative matching is a first-class output

Lite should record why candidates were excluded.

Reasons may include:

- wrong jurisdiction;
- insufficient goods coverage;
- expired Authorization;
- unavailable or reserved;
- price outside hard range;
- negative meaning;
- seller restrictions;
- ownership uncertainty;
- high expansion cost;
- excessive transfer complexity;
- buyer’s prior rejection;
- confidentiality mismatch.

Negative matching prevents the same poor candidates from returning repeatedly and reveals supply gaps.

It should not create permanent blacklists from one buyer’s preference.

## 18. Professional judgment adds context

Professionals may contribute:

- interpretation of real business need;
- goods and class usefulness;
- local-language perception;
- cultural meaning;
- practical transfer complexity;
- seller credibility;
- price realism;
- rebranding implications;
- legal-review priorities.

A paid professional matching review should be a bounded Contribution with scope, evidence and review criteria.

The professional should not be asked to guarantee transaction success.

## 19. Explainable recommendation format

Each candidate recommendation should state:

### Why it matches

- relevant name, industry, goods, jurisdiction, price or brand factors.

### Where it differs

- important deviations from the Request.

### What is verified

- status, Authorization, price, ownership source and verification date.

### What remains unknown

- unverified facts, seller confirmation, missing documents or expansion requirements.

### Commercial route

- public Listing, private inquiry, partner route, new filing or other path.

### Expected costs and timing

- clearly labeled estimates, quotations or unknowns.

### Recommended checks

- legal status refresh, due diligence, clearance, goods review, transfer advice or brand evaluation.

### Next action

- reject, request more information, present to buyer, open Inquiry or seek professional review.

## 20. Matching should preserve the recipient context

The same candidate may be explained differently to:

- the originating agency;
- the ultimate buyer;
- an internal reviewer;
- a network partner;
- the seller.

This does not permit inconsistent facts. It permits purpose-specific presentation.

The buyer may see commercial fit and known gaps. The agency may also see attribution, commission and private seller conditions. The seller may see the buyer profile permitted for disclosure.

## 21. Feedback should update the Request and matching evidence

Buyer responses may include:

- like;
- dislike;
- save for later;
- request more details;
- price concern;
- name concern;
- goods concern;
- jurisdiction concern;
- seller-condition concern;
- request for similar candidates.

Lite should ask for the reason only when useful and low-friction.

Confirmed feedback may update Request preferences. It should not alter shared ranking rules automatically.

## 22. Bias and self-preference require governance

Matching can be distorted by:

- platform-owned Inventory;
- higher commission;
- paid promotion;
- preferred partners;
- seller pressure;
- incomplete private data;
- engagement optimization;
- model bias toward familiar languages or styles.

The system should separate:

```text
Eligibility
Organic relevance
Commercial sponsorship
Platform interest
Workplace preference
```

Paid promotion may affect placement only within eligible candidates and should be disclosed. It should not change the underlying fit explanation or hide stronger alternatives where the product promises neutral matching.

## 23. Example: four candidates, four strengths

A buyer seeks a short English mark for premium kitchen appliances in the United States, budget below USD 10,000, with speed important.

Lite finds:

### Candidate A

- excellent name fit;
- registered in the United States;
- goods cover selected kitchen appliances;
- price USD 12,000;
- seller open to negotiation.

### Candidate B

- moderate name fit;
- broad useful goods coverage;
- price USD 8,000;
- Authorization verified;
- older visual identity requiring refinement.

### Candidate C

- strong brand potential;
- registration covers lighting rather than kitchen appliances;
- low price;
- would require new filing for core goods.

### Candidate D

- exact name preference;
- public registration record only;
- no established sale route;
- owner contact and authority unknown.

A responsible recommendation may present A and B as transaction candidates, C as a strategic acquisition-plus-filing option and D only as market intelligence—not as an available asset.

## 24. Failure modes

Matching fails when:

- retrieval results are presented as eligible supply;
- class equality is treated as goods fit;
- semantic similarity is treated as public meaning;
- official status is stale;
- sale authority is assumed;
- total transaction cost is hidden;
- public records are labeled available;
- one composite score hides hard failures;
- professional judgment is averaged into a score without rationale;
- negative reasons are discarded;
- paid promotion changes fit claims;
- platform Inventory is secretly preferred;
- buyer feedback becomes a universal rule;
- AI matching is presented as legal clearance.

## 25. Product and technical implications

Lite needs:

- permitted-source retrieval;
- candidate eligibility gates;
- multidimensional feature models;
- goods and service normalization;
- jurisdiction and status profiles;
- Authorization and Listing integration;
- hard constraint handling;
- preference weights with provenance;
- Pareto-set generation;
- negative matching reasons;
- explanation generation;
- verification timestamps;
- recipient-specific views;
- feedback capture;
- bias and sponsorship controls;
- versioned matching runs;
- audit and correction.

The system should retain which Request version, candidate version, model or rule set and evidence snapshot produced a recommendation.

## 26. Commercial implications

Explainable matching supports:

- subscription value;
- paid professional shortlist review;
- buyer advisory services;
- seller and Listing promotion;
- network brokerage;
- due-diligence and transfer services;
- supply-gap intelligence.

The product should avoid monetizing opacity. Sellers may pay for disclosed promotion, but buyers should not be told that a candidate is the best fit merely because it yields more revenue.

## 27. Metrics that matter

Useful metrics include:

- eligible candidates per qualified Request;
- time to first useful shortlist;
- shortlist acceptance;
- Inquiry conversion;
- exclusion reasons;
- verification freshness;
- buyer correction rate;
- professional-review impact;
- total-cost surprise;
- repeated poor-candidate rate;
- supply gaps by jurisdiction and goods;
- bias and sponsorship complaints.

High retrieval volume is not matching quality.

## 28. The operating principle

```text
Retrieve broadly where permitted
Gate eligibility before recommendation
Compare across several dimensions
Respect hard constraints
Expose trade-offs
Preserve Unknown
Explain commercial route and evidence
Never turn a match score into legal certainty
```

> A useful matching system does not tell the buyer that one trademark is objectively best. It shows which candidates fit which parts of the need, what each option costs, what remains uncertain and which decision should come next.
