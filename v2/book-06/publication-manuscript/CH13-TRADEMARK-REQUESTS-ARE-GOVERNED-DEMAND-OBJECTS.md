# Chapter 13 — Trademark Requests Are Governed Demand Objects

A buyer rarely arrives with a complete trademark specification.

The initial message may be only:

- “I need a beauty brand.”
- “Find something similar to this Chinese name.”
- “The customer wants a premium European feeling.”
- “Class 25, under USD 5,000.”
- “We need something that can work in the United States and China.”
- “The client wants to buy a registered mark quickly.”

These statements contain real demand, but they are not yet instructions, Orders or transaction commitments.

Lite should convert incomplete conversation into a governed Trademark Request without forcing the buyer or intermediary to complete a long legal form before the need is understood.

```text
Demand signal
→ captured Trademark Request
→ clarified Request
→ permitted candidate search
→ explained recommendation
→ buyer response
→ Inquiry or closure
```

The Request is the demand-side operating object. It preserves what is known, what is inferred, what remains unknown, who owns the relationship and how the demand may be exposed to supply.

## 1. A Request is not a search query

A search query may be a few words submitted to a database. A Trademark Request contains business context, decision constraints and governance.

```text
Search query
≠ Trademark Request
```

A Request may influence:

- which inventory pools may be searched;
- which candidates may be shown;
- what information may be disclosed;
- who may respond;
- how buyer identity is protected;
- which legal or commercial checks are needed;
- how commission attribution is preserved;
- when the Request expires;
- whether the buyer wants an existing registration, a filing route or both.

A search engine returns records. A governed demand object supports a commercial decision path.

## 2. Request, Need, Inquiry and Opportunity remain distinct

Lite should preserve the following sequence:

```text
Need
→ Trademark Request
→ Candidate Recommendation
→ Buyer Inquiry
→ Transaction Opportunity
→ Engagement
```

### Need

A Need expresses an unresolved goal, such as obtaining a usable brand name for a new product line.

### Trademark Request

The Request structures the demand sufficiently for governed matching.

### Candidate Recommendation

A Recommendation is an explained proposal generated from one or more inventory pools.

### Buyer Inquiry

An Inquiry records interest in a particular asset or route.

### Transaction Opportunity

An Opportunity exists when the buyer, asset, seller route, commercial conditions and next steps are sufficiently plausible to justify active transaction work.

### Engagement

An Engagement records accepted roles, scope, commercial terms and responsibilities.

```text
Request submitted
≠ buyer committed

Candidate shown
≠ buyer interested

Buyer interested
≠ transaction viable

Transaction viable
≠ Engagement formed
```

## 3. A Request belongs to a Workplace business context

A buyer may communicate directly with an asset owner, through a trademark agency, through a broker, through a brand consultant or through a customer-service relationship already owned by another Workplace.

The Request should record:

- Demand Originator;
- represented buyer or buyer profile;
- Relationship Owner;
- originating Workplace;
- Person acting for that Workplace;
- permitted matching pools;
- confidentiality level;
- permitted disclosure fields;
- whether other Workplaces may respond;
- contact and non-circumvention rules;
- commission and attribution conditions;
- expiry and renewal rules.

Publishing or projecting the Request does not transfer the buyer relationship.

```text
Request visibility
≠ buyer ownership
≠ permission to contact buyer directly
```

## 4. Demand provenance matters

The system should know how the Request arose.

Possible origins include:

- direct buyer submission;
- existing customer conversation;
- agency-entered request;
- imported email or message;
- public request board;
- partner referral;
- campaign response;
- recurring demand inferred from prior inquiries;
- AI-extracted draft awaiting confirmation.

Each origin has different confidence and communication rights.

An AI-extracted Request from a message should remain a draft until the responsible user confirms that the interpretation is correct. A public form submission may be a lead rather than a qualified buyer. A partner referral may carry attribution and protection conditions.

## 5. The Request should preserve unknowns

A weak system forces every field to be filled. The user then guesses, selects “other” or enters unreliable values merely to continue.

Lite should allow:

```text
Known
Inferred
Preferred
Flexible
Unknown
Not Applicable
Requires Professional Advice
```

Examples:

- jurisdiction may be unknown because the buyer has not chosen an export market;
- class may be inferred from product description;
- budget may be flexible if the name fit is exceptional;
- exact spelling may be optional if pronunciation is more important;
- an existing registration may be preferred but not mandatory;
- legal clearance may require later professional work.

Unknown is not a defect. It is a state that determines the next useful question or matching limitation.

## 6. A Request has multiple dimensions

A mature Request may include the following sections.

### Naming direction

- exact wording;
- keywords;
- syllable count;
- pronunciation;
- language;
- transliteration preference;
- semantic direction;
- acceptable variants;
- prohibited meanings;
- initials or device preferences.

### Business context

- industry;
- products and services;
- target audience;
- price position;
- distribution channels;
- desired brand personality;
- current or planned company name;
- existing brand architecture.

### Legal and territorial context

- target jurisdictions;
- required classes;
- goods and services;
- existing registration required or optional;
- acceptable application status;
- renewal or maintenance expectations;
- tolerance for recordal complexity;
- need for clearance, due diligence or new filing.

### Commercial constraints

- budget range;
- target completion time;
- acceptable negotiation range;
- requirement for owner-direct or brokered route;
- need for associated domains, logos or other assets;
- payment or transfer expectations;
- appetite for legal and transaction costs.

### Matching preferences

- exact-name priority;
- industry-fit priority;
- legal-scope priority;
- price priority;
- speed priority;
- visual potential;
- rebranding tolerance;
- willingness to expand classes or jurisdictions.

### Governance

- confidentiality;
- visibility mode;
- permitted supply pools;
- permitted responders;
- contact rules;
- attribution;
- expiry;
- buyer-consent status.

## 7. The buyer may not be the decision maker

A Request may be submitted by:

- the ultimate buyer;
- an employee;
- a procurement representative;
- a trademark agent;
- a lawyer;
- a brand consultant;
- a distributor;
- a reseller;
- an investor;
- an intermediary without final authority.

The system should distinguish:

```text
Request Submitter
≠ Ultimate Buyer
≠ Decision Maker
≠ Payment Party
≠ Contracting Party
```

This affects confidentiality, approval, pricing and communication.

A consultant may be authorized to shortlist marks but not reveal the buyer identity. An employee may gather options but require management approval. A trademark agency may own the customer relationship and present candidates under its own brand.

## 8. Demand qualification should be proportionate

Lite should not require a full due-diligence process before presenting any ideas. It should also avoid exposing sensitive inventory to anonymous or unserious requests.

Qualification may consider:

- identity confidence;
- represented organization;
- budget plausibility;
- urgency;
- decision authority;
- clarity of need;
- response history;
- confidentiality requirements;
- transaction readiness;
- willingness to engage in due diligence;
- prior abuse, scraping or circumvention risk.

Possible stages include:

```text
Unverified Lead
Captured Request
Clarified Request
Qualified Request
Buyer-confirmed Shortlist
Inquiry-active Request
Fulfilled / Closed / Expired
```

Qualification should control disclosure depth, not become a covert mechanism for excluding small buyers.

## 9. Requests can be private, selected, networked or public

A buyer may not want competitors to know that a new product is being prepared.

Lite should support:

- Private Request — visible only inside the originating Workplace;
- Selected Request — shared with named Workplaces or asset owners;
- Authorized Network Request — discoverable within a governed professional network;
- Qualified Supply Request — exposed only to participants satisfying defined criteria;
- Public Request — broadly visible with protected buyer identity and clear response rules.

Each mode should specify:

- visible fields;
- whether buyer identity is hidden;
- whether responders may contact the buyer;
- whether onward sharing is allowed;
- whether candidates may be sourced outside Lite;
- response deadline;
- attribution and commission rules.

## 10. Public demand can attract noise

A public request board may create liquidity, but it also creates:

- spam;
- irrelevant submissions;
- duplicate candidates;
- unauthorized assets;
- price manipulation;
- buyer circumvention;
- disclosure of confidential strategy;
- race-to-the-bottom brokerage;
- false claims of availability.

Lite should therefore support controlled response requirements, such as:

- responder identity;
- source Inventory;
- active Resale Authorization;
- candidate fit explanation;
- price and fee clarity;
- known gaps;
- response expiry;
- relationship and contact rules.

The product should not reward the first participant who uploads a large unverified list.

## 11. Request ownership does not mean ownership of the buyer

Relationship protection should not become customer captivity.

A Relationship Owner may have legitimate rights regarding:

- introduction provenance;
- private buyer information;
- agreed communication route;
- commission attribution;
- confidential strategy;
- active service responsibility.

It does not own the person or organization indefinitely.

The buyer may:

- withdraw the Request;
- choose another intermediary;
- contact a seller directly where permitted;
- change requirements;
- reject every candidate;
- request data deletion or restricted retention where applicable.

The system should distinguish protected opportunity attribution from permanent control over future business.

## 12. A Request can evolve without losing history

Buyer demand often changes after seeing real options.

Examples:

- exact name becomes less important than goods coverage;
- budget increases for a strong registration;
- the buyer adds another jurisdiction;
- the buyer accepts a coined word instead of a descriptive name;
- the buyer decides to file a new mark rather than buy one;
- the buyer narrows the target product category;
- urgency changes after a launch delay.

Lite should version the Request rather than overwrite it.

Each version should record:

- changed fields;
- reason for change;
- who confirmed it;
- prior recommendations affected;
- whether matching should rerun;
- whether previous responders must be notified;
- whether confidentiality or commission conditions changed.

## 13. One buyer may have several related Requests

A company may need:

- one main house mark;
- several product-line marks;
- English and Chinese versions;
- marks for different jurisdictions;
- fallback names;
- an urgent acquisition and a longer-term filing route.

These should not be collapsed into one oversized Request.

Lite may group them under a Demand Portfolio while preserving separate criteria, budgets, deadlines and outcomes.

```text
Demand Portfolio
→ Request A
→ Request B
→ Request C
```

This supports coordinated strategy without confusing independent decisions.

## 14. AI may infer structure but must expose the inference

AI can transform free-form conversation into candidate Request fields.

For example:

> “The customer wants a short English name for skincare, preferably something elegant, and would like to launch in the US quickly.”

AI may infer:

- industry: skincare;
- likely classes: Class 3, perhaps related classes requiring confirmation;
- language: English;
- style: short and elegant;
- jurisdiction: United States;
- priority: speed;
- existing registration: possibly preferred but unconfirmed.

The user should see which values are:

```text
Explicitly stated
Inferred with confidence
Suggested option
Unknown
```

AI should not silently convert “skincare” into a final legal specification or assume the buyer wants only one class.

## 15. Request quality is not completeness alone

A Request can be complete but commercially unrealistic. Another can be incomplete but highly actionable.

Quality may consider:

- clarity of primary objective;
- internal consistency;
- buyer confirmation;
- budget and timeline plausibility;
- goods and jurisdiction readiness;
- confidentiality clarity;
- decision-maker access;
- responsiveness;
- permitted inventory pools;
- unresolved high-impact unknowns.

Lite should show why a Request is blocked or limited rather than assigning a mysterious quality score.

## 16. Negative demand signals are useful

The buyer may reject a candidate because:

- the name feels wrong;
- pronunciation is difficult;
- legal scope is too narrow;
- the price is too high;
- the jurisdiction is wrong;
- the visual style is unsuitable;
- transfer complexity is excessive;
- the seller conditions are unacceptable;
- the mark would require too much rebranding.

These reasons should update the Request or future matching preferences where the buyer confirms them.

A rejection is not merely a failed conversion. It is structured demand evidence.

## 17. The Request lifecycle

A practical lifecycle may include:

```text
Draft
→ Captured
→ Clarification Needed
→ Clarified
→ Qualified
→ Matching Active
→ Shortlist Presented
→ Inquiry Active
→ Fulfilled
→ Closed Unfulfilled
→ Withdrawn
→ Expired
→ Superseded
```

A Request may pause because:

- the buyer is unresponsive;
- budget is unavailable;
- confidentiality terms are unresolved;
- no viable supply exists;
- the represented project is delayed;
- the buyer is reconsidering acquisition versus new filing.

Paused demand should not remain indefinitely visible as active market demand.

## 18. Fulfilment has several meanings

A Request may be considered fulfilled when:

- the buyer selects a candidate for further due diligence;
- an Inquiry becomes a Transaction Opportunity;
- a transfer closes;
- the buyer chooses a new-filing route;
- the buyer accepts that no suitable asset exists;
- the buyer’s business need changes.

Lite should distinguish commercial fulfilment from legal transfer completion.

```text
Request fulfilled by selected route
≠ transaction completed
≠ ownership transferred
```

## 19. Example: a cross-border beauty-brand request

An agency receives this message:

> “Our customer needs an English beauty brand. They will sell skincare in China and the US. Budget around USD 8,000. They prefer a registered mark but may file a new one if the name is excellent.”

Lite creates a draft Request:

### Explicit

- industry: skincare;
- language: English;
- jurisdictions: China and United States;
- budget: approximately USD 8,000;
- existing registration preferred, not mandatory.

### Inferred and awaiting confirmation

- likely Class 3 focus;
- need for cross-jurisdiction compatibility;
- premium or international tone;
- transfer and due-diligence support may be required.

### Unknown

- exact goods;
- acceptable pronunciation variation;
- whether one mark must cover both jurisdictions;
- timeline;
- whether the buyer identity may be disclosed;
- decision maker;
- willingness to acquire separate marks by jurisdiction.

The system asks only the questions that materially change the search route.

## 20. Failure modes

The Request model fails when:

- a free-text message becomes an Order automatically;
- class or goods are inferred silently;
- public visibility transfers the buyer relationship;
- buyer identity is exposed too early;
- every field is mandatory;
- Unknown is replaced with invented certainty;
- a Request remains active after the buyer withdraws;
- several independent needs are collapsed into one Request;
- partner responders may contact the buyer without permission;
- the system treats high budget as the only qualification signal;
- rejection reasons are discarded;
- private demand is used to train public ranking without authority.

## 21. Product and technical implications

Lite requires a Request system with:

- conversational capture;
- explicit, inferred and unknown field states;
- Relationship Owner and Demand Originator;
- represented buyer context;
- confidentiality and visibility policy;
- allowed supply pools;
- responder eligibility;
- contact and attribution rules;
- version history;
- clarification workflow;
- qualification stages;
- Request grouping;
- matching triggers;
- feedback and rejection reasons;
- expiry and withdrawal;
- audit and correction;
- Workplace-private demand memory.

The Request remains a Lite demand object. It does not become an Order, Engagement or formal transaction state without the required transitions.

## 22. Commercial implications

Governed Requests can support:

- subscription value;
- private matching;
- network brokerage;
- buyer-side advisory services;
- paid professional shortlisting;
- Listing promotion;
- MarkReg due diligence and transfer support;
- demand-led supply recruitment.

Commercial incentives must not encourage inflated demand counts, fake requests or premature exposure of buyer identity.

The strongest demand asset is not a large request board. It is a smaller set of current, qualified and governed Requests that produce useful responses.

## 23. The operating principle

```text
Capture demand early
Preserve uncertainty
Protect the relationship
Ask only what changes the route
Control who may respond
Version the buyer’s changing intent
Do not confuse interest with commitment
```

> A Trademark Request should turn an incomplete business desire into a governable demand object without pretending that the buyer has already made decisions they have not made.
