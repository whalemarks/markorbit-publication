# Chapter 19 — Due Diligence Before Commercial Commitment

A trademark transaction becomes dangerous when commercial momentum outruns verification.

The buyer likes the name. The seller wants a deposit. The intermediary expects commission. Everyone wants to move quickly. Yet the most important facts may still be uncertain:

- Is the seller the current owner?
- Is the authorization valid?
- Is the mark active and transferable?
- Are there licenses, pledges, disputes or co-owners?
- Does the actual logo match the registered mark?
- Are the goods useful for the buyer’s plan?
- Is the transfer route legally and administratively possible?
- What exactly is included in the price?

Lite should therefore treat due diligence as a governed decision stage, not a document folder added after agreement.

```text
Transaction Opportunity
→ due-diligence scope
→ evidence collection
→ professional analysis
→ issue classification
→ remediation or pricing decision
→ proceed / modify / pause / reject
```

## 1. Due diligence is not one universal checklist

The required review depends on:

- jurisdiction;
- application or registration status;
- transaction value;
- number of classes and countries;
- owner type;
- intended buyer use;
- included brand assets;
- urgency;
- known disputes;
- transaction structure.

Lite may provide templates, but qualified professionals should determine the final scope where legal judgment is required.

## 2. Core review domains

### Identity and authority

Review may include:

- seller identity;
- recorded owner or applicant;
- corporate existence;
- signing authority;
- chain of title;
- Resale Authorization;
- co-owner consent;
- power of attorney;
- restrictions on intermediary authority.

### Legal status

Review may include:

- official current status;
- deadlines and maintenance requirements;
- pending office actions;
- oppositions, cancellations or litigation;
- non-use vulnerability;
- limitations, disclaimers or consent agreements;
- territorial scope;
- class and goods wording.

### Asset scope

The parties should identify whether the transaction includes:

- the trademark right;
- pending applications;
- related registrations;
- logos and design files;
- domain names;
- social accounts;
- packaging and content;
- customer or supplier data;
- copyright or design rights;
- goodwill where required or intended;
- physical products or inventory.

A trademark price does not automatically include every associated brand asset.

### Commercial and operational fit

Review may consider:

- buyer’s intended products;
- useful goods coverage;
- need for additional filings;
- required redesign;
- language or cultural issues;
- transfer timing;
- rebranding cost;
- recordal and tax cost;
- seller transition obligations.

## 3. Evidence should retain provenance

Each material finding should record:

- source;
- document or registry reference;
- date checked;
- jurisdiction;
- responsible reviewer;
- confidence;
- conflict with other sources;
- expiry or refresh trigger;
- effect on the transaction.

```text
Document uploaded
≠ document verified

Official-looking document
≠ current authoritative evidence
```

AI extraction may assist, but the source document and review status should remain visible.

## 4. Findings should be classified by consequence

A practical classification may include:

```text
Confirmed
Acceptable with disclosure
Remediable before completion
Requires price or term adjustment
Requires professional opinion
Blocking until resolved
Unknown because evidence unavailable
```

This is more useful than one “risk score.”

A missing high-resolution logo file may be remediable. An unresolved ownership conflict may block the transaction. Narrow goods coverage may be acceptable if the buyer understands the need for additional filings.

## 5. Unknown is not automatically failure

Some evidence may not be available immediately. Lite should preserve Unknown and show:

- why the information is missing;
- who should obtain it;
- when it is needed;
- whether the transaction may proceed conditionally;
- what risk the buyer accepts;
- whether escrow, holdback or condition precedent is appropriate.

The system should not transform missing evidence into a positive assumption.

## 6. Due diligence and legal clearance are distinct

Reviewing the purchased trademark does not necessarily determine whether the buyer can safely use or expand it in every market.

```text
Asset due diligence
≠ full clearance search
≠ freedom-to-operate conclusion
≠ guarantee of enforceability
```

Additional search, filing or local legal advice may be needed.

## 7. Seller representations and independent verification

A seller may state that:

- the mark is valid;
- no dispute exists;
- all fees are paid;
- the seller owns every included asset;
- no license or security interest exists.

These statements may become contractual representations, but they should not automatically replace independent verification.

Lite should distinguish:

```text
Seller statement
Professional finding
Official-source fact
Contractual representation
Warranty
Condition precedent
```

## 8. Versioned due-diligence room

A due-diligence room should control:

- participant access;
- document categories;
- download and onward-sharing;
- watermarking where appropriate;
- document versions;
- questions and responses;
- reviewer assignments;
- issue register;
- expiry and revocation;
- accepted disclosure package.

Access to the room does not transfer ownership or authorize use of the mark.

## 9. Confidentiality and privacy

Documents may contain:

- identity records;
- signatures;
- addresses;
- company records;
- private agreements;
- customer or supplier information;
- bank details;
- unpublished product plans.

Lite should use minimum necessary access and avoid exposing the entire Workplace or Matter to every participant.

## 10. AI assistance and limits

AI may assist with:

- document classification;
- field extraction;
- source comparison;
- missing-document detection;
- deadline identification;
- issue summaries;
- question drafting;
- translation;
- inconsistency detection;
- change tracking.

AI should not:

- authenticate signatures conclusively;
- decide title ownership from incomplete evidence;
- issue unqualified legal conclusions;
- suppress conflicting sources;
- mark a blocking issue resolved without evidence;
- approve the transaction;
- mutate official or contractual state.

## 11. Decision gates

Due diligence should lead to explicit decisions:

```text
Proceed unchanged
Proceed with revised price
Proceed with conditions precedent
Proceed with holdback or staged payment
Require remediation
Pause for further evidence
Change transaction scope
Reject
```

The decision should record who made it and in which represented role.

## 12. Example

A buyer intends to acquire a US word mark for clothing. Review shows:

- the official owner matches the seller;
- the registration is active;
- one maintenance deadline is approaching;
- the seller’s marketing materials use a substantially different logo;
- the domain name is owned by another company;
- the goods cover clothing but not retail services;
- no independent clearance for the buyer’s new product line has been completed.

The Opportunity may proceed with:

- maintenance handled before or immediately after closing;
- the domain excluded unless separately acquired;
- the registered word mark clearly separated from the commercial logo;
- additional filing and clearance advice disclosed;
- price and responsibility allocated in the agreement.

## 13. Failure modes

Due diligence fails when:

- it begins only after payment;
- a generic checklist replaces jurisdictional judgment;
- uploaded documents are treated as verified;
- seller statements become official facts;
- one score hides blocking issues;
- all participants receive unrestricted access;
- corrected documents do not supersede old versions;
- AI conclusions are presented as legal opinions;
- the buyer is told that acquisition guarantees safe use;
- conditions and remediation are not connected to agreement or payment milestones.

## 14. Product and technical implications

Lite and the receiving Products need:

- scoped due-diligence plans;
- evidence provenance;
- controlled data rooms;
- document versions;
- reviewer assignments;
- issue and remediation registers;
- decision gates;
- typed findings;
- conditions and milestone linkage;
- correction and audit history;
- Handoff to MarkReg or qualified professionals.

Lite may coordinate and present the process, while the relevant Owning Service and professional authorities retain formal legal and transaction state.

## 15. Commercial implications

Due diligence creates legitimate value through:

- professional review fees;
- transaction support;
- document preparation;
- recordal services;
- additional filing work;
- risk reduction;
- reduced post-closing disputes.

Commercial incentives must not pressure reviewers to minimize risks merely to preserve the deal.

## 16. The operating principle

> Commercial enthusiasm may justify faster verification, but never the conversion of unverified assumptions into ownership, availability or transfer facts.
