# Part VII — Capability Center and Contribution

## Chapter 69 — Capability should be visible in daily work

The Capability Center should not feel like a separate school disconnected from business.

When a user encounters a task, Lite should be able to show:

- the relevant Capability Unit;
- current user level;
- available AI assistance;
- required review;
- missing prerequisite;
- simulation path;
- production-authorization limit;
- related paid work opportunities.

## Chapter 70 — My Capability Map

A user should see capability by domain and level:

```text
L1 awareness
L2 assisted practice
L3 independent practice
L4 advanced review
L5 expert or mentor
```

The view should include evidence, recency, jurisdiction, restrictions and recommended next steps.

## Chapter 71 — Learning in the workflow

Lite may switch to guided mode when the user lacks sufficient capability.

Guided mode may provide:

- step explanation;
- source retrieval;
- checklists;
- example outputs;
- AI drafts;
- mandatory review;
- simulation recommendation.

The goal is to complete the immediate work safely while developing the user.

## Chapter 72 — Simulation entry

Lite may provide access to de-identified and synthetic cases for:

- trademark commerce;
- buyer-request matching;
- content and visual review;
- United States maintenance;
- customer communication;
- data verification;
- transaction due diligence.

Simulation occurs in an isolated Workplace with no real filing, payment or external communication.

## Chapter 73 — Certification paths

Early Lite-oriented paths may include:

- Trademark Content Growth Practitioner;
- Trademark Commerce Advisor;
- Search and Data Verification Practitioner;
- Trademark Visual Optimization Reviewer;
- United States Maintenance Advisor.

Certification proves capability under a defined MarkOrbit standard. It does not create legal qualification.

## Chapter 74 — Production authorization

A certified user does not automatically gain access to real tasks.

Production authorization may require:

- identity verification;
- confidentiality agreement;
- conflict policy;
- successful simulation;
- shadow work;
- supervised tasks;
- quality history;
- current capability evidence.

## Chapter 75 — Paid task discovery

Qualified users may receive bounded tasks such as:

- official-status verification;
- data anomaly review;
- search-result screening;
- AI image and text review;
- trademark continuity assessment;
- content review;
- request matching opinion;
- simulation assessment;
- MarkReg production support.

Task eligibility is distinct from product subscription.

## Chapter 76 — Task offer

A task offer should state:

- Capability ID and minimum level;
- risk tier;
- data scope;
- expected output;
- deadline;
- review requirement;
- compensation;
- revision policy;
- prohibited actions;
- represented Workplace and engagement context.

## Chapter 77 — Scoped Task Workspace

A contributor should receive only the information necessary for the assignment.

The workspace may hide:

- unrelated customer records;
- internal margin;
- full contact information;
- other trademarks;
- payment data;
- irrelevant documents.

Access expires after task completion or cancellation.

## Chapter 78 — Contribution and review

The task lifecycle is:

```text
offer
→ acceptance
→ scoped access
→ contribution
→ automated checks
→ professional review
→ accept, revise or reject
→ compensation
→ capability evidence
```

A rejected contribution should preserve the reason and appeal path.

## Chapter 79 — Professional review marketplace must remain governed

Lite may help users request expert review, but should not become an unrestricted opinion bazaar.

Review matching should consider:

- subject capability;
- jurisdiction;
- level;
- reviewer calibration;
- conflict;
- response time;
- prior quality;
- language.

## Chapter 80 — Examiners need their own capability

Simulation assessors and production reviewers require:

- L4 or L5 subject capability;
- rubric training;
- calibration;
- conflict restrictions;
- feedback quality;
- leakage prevention;
- consistency monitoring.

Reviewer authority must be reviewable.

## Chapter 81 — Contribution evidence

A contribution record should include:

- assignment context;
- submitted artifact;
- assistance mode;
- review result;
- revisions;
- time and help required;
- later outcome where available;
- rights and reuse permission.

## Chapter 82 — Compensation and growth

Paid contribution should create both income and progression.

```text
complete bounded work
→ earn compensation
→ gain verified evidence
→ expand authorization
→ access higher-value work
→ become reviewer or mentor
```

The platform must avoid using certification merely to create a low-cost labor pool.

## Chapter 83 — Capability supply and demand

Lite should show the platform which capabilities are:

- oversupplied;
- scarce;
- blocked by reviewer shortage;
- outdated after rule change;
- commercially valuable;
- frequently requested but poorly taught.

Training recommendations should respond to real demand rather than course-sales pressure.

## Chapter 84 — Contribution can improve the platform carefully

Accepted contributions may produce candidates for shared learning, but only through rights and review.

```text
contribution
→ attributed practice candidate
→ validation or outcome evidence
→ governed knowledge proposal
```

Private Workplace work does not automatically become shared training material.
