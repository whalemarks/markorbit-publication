# Part V — Inquiry, Transaction and Transfer

## Chapter 43 — Inquiry is a relationship event

An inquiry is not merely a message attached to a listing.

Lite should preserve:

- source content or campaign;
- buyer context;
- represented Workplace;
- requested trademark;
- Relationship Owner;
- communication history;
- confidentiality;
- commercial status;
- next action.

This provenance determines later commission and relationship questions.

## Chapter 44 — From inquiry to Transaction Opportunity

An inquiry becomes a Transaction Opportunity when there is enough evidence of fit to justify coordinated commercial work.

The opportunity should record:

- buyer and seller roles;
- participating Workplaces;
- candidate asset;
- proposed price range;
- commission structure;
- required verification;
- due-diligence scope;
- transfer route;
- probability and blockers;
- expiry and reservation.

## Chapter 45 — Engagement before sensitive disclosure

Before exposing owner identity, confidential price floors, buyer contact or authorization evidence, participants should form an Engagement.

The Engagement defines:

- collaboration mode;
- role allocation;
- permitted disclosure;
- contact policy;
- commercial terms;
- non-circumvention expectations;
- duration and termination;
- dispute path.

## Chapter 46 — Transaction collaboration modes

Lite may support:

- direct owner sale;
- reseller-led sale;
- co-brokerage;
- referral;
- Workplace-retained white-label transaction support;
- MarkReg-assisted transfer;
- qualified external legal support.

The user interface should express the commercial difference clearly.

## Chapter 47 — Buyer presentation

A professional buyer presentation may contain:

- verified trademark facts;
- goods and classes;
- jurisdiction and status;
- price and transaction conditions;
- brand concept and product fit;
- known risks;
- missing verification;
- recommended next step.

Concept material must not be presented as existing market use.

## Chapter 48 — Negotiation memory

Lite should record offers, counteroffers and conditions without confusing discussion with binding agreement.

```text
suggested price
≠ seller authority

buyer expression of interest
≠ accepted offer

reserved status
≠ completed transfer
```

## Chapter 49 — Due diligence

Due diligence may verify:

- current official status;
- ownership and chain of title;
- authorization to sell;
- recorded encumbrances where discoverable;
- pending disputes or limitations;
- goods and jurisdiction scope;
- relevant use or non-use concerns;
- transfer eligibility;
- required documents and signatures.

Lite may organize the request, but formal professional conclusions may require MarkReg or qualified providers.

## Chapter 50 — Payment architecture

The product must distinguish:

- buyer payment obligation;
- seller proceeds;
- commissions;
- transfer service fees;
- taxes and external costs;
- platform service fees;
- payment milestones;
- refund or failure conditions.

Initial product stages should avoid broad payment custody until legal, operational and dispute controls are mature.

## Chapter 51 — Transfer Handoff

When a transaction requires formal transfer work, Lite creates a governed Handoff to MarkReg or an authorized service route.

The Handoff should preserve:

- source opportunity;
- parties and roles;
- agreed commercial terms;
- trademark identity;
- due-diligence record;
- document requirements;
- customer relationship constraints;
- expected Return.

## Chapter 52 — Transaction completion

A transaction should not be marked complete solely because money was discussed or documents were sent.

Completion evidence may include:

- executed agreement;
- payment confirmation;
- signed transfer documents;
- official recordal receipt;
- accepted ownership change where applicable;
- final commission settlement;
- buyer and seller delivery acknowledgment.

The product should represent intermediate and unknown states.

## Chapter 53 — Failed transactions remain valuable evidence

Lite should preserve structured failure reasons:

- seller withdrew;
- buyer changed direction;
- authorization invalid;
- status problem;
- price disagreement;
- due-diligence concern;
- response delay;
- competing sale;
- transfer restriction.

Failure evidence improves pricing, inventory quality and future matching.

## Chapter 54 — Commission claims

Where several parties contribute, Lite should record the basis of each claim:

- who supplied the asset;
- who originated the buyer;
- who maintained the relationship;
- who created the qualifying introduction;
- who performed transaction work;
- which terms were accepted before work began.

The system should not infer commission solely from message participation.

## Chapter 55 — Trust after completion

A successful transaction produces more than revenue.

It creates evidence about:

- seller responsiveness;
- listing accuracy;
- buyer seriousness;
- contributor quality;
- transfer route performance;
- time and cost;
- dispute frequency.

Only appropriate, rights-respecting summaries should contribute to shared platform trust.
