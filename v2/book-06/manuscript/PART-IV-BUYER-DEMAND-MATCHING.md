# Part IV — Buyer Demand and Matching

## Chapter 31 — Demand should be structured early

A buyer rarely begins with a perfect trademark specification.

The request may be:

- “I need a beauty brand”;
- “find something close to this Chinese name”;
- “Class 25, budget below a certain amount”;
- “I need a mark that can work in the United States and China”;
- “the customer wants a premium European feeling.”

Lite should convert this conversation into a structured Trademark Request without forcing the user to complete a complicated form.

## Chapter 32 — Trademark Request

A request may contain:

- name direction and keywords;
- language and pronunciation preference;
- industry and target audience;
- goods and services;
- Nice classes;
- jurisdiction;
- mark type;
- acceptable semantic or visual variation;
- budget;
- timeline;
- need for an existing registration or new filing;
- need for brand concepts, due diligence or transfer service;
- expiry and confidentiality level.

## Chapter 33 — AI-assisted clarification

Brain may infer missing structure and ask only high-value questions.

Instead of presenting a long questionnaire, Lite may offer choices:

```text
A. Exact or near-exact name priority
B. Industry fit priority
C. Registered-right scope priority
D. Budget and speed priority
```

The user confirms, corrects or rejects the inference.

## Chapter 34 — Request ownership

A request belongs to a Workplace business context.

The system should record:

- Demand Originator;
- Relationship Owner;
- represented buyer context;
- confidentiality;
- permitted matching pools;
- whether other Workplaces may respond;
- commission and contact conditions.

Publishing a request does not transfer the buyer relationship.

## Chapter 35 — Candidate generation

Candidate generation may search:

- the Workplace's own inventory;
- customer-authorized inventory;
- selected partner inventory;
- network inventory;
- public platform listings;
- official registration data where a purchase route may later be established.

Only assets with a viable commercial path should be presented as transaction-ready.

## Chapter 36 — Matching dimensions

Matching should consider:

- name and semantic fit;
- pronunciation;
- visual impression;
- goods and industry fit;
- class and jurisdiction;
- legal status;
- authorization and availability;
- price;
- seller restrictions;
- brand potential;
- transfer complexity;
- prior buyer feedback.

No single score should hide important trade-offs.

## Chapter 37 — Explainable recommendations

A recommendation should state:

```text
why it matches
where it differs
what remains unverified
what commercial terms apply
what professional review is recommended
```

A short list of well-explained candidates is more useful than hundreds of unranked records.

## Chapter 38 — Human judgment in matching

AI can retrieve and compare at scale. Professionals contribute context:

- whether the buyer's stated preference reflects the real business need;
- whether a name feels appropriate in the target market;
- whether goods coverage is practically useful;
- whether a visually appealing mark creates legal or commercial problems;
- whether the asking price is realistic for this buyer.

Professional matching opinions may become paid contributions where appropriate.

## Chapter 39 — Negative matching is valuable

Lite should also explain why a candidate should not be recommended.

Common reasons include:

- wrong jurisdiction;
- insufficient goods coverage;
- status uncertainty;
- authorization expired;
- name meaning conflicts with the buyer's market;
- price outside range;
- excessive transfer complexity;
- high rebranding cost.

Recording rejection reasons improves future matching.

## Chapter 40 — Request response workflow

A response may move through:

```text
request received
→ clarified
→ candidate search
→ professional shortlist
→ buyer presentation
→ feedback
→ inquiry
→ transaction opportunity or closure
```

The request remains active until fulfilled, expired, withdrawn or intentionally closed.

## Chapter 41 — Private demand networks

Some Workplaces will not publish buyer demand openly.

Lite should support trusted circles in which selected agencies or asset owners may respond under agreed rules. This can create useful liquidity before a fully public market exists.

## Chapter 42 — Demand quality metrics

Lite should measure:

- time to clarification;
- number of viable candidates;
- buyer response rate;
- shortlist-to-inquiry conversion;
- reasons for failure;
- fulfilled requests;
- repeated demand patterns;
- inventory gaps.

These signals inform what supply the network should recruit or prepare.
