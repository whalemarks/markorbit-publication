# Part III — Trademark Inventory and Supply

## Chapter 19 — A trademark record is not inventory

A legal trademark record describes a right, application or registration. It does not by itself prove that a Workplace may sell, promote or recommend that asset.

Lite should distinguish:

```text
Trademark
Trademark Inventory
Resale Authorization
Trademark Listing
```

The separation prevents legal facts, commercial rights and marketing projections from collapsing into one object.

## Chapter 20 — Sources of inventory

A Workplace may manage:

- trademarks it owns;
- trademarks owned by customers under documented instruction;
- entrusted resale assets;
- authorized shared-network listings;
- platform or MarkReg inventory;
- private candidate assets not yet listed.

Each source requires different permissions and disclosure rules.

## Chapter 21 — Resale Authorization

A sellable asset should record:

- granting party;
- authority basis;
- exclusivity;
- permitted jurisdictions and channels;
- minimum or target price;
- commission rules;
- authority to negotiate;
- authority to disclose owner identity;
- term and expiry;
- evidence and update obligation;
- cancellation conditions.

No listing should remain active indefinitely without revalidation.

## Chapter 22 — Inventory quality

Lite should assign inventory-quality signals based on:

- status freshness;
- ownership confidence;
- authorization validity;
- goods and class clarity;
- price availability;
- media completeness;
- transaction restrictions;
- response history.

A large inventory with weak evidence is less valuable than a smaller trusted inventory.

## Chapter 23 — Listing is a projection

A Listing is a purpose-limited sales projection.

It may contain:

- selected legal facts;
- asking price or inquiry rule;
- commission statement;
- brand concept;
- media;
- suitable industries;
- seller context;
- availability status;
- contact and routing rules.

The listing does not transfer ownership or customer relationship.

## Chapter 24 — Visibility modes

Lite should support:

```text
Private
Selected Workplaces
Authorized Network
Public
```

A Workplace may keep price, owner identity or authorization evidence hidden while exposing enough information to test demand.

## Chapter 25 — Duplicate listings and provenance

The same trademark may appear through several resellers.

Lite should not silently merge commercial rights. It should recognize:

- the underlying trademark identity;
- each authorization chain;
- each listing source;
- exclusivity conflicts;
- differing price and commission terms;
- prior buyer introduction.

A duplicate record problem is different from multiple legitimate commercial projections.

## Chapter 26 — Inventory lifecycle

The inventory lifecycle may include:

```text
candidate
→ rights review
→ authorization pending
→ ready
→ listed
→ inquiry active
→ reserved
→ transaction pending
→ sold or withdrawn
→ archived
```

Legal-status change may suspend a listing without deleting historical activity.

## Chapter 27 — Pricing

Lite may assist with price presentation using:

- owner expectation;
- registration scope;
- jurisdiction;
- classes and goods;
- age and status;
- linguistic and brand potential;
- market demand;
- comparable internal transactions;
- transfer cost.

A suggested price remains an estimate, not an objective valuation fact.

## Chapter 28 — Commission logic

The system should distinguish:

- owner net price;
- public asking price;
- permitted discount;
- sourcing commission;
- buyer-side service fee;
- co-broker share;
- transfer service fee;
- platform fee.

Commercial terms must be visible to authorized participants before they invest in promotion.

## Chapter 29 — Inventory as a marketing engine

Each verified asset can generate:

- brand concepts;
- product collections;
- industry-specific recommendations;
- social content;
- buyer proposals;
- thematic catalogues;
- campaign pages.

Inventory therefore becomes both supply and content source.

## Chapter 30 — Supply without demand is not liquidity

Lite should not celebrate listing count alone.

The stronger metrics are:

- percentage of listings recently verified;
- request-to-candidate coverage;
- qualified inquiry rate;
- time to first relevant demand;
- transaction progression;
- seller response time;
- failed-match reasons.
