# Part VIII — Business Model, Governance and Roadmap

## Chapter 85 — Lite must charge for operating value

Lite should not charge merely because an AI model was invoked.

Subscription value comes from the combined operating system:

- daily priorities;
- content creation;
- trademark inventory;
- buyer demand;
- matching;
- inquiry and transaction pipeline;
- service opportunities;
- customer memory;
- capability growth;
- collaboration and handoff.

The user pays for a stronger business operating capability, not isolated generations.

## Chapter 86 — Free, Pro and Business

A possible edition structure is:

### Free

- limited personal Workplace;
- basic inventory;
- limited public browsing;
- selected templates;
- basic capability map;
- limited request participation.

### Pro

- full content workflow;
- larger inventory and authorized resale;
- request creation and matching;
- inquiry and opportunity management;
- customer and opportunity context;
- capability learning and simulation access;
- professional review options.

### Business

- multi-member Workplace;
- private inventory network;
- role and permission control;
- team opportunity allocation;
- white-label brand settings;
- commission and collaboration records;
- organization capability analytics;
- advanced audit and data controls.

The exact limits should follow cost and user value, not arbitrary obstruction.

## Chapter 87 — Revenue beyond subscription

Separate revenue may arise from:

- trademark transaction commissions;
- transfer support;
- professional review;
- training and certification;
- paid simulation;
- human contribution;
- MarkReg white-label delivery;
- MGSN managed fulfillment;
- optional Sites and public promotion.

Revenue sources must state the service and responsibility clearly.

## Chapter 88 — Reasonable use rather than token anxiety

Ordinary Capability use within the subscribed edition should not be metered per invocation.

High-cost operations such as large image batches, long video rendering or premium third-party data may use:

- reasonable-use policy;
- queue priority;
- output quality tiers;
- enterprise resource agreements;
- explicit high-cost confirmation.

## Chapter 89 — Product boundaries

Lite discovers, grows and organizes opportunity.

MarkReg performs full lifecycle delivery where selected.

Sites publishes controlled external projections.

MGSN fulfills managed external professional-service needs.

Core defines shared meaning. Owning Services control formal business state. Execution governs handoffs and protected actions.

## Chapter 90 — Lite and MarkReg

A Lite opportunity may become a MarkReg service without transferring the customer relationship automatically.

The Handoff must preserve:

- originating Workplace;
- Relationship Owner;
- approved service scope;
- delivery brand;
- communication route;
- commercial arrangement;
- expected Return.

## Chapter 91 — Lite and Sites

Sites may publish:

- selected content;
- public listings;
- service pages;
- cases and FAQs;
- intake projections.

Sites does not become authority for Customer, Order, Payment or professional decisions.

## Chapter 92 — Lite and MGSN

Lite may initiate a Capability Need through the Workplace's MGSN Connection.

It should not expose an unrestricted provider directory or silently appoint a provider. Matching, acceptance, instruction and Return remain governed.

## Chapter 93 — Customer and relationship governance

Lite must show users that their customer relationship is protected by architecture, not just promises.

Controls include:

- Relationship Owner;
- minimum necessary disclosure;
- engagement-specific communication rights;
- source attribution;
- scoped provider and contributor views;
- relationship history;
- access expiry;
- dispute records.

## Chapter 94 — Marketplace quality governance

The trademark market should control:

- stale inventory;
- unsupported ownership claims;
- expired authorization;
- misleading concept imagery;
- duplicate commercial projections;
- commission ambiguity;
- fabricated buyer demand;
- spam responses;
- manipulated ranking.

## Chapter 95 — Ranking principles

Search and recommendation should consider relevance, evidence quality, freshness and commercial fit.

Paid promotion, where allowed, must be visibly separated from organic matching and must not hide legal-status or authorization problems.

## Chapter 96 — Safety and professional boundaries

Lite should never imply that:

- AI output is official legal advice by itself;
- platform certification is a legal qualification;
- a listing guarantees transferability;
- a transaction concept proves business use;
- a service opportunity guarantees customer need;
- a provider is appointed before acceptance.

## Chapter 97 — Product health metrics

Lite should monitor:

- active Workplaces;
- meaningful weekly actions;
- publish-to-inquiry conversion;
- verified inventory;
- buyer requests;
- qualified matches;
- inquiry progression;
- transaction completion;
- service-opportunity conversion;
- capability progression;
- contributor quality;
- customer retention;
- subscription retention.

## Chapter 98 — Phase 1: prove the dual core

The first product stage should prove:

```text
content and trademark brand presentation
+ inventory and resale authorization
+ buyer request and matching
+ inquiry and transaction opportunity
```

A large public marketplace is not required for this proof.

## Chapter 99 — Phase 2: controlled transaction collaboration

The second stage adds:

- Engagement;
- commission records;
- due-diligence handoff;
- transfer support;
- selected partner networks;
- stronger customer and team workflows.

## Chapter 100 — Phase 3: business opportunities and capability work

The third stage expands:

- maintenance and filing opportunities;
- simulation and certification;
- paid contribution;
- MarkReg white-label production;
- organization capability analytics.

## Chapter 101 — Phase 4: external growth and liquidity

After inventory quality and transaction workflows are proven, Lite may expand:

- public marketplace reach;
- Sites integration;
- broader buyer acquisition;
- cross-Workplace networks;
- advanced transaction support;
- deeper MGSN routes.

## Chapter 102 — What Lite should refuse to become

Lite should refuse to become:

- a generic social-content generator;
- a mass stale-listing warehouse;
- an open customer-lead auction;
- an uncontrolled gig platform;
- a smaller copy of MarkReg;
- a product whose retention depends on unused credits;
- a feature collection without a daily economic loop.

## Chapter 103 — Ten-year product lock

Lite must remain close to the daily economics of trademark professionals.

Every major feature should improve at least one of:

```text
visibility
supply
demand
matching
conversion
customer retention
service opportunity
capability
collaboration
```

A feature that improves none of these should not dominate the Lite roadmap.
