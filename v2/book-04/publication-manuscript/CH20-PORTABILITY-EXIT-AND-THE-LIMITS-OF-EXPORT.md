# Chapter 20 — Portability, Exit and the Limits of Export

A Workplace is not sovereign if it can enter a platform but cannot leave it. Portability is therefore not an optional convenience. It is part of the trust architecture.

At the same time, portability does not mean that every capability, ranking model, provider relationship or shared platform asset becomes the property of the departing Workplace. MarkOrbit must support meaningful exit without pretending that all connected value has a single owner.

## 1. What a Workplace should be able to take

Subject to applicable rights and obligations, a Workplace should be able to export its concrete business records, including:

- customer and contact records it is authorized to hold;
- Matters, Orders and Engagement references;
- documents and evidence;
- private notes and private Knowledge;
- pricing and commercial history;
- customer approvals and communications;
- owned or authorized trademark asset records;
- Contributions and Outcomes it has rights to retain;
- Product configuration and role mappings;
- audit records necessary for accountability;
- provider and collaboration history within lawful limits.

The export should preserve identifiers, timestamps, provenance and relationships where possible. A folder of disconnected PDFs is not meaningful portability.

## 2. Export is not universal ownership

A Workplace may have lawful access to information that it does not own and cannot freely reuse.

Examples include:

- official registry data subject to source terms;
- provider credentials disclosed for a specific Engagement;
- another Workplace's private pricing;
- licensed Knowledge;
- platform-generated rankings;
- customer data held only for a limited purpose;
- confidential reviewer comments;
- third-party copyrighted materials.

```text
Was visible in the Workplace
≠ freely exportable
≠ freely reusable
```

The export system should identify restrictions rather than silently omitting or over-transferring protected material.

## 3. Platform assets that do not transfer

A departing Workplace does not automatically acquire:

- Core semantic ownership;
- shared Capability code;
- platform routing models;
- MGSN provider pools;
- other Workplaces' private data;
- shared Knowledge without an applicable license;
- platform-wide performance benchmarks;
- Product source code;
- credentials issued under platform governance;
- payment or identity infrastructure.

The Workplace may retain references, evidence and results generated through those assets, but not the assets themselves unless separately licensed or transferred.

## 4. Product configuration portability

Configuration has several layers.

A Workplace should generally be able to export:

- its role assignments;
- enabled workflows and preferences;
- custom fields;
- private templates;
- notification rules;
- connector mappings;
- private classification or content settings.

It may not be able to export executable Product logic, proprietary orchestration or shared Capability implementations. A useful export should distinguish portable configuration from non-portable platform functionality.

## 5. Exit must not destroy active professional work

A Workplace may decide to leave while Matters, deadlines, Engagements or provider instructions remain active. Immediate deletion could harm customers and professional obligations.

A controlled exit should include:

- inventory of active obligations;
- deadline and risk review;
- customer notification where required;
- reassignment or handover plan;
- provider and contributor closure;
- settlement status;
- document and evidence transfer;
- retention obligations;
- revocation of continuing access;
- final export and verification.

Commercial termination does not erase professional duties already undertaken.

## 6. Member departure is not Workplace exit

When a Person leaves an Organization, the Workplace continues. The departing Person may retain personal identity, public credentials and portable capability evidence, but not the Organization's customer records or private operating memory.

The system must separate:

```text
Person portability
≠ Workplace portability
≠ Organization dissolution
```

Historical records should continue to show who acted, while current access and representation are revoked.

## 7. Customer-directed transfer

A customer may instruct that a Matter or portfolio move to another agent or Workplace. Customer choice may override commercial non-circumvention expectations, but the transfer still requires governed handling.

The process should address:

- customer identity and authority;
- scope of transfer;
- documents and evidence;
- official representative changes;
- unpaid fees or lawful lien questions;
- deadlines;
- privilege and confidentiality;
- future communication rights;
- closure of the prior Engagement.

The platform should not use relationship protection to obstruct a lawful customer decision.

## 8. Data deletion and retention

Exit does not always permit immediate deletion. Records may need to be retained for:

- legal or regulatory duties;
- professional responsibility;
- financial and tax records;
- dispute preservation;
- security and audit evidence;
- official filing history.

Retention should be purpose-specific and access-restricted. “Retained” must not mean “available for future marketing or product training.”

A retention schedule should identify what remains, why, for how long and who may access it.

## 9. Verification of export

The Workplace should be able to verify that an export is complete enough for its declared purpose.

Useful mechanisms include:

- manifest of included objects;
- excluded-item report with reasons;
- checksums;
- relationship maps;
- schema documentation;
- document inventory;
- evidence and provenance references;
- import validation into another system where supported.

A screenshot or manual report is not a substitute for structured portability.

## 10. Avoiding hostage economics

Platforms often create retention through switching cost rather than continuing value. MarkOrbit should not depend on inaccessible data, hidden export fees or deliberately incompatible formats to keep Workplaces.

Reasonable charges may exist for exceptional migration work, but ordinary structured export should be a product capability, not a negotiation under pressure.

The stronger long-term position is:

> Workplaces remain because the system continues to improve their work, not because leaving would destroy their business memory.

## 11. Failure modes

Portability fails when:

- exports omit relationships and provenance;
- private third-party data is over-exported;
- active deadlines are abandoned during termination;
- a departing member receives organizational data;
- retention data is reused for unrelated purposes;
- the platform claims ownership over customer-created records;
- customer choice is blocked by commercial policy;
- credentials are exported without their governing status or expiry;
- deletion claims ignore backups and subprocessors.

## 12. Product implications

Products should provide:

- export scope selection;
- rights and restriction review;
- active-obligation checklist;
- structured data plus human-readable archives;
- exclusion explanations;
- handover workflow;
- retention and deletion status;
- revocation of connectors, sessions and Projection links;
- evidence that exit was completed.

## 13. The operating principle

Portability protects Workplace independence, while export limits protect the rights of customers, providers, contributors and the platform itself.

> A trustworthy operating system lets each Workplace leave with its own business memory and accountable records, but not with authority or assets that never belonged to it.
