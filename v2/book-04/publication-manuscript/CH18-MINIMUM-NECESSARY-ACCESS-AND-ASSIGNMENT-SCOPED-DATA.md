# Chapter 18 — Minimum Necessary Access and Assignment-scoped Data

Professional collaboration cannot operate on trust alone. A participant may be reliable and still receive too much information because the system grants access at the Workplace, folder or Matter level when the real unit of work is narrower.

MarkOrbit therefore treats access as purpose-specific and, where execution is involved, Assignment-scoped.

## 1. The wrong default: access first, responsibility later

Many systems invite an external user into a project and then depend on human discipline to avoid unrelated records. That model fails because it grants broad visibility before the participant's actual responsibility has been defined.

The safer sequence is:

```text
Engagement
→ Work Package
→ Assignment
→ Data Scope
→ Authorized Action
→ Contribution or Return
```

The Assignment provides the reason for access. Access does not create the Assignment.

## 2. Assignment-scoped access

An Assignment should identify:

- assignee Person and represented Workplace;
- source Engagement, Order or Matter;
- Work Package and target Outcome;
- allowed Capability;
- required Production Authorization or qualification;
- permitted objects, fields and documents;
- permitted actions;
- start, expiry and revocation conditions;
- evidence and reporting duties;
- review and acceptance criteria.

The assignee should receive only the information needed to complete that bounded responsibility.

## 3. Access must follow the work, not organizational convenience

A contributor verifying use evidence may need a specimen, registration data and evaluation criteria. That contributor may not need invoices, unrelated customer matters, provider quotations or future marketing plans.

A local counsel preparing a filing may require broader access, including applicant identity, instructions and material legal facts. The greater access is justified by a different purpose and authority requirement, not by a higher status label.

```text
More senior participant
≠ automatic access to more unrelated data
```

## 4. Action rights are distinct from visibility

Permissions should separate at least:

- view;
- download;
- annotate;
- upload;
- propose change;
- submit Contribution;
- validate;
- approve;
- change formal state;
- disclose onward.

A contributor may submit a proposed classification without approving the filing. A provider may report that an application was filed without directly changing the formal Matter state. A customer may upload a POA without validating its legal sufficiency.

Typed actions make these distinctions visible.

## 5. Material completeness still matters

Minimum access must not withhold information necessary for competent performance. The Assignment package should include all material facts, known uncertainties and source evidence required for the assigned action.

A system that provides only selected facts favorable to the desired answer creates professional risk. The Delivery Owner and Owning Service must be able to detect when an Assignment package is incomplete.

Useful checks include:

- required-field completeness;
- document readiness;
- known conflict disclosure;
- deadline accuracy;
- identity and authority verification;
- unresolved inconsistencies;
- jurisdiction-specific prerequisites.

## 6. Temporal access and revocation

Access should have a lifecycle.

```text
Prepared
→ Active
→ Suspended
→ Expired
→ Revoked
→ Archived
```

When the Assignment ends, access should end unless another lawful purpose justifies retention. Revocation should address active sessions, links, exports and connected tools where technically possible.

Historical evidence that the participant had access may remain for audit, but historical entitlement must not become continuing visibility.

## 7. Reassignment and continuity

Professional work may need reassignment because of capacity, conflict, quality or departure. Reassignment should not simply add a second participant and leave the first participant's access unchanged.

A controlled reassignment should:

- pause or close the original Assignment;
- preserve submitted work and evidence;
- determine what the replacement must receive;
- revoke unnecessary prior access;
- disclose unresolved issues;
- record responsibility transition;
- notify affected parties where required.

This protects continuity without creating parallel hidden responsibility.

## 8. Download and offline risk

Once information is downloaded, technical revocation becomes incomplete. The system should therefore use proportionate controls:

- view-only presentation where practical;
- watermarking or recipient identification;
- limited download for sensitive packages;
- export logging;
- contractual reuse restrictions;
- retention obligations;
- incident reporting.

These controls do not guarantee compliance. They create evidence and reduce unnecessary exposure.

## 9. Connectors and delegated tools

An Assignment may use email, cloud storage, translation services, AI models or local filing systems. Access granted to a Person does not automatically authorize every external tool used by that Person.

The system should distinguish:

- human assignee access;
- Product connector access;
- subprocessors;
- AI processing;
- external professional systems;
- onward disclosure.

Material external processing should be governed by the Product Installation and Engagement policy.

## 10. Emergency access

Deadline recovery, security response or professional escalation may require temporary broader access. Emergency access should be:

- explicitly justified;
- limited in scope and duration;
- visible to an accountable reviewer;
- fully logged;
- reviewed after use;
- revoked promptly.

An emergency mechanism that is easier than normal authorization will become the normal mechanism.

## 11. Product implications

Products should generate access from responsibility rather than from ad hoc sharing. Interfaces should show:

- why access exists;
- which Assignment authorizes it;
- what actions are permitted;
- when it ends;
- who can expand it;
- which external tools receive the data;
- what evidence must be returned.

The system should warn when a participant can see information unrelated to any current responsibility.

## 12. The operating principle

Assignment-scoped data access aligns visibility with accountable work.

> A participant should receive enough information to perform the authorized responsibility competently, but no broader claim over the Workplace merely because collaboration requires temporary access.
