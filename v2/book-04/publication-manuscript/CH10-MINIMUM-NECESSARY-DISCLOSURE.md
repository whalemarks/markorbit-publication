# Chapter 10 — Minimum Necessary Disclosure

Projection becomes trustworthy only when disclosure is governed by purpose. The relevant question is not whether information may be shared in the abstract, but what information is necessary for this recipient, at this stage, to perform this specific action.

That is the principle of minimum necessary disclosure.

## 1. Minimum does not mean incomplete

The phrase can be misunderstood as a preference for secrecy. That would be dangerous in professional work. A local attorney cannot evaluate a filing instruction if material ownership facts are hidden. A reviewer cannot validate a declaration if the evidence set is incomplete. A buyer cannot complete due diligence if title defects are concealed.

Minimum necessary disclosure therefore has two simultaneous duties:

1. do not reveal information unrelated to the authorized purpose;
2. do not omit information necessary for competent, lawful or honest performance.

The first protects sovereignty and privacy. The second protects professional integrity and outcome quality.

## 2. Disclosure depends on stage

The same participant may legitimately receive different information at different stages.

### Discovery

A candidate may receive jurisdiction, Capability requirement, expected timing, language, broad matter type and conflict-screening indicators.

### Qualification

Additional credentials, service scope, capacity, pricing structure and conflict details may be exchanged.

### Recommendation

The originating Workplace may review identity, evidence, historical performance and material commercial conditions.

### Approval and appointment

The selected provider may receive customer identity, formal records, instruction authority and required documents.

### Execution

The provider receives the operational package necessary to perform the assigned work.

### Review and settlement

Outcome evidence, exceptions, invoices and acceptance records become visible to the parties authorized to validate and settle the engagement.

This staged model prevents the common mistake of granting full access during initial discovery.

## 3. Field-level and object-level control

Minimum necessary disclosure cannot be implemented only through folder permissions. It may require control at several levels:

- object selection;
- field selection;
- document selection;
- redaction;
- transformation;
- aggregation;
- time-limited access;
- action restrictions;
- downstream reuse restrictions.

For example, a Trademark Request may expose target jurisdiction, classes, preferred price range and desired characteristics while withholding the buyer's identity. A later due-diligence Projection may expose buyer identity and financial readiness to the authorized seller, but still omit unrelated customer records.

## 4. Relationship-sensitive disclosure

Customer information deserves special treatment because it carries both personal data and commercial relationship value.

A provider may need customer identity for conflict checks or formal representation. That does not create permission to market unrelated services. A contributor may need a redacted specimen to assess use. That does not create access to the full customer portfolio. A payment processor may need billing information. That does not create visibility into legal strategy.

The Projection should therefore express not only what may be seen, but why it may be seen and what reuse is prohibited.

## 5. Professional truth cannot be redacted away

A platform that optimizes only for privacy may create misleading professional instructions. Material facts must remain available when they affect eligibility, conflict, legal analysis, authority, deadline, evidence quality or risk.

Examples include:

- the true applicant or owner where identity affects filing;
- the actual registration date where a maintenance window depends on it;
- known adverse proceedings;
- limitations in the authorization to sell a trademark;
- missing originals where a jurisdiction requires them;
- uncertainty about chain of title;
- known conflicts between provided evidence and formal records.

Minimum necessary disclosure is not permission to produce a sanitized fiction.

## 6. Disclosure policies should be explainable

A user should be able to understand why a field is included, hidden or transformed. A recipient should know whether the information is authoritative, provisional, redacted or derived.

Useful labels include:

- source fact;
- user-provided statement;
- verified evidence;
- derived summary;
- redacted value;
- unresolved or Unknown;
- confidential commercial term;
- formal instruction.

This prevents a polished Projection from appearing more certain than its source.

## 7. AI and minimum disclosure

AI assistants create a special risk because broad context often improves output quality. That does not justify unrestricted access.

An AI function should receive only the data required for the declared task, under the policy of the relevant Product Installation and Workplace. Access for generation or retrieval does not imply consent for model training, cross-Workplace memory or unrelated reuse.

The system should record:

- the task purpose;
- the data scope supplied;
- whether data left the controlled environment;
- retention policy;
- model or service used;
- whether output requires human or professional review.

## 8. Exceptions and emergency access

Some situations require broader temporary access, such as deadline recovery, incident response or professional escalation. Emergency access must not become a hidden permanent privilege.

It should be:

- justified;
- time-limited;
- logged;
- reviewed afterward;
- revoked when the exception ends.

## 9. Product design implications

Every Projection flow should support:

- a disclosure preview;
- purpose selection;
- audience definition;
- field and document policy;
- expiry;
- recipient obligations;
- later-stage expansion of access;
- revocation and audit.

Defaults should be conservative, but the product must warn when the proposed package is insufficient for the intended professional action.

## 10. The operating principle

Minimum necessary disclosure is a balance between two failures: exposing too much and hiding too much.

> A trustworthy Workplace reveals no more than the purpose requires and no less than competent, lawful and honest performance demands.
