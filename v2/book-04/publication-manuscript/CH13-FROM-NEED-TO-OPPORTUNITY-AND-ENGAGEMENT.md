# Chapter 13 — From Need to Opportunity and Engagement

A Need is not yet a deal, a task or a professional mandate. It is an unresolved gap inside a Workplace: a missing capability, unavailable capacity, absent local authority, unmet demand, asset-liquidity problem or delivery dependency. Turning that gap into coordinated work requires several controlled transitions.

The industry often skips those transitions. An email asking for help is treated as an instruction. A provider who replies with a quotation is treated as appointed. A promising match is treated as an Opportunity. A payment request is treated as proof that the engagement exists. Those shortcuts create disputes about scope, authority, responsibility, price and customer ownership.

MarkOrbit therefore separates Need, Opportunity and Engagement.

## 1. Need remains inside the originating sovereignty boundary

A Need belongs first to the originating Workplace. It may arise from:

- a customer request;
- a deadline or lifecycle event;
- insufficient internal capacity;
- absence of a qualified professional;
- a commercial request for inventory or distribution;
- a quality-control requirement;
- a failed or delayed prior route;
- a strategic goal such as entering a new market.

At this stage, the Workplace may not yet know the correct solution. It may still need to determine:

- whether the Need is genuine;
- whether it can be solved internally;
- whether external professional authority is required;
- whether the customer has approved external participation;
- which disclosure level is appropriate;
- what budget and timing constraints apply;
- whether the Need should be paused, combined or abandoned.

A Need should therefore retain states such as Draft, Unverified, Clarifying, Internal Route Available, External Route Required, Paused and Closed.

## 2. Opportunity is a viable route, not merely demand

A Need becomes an Opportunity only when a plausible route has been identified and enough information exists for participants to evaluate it responsibly.

An Opportunity should identify:

- the originating Workplace;
- the source Need;
- the target Outcome;
- the proposed collaboration mode;
- the required Capability and authority;
- the proposed participants or participant class;
- the disclosure scope;
- material commercial assumptions;
- expected timing;
- known dependencies and risks;
- relationship constraints;
- expiry or decision date.

```text
Need
≠ Opportunity

Opportunity
≠ Engagement
```

A Need may generate several competing Opportunities. For example, a foreign filing matter may be routed through an existing provider, a new MGSN provider, a white-label MarkReg service or internal preparation plus local counsel. The originating Workplace should be able to compare routes before committing.

## 3. Route evaluation must be evidence-bearing

An Opportunity is stronger when route assumptions are explicit. A route may appear attractive because of low price but fail on capacity, qualification, communication quality, document requirements or customer constraints.

Evaluation may include:

- eligibility;
- professional qualification;
- Production Authorization;
- current Availability;
- expected turnaround;
- conflict status;
- commercial terms;
- historical evidence;
- language and jurisdiction fit;
- data and confidentiality policy;
- customer-facing communication policy;
- recovery and escalation capability.

A score or recommendation should expose the evidence and trade-offs behind it. It must not silently become an appointment.

## 4. Engagement begins with reciprocal acceptance

An Engagement exists only when the participating parties accept a bounded relationship.

At minimum, the Engagement should record:

- participating Workplaces and Persons;
- represented Organizations;
- engagement-specific roles;
- scope and exclusions;
- target Outcome;
- commercial terms and payment responsibility;
- authority and qualification requirements;
- disclosure rights;
- communication rights;
- customer relationship protections;
- evidence and reporting obligations;
- review and acceptance conditions;
- termination and recovery terms;
- effective period.

This acceptance may be represented by a contract, platform confirmation, formal instruction, signed power of attorney, purchase order or another valid mechanism. The exact legal form varies, but the semantic transition must be explicit.

## 5. Engagement is not execution

Even after an Engagement exists, execution may require further objects and approvals:

```text
Engagement
→ Order or Work Package
→ Assignment or Provider Appointment
→ Authorized Execution
→ Contribution / Return
→ Review
→ Outcome acceptance
```

An Engagement defines the relationship and operating conditions. An Assignment authorizes a bounded contributor to perform work. A Provider Appointment formally activates an external provider. An Order represents a commercial request. A Matter may remain the Owning Service's formal business-state object.

Collapsing these objects makes it impossible to identify who was responsible for what.

## 6. Customer approval may be a separate gate

The originating Workplace may have authority to explore routes but not to appoint an external provider, share customer identity or incur additional cost without customer approval.

The platform should therefore support distinct approvals for:

- route exploration;
- disclosure expansion;
- quotation acceptance;
- external provider appointment;
- formal filing or submission;
- material scope change;
- additional cost;
- settlement or closure.

A generic “accept” button is insufficient when different decisions carry different consequences.

## 7. Engagement changes must be governed

Professional work frequently changes after commencement. A filing may receive an objection. A document may require legalization. A provider may become unavailable. A customer may change the applicant or goods list.

Material changes should create an Engagement Amendment or equivalent recorded event. The amendment should identify:

- what changed;
- why it changed;
- who proposed it;
- impact on scope, cost, timing and authority;
- required approvals;
- whether prior instructions remain valid.

Silent scope expansion is a common source of conflict and financial opacity.

## 8. Failed Opportunities should still produce learning

Not every Opportunity becomes an Engagement. Rejection may result from price, timing, qualification, conflict, relationship concern, insufficient readiness or customer choice.

The platform should preserve bounded evidence about why a route failed, without turning private commercial judgment into shared canonical truth.

Useful learning may include:

- recurring readiness gaps;
- unrealistic timing assumptions;
- unavailable capability corridors;
- provider-capacity shortages;
- disclosure levels that prevent meaningful evaluation;
- pricing structures that repeatedly cause rejection.

These observations remain candidates for review, not automatic platform rules.

## 9. Product implications

A Product should make the transitions visible:

```text
Need captured
→ route options prepared
→ Opportunity reviewed
→ approvals obtained
→ Engagement accepted
→ execution objects activated
```

Users should be able to see the current stage, pending decision, responsible party and consequences of the next action. The system should prevent actions that jump across authority boundaries.

## 10. The operating principle

Professional collaboration becomes trustworthy when unresolved demand is converted into a viable route, and a viable route becomes a reciprocal, bounded Engagement before execution begins.

> MarkOrbit does not treat interest as commitment or visibility as authority. It makes the path from Need to Engagement explicit enough that every participant knows what has—and has not—been agreed.
