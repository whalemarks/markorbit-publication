# Chapter 16 — Customer Relationship Protection and Non-circumvention

Professional collaboration often fails for a reason that has little to do with technical quality: participants do not trust what will happen to the customer relationship after information is shared.

An originating agency may need local counsel, a specialist reviewer, a production partner or a transaction participant. Yet every disclosure creates a fear that another party will approach the customer directly, undercut pricing, reuse confidential information or claim the relationship for future work.

This fear is economically rational. Customer relationships are not simple contact records. They are accumulated trust, history, pricing knowledge, service expectations and future opportunity. MarkOrbit must protect that value without pretending that one participant can own a person or prohibit every future interaction.

## 1. What relationship protection covers

Customer relationship protection may include:

- origin and provenance of the relationship;
- current scope of representation;
- agreed communication channel;
- customer-facing brand;
- commercial terms and private margins;
- future-service rights within a defined scope;
- marketing and solicitation restrictions;
- referral or transfer conditions;
- complaint and escalation handling;
- duration and termination effects.

The relevant object is not the customer as property. It is the bounded commercial and service relationship created by legitimate work and agreement.

## 2. Relationship Owner is scope-specific

A Workplace may be Relationship Owner for trademark filing services but not for unrelated tax, corporate or litigation work. A referral may transfer one matter but preserve other portfolio relationships. A group company may manage regional communication while a local affiliate contracts for a particular service.

Therefore Relationship Ownership should identify:

- customer or customer group;
- service domain or Matter scope;
- jurisdiction or market where relevant;
- effective period;
- origin evidence;
- shared or exclusive status;
- agreed exceptions.

A single permanent `customer_owner` field is too crude.

## 3. Access does not create solicitation rights

A participant may receive customer identity for:

- conflict checks;
- formal representation;
- document execution;
- direct professional advice;
- payment or compliance processing;
- delivery coordination.

Those purposes do not automatically authorize unrelated marketing.

```text
Customer access
≠ marketing consent
≠ future-service entitlement
≠ relationship transfer
```

Projection and Engagement policies should state whether direct contact is allowed, for what purpose and for how long.

## 4. Non-circumvention must be more than a contract clause

A contractual prohibition helps, but platform design should also reduce the opportunity and incentive to bypass participants.

Controls may include:

- staged disclosure of customer identity;
- purpose-limited communication channels;
- visible Relationship Owner and contact policy;
- recipient audit trails;
- quotation and appointment records;
- restrictions on exporting customer data;
- alerts for unusual direct contact or new Engagement attempts;
- settlement rules that fairly compensate legitimate relationship value;
- complaint and remediation procedures.

The objective is not total surveillance. It is to make agreed boundaries operational and reviewable.

## 5. Direct communication may still be necessary

Relationship protection must not obstruct competent professional service. Local counsel may need to speak with the customer. A qualified attorney may have direct duties that cannot be filtered through an intermediary. A buyer and seller may need controlled due-diligence communication.

The correct question is not whether direct communication is always forbidden. It is:

- who may communicate;
- for what purpose;
- through which channel;
- what must be copied or recorded;
- whether commercial solicitation is excluded;
- when the permission ends.

White-label presentation cannot override legal or ethical disclosure duties.

## 6. Customer choice remains decisive

A customer is not trapped by a platform relationship designation. The customer may choose another provider, terminate an agency or request direct representation.

When that occurs, the system should distinguish:

- voluntary customer choice;
- active solicitation using privileged access;
- service failure causing legitimate reassignment;
- agreed referral or transfer;
- emergency professional intervention.

Relationship protection cannot become a mechanism for holding customers against their interests.

## 7. Relationship transfer requires an explicit event

A relationship may be referred, shared, transferred or terminated. Each outcome should be recorded explicitly.

A transfer record should identify:

- transferring and receiving Workplaces;
- customer consent where required;
- transferred scope;
- retained scope;
- commercial consideration;
- records and data included;
- open Matters and obligations;
- effective date;
- future communication and marketing rights.

Participation in delivery does not create such a transfer by implication.

## 8. Relationship provenance matters in disputes

When two parties claim the same relationship, the platform should preserve evidence such as:

- initial introduction;
- customer instruction;
- prior Engagements;
- communication history;
- contracts and invoices;
- referral records;
- accepted transfer events;
- customer preference.

The platform should not decide complex legal ownership merely from activity volume. It can present evidence, enforce platform policies and escalate unresolved disputes.

## 9. Pricing confidentiality and undercutting

A provider may need to quote its price to the originating Workplace without seeing the customer's final price. Conversely, a customer-facing party may need enough cost transparency to avoid misleading the customer.

Product design should support layers such as:

- provider net price;
- coordination fee;
- originating Workplace margin;
- official fees;
- customer-facing total;
- disclosure policy for each participant.

The existence of private margins is not inherently improper. Hidden conflicts, false official-fee claims and unauthorized price use are.

## 10. Platform conflicts

MarkOrbit itself may create relationship risk if it:

- uses Workplace customer data for direct prospecting;
- recommends its own services without disclosure;
- allows paid participants to access demand more broadly;
- treats aggregated demand as platform-owned inventory;
- markets to customers introduced through white-label Workplaces;
- makes exit difficult to preserve platform control.

The platform must therefore bind itself to the same purpose-limitation and relationship-protection rules it expects from participants.

## 11. Remedies and recovery

When circumvention or misuse occurs, possible responses include:

- access suspension;
- evidence preservation;
- communication restrictions;
- compensation or fee adjustment under agreed terms;
- relationship-status correction;
- provider or participant review;
- termination of Engagement or network eligibility;
- legal escalation outside the platform.

The system should distinguish allegation, evidence, finding and remedy. An accusation must not silently become canonical reputation data.

## 12. Exit and portability

A Workplace should be able to export its legitimate customer records, history, documents and evidence when leaving the platform, subject to rights and retention obligations.

Portability reduces the fear that MarkOrbit will become the ultimate Relationship Owner merely because it hosts the operating environment.

## 13. The operating principle

Relationship protection makes collaboration economically possible, but it must remain bounded by customer choice, professional duty and explicit agreement.

> MarkOrbit protects the value a Workplace has built around a customer without turning the customer into property or allowing temporary access to become silent commercial appropriation.
