# Part IV — Opportunity, Engagement and Collaboration

## Chapter 41 — Need is not yet business

A Need expresses a desired outcome or missing resource.

It may be internal and unresolved. It may be too vague, too risky or commercially unsuitable.

A Need becomes an Opportunity only when the system or a responsible participant identifies a plausible route.

```text
Need
→ candidate routes
→ viable Opportunity
```

Opportunity does not commit any party.

## Chapter 42 — Opportunity is a structured possibility

An Opportunity should describe:

- originating Workplace;
- underlying Need;
- proposed outcome;
- candidate capabilities;
- candidate collaboration model;
- expected parties;
- commercial potential;
- risk and constraints;
- next decision;
- expiry.

For Lite, an Opportunity may be a buyer request matched to a trademark. For MarkReg, it may be a maintenance deadline matched to an existing customer. For Workplace collaboration, it may be a need matched to a qualified contributor or MGSN route.

## Chapter 43 — Engagement is the collaboration contract

Engagement begins when participants accept the operating relationship.

It should define:

- parties and represented Workplaces;
- role allocation;
- scope;
- customer relationship;
- branding and white-label rules;
- data disclosure;
- communication rights;
- professional authority;
- fees and compensation;
- deliverables;
- review;
- termination;
- dispute handling;
- rights in outputs and evidence.

An Engagement may govern one Order or a longer collaboration arrangement.

## Chapter 44 — Engagement roles

The model should support at least:

### Demand Originator

The party that first introduced the need or opportunity.

### Relationship Owner

The party responsible for the continuing customer relationship.

### Contracting Party

The party that makes the commercial promise to the customer.

### Payment Receiver

The party authorized to receive the relevant customer payment.

### Solution Orchestrator

The party that designs the service route and work decomposition.

### Capability Contributor

A party completing a bounded Work Package.

### Professional Authority

The qualified party accountable for a restricted professional conclusion or act.

### Delivery Owner

The party accountable for the completeness of the final outcome.

One party may hold several roles, but the system should not infer that automatically.

## Chapter 45 — Referral

In a Referral, one Workplace introduces demand to another party that becomes the main contracting and delivery party.

The Engagement should define:

- whether the original party retains any relationship rights;
- referral compensation;
- permitted future contact;
- attribution duration;
- customer consent;
- responsibility after transfer.

Referral is a genuine transfer or introduction model, not disguised white-label delivery.

## Chapter 46 — Co-delivery

In Co-delivery, two or more Workplaces share defined responsibility.

For example:

- one Workplace manages the customer;
- MarkReg organizes the lifecycle service;
- an MGSN provider performs local filing;
- a certified contributor reviews evidence.

The customer should receive a coherent service even when internal responsibilities are distributed.

## Chapter 47 — White-label delivery

White-label delivery allows one Workplace to retain the customer-facing brand while another party performs bounded production or full delivery.

The Engagement must define:

- what the customer is told;
- who may communicate directly;
- whose templates and brand are used;
- who approves advice;
- who carries professional authority;
- who handles complaints;
- what happens when disclosure is legally required.

White-label must not conceal legally required provider identity or professional responsibility.

## Chapter 48 — Work Package contribution

A Work Package Contribution assigns a bounded unit of work rather than an entire customer matter.

Examples include:

- status verification;
- search-result screening;
- visual review;
- goods normalization;
- evidence classification;
- content review;
- simulated assessment.

The contributor receives only the minimum context required and normally does not acquire direct customer rights.

## Chapter 49 — Expert review

Expert Review provides a defined opinion or approval without transferring full delivery responsibility.

The review request should identify:

- question;
- materials;
- jurisdiction;
- expected output;
- reliance level;
- time limit;
- conflict status;
- whether the reviewer may contact other participants.

The Delivery Owner remains responsible for integrating the review into the final service.

## Chapter 50 — Managed network fulfillment

Managed Network Fulfillment uses MGSN for work requiring formal external provider participation.

The originating Workplace projects a Capability Need through its MGSN Connection. The platform manages eligibility, route recommendation, allocation, acceptance, funds checkpoints, fulfillment monitoring and Return.

MGSN Engagement does not erase the originating Workplace's customer relationship.

## Chapter 51 — Internal delivery remains valid

A Workplace with sufficient capability, capacity and authority may perform work entirely internally.

The platform should not force external collaboration merely to increase transaction volume.

Internal delivery still benefits from:

- shared data and knowledge;
- Capability workflows;
- review;
- audit;
- product integration;
- evidence.

## Chapter 52 — Relationship protection is default

The default rule is:

> The originating Relationship Owner remains the Relationship Owner unless an explicit Engagement changes that status.

Other participants receive only the communication rights required by their role.

The system should preserve introduction provenance and prevent silent reassignment.

## Chapter 53 — Relationship protection is not absolute ownership of a person

A customer is not property.

Relationship protection governs platform conduct and participant obligations. It does not prevent a customer from making an informed independent choice, nor should it enable abusive lock-in.

The platform should balance:

- attribution;
- contractual commitments;
- customer freedom;
- privacy;
- fair competition;
- non-circumvention.

## Chapter 54 — Communication architecture

Cross-Workplace communication may occur through:

- platform messages;
- Engagement channels;
- controlled email relay;
- recorded calls or summaries;
- document requests;
- task comments;
- direct contact where authorized.

The system should record material instructions and decisions without requiring every informal conversation to become a formal business record.

## Chapter 55 — Engagement lifecycle

A standard lifecycle may be:

```text
proposed
→ due diligence
→ terms pending
→ accepted
→ active
→ suspended
→ completed
→ terminated
→ disputed
→ archived
```

Suspension should be available when authority, payment, conflict or data issues arise.

## Chapter 56 — Failure and replacement

An Engagement must anticipate:

- contributor non-performance;
- provider refusal;
- missed deadlines;
- poor-quality output;
- conflict discovery;
- customer withdrawal;
- payment failure;
- unknown external outcome.

Replacement should preserve provenance and avoid silently overwriting failed work.

## Chapter 57 — Settlement follows accepted contribution

Compensation should connect to an accepted contribution, milestone or defined commercial event.

The Engagement should state whether payment is based on:

- task completion;
- accepted review;
- referral conversion;
- transaction completion;
- service milestone;
- ongoing revenue share.

Settlement records should not become proof that the professional work was correct. Quality and payment are related but distinct.
