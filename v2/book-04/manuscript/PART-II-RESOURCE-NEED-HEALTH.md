# Part II — Workplace Resources, Needs and Health

## Chapter 13 — Every Workplace has a resource profile

A Resource Profile describes what the Workplace can legitimately contribute to its own work or to governed collaboration.

It may include:

- customer relationships;
- audience and marketing channels;
- trademark inventory;
- professional capabilities;
- qualified personnel;
- review authority;
- local provider relationships;
- technology and data access;
- cases and knowledge;
- operating capacity;
- brand and reputation.

The Resource Profile is not automatically public. It is the internal source from which controlled projections may be created.

## Chapter 14 — Customer resources are not customer lists

A Workplace may possess valuable customer reach without exposing identifiable customers.

The platform should distinguish:

```text
customer record
relationship provenance
audience characteristic
qualified demand
public market signal
```

A Workplace may state that it serves exporters in a particular industry without publishing names, contacts or transaction history.

Audience Projection must never become a disguised customer-data leak.

## Chapter 15 — Capability Profile

The Capability Profile describes what the Workplace and its members can perform.

It may include:

- Capability IDs;
- proficiency levels;
- production authorization;
- professional qualification;
- jurisdiction and language;
- evidence freshness;
- supervision requirements;
- available reviewers;
- delivery history;
- current participation preference.

Self-declared capability and verified capability must remain distinguishable.

## Chapter 16 — Capacity Profile

Capability answers whether work can be performed. Capacity answers whether it can be performed now.

Capacity may include:

- available hours or task slots;
- maximum concurrent matters;
- normal turnaround;
- urgent capacity;
- review capacity;
- language capacity;
- planned absence;
- workload confidence.

Capacity is dynamic and may be projected with an expiry time.

## Chapter 17 — Asset Profile

A Workplace may hold or manage commercial assets such as:

- trademarks available for sale;
- authorized resale inventory;
- brand concepts;
- templates;
- rights-cleared case materials;
- content libraries;
- reusable service packages;
- public-domain or licensed datasets.

Possession, ownership, authorization and visibility must be separate fields.

## Chapter 18 — Provider and relationship resources

A Workplace may have trusted external relationships that improve delivery.

Examples include:

- local law firms;
- translators;
- designers;
- investigators;
- payment and courier providers;
- industry associations;
- marketing partners.

A private relationship history does not make the underlying provider pool platform-owned, nor does it authorize undisclosed rebrokering.

## Chapter 19 — Every Workplace also has a Need Profile

A Need Profile describes current or recurring gaps.

A Workplace may need:

- new customers;
- content and promotion;
- trademark inventory;
- search or classification support;
- local professional service;
- temporary production capacity;
- expert review;
- transaction assistance;
- training and certification;
- better data or knowledge;
- workflow modernization.

Need should be treated as an operating signal, not a permanent weakness.

## Chapter 20 — Needs vary by time and engagement

A Workplace may have strong filing capability and weak capacity this month. It may have marketing reach but no suitable trademark inventory for a buyer. It may be able to manage standard matters but need expert review for one exception.

Need Profile therefore includes:

- need type;
- urgency;
- duration;
- applicable jurisdiction;
- required capability;
- acceptable collaboration modes;
- disclosure limit;
- budget or commercial range;
- expiry.

## Chapter 21 — Asymmetry is the basis of network value

The network becomes valuable because participants differ.

```text
customer-rich + capability-poor
capability-rich + demand-poor
asset-rich + channel-poor
marketing-rich + professional-authority-poor
local-authority-rich + international-demand-poor
```

MarkOrbit should not classify these participants as inferior or incomplete. It should make complementarity visible and governable.

## Chapter 22 — Capability gaps should drive development

A Workplace should be able to compare:

- capabilities required by its service catalog;
- capabilities currently verified;
- capabilities available only through collaboration;
- review bottlenecks;
- jurisdiction gaps;
- upcoming legal changes;
- business opportunities lost due to missing capability.

The result may recommend:

- training;
- hiring;
- certification;
- external contribution;
- MarkReg white-label delivery;
- MGSN fulfillment.

## Chapter 23 — Workplace health is multi-dimensional

A healthy Workplace is not measured only by revenue.

Health indicators may include:

- customer concentration;
- opportunity pipeline;
- capability coverage;
- capacity utilization;
- review bottlenecks;
- deadline risk;
- data quality;
- knowledge freshness;
- transaction liquidity;
- collaboration dependency;
- settlement exposure;
- evidence completeness.

## Chapter 24 — Health indicators must not become opaque control

The platform may recommend actions, but it should explain why.

A Workplace health score must not silently determine access to customers, tasks or providers without appeal and evidence.

The system should prefer component indicators over one mysterious number.

## Chapter 25 — Internal strategy before external projection

Before projecting a need, the Workplace should decide:

```text
Can we perform internally?
Can AI or a Product Capability close the gap?
Do we need a bounded contributor?
Do we need a qualified external provider?
Should we refer or transfer the engagement?
```

Projection is a strategic choice, not the default response to every need.

## Chapter 26 — Resource development is a product outcome

MarkOrbit should help a Workplace become stronger over time.

A repeated external dependency may reveal a training opportunity. A growing transaction pipeline may justify a dedicated sales role. Repeated review failures may indicate a missing capability or weak workflow.

The Resource and Need Profiles should support planning, not merely matching.
