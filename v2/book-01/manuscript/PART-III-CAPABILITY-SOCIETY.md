# Part III — The Capability Society

## Chapter 16 — The profession can be mapped

A mature profession is usually described through titles, institutions and years of experience. Those signals are useful, but they are too coarse for a digital operating system.

MarkOrbit should describe the global trademark profession through a structured capability map.

A practical first target is approximately 300 capability units across twelve domains:

1. customer and business development;
2. trademark search and data analysis;
3. naming and filing strategy;
4. goods, services and classification;
5. application preparation and filing;
6. prosecution, opposition and disputes;
7. post-registration maintenance;
8. international planning and quotation;
9. trademark transactions and asset operations;
10. marketing content and brand commercialization;
11. operational quality and governance;
12. global collaboration and professional networks.

The number 300 is not a claim that the profession can never contain 299 or 301 capabilities. It is a communication and design frame: broad enough to represent the industry, finite enough for users to perceive progress.

## Chapter 17 — A capability is not a course title

Each Capability Unit should define an observable professional outcome.

For example:

```text
Capability: Evaluate a United States Section 8 filing window
Outcome: a sourced timing and requirement assessment
Inputs: registration record, current status, filing basis and goods
AI role: retrieve, calculate and identify inconsistencies
Human role: validate use, context and exceptions
Review: required when risk indicators are present
Evidence: accepted assessment and later filing result
```

A broad title such as “United States Trademark Expert” is not a useful atomic capability. It may become a certification path composed of many capability units.

## Chapter 18 — Progression must be visible

MarkOrbit should define five proficiency levels.

### L1 — Awareness

The user understands the concept, recognizes when it is relevant and can interpret a basic result.

### L2 — Assisted practitioner

The user can complete a standard task with guided workflow, AI assistance and mandatory review.

### L3 — Independent practitioner

The user can independently perform standard work, identify common exceptions and take responsibility for a reviewed result.

### L4 — Advanced specialist

The user can handle complex cases, review others, explain reasoning and decide when expert or qualified intervention is required.

### L5 — Expert or mentor

The user can establish standards, design cases, calibrate assessors, train others and contribute to the evolution of the capability.

Progress must be based on evidence, not only course completion.

## Chapter 19 — Human and AI roles must change with risk

The same Capability may be delivered through different collaboration modes.

```text
M0 Human only
M1 AI reference
M2 AI assist
M3 AI draft, human approve
M4 AI execute under governance
M5 Hybrid professional network
```

A low-risk data-formatting task may operate under M4. A professional opinion may require M3 or M2. A filing requiring a locally qualified lawyer may use M5. A highly sensitive strategic decision may remain M0 with AI limited to reference support.

The mode should be chosen from capability type, user level, case complexity, jurisdiction and risk—not from enthusiasm for automation.

## Chapter 20 — Simulation democratizes rare experience

Complex professional work is often concentrated in large firms because junior practitioners do not have safe access to difficult cases.

MarkOrbit can convert experience into governed simulation.

A Simulation Workplace may contain:

- a de-identified or synthetic customer;
- a simulated trademark record;
- documents with controlled inconsistencies;
- dynamic customer replies;
- hidden assessment points;
- deadlines and interruptions;
- an AI or human examiner;
- a scoring rubric;
- a reference outcome.

A renewal simulation should test more than date calculation. It should require the learner to verify ownership, identify unused goods, ask for better evidence, explain risk, prepare a quotation, create the Matter and Tasks, request review and deliver a customer-facing result.

## Chapter 21 — Assessment should observe work, not memory alone

A reliable assessment records the process:

- what information the learner inspected;
- what questions were asked;
- which AI suggestions were accepted or rejected;
- whether contradictions were noticed;
- how risk was escalated;
- how many revisions were required;
- whether the final work was usable;
- how much assessor help was needed.

The result is a Capability Evidence Record, not merely a score.

## Chapter 22 — Certification is not legal qualification

MarkOrbit certification proves competence under a defined MarkOrbit standard.

It does not itself make someone a lawyer, official trademark agent or person authorized to submit in a jurisdiction.

The platform must show three separate dimensions:

```text
Capability Certification
Production Authorization
Professional Qualification
```

A user may be certified at L3 for evidence review, authorized to perform supervised production tasks and still lack authority to sign or submit a filing.

This distinction protects customers, professionals and the credibility of the certification system.

## Chapter 23 — Production authorization is earned gradually

A safe progression path is:

```text
simulation
→ shadow task
→ supervised real task
→ bounded independent task
→ complex task
→ review authority
```

Passing one examination should not unlock unrestricted access to real customer work.

Production authorization also requires identity verification, confidentiality commitments, conflict checks, quality history and an appropriate engagement or employment relationship.

## Chapter 24 — Contribution is broader than case delivery

Many useful human contributions are small and bounded:

- verify a status record;
- review a search result;
- assess AI-generated trademark visuals;
- detect incorrect text in an image;
- evaluate goods classification;
- review marketing content;
- distill a professional email into a reusable practice note;
- act as a simulated customer;
- assess a learner's work;
- review a MarkReg production package.

These tasks belong to a Professional Contribution Network.

They should be structured, attributable, reviewed and compensated. They are not anonymous crowdsourcing.

## Chapter 25 — Capability density is a strategic asset

The platform's deepest human asset is not total registrations. It is the distribution of verified capability.

MarkOrbit should be able to answer:

- how many L3 practitioners can handle a standard maintenance task;
- how many L4 reviewers are available in each jurisdiction;
- which capabilities have no qualified supply;
- which capability paths have too many learners and too little work;
- which experts can teach, assess or accept MGSN work;
- how current each capability remains after legal change.

A platform with a dense, trusted and dispatchable capability graph is harder to copy than a platform with a large but undifferentiated user list.

## Chapter 26 — Users should become stronger, not dependent

The platform must avoid a harmful incentive: keeping users permanently weak so they remain dependent on automation.

The stronger principle is:

> MarkOrbit should give users immediate access to capabilities while helping them convert repeated assisted work into personal competence.

A user may begin by relying heavily on AI and reviewers. Over time, evidence should support lower supervision, broader production authority and access to higher-value work.

The growth of the user and the growth of the platform should reinforce each other.
