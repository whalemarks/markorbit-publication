# Part V — The Economic and Governance Model

## Chapter 38 — What users should pay for

MarkOrbit should not monetize every Capability invocation as if professional competence were a prepaid utility token.

Within an entitled product, routine Capability use should feel available rather than rationed.

Revenue may come from:

- product subscriptions;
- advanced training;
- simulation programs;
- certification and reassessment;
- expert review;
- trademark transactions;
- real human contribution;
- professional services;
- managed fulfillment;
- enterprise governance and analytics.

External costs such as official fees, qualified local representation, notarization, translation, courier services or high-cost third-party data remain separate from platform capability access.

## Chapter 39 — Why per-use charging is strategically weak

Per-use pricing discourages experimentation, learning and daily reliance.

A professional who hesitates before every search, content generation, analysis or recommendation does not build a deep operating habit.

The stronger model is:

```text
subscription grants operating capability
training develops personal capability
certification proves capability
real contribution earns compensation
transactions and services generate outcome-based revenue
```

High-cost compute may still require reasonable-use policies, quality tiers, queues or enterprise resource agreements. These controls should not obscure the central promise that a subscribed user has access to the operating system.

## Chapter 40 — Training revenue must not corrupt certification

Training and certification can be meaningful businesses, but the certificate loses value if payment implies passage.

MarkOrbit should preserve:

```text
purchase of training
≠ certification

completion of videos
≠ capability evidence

AI-generated answer
≠ learner competence

one successful case
≠ permanent authorization
```

High-level certification should require simulation, observed performance, assessor calibration, periodic updating and a revocation or appeal process.

## Chapter 41 — Paid contribution must be fair and explicit

When a person contributes labor or professional judgment, the economic arrangement must be visible before acceptance.

A task offer should state:

- the required capability and level;
- production authorization;
- expected work;
- data access;
- deadline;
- review standard;
- revision obligation;
- compensation;
- payment condition;
- confidentiality and rights.

MarkOrbit should prefer standard task compensation and controlled routing over a race to the lowest bid.

## Chapter 42 — Value is broader than labor time

A service outcome may combine several forms of value:

```text
customer acquisition
relationship management
solution design
production work
review responsibility
professional authority
local fulfillment
platform technology and governance
```

The economic model may include fixed task payments, referral commissions, transaction commissions, professional service fees and platform margin.

The platform should begin with simple arrangements and add complex revenue sharing only when attribution and disputes can be governed reliably.

## Chapter 43 — Trademark commerce requires trust infrastructure

A trademark transaction is not merely a buyer contacting a seller.

The platform must manage uncertainty around:

- current legal status;
- ownership;
- authority to sell;
- price and commission;
- exclusivity;
- prior buyer introduction;
- due diligence;
- payment milestones;
- transfer documents;
- completion evidence.

Liquidity depends on trustworthy inventory and credible demand, not only listing count.

## Chapter 44 — Customer sovereignty is an economic feature

Professional Workplaces will not project valuable demand into a platform that may take over their customers.

Relationship protection is therefore not only a privacy or ethical concern. It is a growth mechanism.

Every Engagement should identify:

```text
Relationship Owner
Contracting Party
Payment Receiver
Delivery Owner
Professional Authority
```

Different collaboration models—referral, co-delivery, white-label, bounded contribution or network fulfillment—may allocate these roles differently.

## Chapter 45 — Governance prevents platform decay

Without governance, successful platforms often decay into spam, low-quality supply, opaque rankings, price competition and attempts to bypass the system.

MarkOrbit requires controls for:

- qualification;
- conflict checking;
- minimum necessary access;
- contribution attribution;
- review quality;
- assessor consistency;
- task cancellation;
- dispute handling;
- misuse and suspension;
- knowledge promotion;
- algorithmic explanation.

Governance should be proportional. Low-risk automated tasks should not require the same process as a complex legal opinion.

## Chapter 46 — Risk tiers guide collaboration

A practical risk model may use:

```text
R0 — automated, deterministic and reversible
R1 — automated or assisted with sampling
R2 — single professional review
R3 — cross-disciplinary or senior review
R4 — qualified or expert final authority
```

Risk is influenced by:

- legal consequence;
- financial value;
- reversibility;
- customer impact;
- uncertainty;
- data sensitivity;
- user capability;
- jurisdictional restriction.

The purpose of risk tiers is not bureaucracy. It is to spend human judgment where it creates the most protection.

## Chapter 47 — Contribution can improve Brain only through evidence

Human judgment is valuable, but not every opinion should become platform knowledge.

The promotion chain should be explicit:

```text
User Opinion
→ attributed Professional Contribution
→ reviewed practice candidate
→ validated or outcome-supported practice
→ governed knowledge asset
```

The platform should retain contributor identity, capability level, source basis, confidence, review status and later outcome.

Disagreement should not be erased. In some professional questions, the most useful knowledge is a structured representation of competing reasonable views.

## Chapter 48 — Privacy, rights and reuse

Data, cases, images, documents, knowledge and professional contributions require distinct rights.

A user uploading a case does not automatically grant unlimited model-training or commercial publication rights. De-identification does not automatically remove contractual or professional confidentiality obligations.

The platform should support explicit permissions for:

- operational use;
- assessment use;
- training publication;
- internal model improvement;
- commercial knowledge products;
- attribution;
- withdrawal or retirement.

Rights-aware knowledge compounds more slowly than indiscriminate collection, but it remains defensible.

## Chapter 49 — The platform must create reasons not to bypass it

Contractual anti-circumvention clauses are not a sufficient moat.

Participants remain when the platform provides value they lose by leaving:

- trusted data and knowledge;
- customer relationship protection;
- AI and workflow support;
- certification and reputation;
- reliable task demand;
- settlement assurance;
- dispute resolution;
- capability progression;
- provider replacement and continuity;
- accumulated evidence.

The strongest defense against bypass is a system that makes collaboration safer and more productive than informal alternatives.
