# Chapter 11 — Projection, Minimum Disclosure and Trust

Collaboration requires information to move beyond its original context.

That movement creates value and risk at the same time.

A Workplace cannot receive a quotation, find a provider, match a trademark buyer, request expert review or assign a Work Package without exposing something. Yet exposing too much too early can damage confidentiality, customer relationships, negotiating power and professional responsibility.

MarkOrbit addresses this tension through **Projection**.

## Projection is not copying

A Projection is a purpose-limited view created from an authorized source context.

It answers five questions:

```text
what is exposed
for which purpose
under whose authority
to which audience
for how long
```

A Projection may contain references, summaries, masked fields, calculated attributes or controlled documents. It does not need to duplicate the complete source object.

The source remains authoritative unless an explicit transfer or new formal record is created.

```text
Projection
≠ ownership transfer
≠ unrestricted copy
≠ permanent disclosure
≠ formal-state mutation
```

## Why ordinary sharing is too coarse

Most software offers a small set of choices:

- private;
- shared by link;
- visible to organization;
- public.

Professional collaboration needs greater precision.

A trademark listing may be visible publicly while the owner’s contact details remain private. A buyer request may be visible only to approved Workplaces. An MGSN demand may reveal jurisdiction and service scope before customer identity. A contributor may see one document but not the entire Matter.

The system must therefore separate object visibility from purpose and action permission.

## Types of Projection

Several recurring Projection profiles appear across MarkOrbit.

### Demand Projection

Exposes a Need or part of it to find capability, asset, capacity or provider supply.

### Capability Projection

Exposes what a Person or Workplace is prepared and authorized to perform.

### Asset Projection

Exposes a trademark or other asset for matching, evaluation, promotion or transaction.

### Capacity Projection

Exposes current availability under time and scope constraints.

### Audience Projection

Exposes controlled reach or channel access for a marketing collaboration.

These profiles may use different Product-local records, but they share the same governance principle.

## Minimum disclosure is progressive

Minimum disclosure does not mean sending the smallest possible number of fields regardless of context.

It means disclosing the minimum information necessary for the current stage.

A professional-service route may progress through stages:

```text
exploration
→ eligibility
→ recommendation
→ approval
→ acceptance
→ appointment
→ execution
→ review
→ Return
```

At exploration, masked service facts may be sufficient. At conflict checking, identity may become necessary. At formal filing, complete applicant and document details may be required.

The disclosure boundary should expand only when the purpose justifies it.

## Example: selecting a local provider

A Workplace needs a quotation for a trademark filing in a foreign jurisdiction.

At first, the Projection may include:

- country;
- class count;
- mark type;
- applicant type;
- goods length;
- urgency;
- special-document indicators.

The system can use this to identify eligible routes and estimate price.

Before appointment, the provider may require:

- applicant identity for conflict checking;
- full goods specification;
- document status;
- priority claim details.

After appointment, the provider receives the Instruction Package and required documents.

This staged process avoids broadcasting full customer data to every candidate provider.

## Example: trademark commerce

A Workplace has authority to market a trademark asset.

A public Asset Projection may show:

- mark image or wording;
- jurisdiction;
- classes;
- verified status;
- selected goods;
- indicative price;
- concept materials;
- inquiry route.

It need not expose:

- the owner’s direct contact information;
- private minimum price;
- commission arrangements;
- customer history;
- internal due-diligence notes.

A qualified buyer may receive more information after an Inquiry and confidentiality step.

## Projection requires authority

Possession of data does not create authority to project it.

The system must identify whether the actor may disclose:

- customer identity;
- professional documents;
- trademark ownership evidence;
- provider terms;
- personal information;
- confidential strategy;
- images or copyrighted materials;
- AI training rights.

Authority may derive from ownership, contract, customer instruction, organization role, professional obligation or platform policy.

When authority is unclear, the correct state is not assumed permission. It is unresolved.

## Revocation and downstream reality

A Projection may be revoked, but revocation has limits.

The platform can stop future access, disable links and update audience policy. It cannot guarantee that an authorized recipient has forgotten information already viewed or lawfully retained.

The system should therefore record:

- what was disclosed;
- who received it;
- when;
- under which purpose;
- download or export events;
- retention obligations;
- revocation state.

Trust depends partly on making downstream consequences visible before disclosure.

## Projection and AI

AI introduces a subtle disclosure risk.

Information supplied to a model or agent may leave the immediate Product boundary even when no human provider is involved.

A governed AI Projection should consider:

- model provider;
- data-retention terms;
- training use;
- region;
- sensitivity;
- prompt and output logging;
- allowed tools;
- memory scope;
- redaction.

A Workplace should not assume that calling an AI feature is equivalent to processing data entirely inside its own boundary.

## Trust is contextual evidence

Trust should not be reduced to a universal score.

A provider may be highly reliable for routine filings in one country and weak for contentious work. A contributor may be excellent at data verification but unqualified for legal review. A Workplace may be commercially trustworthy while lacking technical security maturity.

Trust should therefore be based on contextual evidence:

- verified identity;
- qualifications;
- accepted scope;
- deadline performance;
- evidence completeness;
- correction history;
- communication;
- dispute behavior;
- rights compliance;
- recent outcomes.

Projection decisions can use this evidence to determine audience and disclosure depth.

## Explainable access

A participant should be able to understand why access exists.

Instead of merely showing a document, the system should be able to state:

```text
You can view this because:
- your Workplace is a party to Engagement E;
- you hold Assignment A;
- the document is required for Work Package W;
- access expires on date D;
- export is prohibited;
```

Explainable access strengthens both usability and accountability.

## The cost of excessive secrecy

Minimum disclosure must not become an excuse for concealing information necessary for lawful or competent service.

A provider cannot assess conflict without relevant identity. A lawyer cannot give responsible advice without material facts. A customer cannot give informed approval if key cost or responsibility information is hidden.

The principle is proportional disclosure, not strategic omission.

## The cost of excessive openness

The opposite error is equally damaging.

Broad visibility can create:

- customer poaching;
- price leakage;
- unauthorized reuse;
- conflict exposure;
- privacy violations;
- poor negotiation position;
- accidental AI training;
- uncontrolled reseller chains.

Open access may simplify early development while making the future network untrustworthy.

## Trust grows through controlled exposure

Participants do not need to trust every other participant completely before collaborating.

They need to trust the process enough to expose the next necessary piece of information.

That is the strategic role of Projection:

```text
bounded visibility
+ explicit purpose
+ verified authority
+ staged disclosure
+ audit evidence
+ enforceable consequence
= practical trust
```

MarkOrbit does not eliminate uncertainty between independent parties. It creates a disciplined way to move forward without requiring blind disclosure or total platform control.
