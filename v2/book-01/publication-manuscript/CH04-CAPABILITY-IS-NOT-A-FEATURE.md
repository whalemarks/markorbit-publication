# Chapter 04 — Capability Is Not a Feature

A software product is usually described by its features. It has search, filing, reminders, dashboards, document upload, messaging, image generation or analytics. That vocabulary helps compare interfaces. It is too shallow for a professional operating system.

A feature tells us what a screen can do. A Capability tells us what governed outcome the system can help produce, under which facts, authority, evidence, risk and review conditions.

That distinction matters whenever the work is consequential.

A button labelled “Generate filing strategy” may produce a polished answer. Yet a reliable result still depends on jurisdiction, business objectives, applicant identity, use status, classes, current law, source freshness, unresolved assumptions, professional responsibility and the authority to act. The feature is visible. The real Capability is the operating structure behind it.

MarkOrbit must be designed around that hidden structure.

## 1. The feature trap

Feature-led products often succeed in demonstrations and fail in sustained professional use.

A demonstration rewards speed, novelty and apparent intelligence. Professional work rewards reliability, traceability, appropriate judgment, controlled authority, usable delivery and recoverability.

Consider five familiar features:

```text
AI search
AI classification
AI filing recommendation
AI evidence review
AI content generation
```

None is a complete professional Capability without an outcome contract.

An AI search feature needs a defined objective, source coverage, retrieval date, matching method, goods relationship logic, transliteration policy, uncertainty labels and review responsibility.

An AI classification feature needs jurisdiction, classification edition, office practice, acceptable wording, item-count constraints and a way to preserve the customer’s actual commercial scope.

An AI filing recommendation needs market priorities, budget, applicant structure, use status, urgency, risk tolerance and a distinction between mandatory, recommended and optional routes.

An AI evidence review feature needs the relevant legal event, covered goods, dates, authenticity, source, manipulation risk and professional disposition.

An AI content feature must distinguish verified trademark facts from invented brand concepts, promotional claims from legal status and visual inspiration from evidence of use.

When these conditions are missing, a product may have many features and little dependable Capability.

## 2. Capability begins with a stable outcome contract

A Capability is the governed ability to produce a defined outcome under explicit conditions.

```text
Capability
= Stable Outcome Contract
+ Governed Implementation
+ Evidence Base
+ Version Lineage
+ Controlled Evolution
```

For example:

```text
Capability:
Assess United States post-registration maintenance readiness

Outcome:
A sourced and reviewable assessment of the filing window,
current owner, registered goods, evidence readiness,
unresolved risks, recommended next action and
professional-review requirement.
```

The implementation may combine official trademark data, jurisdiction knowledge, date calculation, document extraction, deterministic checks, a language model, a trained user, an expert reviewer and an authorized attorney.

Those components may change. The outcome contract should remain stable enough for the Product, user, reviewer and customer to understand what is being requested and what counts as success.

A mature Capability Definition therefore identifies:

- identity and version;
- purpose and expected outcome;
- accepted inputs and assumptions;
- data and Knowledge dependencies;
- permitted implementation modes;
- risk and authority ceiling;
- review policy;
- output and Evidence contracts;
- failure, escalation and Unknown behavior;
- compatibility and deprecation expectations.

A generated file is not proof that the Capability outcome has been satisfied.

## 3. The hierarchy prevents category confusion

MarkOrbit uses a four-level hierarchy:

```text
Capability Domain
→ Capability
→ Skill
→ Action / Invocation
```

A **Capability Domain** groups durable areas of professional or business competence.

A **Capability** defines the governed outcome.

A **Skill** is a scenario-specific reusable implementation of part or all of that Capability.

An **Action or Invocation** is one bounded use of a Skill, Tool or human procedure in a particular context.

For example:

```text
Domain:
Trademark Maintenance

Capability:
Assess Section 8 filing readiness

Skill:
Reconcile registered goods with candidate use evidence

Action:
Run evidence reconciliation for Matter M-1042
against Registration version R-7
```

This hierarchy keeps the platform from treating a prompt, script, checklist, button or model call as the durable unit.

## 4. Capability is not Skill, Tool, Workflow or Product

A Skill is one implementation method. A parser, review checklist, drafting procedure or source-retrieval package may be a Skill.

A Tool performs a bounded operation. A browser retrieves a page. A database runs a query. An image model generates a visual. An email connector sends a message.

A Workflow coordinates a sequence of stages toward a broader goal. One Workflow may invoke several Capabilities, and one Capability may appear in many Workflows.

A Product packages Capabilities for a user segment and commercial promise. Lite, MarkReg and MGSN expose different arrangements without owning the shared meaning of the underlying Capability.

```text
Capability ≠ Skill
Capability ≠ Tool
Capability ≠ Workflow
Capability ≠ Product
Capability ≠ Person or Provider
```

The distinction allows models, Tools, people and providers to change without silently changing the promised outcome.

## 5. Stable does not mean frozen

Capability must be stable enough to govern work and capable of improving when evidence justifies change.

MarkOrbit therefore separates three lifecycles:

```text
Capability production
Source → Distillation → Source Capability

Capability runtime
Request → Eligibility → Composition → Execution → Outcome

Capability evolution
Outcome and Evidence
→ Reflection Candidate
→ Case Candidate
→ Principle Candidate
→ Change Proposal
→ Version Review
→ Release
```

These lifecycles must not collapse into one another.

A book can produce a Source Capability without becoming platform canon. A successful runtime can produce evidence without rewriting the Capability. A Change Proposal can be reviewed without authorizing production migration.

```text
Outcome ≠ automatic Capability upgrade
Frequent use ≠ canonical truth
Model confidence ≠ release authority
```

## 6. Source, Composite, Experimental and Verified MO Capability

Not every reusable method has the same status.

A **Source Capability** preserves the reasoning of a particular book, course, practitioner or body of work. It should retain thesis, assumptions, methods, cases, boundaries and provenance rather than being silently averaged into platform advice.

A **Composite Capability** combines selected Source Capabilities, Data, Knowledge and practice evidence for a defined problem. It is a governed synthesis, not automatic canon.

An **Experimental Capability** is a bounded candidate under evaluation. Its users, risks, review burden and permitted contexts must be explicit.

A **Verified MO Capability** is a released platform version supported by sufficient evidence, governance, compatibility review and controlled adoption.

```text
Source Capability
→ Composite Capability
→ Experimental Capability
→ Verified MO Capability
```

This sequence is not mandatory in every case, but the status distinctions are mandatory whenever the platform could otherwise confuse sourced reasoning with verified platform competence.

## 7. Capability composition is bounded

Complex problems often require more than one Capability. The answer is not to load every potentially relevant Skill into one context and ask a model to average them.

The default composition pattern is:

```text
One Primary Capability
+ zero to three Supporting Capabilities
+ zero or one Critic Capability
+ Capability Budget
+ explicit Conflict handling
```

The Primary Capability owns the decision structure. Supporting Capabilities answer bounded decision nodes. A Critic Capability examines a defined risk. The Capability Budget limits context, time, cost and complexity.

When methods conflict, MarkOrbit should preserve assumptions, trade-offs and resolution rationale rather than hiding disagreement behind one score.

```text
Capability Composition ≠ authority to act
Useful synthesis ≠ approved professional conclusion
```

## 8. Access, competence and authority remain separate

A small agency may gain immediate access to a sophisticated Capability through official data, maintained Knowledge, AI assistance, guided workflows, certified reviewers and qualified providers.

That access does not mean every user personally possesses the full competence or legal authority.

```text
Capability access
≠ demonstrated proficiency
≠ Production Authorization
≠ Professional Qualification
≠ Matter authority
```

Repeated, reviewed work may support a Capability Profile. It must preserve whether the result was assisted, supervised, independently produced or professionally authorized.

## 9. Capability is a better investment unit

Features are easy to add and easy to copy. A dependable Capability is harder to build because it requires trusted Data, maintained Knowledge, a stable contract, tests, fixtures, guidance, execution policy, review, evidence and correction.

Investment in `VerifyTrademarkStatus` can benefit Lite opportunity discovery, MarkReg delivery, MGSN instruction, customer reporting and simulation. Investment in one isolated page may benefit only one interface.

The platform should prioritize Capabilities that:

- solve a recurring and valuable outcome;
- can be reused across Products or Workflows;
- may have multiple implementations;
- carry meaningful risk or review needs;
- can improve through governed evidence;
- remain useful when a model, Tool or provider changes.

## 10. The abstraction can become bureaucracy

Capability-centered architecture has its own failure mode: excessive taxonomy without useful delivery.

A team may divide simple work into hundreds of artificial units, impose versioning on ordinary Product behavior or spend months describing Capabilities that no user needs.

A behavior deserves first-class Capability treatment when users can recognize the outcome, the outcome recurs, implementation may vary, governance matters and evidence can improve it. Otherwise it may remain a local feature, Workflow step or implementation detail.

## 11. The practical test

Before calling something a Capability, MarkOrbit should ask:

```text
What outcome is promised?
Which Domain contains it?
Which Skills may implement it?
What inputs and authority are required?
What Evidence proves completion?
What can go wrong?
Which review is required?
Can another Product reuse it?
Can the implementation change without changing the promise?
How may evidence propose—but not automatically enact—its evolution?
```

The strategic choice is clear:

> MarkOrbit should compete by making difficult professional outcomes accessible, reliable, governable and capable of controlled improvement—not by accumulating the largest menu of visible features.
