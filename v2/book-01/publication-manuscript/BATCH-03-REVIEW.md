## Depth assessment

This batch moves beyond architecture labels and establishes a sustained business argument for why MarkOrbit must preserve independent organizational contexts while enabling governed collaboration.

The chapters explain:

- why Workplace is more than a tenant or UI container;
- how Person, Organization, Workplace and Membership differ;
- why Products enter through Product Installation;
- how personal and organizational Workplaces coexist;
- why professional firms possess asymmetric resources;
- how referral, white-label, co-delivery, bounded contribution, expert review and MGSN fulfillment differ;
- why customer relationship sovereignty is necessary for network participation;
- how Relationship Owner differs from Contracting Party, Payment Receiver, Delivery Owner and Professional Authority;
- how Projection enables staged and minimum-necessary disclosure;
- why trust must be contextual and evidence-based.

## Boundary checks

- Workplace does not become a legal entity by itself.
- Product Installation does not own all displayed records.
- customer relationship does not transfer through storage, access or delivery participation.
- white-label does not authorize professional misrepresentation.
- Projection does not transfer ownership or create unrestricted reuse.
- minimum disclosure does not permit withholding facts necessary for lawful service.
- Provider recommendation and appointment remain separate.
- no Core candidate is activated.

## Editorial assessment

The four chapters form one coherent section:

```text
Workplace boundary
→ resource asymmetry
→ collaboration modes
→ relationship protection
→ controlled projection
→ practical trust
```

They should remain separate publication chapters because each carries a distinct thesis and operational consequence.
