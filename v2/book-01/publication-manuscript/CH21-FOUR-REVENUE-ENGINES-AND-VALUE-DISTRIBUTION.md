# Chapter 21 — Four Revenue Engines and Value Distribution

A platform becomes economically credible when its revenue follows real value creation and when participants can understand how value is distributed.

MarkOrbit should not rely on one universal fee. Its architecture supports four primary revenue engines:

```text
1. Product subscription
2. Capability development
3. Transaction and network coordination
4. Professional delivery
```

These engines overlap operationally, but they should remain economically legible.

## 1. Product subscription

Subscription pays for persistent operating value.

For Lite, that may include:

- daily priority views;
- content and campaign tools;
- trademark asset presentation;
- customer and opportunity management;
- controlled matching;
- capability access;
- reusable workflows.

For MarkReg, subscription may include:

- portfolio visibility;
- lifecycle monitoring;
- deadline intelligence;
- readiness guidance;
- customer collaboration;
- reporting;
- reusable intake and document management.

For organizational Workplaces, subscription may also support:

- team roles;
- permissions;
- private knowledge;
- Product Installations;
- internal metrics;
- controlled network connections.

The subscription price should reflect recurring operating value rather than the number of database rows stored.

## 2. Capability development

The second engine supports learning, simulation, assessment and capability verification.

Possible paid components include:

- curriculum access;
- simulation environments;
- assessment administration;
- assessor review;
- supervised-production programs;
- organizational capability diagnostics.

However, the economic model must avoid three failures.

First, payment cannot guarantee certification.

Second, unpaid production work cannot be disguised as training.

Third, certification should not become an artificial toll required only to access all platform work.

A healthy model allows people to build capability while preserving independent professional qualifications and alternative evidence routes.

## 3. Transaction and network coordination

The third engine captures value when the platform creates or completes coordinated economic activity.

Examples include:

- trademark-sale commissions;
- qualified service-opportunity fees;
- referral fees;
- network administration fees;
- settlement-processing fees where legally and operationally appropriate;
- MGSN routing or engagement fees;
- completion-linked platform participation.

Transaction fees should correspond to a defined contribution.

The platform may provide:

- verified supply;
- demand capture;
- matching;
- engagement formation;
- document coordination;
- milestone tracking;
- evidence validation;
- dispute support.

The more responsibility the platform assumes, the stronger the basis for the fee—and the greater the corresponding liability and governance burden.

## 4. Professional delivery

The fourth engine pays for actual professional work.

This may be delivered by:

- MarkReg's own authorized production organization;
- a partner Workplace;
- a certified contributor under supervision;
- an MGSN Provider Organization;
- a hybrid delivery team.

Professional delivery can include:

- searches;
- filing preparation;
- classification work;
- evidence review;
- office-action analysis;
- maintenance preparation;
- transaction due diligence;
- local filing or representation;
- portfolio strategy.

Fees must distinguish the platform component, the delivery component and official disbursements.

## 5. Value distribution across participants

MarkOrbit coordinates value created by multiple actors.

A single customer payment may need to be distributed among:

- the originating Workplace;
- the Relationship Owner;
- the contracting party;
- a contributor;
- a reviewer;
- a Delivery Owner;
- a local Provider;
- the platform;
- the authority receiving official fees.

This distribution should not be inferred from who happens to receive the money first.

```text
Payment Receiver
≠ sole value creator
≠ Relationship Owner
≠ Professional Authority
```

Each economic claim should have a basis:

- referral;
- customer acquisition;
- commercial contracting;
- work performed;
- review responsibility;
- professional responsibility;
- platform coordination;
- risk assumed.

## 6. Avoiding double charging

Blended models create a risk of charging multiple fees for the same value.

For example, a user might pay:

- a subscription;
- an opportunity fee;
- a platform fee;
- a professional service fee;
- a Provider fee.

That can be legitimate only when each fee corresponds to a separate service.

MarkOrbit should present a normalized fee view showing:

```text
platform access
coordination
professional work
official fees
third-party disbursements
taxes
optional services
```

Users should not need to reverse-engineer the economics from several invoices.

## 7. White-label economics

White-label delivery requires special care.

The originating agency may retain the customer relationship and set the customer-facing price. MarkReg or an MGSN Provider may deliver part of the work.

The system must record:

- who contracts with the customer;
- who sets the retail price;
- who receives funds;
- what wholesale or delivery fee applies;
- who bears refund risk;
- who communicates with the customer;
- who bears professional responsibility.

White-label does not mean invisible economics internally. The system needs complete internal attribution even when the customer sees one commercial interface.

## 8. Contributor economics

Professional contribution should be priced according to bounded work, evidence and review burden.

A contribution fee may depend on:

- capability level;
- jurisdiction;
- risk;
- complexity;
- deadline;
- language;
- required authority;
- expected revision cycles;
- evidence standard.

The system should avoid a race to the bottom.

Lowest price is not always efficient when low-quality work creates expensive review and rework.

A better economic measure is total accepted-outcome cost:

```text
initial contribution cost
+ review cost
+ rework cost
+ delay cost
+ failure risk
```

## 9. Provider economics

MGSN Provider pricing must normalize what is included.

A quoted amount may combine:

- filing service;
- official fee;
- publication;
- registration;
- tax;
- local disbursement;
- certificate handling;
- translation;
- courier.

Without normalized components, route comparison becomes misleading.

Provider pricing should also preserve the difference between estimates and fixed commitments.

## 10. Settlement follows accepted evidence

Payment timing should align with verifiable milestones.

Possible milestones include:

- engagement accepted;
- instruction package accepted;
- filing evidence returned;
- publication completed;
- registration confirmed;
- certificate validated;
- Work Package accepted.

Settlement should not be based solely on an unverified status message.

At the same time, the platform must not impose unreasonable payment delay after valid work has been accepted.

## 11. Disputes and reserves

Multi-party delivery creates disputes about:

- whether scope changed;
- whether work was defective;
- whether official fees were paid;
- whether a deadline was missed;
- whether a customer canceled;
- whether a referral or commission was earned;
- whether evidence was sufficient.

The economic model therefore needs:

- milestone evidence;
- versioned scope;
- acceptance rules;
- dispute windows;
- reserve rules where appropriate;
- refund attribution;
- escalation paths.

These are not back-office details. They determine whether professionals trust the platform with real work.

## Closing

MarkOrbit's revenue should not come from owning every customer or absorbing every professional fee.

It should come from creating recurring operating value, improving capability, enabling economic activity and coordinating trustworthy delivery.

The platform becomes defensible when participants earn more, waste less and can see why each share of value exists.
