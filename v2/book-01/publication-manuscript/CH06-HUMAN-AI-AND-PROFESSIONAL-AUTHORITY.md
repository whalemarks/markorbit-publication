# Chapter 06 — Human, AI and Professional Authority

Artificial intelligence changes how professional work is produced, but it does not remove the need to answer a basic question:

> Who is allowed to decide, act and remain responsible?

A professional system becomes dangerous when it treats intelligence, competence, authorization and responsibility as the same thing.

They are not the same.

An AI model may produce a strong analysis without holding legal authority. A junior employee may understand a procedure but lack production authorization. A qualified lawyer may possess formal authority but lack current operational evidence in a specialized task. A customer may approve a commercial direction but cannot authorize an unlawful filing. A platform may coordinate work but should not silently become the professional decision-maker.

MarkOrbit must make these distinctions visible.

## 1. Intelligence does not create authority

Generative AI can retrieve, compare, summarize, draft and recommend. In some bounded areas it may outperform an inexperienced human.

That performance does not give the model authority over:

- official facts;
- customer consent;
- protected data;
- professional legal judgment;
- formal filing;
- provider appointment;
- payment release;
- business-state mutation.

Authority comes from an accepted source: law, organizational role, customer mandate, professional qualification, production policy or system contract.

AI participates through delegated and constrained execution.

## 2. Four separate questions

Every consequential action should answer four questions.

```text
Can the actor produce the result?
May the actor perform this work in this context?
May the actor perform the legally or professionally reserved action?
Who remains accountable for the final outcome?
```

These correspond broadly to:

```text
Capability
Production Authorization
Professional Authority
Delivery Responsibility
```

Collapsing them creates predictable failures.

A person may be capable but not authorized because the customer data is outside scope. A person may be authorized to prepare a draft but not qualified to submit it. A lawyer may be qualified to sign but rely on operational staff for evidence collection. The Delivery Owner may coordinate the result without personally performing every contribution.

## 3. The human–AI collaboration spectrum

MarkOrbit uses six collaboration modes.

### M0 — Human only

AI is not used in the governed production path.

This may be required by policy, customer instruction, confidentiality, source restrictions or the nature of the professional judgment.

### M1 — AI reference

AI retrieves, summarizes or presents relevant material. The human performs the analysis and creates the result.

### M2 — AI assist

AI helps structure facts, identify issues, calculate or compare. The human remains the primary author and decision-maker.

### M3 — AI draft, human approve

AI produces a proposed result. A qualified or authorized human must review and actively approve, revise or reject it.

Silence is not approval.

### M4 — AI execute under governance

AI or deterministic automation performs a bounded action under a predefined policy, with logging, validation, monitoring and a recovery path.

This mode is appropriate only where the action, risk and reversibility permit it.

### M5 — Hybrid professional network

The outcome requires coordinated AI, internal personnel, certified contributors, reviewers and external qualified providers.

M5 is not a sign of weak automation. For many cross-border professional outcomes it is the correct mature mode.

## 4. Automation should follow observability

Organizations often ask which tasks AI can automate before they can describe the current work accurately.

MarkOrbit should reverse the sequence:

```text
understand the work
→ define the outcome
→ identify sources and authority
→ structure the workflow
→ measure quality and failure
→ automate bounded parts
→ monitor and improve
```

Automation introduced before observability hides risk.

A human process may be slow but visible. An AI process may be fast while silently producing wrong dates, outdated requirements or unsupported confidence. The system should not increase autonomy until it can detect and recover from failure.

## 5. Professional Authority is contextual

Professional qualification is usually jurisdictional and role-specific.

A person admitted to practice law in one jurisdiction may not be authorized in another. A trademark agent may perform certain office procedures but not broader legal services. A provider Organization may be eligible only when a named professional accepts the matter.

MarkOrbit therefore cannot treat “lawyer” or “agent” as a universal permission.

Professional Authority should reference:

- the person or provider;
- jurisdiction;
- qualification type;
- current status;
- permitted action;
- engagement or matter context;
- conflict status;
- effective period;
- verification evidence.

The system must also recognize that some work requires professional judgment without a legally protected signature, while other work is reserved even when the operational steps appear simple.

## 6. Certification is not professional qualification

MarkOrbit may certify that a user can perform a capability under a defined standard.

That certificate can be valuable. It may support task eligibility, reviewer trust and career progression.

It does not create a government-issued or legally recognized professional qualification.

```text
Capability Certification
= evidence of competence under a defined platform standard

Professional Qualification
= authority recognized by the applicable legal or professional system
```

A certified user may prepare a filing package and still require an authorized attorney to approve and submit it.

## 7. Production Authorization is a separate gate

Even a capable and qualified person should not automatically gain unrestricted production access.

Production Authorization answers whether the actor may work on real customer matters through a specific system and scope.

It may depend on:

- identity verification;
- confidentiality agreement;
- conflict controls;
- capability evidence;
- supervision level;
- quality history;
- Product and Workplace context;
- data-access approval;
- current capacity.

Production Authorization can be narrower than capability. It can also be suspended without denying that the person remains competent.

## 8. The customer has authority, but not every authority

The customer controls objectives, factual instructions, budget, risk preferences and many approvals.

The customer does not control official law or professional obligations.

A customer may request that unused goods remain in a declaration. The system and professional cannot treat approval as permission to make a false statement. A customer may request immediate filing without a required document. The professional must explain whether later filing is lawful, risky or impossible.

Good service is not blind execution. It is the translation of customer intent into lawful and professionally responsible action.

## 9. The platform must disclose AI participation meaningfully

A generic notice that “AI may be used” is not enough for consequential professional work.

Disclosure should be proportionate to the action.

The system may need to show:

- whether AI retrieved sources;
- whether AI generated the draft;
- whether a human reviewed it;
- the reviewer’s role;
- unresolved uncertainty;
- whether the output is informational, recommended or approved;
- whether formal action has occurred.

The purpose is not to overwhelm the customer with technical details. It is to prevent false impressions of authority and completion.

## 10. AI provenance is part of professional evidence

For important results, MarkOrbit should retain enough provenance to reconstruct how the result was produced.

Depending on the capability, this may include:

- model and version;
- instruction or prompt reference;
- retrieved sources;
- input snapshot;
- tool calls;
- generated output;
- confidence or uncertainty signals;
- human modifications;
- approval or rejection;
- later outcome.

This evidence supports quality review, incident investigation and model replacement.

It should be retained under privacy and rights controls rather than indiscriminately.

## 11. Human review must be a real action

Many systems claim “human in the loop” when a person merely receives the output or clicks a button.

A meaningful review should state:

```text
what was reviewed
against which standard
with which authority
what decision was made
what exceptions remain
who is responsible for the disposition
```

Review may produce:

- approved;
- approved with conditions;
- revision required;
- rejected;
- escalated;
- unable to determine.

The reviewer should not be asked to approve beyond the reviewer’s own capability and authority.

## 12. Segregation of duties protects high-risk actions

Some actions should require more than one actor or role.

Examples include:

- preparation and final legal approval;
- provider recommendation and provider appointment;
- invoice creation and payment release;
- evidence collection and declaration approval;
- contribution submission and final acceptance;
- data correction and canonical publication.

Segregation of duties reduces fraud, error and conflicts of interest.

It should be applied according to risk rather than to every trivial action.

## 13. AI agents require bounded identity and permission

An AI agent should not operate as an invisible extension of the platform.

It should have a declared execution identity, permitted tools, data scope, purpose, duration and escalation policy.

An agent working in Lite Content Studio should not automatically access private MarkReg matters. An agent preparing an MGSN instruction should not appoint a provider. An agent that discovers a maintenance opportunity should not create an order or send a legal notice without authority.

The same least-privilege principle that applies to human users should apply to AI agents.

## 14. Unknown is safer than invented certainty

Professional systems frequently encounter ambiguity:

- the official system has not updated;
- a provider claims filing but has not supplied a receipt;
- the source conflicts with a previous record;
- the model cannot determine whether evidence covers the goods;
- a customer answer is incomplete;
- a deadline depends on an uncertain event.

MarkOrbit must allow an explicit Unknown state.

Unknown is not failure. It is a condition requiring reconciliation, additional evidence or escalation.

A system that forces every case into success or failure will eventually create false facts.

## 15. Responsibility remains distributed but explicit

The final outcome may involve many actors:

```text
customer
Relationship Owner
MarkReg production team
AI systems
certified contributor
reviewer
local attorney
MGSN provider
Delivery Owner
```

Distributed work does not require ambiguous responsibility.

Each engagement should identify who owns the customer relationship, who coordinates delivery, who has professional authority, who performs each contribution and who accepts the final Outcome.

No participant should acquire rights merely because the participant was technically able to access the work.

## 16. The correct goal is governed leverage

The purpose of AI in MarkOrbit is not to remove humans from every step.

It is to increase the number and quality of professional outcomes that responsible people and Workplaces can deliver.

AI should:

- reduce repetitive work;
- make sources easier to use;
- expose missing facts;
- support inexperienced users;
- accelerate drafting;
- improve consistency;
- expand access to scarce capability.

Human and professional governance should:

- preserve context;
- resolve ambiguity;
- protect customers;
- exercise reserved authority;
- accept responsibility;
- correct the system.

The mature architecture is therefore not human versus AI.

It is a governed allocation of intelligence, competence, authority and responsibility.
