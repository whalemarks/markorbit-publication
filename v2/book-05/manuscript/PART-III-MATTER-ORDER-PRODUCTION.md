# Part III — Matter, Order and Production Architecture

## Chapter 22 — Matter and Order are different

A Matter represents the continuing professional and legal context around a trademark objective. An Order represents a commercial commitment to deliver a defined service.

One Matter may contain several Orders across time:

- initial search;
- filing;
- office-action response;
- registration service;
- declaration or renewal;
- transfer;
- restoration;
- watch or enforcement support.

Keeping Matter and Order separate allows the customer history to remain continuous without treating every future action as already purchased.

## Chapter 23 — Service scope must be explicit

An Order should state:

- included jurisdictions and classes;
- included phases;
- official and professional fees;
- assumptions;
- required customer actions;
- exclusions;
- validity period;
- completion definition;
- refund or cancellation rules;
- expected Handoffs.

A low initial filing price must not conceal later mandatory phases.

## Chapter 24 — Order decomposition

A complete Order is decomposed into Work Packages that can be planned and governed.

For a United States maintenance Matter, Work Packages may include:

```text
WP01 official status and deadline verification
WP02 owner and address reconciliation
WP03 goods-use questionnaire
WP04 specimen and evidence screening
WP05 customer clarification
WP06 filing-plan and fee approval
WP07 attorney instruction
WP08 professional review
WP09 formal submission
WP10 receipt validation
WP11 customer delivery and future monitoring
```

The decomposition is reusable, but each Matter may add or omit packages based on risk and jurisdiction.

## Chapter 25 — Work Package contract

A Work Package defines:

- required capability;
- input version;
- expected output;
- permitted tools and AI mode;
- risk tier;
- production authorization;
- review requirement;
- deadline;
- data scope;
- completion evidence;
- compensation where applicable.

It is a controlled production unit, not an informal instruction sent through chat.

## Chapter 26 — Assignment

Assignment connects a Work Package to an eligible performer.

The performer may be:

- deterministic software;
- AI under a defined mode;
- MarkReg staff;
- a certified contributor;
- an expert reviewer;
- an MGSN provider;
- an approved external provider.

Assignment does not alter the Delivery Owner or customer relationship unless the Engagement explicitly says so.

## Chapter 27 — Production planning

The system plans dependencies and parallel work.

For example, search review may proceed while applicant documents are collected, but formal filing cannot proceed until mark, goods, applicant and approval versions are locked.

Planning should identify:

- critical path;
- customer dependencies;
- official deadlines;
- provider lead time;
- original-document courier time;
- payment checkpoints;
- review bottlenecks;
- fallback routes.

## Chapter 28 — Version locks

Professional mistakes often arise because different participants use different versions of the mark, goods or applicant data.

Before protected actions, MarkReg creates a version lock for:

```text
mark representation
applicant identity
address
classes and goods
filing basis
priority
translation and description
approved fees
provider instruction
```

A later change creates a new version and may invalidate earlier approval.

## Chapter 29 — Customer approval

Customer approval should be specific and intelligible.

The customer confirms the exact decision rather than clicking a generic approval button. The record should show:

- what was presented;
- material risks and trade-offs;
- what changed from prior versions;
- who approved;
- represented authority;
- timestamp;
- whether approval remains valid.

## Chapter 30 — Provider instruction package

A provider should receive a complete, structured instruction package rather than a long email chain.

The package includes:

- Matter identity;
- customer and relationship boundaries;
- approved filing data;
- documents;
- desired action;
- deadlines;
- agreed fees;
- required acknowledgement;
- expected official receipt or evidence;
- escalation channel.

## Chapter 31 — Contribution

A Contribution is the actual work returned against an Assignment.

Examples include:

- verified status report;
- search-review memo;
- evidence-screening result;
- corrected instruction sheet;
- provider filing receipt;
- expert review decision;
- customer delivery report.

Submission does not equal acceptance. Contributions pass validation and review.

## Chapter 32 — Review and acceptance

Review compares the Contribution against the Work Package contract.

Possible outcomes are:

```text
accepted
accepted with recorded limitation
revision required
escalated
rejected
outcome unknown
```

Review preserves comments, evidence and version history. Silent correction without attribution weakens the capability record and audit trail.

## Chapter 33 — Outcome assembly

The customer Outcome may combine many accepted Contributions.

MarkReg assembles them into a coherent delivery that explains:

- what was completed;
- current official status;
- relevant dates;
- documents and receipts;
- unresolved risk;
- customer next action;
- future lifecycle events.

The customer should not need to interpret internal task fragments.
