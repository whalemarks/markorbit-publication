# Part II — Customer Decision and Lifecycle Architecture

## Chapter 10 — Start from the customer's objective

A filing form is not the beginning of a trademark service. The beginning is the business objective.

MarkReg should determine:

- what name, logo or brand element must be protected;
- where the customer currently uses or plans to use it;
- which markets are essential and which are optional;
- what goods and services generate commercial risk;
- whether speed, budget, defensibility or broad coverage has priority;
- what evidence and documents already exist;
- who owns and controls the mark.

The product should ask as few questions as possible, but it must not hide uncertainty behind confident recommendations.

## Chapter 11 — Guided decision readiness

MarkReg converts incomplete customer information into a decision-ready set of options.

A good interaction does not force the customer to understand legal terminology before receiving value. It uses known customer, brand, product and jurisdiction context to infer likely answers, then asks only for unresolved decisions.

The output may contain:

```text
minimum required plan
recommended plan
broader strategic plan
cost-saving alternatives
identified risks
missing information
```

## Chapter 12 — Country recommendation

Country selection should reflect actual business need, risk and budget.

MarkReg may distinguish:

- essential jurisdictions;
- recommended jurisdictions;
- optional expansion jurisdictions;
- countries where protection should wait;
- grouped routes that may reduce cost or operational complexity.

Recommendations must state their assumptions. They are not promises of registrability or enforcement success.

## Chapter 13 — Goods and classification

Classification should begin with the customer's real products and services, not with an empty Nice-classification form.

MarkReg should identify:

- primary commercial goods;
- required goods;
- recommended adjacent goods;
- jurisdiction-specific item limits;
- unacceptable or overly broad descriptions;
- cross-class protection needs;
- goods that require proof of use or raise cost.

The system may propose A/B/C packages rather than asking the customer to select from hundreds of unfamiliar terms.

## Chapter 14 — Search and risk assessment

Search combines:

- exact and variation search;
- phonetic, semantic and visual candidates;
- goods and class relationships;
- owner and series-mark context;
- official status;
- professional review.

The report must distinguish:

```text
source fact
system-derived candidate
AI inference
professional opinion
reserved legal advice
```

A high volume of results is not a high-quality search. The value lies in relevance, explanation and an actionable plan.

## Chapter 15 — Applicant and ownership readiness

Errors in applicant identity, legal form or address can persist across the entire lifecycle.

MarkReg should reconcile:

- legal name;
- business-registration information;
- entity type;
- address order and official formatting;
- prior names;
- beneficial or commercial context where relevant;
- authority of the signatory;
- ownership consistency across jurisdictions.

Candidate entity matches remain candidates until verified.

## Chapter 16 — Mark representation

The system should govern:

- word versus device filing;
- color claims;
- translation and transliteration;
- mark description;
- disclaimers;
- non-Latin characters;
- image quality;
- consistency with actual intended use.

AI may detect and draft, but the filing representation must be confirmed under the relevant jurisdictional rules.

## Chapter 17 — Document readiness

Document requirements may include:

- Power of Attorney;
- business registration;
- identity document;
- priority document;
- translation;
- notarization or legalization;
- use evidence;
- consent or assignment documents.

MarkReg distinguishes:

```text
required before filing
permitted after filing
original required
scan accepted
legalization required
requirement uncertain and needing provider confirmation
```

## Chapter 18 — Filing Handoff

Where local authority is required, MarkReg creates a typed Handoff containing only approved information and instructions.

The Handoff records:

- origin and destination;
- customer and relationship context;
- mark and goods version;
- documents;
- filing basis;
- deadlines;
- commercial terms;
- required Return artifacts;
- unresolved questions.

Transmission is not completion. Provider acceptance and formal filing remain separate states.

## Chapter 19 — Prosecution

Office actions, formalities, objections, oppositions and disputes vary widely in risk.

MarkReg should classify:

- purely formal correction;
- evidence request;
- identification amendment;
- substantive registrability refusal;
- prior-right conflict;
- opposition or cancellation;
- action requiring locally qualified strategy.

AI may summarize and draft. Professional Authority decides the final legal position where required.

## Chapter 20 — Registration and post-registration lifecycle

Registration is not the end of the Matter.

MarkReg maintains:

- certificate receipt and validation;
- declaration and renewal windows;
- ownership and address changes;
- use-evidence preparation;
- watch and enforcement opportunities;
- assignment and licensing;
- restoration and re-filing;
- customer delivery and future reminders.

The lifecycle should remain connected to the original decision, goods and evidence history.

## Chapter 21 — Transaction and transfer services

Trademark commerce initiated in Lite may enter MarkReg for:

- ownership and status due diligence;
- authorization verification;
- agreement and document preparation;
- payment and completion milestones;
- assignment recordal;
- evidence of completed transfer;
- final buyer and seller delivery.

A commercial match is not a completed legal transfer. MarkReg provides the governed bridge between them.
