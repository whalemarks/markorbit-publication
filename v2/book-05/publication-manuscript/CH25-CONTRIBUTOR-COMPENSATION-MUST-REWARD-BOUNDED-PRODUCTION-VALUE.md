# Chapter 25 — Contributor Compensation Must Reward Bounded Production Value

A governed production network needs fair compensation as much as it needs capability evidence and review.

If MarkReg treats contributors as interchangeable low-cost labor, quality will collapse, customer relationships will be exposed and the strongest professionals will leave. If it pays only for time without defining Outcomes, cost becomes unpredictable and accountability weakens.

The economic unit should therefore be the bounded Work Package.

```text
Work Package
→ clear offer
→ accepted Assignment
→ scoped Contribution
→ review result
→ compensation record
→ capability evidence
```

## 1. Compensation and capability evidence are separate

Accepted paid work should create two distinct records:

```text
Compensation Record
Capability Evidence Record
```

Compensation reflects agreed production value. Capability evidence reflects quality, independence, review findings and scope.

```text
Payment
≠ Proof of Competence

Certification Score
≠ Payment Entitlement
```

A contributor should not have to buy training or certification to obtain fair payment for accepted production.

## 2. The offer must precede the work

Before acceptance, the contributor should see:

- Work Package purpose;
- required Capability and authorization level;
- source Product and Workplace;
- inputs and data sensitivity;
- expected deliverable;
- deadline;
- estimated effort;
- review criteria;
- revision obligations;
- compensation;
- premiums or deductions;
- payment trigger;
- cancellation and dispute rules;
- permitted AI tools;
- rights and confidentiality.

```text
Task Visibility
≠ Task Acceptance
```

Hidden scope is not a legitimate compensation model.

## 3. Compensation components may be typed

A Work Package may use:

- standard task price;
- complexity premium;
- urgency premium;
- language premium;
- jurisdiction premium;
- data-sensitivity premium;
- professional-qualification premium;
- review compensation;
- milestone payment;
- quality incentive;
- correction adjustment where contractually defined.

The formula must not encourage contributors to conceal problems or avoid escalation merely to preserve payment.

## 4. Value is more than time

Compensation may reflect:

```text
Effort
+ Scarcity of Capability
+ Consequence of Error
+ Responsibility
+ Urgency
+ Required Independence
+ Rights Transferred
```

A five-minute expert review may create more value than hours of routine data normalization. Time remains relevant, but it is not the only measure.

## 5. Training cannot replace payment for real production

Simulation, assessment and shadow work may be unpaid when they do not control or contribute to a real customer Outcome and the terms are clear.

Once work creates actual production value, the system should address compensation.

```text
Learning Opportunity
≠ Free Customer Production
```

Supervised work may create both learning value and payable value.

## 6. Revision rules must distinguish cause

A revision may result from:

- contributor error;
- incomplete original work;
- ambiguous instructions;
- new customer facts;
- changed official requirements;
- changed scope;
- reviewer preference not stated in the rubric;
- source failure.

Only the first categories may justify unpaid correction under agreed terms. Material scope change requires an amendment.

```text
Revision Requested
≠ Contributor Fault Proven
```

## 7. Rejection requires a reason and evidence

Possible review outcomes include:

```text
Accepted
Accepted with Recorded Limitation
Partially Accepted
Revision Required
Rejected for Defined Failure
Cancelled because Inputs or Scope Failed
Outcome Unknown
```

A rejection should identify the unmet criterion and preserve the work, review and appeal trail.

Vague statements such as “not good enough” cannot support reliable capability or payment decisions.

## 8. Reviewer compensation must be explicit

Review is production work. Reviewer compensation may account for:

- risk tier;
- volume;
- specialist knowledge;
- calibration responsibility;
- correction feedback;
- escalation;
- final-authority responsibility where applicable.

The platform should not rely on unpaid senior review to subsidize low-priced production.

## 9. Contributor access does not create customer rights

Compensation for a Work Package does not authorize the contributor to:

- contact the customer outside scope;
- solicit future work;
- retain customer contacts;
- reuse private pricing;
- publish the case;
- bypass the Relationship Owner.

The restrictions should be purpose-limited and proportionate. They should not prevent service to independently sourced customers or use of general professional knowledge.

## 10. Rights in Contributions need explicit treatment

The terms should state whether the Contribution is:

- customer-owned production;
- Workplace-owned work product;
- licensed material;
- confidential review feedback;
- a Knowledge Candidate;
- eligible for de-identified learning;
- eligible for public portfolio use.

```text
Compensation Paid
≠ Unrestricted Reuse Right

Contribution Accepted
≠ AI Training Consent
```

## 11. Payment triggers must be visible

Triggers may include:

- submission;
- reviewer acceptance;
- milestone completion;
- Delivery Owner acceptance;
- customer Outcome completion;
- expiry of a defined dispute period.

The system should also record:

- gross amount;
- currency;
- platform or Workplace fee;
- tax or withholding;
- adjustment;
- payment status;
- payment evidence;
- dispute state.

This chapter does not authorize platform payment custody.

## 12. Cancellation and partial work

When a task is cancelled, compensation should consider:

- work completed;
- reusable accepted artifacts;
- reason for cancellation;
- whether the contributor caused the failure;
- customer or source changes;
- urgency reservation;
- access revocation and evidence retention.

A contributor should not bear the entire loss when a customer changes instructions after substantial work.

## 13. Quality incentives must not create bad behavior

A quality incentive may reward:

- first-pass acceptance;
- reliable source use;
- clear Unknowns;
- timely escalation;
- low justified rework;
- later Outcome success.

It should not reward:

- silence about risk;
- avoidance of difficult tasks;
- overconfident conclusions;
- manipulation of reviewers;
- acceptance-rate gaming.

## 14. No reverse auction for the lowest price

MarkReg should not route professional work through anonymous lowest-price bidding.

Routing should consider:

- eligibility;
- quality history;
- Capability fit;
- capacity;
- conflict;
- review availability;
- total expected cost;
- relationship and data risk.

```text
Lowest Bid
≠ Best Delivery Route
```

## 15. Appeals and disputes

A contributor should be able to challenge:

- wrong task attribution;
- changed scope;
- reviewer inconsistency;
- missing source materials;
- unfair rejection;
- unpaid accepted work;
- incorrect quality records.

The appeal route should preserve the original review and subsequent decision.

## 16. Product implications

MarkReg requires:

- typed compensation components;
- task-offer versions;
- Assignment acceptance;
- review and rejection reasons;
- amendment and revision classification;
- reviewer compensation;
- rights and reuse terms;
- payment triggers and evidence;
- cancellation and partial-payment logic;
- contributor dispute and appeal;
- links to Capability evidence.

## 17. Operating principle

```text
Define the Outcome
Show the terms
Pay for real value
Separate learning from production
Do not make revision unlimited
Protect rights and relationships
Record compensation separately from capability
```

> A professional contribution network becomes durable only when accepted work is paid fairly, rejected work is explained, and customer protection does not become a pretext for extracting invisible labor.
