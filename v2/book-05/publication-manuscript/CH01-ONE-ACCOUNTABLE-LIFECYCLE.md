# Chapter 01 — One Accountable Lifecycle

A customer rarely arrives with a complete trademark-service specification.

They arrive with a business objective:

- protect a new name before launch;
- register a logo in several markets;
- respond to an objection;
- rescue an abandoned application;
- renew a portfolio;
- transfer a registered mark;
- understand whether a foreign agent has actually filed;
- decide which countries and classes matter most.

The apparent simplicity belongs to the objective. The work underneath it is distributed across legal identity, commercial choice, professional judgment, official rules, documents, payments, local Providers, deadlines and evidence.

MarkReg exists to turn that fragmentation into one accountable lifecycle.

The word **one** requires care. It does not mean one database, one organization, one professional or one status. It means the customer and responsible Workplace can follow a coherent chain of decisions and outcomes without personally managing every system and participant.

```text
Customer objective
→ decision-ready options
→ accepted service scope
→ sufficient intake
→ governed preparation
→ professional review and approval
→ authorized execution
→ evidence-backed external result
→ continuing lifecycle responsibility
```

## 1. The customer asks for an outcome

A customer who says, “Please register this trademark in the United States,” has not yet decided every element required for a safe filing.

The service may still need to resolve:

- whether the customer means a word mark, a logo or both;
- who should be the applicant;
- whether the applicant already exists in Workplace records and is still current;
- whether use has begun and what evidence exists;
- which goods and services support the real business;
- whether search is justified before filing;
- whether speed, breadth, budget or defensibility has priority;
- whether the mark contains non-Latin characters requiring translation or transliteration;
- whether a description or disclaimer may be needed;
- who can sign;
- which professional is qualified and authorized;
- what fees are official, professional, external or contingent;
- what later examination and maintenance obligations may arise.

A system that records only the customer's first sentence has captured demand. It has not yet created an executable service.

## 2. Fragmentation becomes customer uncertainty

International trademark work is fragmented for legitimate reasons.

Official offices control their own procedures. Local professionals operate under jurisdiction-specific qualifications. Customer relationships belong to independent businesses. Payments, documents, evidence and formal business state may be maintained by different services. Rules and fees change at different times.

The problem is not that these responsibilities are distributed. The problem is that customers and professionals are often forced to reconstruct the distribution manually through email, attachments, spreadsheets and memory.

The customer may see only:

```text
Instruction sent
→ silence
→ agent says filed
→ no receipt
→ later objection
→ new fee request
→ certificate or abandonment
```

The hidden uncertainty includes:

- what was actually instructed;
- which version was approved;
- who was responsible;
- whether payment was required before filing;
- whether a Provider accepted the work;
- whether the office acknowledged the filing;
- whether a later deadline was identified;
- what evidence supports the displayed status.

MarkReg makes these transitions visible and attributable.

## 3. Partial tools solve partial moments

A form can collect filing fields. A case system can coordinate work after a Matter exists. An AI conversation can explain and draft. A directory can show Providers. A connector can transmit data. A status dashboard can display external events.

Each may be valuable. None alone creates the full lifecycle.

| Partial solution | Valuable contribution | Missing lifecycle responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| filing form | formal data collection | earlier strategy, alternatives and readiness |
| questionnaire | broader information capture | prioritization, inference discipline and decision support |
| AI chat | extraction, comparison and drafting | stable authority, version, review and formal state |
| case system | post-instruction coordination | pre-Order need, option and commercial formation |
| Provider directory | possible external routes | eligibility, conflict, appointment, acceptance and recovery |
| connector | transmission | preparation, approval, acknowledgement and later obligations |
| status dashboard | convenient visibility | source hierarchy, interpretation and correction |

MarkReg does not need to replace every tool. It must compose their outputs into a governed Product journey.

## 4. A filing form begins after important decisions

Traditional digital filing systems often begin with:

- jurisdiction;
- applicant;
- mark;
- class;
- goods and services;
- priority;
- representative;
- signature;
- payment.

Those fields appear objective because they resemble official forms. They are the result of earlier decisions.

Before the form, someone must decide:

- which entity should own the right;
- whether separate filing units are needed;
- which markets are essential;
- which route balances cost and coverage;
- which goods reflect present and planned use;
- whether the specification creates unnecessary cost or vulnerability;
- whether documentary requirements can be satisfied;
- whether the timing is realistic.

When the Product begins with the form, it transfers professional complexity to the customer and hides the reasoning that produced the final fields.

MarkReg begins with the objective and moves toward the form only after the relevant context becomes sufficiently clear.

## 5. One lifecycle does not mean one progress bar

A trademark service contains several state planes.

At minimum, MarkReg must distinguish:

- Product Journey State;
- recommendation and Decision State;
- commercial State;
- formal Customer, Order and Matter State;
- intake and readiness State;
- preparation, Review and approval State;
- execution State;
- Provider State;
- official State;
- continuing-right and portfolio State.

These planes change independently.

A customer may accept a quotation while the applicant remains unresolved. A filing package may pass professional review while payment is missing. A Provider may report submission while the official receipt remains unavailable. A registration may be verified while maintenance obligations remain unconfirmed.

The interface may present a coherent journey. It must not compress every plane into “20%, 60%, complete.”

## 6. Critical distinctions define the lifecycle

The following separations are ordinary operating discipline:

```text
Need understood
≠ recommendation complete

Recommendation presented
≠ customer Decision

Customer Decision
≠ accepted commercial scope

Quotation accepted
≠ Customer Instruction

Customer Instruction
≠ formal Order or Matter

Intake complete
≠ professionally sufficient

Prepared package
≠ reviewed package

Reviewed package
≠ approved external action

Execution requested
≠ submission sent

Provider reports filed
≠ official acknowledgement

Official acknowledgement
≠ registration

Registration
≠ maintenance compliance

Assignment signed
≠ official ownership recordal completed
```

A trustworthy Product makes the distinction easier to understand. It does not remove the distinction from the underlying records.

## 7. Accountability requires identifiable responsibility

A coherent lifecycle should be able to answer:

```text
Who acted?
For which Workplace or represented party?
In what role?
Under what authority?
On which object and version?
Using which evidence?
What Decision or action occurred?
What consequence followed?
What remains unresolved?
```

Responsibility may be distributed among:

- Relationship Owner;
- Contracting Party;
- Payment Receiver;
- Delivery Owner;
- Professional Authority;
- internal staff;
- Contributor;
- Reviewer;
- external Provider;
- Owning Service;
- official office.

The customer should not have to coordinate all of them. MarkReg should preserve their attribution so that unified service does not become ambiguous responsibility.

The lifecycle sits inside the broader MarkOrbit architecture.

```text

Governed execution
→ coordinates Tasks, Reviews, approvals and protected actions

Owning Service
```

MarkReg may display information from all these layers. Display does not transfer ownership.

A unified customer screen can therefore show an Order, a Matter, a prepared filing package, a Provider status and an official receipt while preserving the authoritative source of each.

## 9. Full lifecycle begins before filing

The lifecycle begins when a real need becomes understandable enough to evaluate.

Early stages may include:

- business objective;
- brand and product context;
- applicant and ownership context;
- market priority;
- jurisdiction strategy;
- mark versions;
- search options;
- commercial alternatives;
- preliminary document feasibility.

These stages matter because later formal fields depend on them.

If MarkReg starts only after an Order or Matter exists, it becomes a trademark-specific case-management surface. It loses the ability to improve how customers and professionals arrive at the formal work.

## 10. Full lifecycle continues after registration

Registration creates a right baseline, not an endpoint.

Continuing work may include:

- certificate and official-record verification;
- use requirements;
- declarations and maintenance filings;
- renewal and grace periods;
- owner, address or representative changes;
- assignments and licences;
- vulnerability and monitoring;
- enforcement and defensive work;
- portfolio rationalization;
- transaction preparation.

The decisions made during filing remain relevant. Applicant history, mark version, goods wording, evidence and Provider communications may affect later prosecution, maintenance or transfer.

A full-lifecycle Product preserves the lineage so the Workplace does not reconstruct it from old email every few years.

## 11. One brand may contain many independent rights

A global brand initiative is not one global trademark state.

One brand may have:

- a registered UK right;
- a US application under examination;
- an EU opposition;
- a Japan filing candidate not yet instructed;
- an Australian application waiting for documents.

A Portfolio view may summarize them, but each right retains its own:

- applicant and owner;
- filing unit;
- package and approval;
- Provider and cost;
- official identifier;
- procedural history;
- deadlines;
- evidence;
- outcome.

```text
Portfolio view
≠ one combined legal right
```

## 12. Version continuity matters as much as status

Trademark work changes through versions.

Material changes may involve:

- applicant identity;
- mark representation;
- country or route;
- class and goods;
- priority;
- search findings;
- document availability;
- official fee;
- Provider;
- response strategy;
- deadline evidence.

A new version may invalidate an earlier quotation, Review, approval or instruction.

MarkReg should preserve:

```text
source version
→ Decision made from that version
→ material change
→ impact assessment
→ superseding version
→ revalidation, repricing or reapproval
```

Supersession is not deletion. Historical versions explain why earlier actions were reasonable and where later corrections began.

## 13. Unknown is an honest lifecycle state

External work frequently produces uncertain states.

Examples include:

- a submission request timed out after transmission;
- a Provider says the application was filed but has not supplied a receipt;
- a payment was debited without a confirmed order result;
- an official database is unavailable;
- two sources show different deadlines;
- an original document was sent but delivery is unclear.

The unsafe response is to mark the task complete or retry blindly.

The accountable response records:

- what was attempted;
- what evidence exists;
- what remains unknown;
- whether duplicate or deadline risk exists;
- who must reconcile the state;
- when escalation occurs;
- what would permit a safe retry.

```text
Unknown
≠ failed automatically
≠ completed automatically
≠ safe to repeat automatically
```

## 14. Evidence turns progress into trustworthy state

The Product should distinguish:

- internal preparation record;
- approval event;
- connector delivery evidence;
- Provider acceptance;
- Provider report;
- official acknowledgement;
- official record;
- professional interpretation;
- customer-facing projection.

A Provider email saying “done” may be operationally important. It is not the same as an official filing receipt.

A copied registry status may be useful for display. It is not authoritative without source and freshness context.

Each consequential state should carry enough provenance to support the claim being made.

## 15. Honest stopping is part of service quality

The lifecycle may stop because:

- the applicant cannot be verified;
- the mark is unstable;
- the customer has not chosen between alternatives;
- a required original cannot be obtained;
- search risk is unacceptable;
- no qualified Provider is available;
- the customer declines the cost;
- a deadline cannot be verified;
- professional review rejects the proposed route.

A Product failure occurs when the system fabricates progress to keep the funnel moving.

A Product success can be a clear recommendation not to proceed, accompanied by the evidence and conditions that would allow reconsideration.

## 16. The customer experience should remain intelligible

Internal state discipline should improve, not overwhelm, the customer experience.

Customer-facing language may say:

- “We are confirming the applicant and filing scope before preparing the application.”
- “The filing package is under professional review.”
- “The package is approved and awaiting submission.”
- “The local Provider reports submission; the official receipt is still pending.”
- “Registration has been verified. We are confirming future maintenance obligations.”

The Product can hide unnecessary internal implementation detail while preserving meaningful uncertainty and next action.

## 17. A reference journey

Consider a customer seeking protection for one brand in the United States, European Union and Japan.

### Objective

The customer wants to launch within four months and has a limited first-year budget.

### Decision readiness

MarkReg identifies:

- the United States as essential;
- the European Union as strategically valuable but higher risk;
- Japan as important but document-sensitive;
- separate word and device filing options;
- a search decision before final filing scope.

### Commercial formation

The customer selects a minimum plan for the United States and EU, deferring Japan until documentation is ready.

### Intake

The applicant name conflicts with an earlier company record. The Product keeps the match provisional and requests current registration evidence.

### Preparation

The US and EU packages diverge because goods wording, filing basis and Provider requirements differ.

### Execution

The EU Provider accepts. The US route uses a separate authorized professional. Each package receives its own approval and instruction.

### Return

The EU Provider reports filing and supplies a receipt. The US professional reports submission but no official acknowledgement is available. MarkReg shows different states.

### Continuing lifecycle

An EU opposition later arises while the US application enters examination. Japan remains a candidate. The portfolio view remains coherent without declaring the brand globally registered.

The value of MarkReg is the preserved relationship between every stage, not a universal green status.

## 18. Product and technical implications

One accountable lifecycle requires:

- stable cross-system identifiers;
- typed state domains;
- source and evidence provenance;
- version and supersession relationships;
- explicit role and authority records;
- Product-local journey state;
- references to formal Owning Service state;
- typed Handoff and Return contracts;
- independent jurisdiction and filing-unit records;
- Unknown and reconciliation workflows;
- continuing-right obligations;
- customer-facing projections with freshness.

The interface may be unified. The data and authority model must remain plural where reality is plural.

## 19. Commercial implications

A lifecycle Product can earn more durable revenue than a filing portal because it supports:

- strategy and intake;
- search and preparation;
- filing and external fulfillment;
- prosecution;
- registration delivery;
- maintenance and monitoring;
- transaction and recordal;
- premium review and recovery.

The commercial opportunity does not justify forcing every customer into one contracting relationship or inventing later-stage work.

Revenue should follow an accepted service scope and an accountable role.

```text
One lifecycle
```
