# Part VII — Evidence, Settlement and Platform Learning

## Chapter 101 — Execution produces evidence

Every meaningful transition should leave sufficient evidence for review, customer explanation, dispute resolution and system improvement.

Evidence may include:

- request and input versions;
- eligibility result;
- Assignment;
- Contribution;
- Review;
- approval;
- external attempt;
- Return;
- settlement trigger;
- final Outcome;
- incident and correction records.

## Chapter 102 — Evidence is typed

Not all evidence has the same authority.

Execution distinguishes:

```text
user statement
official record
provider statement
professional opinion
AI inference
system observation
payment evidence
delivery evidence
```

The type determines how the evidence may support formal state or later learning.

## Chapter 103 — Provenance

Every evidence object should preserve origin, capture time, version, integrity reference, permissions and relevant authority.

A screenshot without source context may be useful but should not be treated as equivalent to an official downloadable receipt.

## Chapter 104 — Audit event

Audit events explain who did what, when, under which authority and against which version.

Audit is append-oriented. Corrections create new events rather than rewriting history.

## Chapter 105 — Event and business state

An Event records that something occurred. An Owning Service determines whether and how that event changes formal business state.

Execution may emit a `ProviderReturnReceived` event without marking a filing accepted until validation succeeds.

## Chapter 106 — Settlement trigger

Compensation and payment should connect to defined execution evidence.

Examples include:

- Task Offer accepted;
- Contribution submitted;
- Contribution accepted;
- review completed;
- provider milestone confirmed;
- customer payment captured;
- transaction completed.

The commercial arrangement determines which trigger creates an amount due.

## Chapter 107 — Contributor compensation

Contributor compensation records:

- Work Package;
- accepted scope;
- base fee;
- complexity or urgency premium;
- review or mentor fee;
- quality incentive;
- revision deduction if contractually valid;
- tax or payout status;
- dispute state.

Capability use and human labor remain economically distinct.

## Chapter 108 — Provider settlement

MGSN provider economics remain distinct from internal contributor compensation.

Provider settlement may include official fees, professional fees, disbursements, exchange handling, deposits, release milestones, refunds and replacement policy.

## Chapter 109 — Transaction settlement

Trademark-commerce execution may require:

- buyer funds milestone;
- seller entitlement;
- referral or brokerage commission;
- transfer-service fee;
- professional fee;
- refund or failure policy;
- completion evidence.

Execution records the conditions but does not assume payment custody unless the applicable authority and product explicitly support it.

## Chapter 110 — Dispute evidence

A dispute package should reconstruct:

- agreed Engagement;
- scope and versions;
- communication;
- work performed;
- reviews;
- customer decisions;
- provider actions;
- payments;
- final or partial Outcomes.

This reduces dependence on selective email recollection.

## Chapter 111 — Contribution evidence

Accepted work can improve a person's Capability record.

Evidence weight depends on:

- task difficulty;
- independence level;
- reviewer authority;
- revision count;
- later outcome;
- recency;
- jurisdiction relevance.

## Chapter 112 — Performance Profile

Execution can derive a governed performance profile containing:

- acceptance rate;
- revision rate;
- deadline reliability;
- reviewer findings;
- complexity handled;
- escalation quality;
- outcome stability.

Private performance data is not automatically exposed to the full network.

## Chapter 113 — Capability feedback

Repeated execution patterns may reveal:

- unclear input contracts;
- weak checklists;
- missing training;
- inappropriate automation;
- reviewer bottlenecks;
- jurisdiction exceptions;
- outdated knowledge.

These become Capability Improvement Candidates rather than immediate specification changes.

## Chapter 114 — Data correction candidate

When execution detects an incorrect owner, status, event, contact or deadline source, it creates a candidate with evidence and confidence.

The Data Refinery validates and publishes corrections under its own governance.

## Chapter 115 — Knowledge candidate

A useful professional explanation, provider requirement or case lesson may become a Knowledge Candidate.

Promotion requires rights, provenance, de-identification, source classification, review and version policy.

## Chapter 116 — Simulation candidate

A real incident may suggest a valuable training case.

The case must be de-identified or synthesized and checked for confidentiality, rights and pedagogical value before entering the Simulation Lab.

## Chapter 117 — No silent canonization

```text
successful outcome
≠ universal rule

expert opinion
≠ canonical knowledge

repeated pattern
≠ automatic policy
```

Execution supplies evidence. Governance determines what becomes shared truth, training or standard.

## Chapter 118 — Metrics

Execution metrics should include more than automation rate.

Important measures include:

- time from Request to accepted Outcome;
- review bottlenecks;
- Unknown duration;
- recovery success;
- duplicate prevention;
- customer approval latency;
- contribution acceptance and revision;
- provider Return quality;
- evidence completeness;
- protected-action compliance.

## Chapter 119 — Ten-year evolution

From 2026 to 2027, Execution should prove two loops: Lite commerce and United States maintenance capability production.

From 2027 to 2030, it should standardize more Work Packages, simulations, contribution routes and MGSN returns.

From 2030 to 2033, it should improve evidence-based routing, organizational controls and cross-product orchestration.

From 2033 to 2036, it should become trusted infrastructure for allocating AI, human judgment and professional authority across global brand services.

## Chapter 120 — The trust-engine test

Execution succeeds when it can answer:

1. Who requested the work and for what purpose?
2. What authority permitted it?
3. Which version and evidence were used?
4. Who or what performed each part?
5. What review occurred?
6. What external action actually happened?
7. What remains uncertain?
8. Who accepted the Outcome?
9. What compensation or settlement followed?
10. What may legitimately be learned from the result?
