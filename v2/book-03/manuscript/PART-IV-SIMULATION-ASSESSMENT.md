# Part IV — Simulation, Assessment and Progression

## Chapter 41 — Simulation is governed execution without production risk

Simulation should resemble real service work while remaining isolated from real customers, official systems, payments and production mutation.

A Simulation Workplace contains synthetic or de-identified objects, explicit simulation status and blocked external actions.

## Chapter 42 — Case Fixture

A Case Fixture packages:

- customer profile;
- trademark and jurisdiction context;
- documents and evidence;
- hidden facts;
- expected events;
- capability targets;
- scoring rubric;
- reference outcomes;
- rights and provenance.

Fixtures are versioned because rule and product changes may alter the correct path.

## Chapter 43 — Scenario engine

The scenario engine may release information dynamically:

- a customer supplies a weak specimen;
- an address conflicts with a register;
- a provider requests an original POA;
- a deadline becomes urgent;
- the customer rejects a recommended deletion;
- an apparent status result proves outdated.

The learner must respond to changing context rather than memorize one answer.

## Chapter 44 — Simulated actors

AI or human participants may act as:

- customer;
- supervisor;
- reviewer;
- foreign associate;
- examiner;
- opposing party;
- platform support.

Every interaction remains attributable and part of the assessment record.

## Chapter 45 — Training mode

Training mode may provide hints, examples, source links and immediate explanations.

Errors become teaching events. The system may permit retries without treating the result as certification evidence.

## Chapter 46 — Practice mode

Practice mode reduces assistance and evaluates independent progress. It may show a score and feedback but does not necessarily create a formal credential.

## Chapter 47 — Formal assessment mode

Formal assessment locks the fixture version, assistance policy, time limits, allowed resources and identity controls.

The system records enough process evidence to detect outsourced or AI-completed work that does not demonstrate the candidate's competence.

## Chapter 48 — Assessment dimensions

Assessment should include:

```text
fact recognition
rule application
risk identification
customer communication
service design
system operation
human–AI judgment
professional escalation
delivery quality
```

A correct final answer may still be insufficient if the process ignored a major risk or used unauthorized action.

## Chapter 49 — Rubric

A rubric identifies:

- critical errors;
- required evidence;
- acceptable alternative reasoning;
- communication standards;
- escalation points;
- scoring weights;
- automatic failure conditions.

Rubrics need calibration across assessors.

## Chapter 50 — Examiner interaction

An assessor may ask the candidate to explain:

- why a source was trusted;
- why a product was removed;
- why AI advice was rejected;
- why a provider was selected;
- what uncertainty remains.

The response tests understanding beyond polished deliverables.

## Chapter 51 — Assessment Result

The result contains more than a pass or fail.

It may record:

- demonstrated capability level;
- assistance used;
- critical errors;
- strengths;
- required remediation;
- evidence references;
- assessor confidence;
- recommended next authorization.

## Chapter 52 — Capability Evidence

Accepted assessment evidence contributes to a user's capability record, subject to validity period and recency requirements.

A legal or procedural change may trigger reassessment or reduce the weight of old evidence.

## Chapter 53 — Shadow task

A shadow task allows the learner to perform real-context work without controlling the production outcome.

The learner's result is compared with the authorized production result and reviewed for reasoning and omissions.

## Chapter 54 — Supervised production

In supervised production, the contribution may enter the real workflow but cannot proceed without mandatory review.

Access remains bounded and the supervisor retains responsibility for acceptance.

## Chapter 55 — Bounded independent production

A user with sufficient evidence may complete defined standard tasks independently, subject to sampling, performance monitoring and escalation rules.

Independent does not mean unreviewable or unrestricted.

## Chapter 56 — Review authority

Review authority requires more than repeated production success.

The user must demonstrate:

- error detection;
- explanation quality;
- consistency;
- conflict awareness;
- ability to recognize limits;
- appropriate escalation;
- assessor calibration.

## Chapter 57 — Assessor qualification

Assessors themselves need defined capabilities, calibration evidence and conflict controls.

An expert practitioner is not automatically a reliable assessor. Assessment quality is a separate capability.

## Chapter 58 — Appeals and reassessment

Formal assessment should support:

- evidence-based appeal;
- independent re-review;
- correction of fixture or rubric defects;
- reassessment after remediation;
- credential suspension or restoration.

## Chapter 59 — Production authorization decision

Certification evidence informs but does not automatically grant production authorization.

Authorization may also require:

- identity and contract status;
- confidentiality;
- conflict checks;
- jurisdiction restrictions;
- quality history;
- available supervision;
- business need.

## Chapter 60 — Progression as a state machine

```text
Learning
→ Simulation Eligible
→ Assessed
→ Certified
→ Shadow Authorized
→ Supervised Production Authorized
→ Independent Standard Authorized
→ Advanced Authorized
→ Reviewer Authorized
→ Suspended or Expired
```

Transitions must be evidence-based and reversible.
