# Part III — Human–AI Modes and Dynamic Assistance

## Chapter 26 — Human–AI collaboration is policy

The relationship between AI and people must be chosen according to risk, capability and authority. It is not merely a user-interface preference.

Execution supports:

```text
M0 Human only
M1 AI reference
M2 AI assist
M3 AI draft, human approve
M4 AI execute under governance
M5 Hybrid professional network
```

## Chapter 27 — M0 Human only

M0 applies when policy, confidentiality, professional judgment or customer instruction excludes generative assistance from the substantive decision.

AI may still support permitted infrastructure such as search indexing or format validation, but it does not participate in the professional conclusion.

## Chapter 28 — M1 AI reference

AI retrieves and summarizes relevant information while the human independently forms the conclusion.

The system should preserve source citations, retrieval time and the distinction between official sources, professional commentary and inferred relevance.

## Chapter 29 — M2 AI assist

AI helps the user inspect, compare and organize work.

Examples include:

- highlighting inconsistent applicant information;
- proposing search strategies;
- grouping similar records;
- identifying missing documents;
- checking a draft against a rubric.

The human creates the substantive work product.

## Chapter 30 — M3 AI draft, human approve

AI produces a draft that remains non-operative until a qualified human reviews and specifically approves it.

Execution must record material edits and final disposition. A human opening or forwarding a draft does not constitute approval.

## Chapter 31 — M4 AI execute under governance

M4 is suitable for bounded, testable and reversible work where controls are mature.

Requirements may include:

- deterministic validation;
- approved data sources;
- confidence thresholds;
- exception routing;
- sampling;
- complete audit;
- reversible output;
- no reserved professional act.

## Chapter 32 — M5 Hybrid professional network

M5 combines AI, internal professionals, certified contributors, expert reviewers and MGSN providers.

Execution must make role transitions visible so the customer and Delivery Owner know which part was automated, reviewed or formally performed by a qualified provider.

## Chapter 33 — Model provenance

AI-assisted work should retain, where relevant:

- model or service identifier;
- model version or effective date;
- system and capability instruction version;
- input references;
- retrieved sources;
- tool calls;
- confidence or uncertainty;
- human disposition.

The purpose is reproducibility and accountability, not indiscriminate storage of sensitive prompts.

## Chapter 34 — Source grounding

An AI conclusion should identify whether it relies on:

- official structured data;
- official knowledge;
- reviewed professional guidance;
- Workplace-private knowledge;
- user-provided facts;
- inference.

Execution should block or escalate outputs that require current authority but lack an acceptable source.

## Chapter 35 — Uncertainty

AI should not hide uncertainty behind fluent language.

The execution record may classify uncertainty as:

```text
missing input
source conflict
outdated source risk
jurisdiction ambiguity
identity ambiguity
professional judgment required
external confirmation required
```

Each type has a different recovery path.

## Chapter 36 — Dynamic assistance by proficiency

The same Capability may expose different guidance according to the user's demonstrated level.

L1 and L2 users may receive:

- structured questions;
- examples;
- mandatory checks;
- required review;
- restricted actions.

L3 to L5 users may receive more flexible tools, but high-risk actions remain governed regardless of experience.

## Chapter 37 — Assistance is not permanent dependence

Execution should measure when a user repeatedly completes assisted work accurately and may qualify for lower supervision.

The platform's goal is not to keep every user in guided mode. It is to support evidence-based progression without weakening safety.

## Chapter 38 — AI memory boundaries

Workplace-private interactions do not automatically become shared memory.

Memory use must identify:

- source Workplace;
- permitted purpose;
- retention period;
- sensitivity;
- whether the content may support shared learning;
- deletion or portability rights.

## Chapter 39 — Agent permissions

An AI Agent receives explicit permissions, not ambient authority.

Permissions may cover:

- read scope;
- draft creation;
- internal task creation;
- communication preparation;
- provider recommendation;
- prohibited Apply actions.

An Agent cannot infer permission from access to an interface.

## Chapter 40 — Human override and escalation

A human may reject AI output, but override itself can require reason and authority when it bypasses a mandatory control.

Execution preserves both the machine recommendation and human decision so later review can distinguish justified professional judgment from control avoidance.
