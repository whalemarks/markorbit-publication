# Part II — Workflow, Work Packages and Collaboration

## Chapter 12 — Workflow, Work Package and Step

These concepts solve different problems.

```text
Workflow
coordinates an end-to-end goal

Work Package
defines a bounded unit of contribution

Step
represents an executable stage
```

A Work Package may be reusable across several Workflows. A Step may be automated or human. A Workflow may contain internal work, contributor assignments and MGSN Handoffs.

## Chapter 13 — Order and execution plan

An Order represents a commercial promise. Execution derives an Execution Plan that specifies how the promised outcome may be produced.

The plan includes:

- required capabilities;
- work packages;
- dependencies;
- risk tiers;
- approval gates;
- provider or contributor requirements;
- expected outputs;
- settlement triggers;
- failure and recovery paths.

The plan may change under governance without silently changing the customer's commercial promise.

## Chapter 14 — Work Package contract

A Work Package defines:

- bounded objective;
- input package;
- expected output;
- required capability and level;
- risk tier;
- deadline;
- visible data;
- allowed tools and actions;
- review standard;
- revision obligation;
- compensation basis;
- completion evidence.

A Work Package should be large enough to produce meaningful accountability and small enough to restrict unnecessary access.

## Chapter 15 — Assignment

Assignment binds a Work Package to an eligible performer.

It records:

- assignee identity and represented Workplace;
- acceptance time;
- production authorization;
- data scope;
- due date;
- communication route;
- compensation;
- reviewer;
- cancellation and replacement policy.

Viewing a task does not grant customer ownership or authority beyond the Assignment.

## Chapter 16 — Task offer and acceptance

For contribution-network work, the platform may create a Task Offer before Assignment.

The offer discloses sufficient information to decide eligibility and effort without exposing unnecessary customer data. Acceptance creates obligations only after conflict and access checks succeed.

## Chapter 17 — Contribution

A Contribution records what the performer actually submitted.

It should preserve:

- contributor;
- capability and level;
- input versions;
- tool and AI provenance;
- sources and reasoning where required;
- output artifacts;
- confidence;
- declared limitations;
- time and revision history.

Contribution is pending until accepted by policy or Review.

## Chapter 18 — Review

Review is a typed professional act, not a generic approval button.

A Review specifies:

- scope reviewed;
- applicable rubric;
- findings;
- accepted and rejected elements;
- required revisions;
- residual risks;
- reviewer authority;
- whether the output may proceed to Apply.

Review does not transfer responsibility outside its stated scope.

## Chapter 19 — Revision

Revision preserves lineage.

The system should not overwrite the prior Contribution. It creates a new version linked to the Review findings and identifies which defects were addressed.

Repeated revision may affect compensation, quality evidence and future eligibility according to disclosed policy.

## Chapter 20 — Outcome assembly

A customer-facing or formal Outcome may combine several accepted Contributions.

For example, a trademark visual optimization outcome may combine:

- AI candidate generation;
- visual designer assessment;
- trademark-continuity review;
- commercialization review;
- customer selection.

Execution assembles typed outputs without erasing disagreement or source roles.

## Chapter 21 — Multi-professional synthesis

Different professions should not be reduced to one undifferentiated score.

A synthesis may preserve dimensions such as:

```text
visual quality
trademark continuity
legal or procedural risk
commercial fit
implementation feasibility
```

Role-weighted synthesis is preferable to majority voting when responsibilities differ.

## Chapter 22 — Parallel and sequential work

Some Work Packages can proceed in parallel; others require locked upstream decisions.

Execution must model dependencies explicitly. Filing preparation should not proceed against an unapproved applicant version. Visual marketing may proceed from a concept version only when it is clearly separated from the filing representation.

## Chapter 23 — Delivery Owner

A distributed workflow still requires one Delivery Owner.

The Delivery Owner ensures:

- required Work Packages exist;
- roles and reviews are complete;
- conflicts are escalated;
- the assembled Outcome is coherent;
- customer communication is controlled;
- closure evidence is present.

The Delivery Owner does not replace the Professional Authority for reserved acts.

## Chapter 24 — Communication as execution evidence

Customer questions, contributor clarifications, reviewer decisions and provider instructions are part of work.

Important communication should be attached to the relevant Request, Assignment, Review or Handoff rather than remaining only in private email or chat.

## Chapter 25 — Engagement boundaries

Execution must operate within the accepted Engagement.

It may not silently:

- expand the scope;
- expose more data;
- add another subcontractor;
- change commercial roles;
- transfer the customer relationship;
- appoint a provider;
- reuse a Contribution for another purpose.

Material changes require an Engagement amendment or new approval.
