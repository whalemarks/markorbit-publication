# Book 03 v2
# MarkOrbit Execution System — Governed Human–AI Work

## Purpose

Execution governs how capabilities become controlled work. It coordinates people, AI, deterministic tools, Products, Owning Services and external providers without confusing orchestration with formal-state authority.

## Part I — Execution constitution

### Chapter 01 — Work is a governed state transition

Every meaningful action has an initiator, context, authority, input, expected outcome, review policy, failure model and evidence trail.

### Chapter 02 — Preview before Apply

Actions that may change formal state, expose protected data, spend funds, appoint providers or communicate externally require a previewable plan and a governed approval path.

### Chapter 03 — Unknown is a first-class state

Timeout, duplicate request, external silence and ambiguous provider response must not be simplified into success or failure. Unknown outcome requires reconciliation.

## Part II — Capability invocation

### Chapter 04 — Capability Request

A Capability Request binds:

- requested capability and version;
- Workplace and Product context;
- requester;
- purpose;
- input contract;
- risk level;
- entitlement;
- review policy;
- permitted implementation bindings.

### Chapter 05 — Eligibility

Before assignment or execution, the system evaluates certification, production authorization, professional qualification, jurisdiction, conflict, language, capacity and data-access scope.

### Chapter 06 — Implementation binding

A capability may be implemented by AI, deterministic software, a certified contributor, an internal MarkReg team, an expert reviewer or an MGSN provider. Binding is selected under policy, not assumed by the calling Product.

## Part III — Workflow and collaboration

### Chapter 07 — Workflow, Step and Work Package

Workflow coordinates a goal. WorkPackage defines a bounded contribution unit. Step is an executable stage. The three must not be collapsed.

### Chapter 08 — Assignment

Assignments specify recipient, deadline, compensation basis, visible data, allowed actions, review requirement and revocation policy.

### Chapter 09 — Contribution

A Contribution records what was submitted, by whom, under which capability and authority, using what evidence and tools. It remains pending until reviewed or accepted by policy.

### Chapter 10 — Multi-professional work

Some outcomes require distinct perspectives: visual design, trademark continuity, commercial fit, legal qualification or jurisdiction practice. Execution supports parallel contributions and typed synthesis rather than undifferentiated voting.

## Part IV — Human–AI collaboration

### Chapter 11 — M0–M5 modes

Execution enforces the selected collaboration mode:

- M0 human-only;
- M1 AI reference;
- M2 AI assist;
- M3 AI draft, human approve;
- M4 AI execute under governance;
- M5 hybrid professional network.

### Chapter 12 — AI disclosure and responsibility

AI-generated material must retain model provenance, prompt or instruction reference where appropriate, source citations, uncertainty and human disposition. Approval cannot be inferred from silence.

### Chapter 13 — Dynamic assistance by proficiency

The same capability may expose more guidance and stronger review for L1–L2 users and more autonomy for L3–L5 users. Assistance level is policy, not merely UI preference.

## Part V — Simulation and assessment

### Chapter 14 — Simulation Workplace

Simulation uses isolated customers, trademarks, documents, communications, deadlines and events. It prohibits external submission, real payment and accidental production mutation.

### Chapter 15 — Scenario engine

Cases support hidden facts, staged document release, customer objections, incorrect evidence, deadline pressure and branching consequences.

### Chapter 16 — Examiner interaction

AI and human assessors may play customer, supervisor or external actor roles. Their interactions are part of the assessment record.

### Chapter 17 — Assessment evidence

Assessment considers facts, rules, risk, communication, service design, MarkOrbit operation, human–AI judgment and delivery quality—not only the final answer.

### Chapter 18 — From simulation to production

```text
simulation
→ shadow task
→ supervised production
→ bounded independent production
→ review authority
```

## Part VI — Risk, review and protected action

### Chapter 19 — R0–R4 execution risk

- R0 automated;
- R1 sampled review;
- R2 single professional review;
- R3 cross-professional review;
- R4 legally qualified or expert final review.

### Chapter 20 — Professional Authority

Execution prevents an operationally capable but legally unqualified actor from performing reserved actions. Certification never bypasses jurisdictional professional requirements.

### Chapter 21 — External Protected Action

Official filing, binding legal communication, provider appointment, payment custody and other protected actions require explicit authority and idempotent execution boundaries.

## Part VII — Reliability and evidence

### Chapter 22 — Retry and duplicate safety

Retries must preserve idempotency keys, prior attempts, external references and reconciliation state.

### Chapter 23 — Failure and recovery

Failures are classified as validation, authorization, dependency, provider, external system, data, review or commercial failures, with defined recovery routes.

### Chapter 24 — Audit and event evidence

Execution produces immutable evidence of requests, assignments, decisions, modifications, approvals, returns, settlement triggers and final outcomes.

### Chapter 25 — Learning without silent canonization

Execution outcomes may generate learning candidates. They enter Data, Knowledge or Capability improvement only after rights checks, de-identification, quality validation and governance.

## Ten-year lock

Execution is the platform’s trust engine. Its success is measured not by automation rate alone, but by reliable outcomes, accountable decisions, reversible mistakes, protected customers and compounding evidence.
