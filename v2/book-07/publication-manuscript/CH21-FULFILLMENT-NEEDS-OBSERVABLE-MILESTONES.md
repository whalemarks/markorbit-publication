# CH21 — Fulfillment Needs Observable Milestones

A Provider can be busy without the engagement being under control.

Emails may move, documents may be drafted and funds may be sent, yet the Originating Workplace may still be unable to answer what has actually been completed, what is blocked, which deadline is exposed and who must act next.

MGSN therefore treats fulfillment as an observable milestone chain rather than a stream of activity.

```text
activity
≠ progress

progress claimed
≠ milestone achieved

milestone achieved
≠ milestone accepted
```

## 1. From status language to inspectable state

Statements such as “we are checking,” “submitted” or “under process” may be sincere, but they are too ambiguous for governed fulfillment.

A material status claim should identify:

- the action;
- the relevant Matter and instruction version;
- the responsible actor;
- the date and authority;
- the supporting Evidence;
- the next required step.

A milestone turns narrative activity into a state transition that can be reviewed.

## 2. What deserves to be a milestone

A milestone should matter to at least one of the following:

- customer expectation;
- deadline safety;
- professional responsibility;
- financial release;
- Evidence completeness;
- correction or replacement readiness;
- formal-state reconciliation.

Typical milestones may include Provider Acceptance, Instruction Package acknowledgement, draft readiness, customer-approved content, performance of a protected action, official acknowledgement, correction completion, final Return and engagement closure.

The exact sequence depends on the Service Package and jurisdiction. MGSN should not turn every minor task into a milestone.

## 3. Milestone grammar

Each milestone should define:

- objective and prerequisite state;
- responsible actor;
- expected output;
- Evidence requirement;
- deadline or target;
- reviewer or acceptance authority;
- correction and escalation route;
- settlement relevance;
- next permitted transition.

```text
objective
+ responsible actor
+ Evidence
+ acceptance criteria
+ next transition
= observable milestone
```

## 4. Reported, evidenced and accepted are different

A milestone may be planned, started, reported complete, under review, accepted, blocked, disputed or sent for correction.

The important boundary is:

```text
Provider reports complete
≠ Evidence received
≠ Evidence validated
≠ milestone accepted
```

If the Provider reports filing but returns no filing copy or acknowledgement, the milestone should remain “reported complete—Evidence pending.”

## 5. Deadlines and dependencies

Deadlines belong to milestones, not only to emails or calendars.

The record should preserve the source, date, timezone, confidence, responsible actor, safe internal target and escalation threshold. An estimated date must not be presented as a verified official deadline.

Milestones should also expose dependencies:

```text
Customer Approval
→ Funds Clearance
→ Provider Filing
→ Official Acknowledgement
```

If customer approval is late, the system should not automatically classify the resulting delay as Provider failure.

## 6. Preparation can proceed while action remains blocked

A Provider may be allowed to review documents, prepare a draft or identify missing materials while still being prohibited from filing, paying an official fee, withdrawing a right or signing for the customer.

```text
preparation ready
≠ protected action authorized
```

Staged readiness prevents an early task from silently authorizing a later one.

## 7. Ownership and exception triggers

Different actors own different milestones:

- the Workplace manages customer instruction readiness;
- the Provider performs professional and local work;
- the customer or authorized representative approves protected content;
- MGSN coordinates and checks Return completeness;
- Finance executes and reconciles funds;
- the Owning Service validates formal business state.

A milestone should open an exception when Evidence is missing, a deadline changes, a new participant appears, funds fall outside the approved package or the Provider stops responding.

No-response is itself an observable fulfillment state. It should trigger escalation and replacement readiness rather than another informal reminder.

## 8. Completion requires closure

Receiving the final deliverable does not necessarily close the engagement.

Open items may remain:

- customer reporting;
- unused funds;
- original documents;
- correction duties;
- formal-state validation;
- archive and retention decisions.

```text
final deliverable received
≠ engagement reconciled
```

A closure milestone confirms that commercial, documentary and formal-state obligations have reached a controlled result.

## 9. Product principle

Fulfillment becomes manageable when each important transition is observable, attributable and evidenced.

```text
accepted route
→ versioned instruction
→ observable milestones
→ Evidence-backed review
→ acceptance or correction
→ typed Return
→ reconciliation and closure
```
