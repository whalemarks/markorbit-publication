# CH16 — Undisclosed Rebrokering Breaks Accountability

A Provider may need help from another professional, local representative, translator, investigator, filing clerk or affiliated office.

That is normal in international professional work.

The risk begins when the accepted Provider silently passes material work to someone else while preserving the appearance that it remains the sole responsible party.

```text
Provider Acceptance
≠ Permission for Undisclosed Substitution
```

## 1. Support and substitution are not the same

A supporting participant may perform a bounded task under the accepted Provider’s supervision while the Provider remains responsible for professional judgment, communication and Return.

A substitute Provider takes over a material part of the accepted responsibility chain.

The distinction depends less on the label and more on what the third party actually does.

Relevant questions include:

- Does the third party exercise professional judgment?
- Does it perform a protected action?
- Does it receive customer identity or sensitive documents?
- Does it require independent qualification?
- Does it issue the invoice or receive funds?
- Does the accepted Provider meaningfully supervise and remain accountable?
- Would the customer reasonably believe the route has changed?

```text
Disclosed Supporting Participant
≠ Undisclosed Substitute Provider
```

## 2. Why rebrokering is dangerous

Undisclosed rebrokering breaks the visible connection between acceptance and performance.

The network may no longer know:

- who performed the work;
- whose qualification applied;
- who checked conflict;
- where the customer data went;
- who received funds;
- who holds the original documents;
- who must correct an error;
- who can return reliable Evidence.

A route that appeared accountable becomes a chain of assumptions.

## 3. Material participation must be disclosed

Where another party materially supports delivery, the engagement should identify:

- Organization and responsible person;
- relationship to the accepted Provider;
- exact role and reason for participation;
- qualification and jurisdiction where relevant;
- conflict and data-access requirements;
- fee and beneficiary impact;
- supervision and accountability;
- Evidence and Return duties;
- whether the arrangement is temporary or continuing;
- whether customer or Workplace approval is required.

A general subcontracting clause may permit routine administrative support. It does not automatically approve every material change in professional route, data recipient, responsible person or payee.

```text
General Subcontracting Clause
≠ Approval of Every Material Route Change
```

## 4. The accepted Provider remains accountable unless the route changes

A Provider should not escape responsibility by saying that a local associate made the error.

If the engagement still presents the original Provider as the accountable party, that Provider should supervise the work, preserve the communication chain and return the required Evidence.

Where responsibility truly transfers, the route should be treated as a replacement or material change. The new Provider must complete the necessary identity, qualification, conflict, Acceptance and authority steps.

## 5. Professional qualification follows the actual actor

Qualification cannot be borrowed invisibly.

A Provider Organization may be admitted because a named professional or office holds the required authority. If another person or entity performs the reserved service, MGSN must determine whether that actor is properly qualified and authorized.

```text
Original Provider Qualified
≠ Every Downstream Actor Qualified
```

The same applies when an affiliate office, freelance professional or filing intermediary is used.

## 6. Conflict follows the real delivery chain

A conflict check limited to the accepted Provider may be incomplete if another Organization or professional will access the Matter or exercise judgment.

The necessary review should cover the actual participants without demanding unnecessary disclosure to everyone in the chain.

Where the third party cannot be named or checked, MGSN should restrict, clarify or reject the arrangement rather than treat it as ordinary support.

## 7. Data disclosure and onward transfer

Customer information disclosed for one Provider’s role should not automatically flow to affiliates or subcontractors.

A material onward transfer should identify:

- recipient;
- purpose;
- data categories;
- jurisdiction and processing location;
- confidentiality and retention obligations;
- further transfer restrictions;
- whether additional approval is required.

```text
Provider Received Data
≠ Provider May Redistribute It Freely
```

## 8. Funds and beneficiary changes are route changes

A request to pay a new company or bank account may signal an affiliate, subcontractor, collection agent or attempted fraud.

```text
New Beneficiary
≠ Harmless Administrative Change
```

The network should verify the beneficiary’s relationship to the Provider, invoice consistency, jurisdiction, tax and sanctions implications, refund responsibility and official-fee Evidence.

Funds should not be redirected merely because new bank details arrived by email.

## 9. Infinite downstream chains destroy managed delivery

A route cannot remain accountable if every Provider may silently pass the work onward.

```text
Provider A
→ Provider B
→ Provider C
→ Unknown Performer
```

MGSN should govern how many material delivery layers are permitted, what must be disclosed and which party remains responsible.

Some services legitimately require several actors. The answer is transparent role design, not hidden depth.

## 10. Customer and Workplace approval

Not every supporting participant requires separate customer approval. Material substitution often does.

Approval is more likely to be required when the change affects:

- responsible professional;
- legal representative;
- protected action;
- privileged or sensitive information;
- new jurisdiction of data processing;
- new beneficiary;
- material cost or deadline;
- conflict position;
- customer relationship access.

The Instruction Package should identify whether the accepted authority covers the proposed participant.

## 11. Emergency substitution

A deadline may require rapid support or replacement. Urgency can shorten the process, but the network should still preserve who is acting, why, under whose supervision, with which authority and what remains to be confirmed.

Where the original Provider can no longer remain accountable, the route should enter replacement and recovery rather than disguising the new Provider as support.

## 12. Failure and recovery

Common failures include:

- work is filed by an unknown local associate;
- the actual professional has not completed conflict review;
- customer documents are sent to an undisclosed affiliate;
- an invoice comes from a new entity with no explanation;
- the accepted Provider blames a subcontractor and refuses correction;
- several downstream layers make Evidence impossible to retrieve.

Recovery requires reconstructing the real delivery chain, verifying actors and authority, containing further data or funds movement, preserving deadlines and deciding whether the original Provider can correct the work or a formal replacement is required.

## 13. Product principle

MGSN permits accountable multi-party delivery, not invisible rebrokering.

```text
Disclosed Roles
+ Verified Material Actors
+ Bounded Data and Funds Access
+ Preserved Provider Accountability
= Governed Multi-party Delivery
```

Part IV has now formed the engagement: the user selects, the Provider accepts, the instruction is versioned, communication is scoped and every material delivery participant is visible. Part V turns to the commercial reality that supports this work—cost, funds, settlement and change.
