# Chapter 04 — The MGSN Product Constitution

MGSN can coordinate a global service chain only if its boundaries are stronger than its convenience features.

Without a Constitution, the Product will drift toward whichever pressure is strongest at the moment. Growth teams will ask for more visible Providers. Procurement will ask for lower prices. Participants will ask for unrestricted contact. Operations will ask to bypass acceptance steps. Finance will ask to treat payment as completion. AI systems will ask to automate route selection. A difficult incident will tempt the platform to take authority it never formally held.

Each request may appear reasonable in isolation. Together they can turn a managed professional network into an opaque broker.

The Constitution defines what MGSN is allowed to optimize—and what it must preserve even when preservation slows the transaction.

## The constitutional topology

The basic operating chain is:

```text
Originating Workplace
→ authorized Capability Need Projection
→ MGSN Connection
→ platform-owned MGSN Network
→ eligible Route Set
→ Recommendation and user disposition
→ Provider Allocation and Acceptance
→ governed fulfillment
→ typed Return
→ Originating Workplace validation
```

This chain contains several systems and authorities. They cooperate, but they are not one actor.

### The Originating Workplace

The Originating Workplace owns its business context. It ordinarily retains:

- the customer relationship;
- represented-party context;
- originating Need and Matter context;
- private pricing and margin;
- internal Knowledge and communications;
- the decision to project demand;
- the decision to accept or reject a proposed route;
- customer-facing accountability unless another arrangement is explicit.

Projection does not transfer all of these to MGSN.

### The MGSN Connection

The Connection is the Workplace-scoped interface through which authorized demand enters the network and governed Returns come back.

It controls what the Workplace is willing and permitted to expose. It may mask customer identity, limit documents, attach purpose restrictions and require internal approval before a Need crosses the boundary.

The Connection is not the Network itself. A Workplace may configure or operate its Connection, but it does not thereby own the shared Provider network, ranking logic, procurement terms or Trust system.

### The MGSN Network

The platform-owned Network governs shared network resources:

- participant and Provider admission;
- network Profiles and evidence requirements;
- supply portfolios and Service Packages;
- eligibility and routing mechanisms;
- Recommendation presentation;
- allocation and Provider Acceptance records;
- network-level fulfillment checkpoints;
- incident, correction, replacement and dispute pathways;
- Trust evidence and learning;
- controlled interfaces and network status.

The Network owns these mechanisms because shared supply cannot be governed separately by every participant.

### The Execution Provider Workplace

The Provider Workplace owns provider-side operations and professional delivery. It retains:

- its organization identity and operating controls;
- responsible professionals and staff;
- local professional judgment;
- internal work methods;
- Provider-side data and evidence duties;
- the right to accept or decline work;
- professional responsibility for actions it performs;
- correction duties arising from its accepted scope.

Admission to MGSN does not make the Provider an employee, agent or unrestricted subcontractor of every participant.

### Core and Owning Services

Core supplies shared semantic authority. It helps participants mean the same thing by terms such as Organization, Matter, Evidence, Permission and Event.

Owning Services retain formal-state authority for the records they own. MGSN may return evidence about a filing, payment, decision or certificate. It does not automatically mutate another Product’s formal state merely because the Return is structurally valid.

### Execution

Book 03’s Execution model governs how authorized work proceeds through validation, Assignment, Contribution, Review, Approval, Apply, Return, reconciliation and recovery.

MGSN defines the network Product and cross-organization fulfillment boundary. It does not absorb the entire execution runtime into the Network.

### Official and professional authority

Official institutions determine official state. Qualified professionals exercise reserved professional judgment and action under applicable authority.

The platform may verify evidence, represent appointments and control its own permissions. It cannot create a professional licence, invent official acceptance or turn a Recommendation into authority to act.

## Seven constitutional locks

Every MGSN feature must preserve seven locks.

### Lock 1 — Workplace sovereignty

The originating Workplace retains its customer relationship, private context and business decisions unless a different arrangement is explicit.

```text
Need Projection
≠ customer transfer
```

MGSN may require enough information to route safely. It may not use that information as unrestricted prospecting inventory.

### Lock 2 — Platform network governance

MGSN resources remain platform-governed. Participants may not convert controlled access into an ungoverned shared directory while still claiming MGSN protection.

```text
access to a Provider
≠ ownership of the Network
```

Platform governance must itself remain transparent enough to prevent self-preference and arbitrary exclusion.

### Lock 3 — Provider professional responsibility

The Provider remains responsible for professional work it accepts and performs.

```text
platform Recommendation
≠ platform performance of the professional service
```

The platform may manage route quality and recovery. It cannot disguise who gave advice, signed, submitted or represented the customer.

### Lock 4 — Authority separation

Identity, capability, admission, qualification, eligibility, appointment and instruction remain separate.

```text
Provider admitted
≠ Provider qualified for every service

Provider eligible
≠ Provider appointed

Provider accepted
≠ customer instructed protected action
```

The closer an action moves toward official or reserved professional consequence, the stronger the authority evidence must become.

### Lock 5 — Human choice and Provider Acceptance

MGSN may automate classification, filtering, route generation and Recommendation explanation. Ordinary professional routing must preserve meaningful user disposition and Provider Acceptance.

```text
automatic matching
≠ automatic appointment
```

A future high-confidence routine route may use more automation, but automation must remain within explicit authority, risk and exception controls.

### Lock 6 — Evidence-backed fulfillment and typed Return

Completion cannot be inferred from silence, payment or a generic “done” message.

A Return should state:

- what the Provider says occurred;
- which service stage it concerns;
- which documents or official sources support it;
- what remains outstanding or Unknown;
- which fees were incurred;
- which deadlines or follow-up obligations remain;
- whether correction or review is required.

```text
Return received
≠ Return accepted
≠ formal state updated
```

### Lock 7 — Separate authorization for funds and protected actions

Commercial coordination is part of the Product. Regulated custody and protected external action are not assumed.

```text
payment requirement recorded
≠ funds custody authorized

customer paid
≠ Provider performed

Provider prepared
≠ filing authorized
```

Escrow, money transmission, payment release, legal representation, filing and signature require their own legal and Product gates.

## The three route families

The Constitution recognizes three broad routes.

### R1 — External self-managed route

The Workplace uses a Provider outside MGSN or handles the relationship independently.

The platform should not imply network procurement, replacement or fulfillment protection. If the result later returns to a MarkOrbit Product, it enters through source and validation controls.

### R2 — MGSN recommended managed route

MGSN forms an eligible Route Set, explains a Recommendation and manages the route accepted by the user and Provider.

This is the default expression of managed network value.

### R3 — Managed preferred-provider route

The Workplace asks to use an existing Provider relationship. MGSN honours the preference when the Provider passes current admission, qualification, conflict, availability, package and risk requirements.

Preference influences route formation. It does not bypass safety.

## What the platform may decide

MGSN may decide matters within its own Product authority, such as:

- whether an Organization may participate in the Network;
- whether a Provider Profile has sufficient evidence for publication inside the Network;
- whether a Service Package meets network comparison requirements;
- whether a route is eligible under declared network rules;
- whether a Provider should be suspended from new allocation;
- whether network correction or appeal procedures are satisfied;
- which network evidence may influence future routing.

These decisions can still require human review, notice and appeal.

## What the platform may not decide alone

MGSN may not unilaterally determine:

- that a customer has appointed a professional;
- that a Provider has no legal conflict;
- that a person holds current professional authority without evidence;
- that an official submission succeeded;
- that official rights changed;
- that a disputed professional fee is legally owed;
- that participant customer data may be reused outside purpose;
- that an adverse official outcome proves Provider failure;
- that a high Trust score overrides jurisdiction or authority requirements.

These conclusions belong to other authorities or require evidence and review beyond platform preference.

## Constitutional failure modes

The Constitution is tested most clearly by attractive shortcuts.

### Shortcut: auto-appoint the top-ranked Provider

Failure: Recommendation becomes authority; conflict, appointment and Provider Acceptance collapse.

### Shortcut: show every Provider’s full contact details

Failure: controlled network access becomes an open graph; provenance and engagement scope disappear.

### Shortcut: route by highest platform margin

Failure: commercial self-preference is presented as suitability.

### Shortcut: treat payment as service commencement

Failure: funds state overrides prerequisite, authority and acceptance state.

### Shortcut: mark a Matter complete when the Provider uploads a file

Failure: Return bypasses validation and Owning Service authority.

### Shortcut: allow a successful Provider to rebroker silently

Failure: responsible organization, professional authority and evidence chain become unclear.

### Shortcut: make one public rating the Trust model

Failure: context, contestability and concentration risk disappear.

A Product that relies on these shortcuts may process more transactions. It would no longer conform to MGSN’s purpose.

## Constitutional review questions

Before accepting a material Product decision, reviewers should ask:

1. Which Workplace or organization retains the customer relationship?
2. Which data crosses the Connection, for what purpose and under whose authority?
3. Which Provider Organization and professional are responsible?
4. Which decision belongs to the platform, the Workplace, the Provider, an Owning Service or an official authority?
5. Does the user have a meaningful disposition where one is required?
6. Has the Provider accepted the exact scope and obligations?
7. What evidence proves progress or completion?
8. What remains Unknown?
9. What happens if the route fails?
10. Does any financial or protected action require separate authorization?

A feature that cannot answer these questions is not ready merely because its interface is clear.

## The fourth lock

The fourth and final lock of Part I is:

```text
Managed coordination
≠ unlimited platform authority
```

MGSN becomes trustworthy not by claiming control over every outcome, but by making the limits and transitions of control explicit.

Part II turns from the network’s reason for existence to its participants: Workplaces, Provider Organizations, responsible professionals, contacts and the supply claims through which global capability becomes visible.
