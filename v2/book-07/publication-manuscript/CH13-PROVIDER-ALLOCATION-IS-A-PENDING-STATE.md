# CH13 — Provider Allocation Is a Pending State

A user has confirmed a route.

The route may have been carefully screened, compared and recommended. The Provider may be qualified, currently eligible and apparently available. None of this means that the Provider has accepted responsibility for the work.

MGSN therefore treats Provider Allocation as a controlled pending state.

```text
Route confirmed
≠ Provider allocated
≠ Provider accepted
≠ engagement active
≠ protected action authorized
```

The distinction protects both sides. The Originating Workplace should not believe that work has begun before the Provider accepts. The Provider should not be treated as responsible for scope, deadlines, documents or funds it has not reviewed.

## 1. Why route confirmation cannot create an engagement

A recommendation is formed from the best available information at a particular time.

Between recommendation and Provider Acceptance, important conditions may change:

- the Provider may develop a conflict;
- capacity may be consumed by another urgent matter;
- a responsible professional may become unavailable;
- the deadline may be earlier than first understood;
- the customer may change scope;
- a document may reveal a new issue;
- the Service Package may not cover the actual work;
- official fees may change;
- funds or data restrictions may prevent performance;
- the Provider may require a different local representative;
- professional rules may require additional disclosure.

A confirmed route therefore creates an intention to seek engagement, not an engagement itself.

```text
User chooses route
→ MGSN creates controlled Allocation
→ Provider evaluates exact engagement
→ Provider accepts, declines or requests change
```

The pending state is not administrative delay. It is the point at which network-level suitability becomes Provider-level accountability.

## 2. What Allocation means

Provider Allocation identifies the route that MGSN proposes to activate for a specific Need version.

It should bind at least:

- Originating Workplace reference;
- Need Projection and version;
- selected Candidate Route;
- Provider Organization;
- Capability and Service Package version;
- route type;
- expected scope;
- preliminary price and funds conditions;
- target deadline;
- conflict status;
- data-disclosure stage;
- required Provider response time;
- responsible professional requirement where relevant;
- known conditions and Unknowns;
- expiry of the Allocation.

The Allocation is a platform-governed record. It does not itself appoint the Provider under professional law, create a customer mandate or authorize filing.

```text
Allocation
= request for bounded Provider commitment

Allocation
≠ legal appointment
≠ customer instruction
≠ commencement of every task
```

## 3. Allocation must be version-bound

The selected route is based on a particular Need, Package and Recommendation version.

If any of those changes materially before acceptance, the Provider should not accept an outdated Allocation.

Material changes may include:

- different applicant or owner;
- different jurisdiction;
- new class or service;
- changed deadline;
- new contentious issue;
- additional document or translation work;
- changed price;
- changed responsible professional;
- changed Provider entity;
- changed data or conflict context;
- changed customer instruction.

```text
same Provider
≠ same Allocation

same Matter
≠ same scope version
```

A material change should create a new or revised Allocation with preserved history.

## 4. The Provider response

A Provider should have meaningful response options.

```text
Accept
Accept with Conditions
Request Clarification
Request Scope Change
Request Price Change
Request Deadline Change
Conflict Review Required
Responsible Professional Confirmation Required
Decline
No Response / Expired
```

The response must be attributable to an authorized representative of the Provider Organization.

A casual message such as “looks fine” should not automatically become formal acceptance if it does not identify the scope, package, deadline and conditions being accepted.

## 5. Provider Acceptance

Provider Acceptance is the explicit commitment by the Provider Organization to perform the defined engagement under the accepted conditions.

It should confirm:

- accepting Organization;
- authorized accepting person;
- relevant Provider Workplace;
- responsible professional where required;
- accepted Need and Allocation version;
- accepted Service Package version;
- included and excluded scope;
- price and fee basis;
- funds prerequisites;
- deadline and timing assumptions;
- conflict result;
- document and data requirements;
- communication route;
- expected deliverables and Evidence;
- required Return;
- subcontracting or local-representative disclosure;
- unresolved conditions;
- acceptance date and validity.

```text
Provider Acceptance
= engagement responsibility accepted by Provider
```

It still does not prove that the customer has approved a protected action or that every prerequisite has been satisfied.

## 6. Conditional Acceptance

Providers often need to accept subject to conditions.

Examples include:

- original Power of Attorney must arrive by a date;
- official fees must be funded before filing;
- conflict clearance remains pending customer identity disclosure;
- translation must be approved;
- goods or services must be narrowed;
- local professional must review a complex issue;
- urgency is accepted only if documents arrive within hours;
- a quoted fee is valid only for a stated number of classes or pages.

Conditional Acceptance should state:

- each condition;
- who owns it;
- deadline;
- evidence required;
- consequence if unmet;
- whether any preparatory work may begin;
- whether the Allocation expires automatically.

```text
conditionally accepted
≠ fully ready to perform
```

The Product must not hide conditions behind an “accepted” badge.

## 7. Decline is a legitimate network result

A qualified Provider should be able to decline an engagement.

Legitimate reasons may include:

- conflict;
- insufficient capacity;
- deadline impossible to meet;
- scope outside Capability;
- professional concern;
- data or sanctions restriction;
- funds path unavailable;
- customer or matter risk;
- responsible professional unavailable;
- package not suitable;
- inability to return required Evidence.

Decline should be typed where possible so that MGSN can determine the next safe step.

```text
Provider declined due to conflict
→ do not ask for unnecessary conflict details
→ form alternative route

Provider declined due to capacity
→ check another qualified route

Provider declined due to scope mismatch
→ revise Need or seek specialist
```

A decline should not automatically become negative Trust evidence. Refusing work that cannot be performed safely may be evidence of responsible behavior.

## 8. Silence is not Acceptance

Provider silence is one of the most common risks in international service work.

A Workplace sends documents, assumes the Provider is handling the matter and waits. Days later, it discovers that the Provider never accepted, the email was missed or the deadline could not be met.

MGSN must preserve:

```text
message delivered
≠ message read
≠ Allocation acknowledged
≠ Provider accepted
```

An Allocation should have:

- response deadline;
- reminder rules;
- escalation contact;
- expiry;
- backup-route trigger;
- emergency deadline policy.

If no acceptance arrives, the state should remain unaccepted or expire. The Product must not infer acceptance from historical practice.

## 9. Allocation expiry and revalidation

An Allocation should expire when the assumptions supporting it are no longer reliable.

Expiry may be triggered by:

- response deadline passed;
- Package validity expired;
- price expired;
- deadline changed;
- Need changed;
- Provider status changed;
- conflict information changed;
- customer delay;
- required document not received;
- responsible professional unavailable.

After expiry, MGSN may:

- revalidate the same route;
- issue a revised Allocation;
- rematch;
- request customer clarification;
- move to a backup route;
- advise an external self-managed route;
- record no safe route.

## 10. Allocation and customer identity disclosure

Conflict review may require customer identity before Provider Acceptance.

The disclosure should remain purpose-limited.

MGSN should release only what is needed for:

- conflict review;
- professional eligibility;
- Provider decision;
- required legal or regulatory checks.

The Provider should not receive the entire customer file merely because an Allocation exists.

```text
Provider Allocation
≠ unrestricted customer access
```

If the Provider declines, further access should stop, while necessary audit and conflict records remain protected according to policy.

## 11. Allocation does not transfer relationship ownership

The Originating Workplace remains the Relationship Owner unless an explicit contracting model says otherwise.

Provider Allocation does not authorize the Provider to:

- solicit unrelated work;
- contact the customer outside engagement scope;
- reuse customer information;
- route the customer to affiliates;
- change commercial terms directly;
- claim ownership of future matters.

Where direct professional communication is required, access should be engagement-scoped and attributable.

```text
Provider receives engagement access
≠ Provider acquires customer relationship
```

## 12. Preferred-provider Allocation

A Workplace may choose an existing Provider through the R3 route.

The Allocation should identify Relationship Provenance and any agreed communication model. It should still require Provider Acceptance under the current scope, package and deadline.

Historical practice such as “they always file when we email them” is not enough.

```text
historical implied practice
≠ current explicit Acceptance
```

The managed route should improve the existing relationship by making responsibility visible, not weaken controls because the parties know each other.

## 13. Emergency Allocation

Urgent deadlines create pressure to skip stages.

An emergency process may compress time but should not collapse authority.

It may include:

- rapid Provider availability confirmation;
- limited preliminary disclosure;
- provisional conflict check where professionally acceptable;
- explicit risk statement;
- immediate operator escalation;
- narrow preparatory authorization;
- backup Provider in parallel where lawful and appropriate;
- hard deadline for full Acceptance.

Emergency Allocation should still preserve:

```text
who accepted
what scope
which deadline
which conditions
what remains Unknown
which action is authorized
```

Urgency changes timing. It does not create missing professional authority.

## 14. Allocation replacement

A pending or accepted Allocation may need replacement.

Reasons include:

- Provider declines;
- Provider does not respond;
- conflict emerges;
- capacity collapses;
- package becomes unavailable;
- Provider is suspended;
- customer chooses another route;
- Provider requests an unacceptable change;
- a specialist becomes necessary.

Replacement must preserve:

- original Allocation;
- Provider response;
- disclosed data;
- funds state;
- document state;
- deadline;
- reason for replacement;
- new route decision;
- customer disposition where required.

The new Provider should not inherit authority merely because the old Provider had it.

```text
replacement Provider
≠ transferred Acceptance
≠ transferred customer mandate
```

## 15. Allocation and Execution

MGSN owns the Product-side allocation and acceptance context.

Book 03 Execution coordinates the governed work that follows, including tasks, review, approvals, checkpoints, exceptions and recovery.

```text
MGSN Allocation
= which external route is proposed and accepted

Execution Assignment
= who is responsible for governed work steps
```

The two may be linked, but they are not interchangeable.

Provider Allocation should not silently create every downstream Task or authorize every action.

## 16. Failure modes

### 16.1 User confirmation treated as Provider Acceptance

The customer selects a route and the Matter is marked “in progress” before the Provider responds.

Correct response: keep Allocation pending and trigger response deadline controls.

### 16.2 Provider silence treated as historical acceptance

The parties have worked together before, so no explicit acceptance is requested.

Correct response: require current acceptance tied to current scope and deadline.

### 16.3 Conditional Acceptance displayed as ready

The Provider requires an original document, but the system shows a green accepted state.

Correct response: show the unmet condition, owner, deadline and consequence.

### 16.4 Material scope change without reallocation

The customer adds a contentious issue after Provider Acceptance.

Correct response: revise Need, recheck Eligibility and obtain new Acceptance.

### 16.5 Replacement inherits authority

A backup Provider is inserted after silence and is assumed to hold the same customer authorization.

Correct response: create a new Allocation, conflict check, Acceptance and authority chain.

### 16.6 Direct customer contact expands beyond scope

The Provider uses conflict-review identity disclosure to approach the customer directly.

Correct response: restrict access, preserve evidence and trigger relationship-governance review.

## 17. Product principle

Provider Allocation is the bridge between a user’s route choice and a Provider’s accountable commitment.

```text
Recommended Route
→ User Disposition
→ Provider Allocation
→ Provider Response
→ Provider Acceptance
→ prerequisite satisfaction
→ governed fulfillment
```

The bridge must remain visible.

A network is not reliable when it can recommend a Provider. It becomes reliable when it can prove that the Provider accepted the exact work, under the exact conditions, before responsibility is assumed.
