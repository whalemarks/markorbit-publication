# CH26 — Incidents Need Severity, Containment and Due Process

An incident is not merely a complaint.

It is a governed record of an event that may have harmed, or may still threaten, a customer, Matter, Provider, Workplace, fund flow, deadline, professional obligation, data boundary or network control.

```text
complaint
≠ verified incident

incident opened
≠ responsibility decided

protective action
≠ final sanction
```

MGSN must move quickly enough to contain harm and carefully enough not to convert incomplete allegations into permanent judgment.

## 1. What may become an incident

Examples include:

- missed or endangered deadlines;
- filing or instruction using the wrong version;
- action without required authority;
- false, incomplete or contradictory Evidence;
- undisclosed subcontracting or substitution;
- conflict failure;
- professional-qualification concern;
- data disclosure outside the authorized purpose;
- beneficiary or funds anomaly;
- official-fee payment discrepancy;
- refusal to return critical documents;
- repeated non-response during an open engagement;
- customer-relationship circumvention;
- abusive conduct or retaliation;
- material misrepresentation of package scope or cost.

An ordinary correction does not always require a formal incident. An incident is appropriate when the event creates material risk, repeated control failure, unresolved responsibility or a need for network-level containment.

## 2. Incident intake must preserve source and uncertainty

The intake record should identify:

- reporter;
- affected engagement, Provider, Workplace and service;
- event date and discovery date;
- allegation or observed fact;
- Evidence currently available;
- immediate deadline or harm;
- affected data, funds or authority;
- known and Unknown facts;
- whether the provider has been notified;
- whether confidential treatment is required.

The original allegation should remain distinguishable from later findings.

```text
reported fact
≠ verified fact
≠ final finding
```

## 3. Severity must reflect impact and urgency

A useful severity model may consider:

- reversibility;
- deadline proximity;
- customer harm;
- legal or professional exposure;
- data sensitivity;
- funds exposure;
- number of affected engagements;
- recurrence;
- evidence reliability;
- active continuation of harm.

Illustrative levels may include:

```text
S0 — observation or minor concern
S1 — limited operational deviation
S2 — material engagement risk
S3 — serious customer, authority, funds or data risk
S4 — critical active harm or network-wide threat
```

Severity is not blame. It determines response speed, containment and review depth.

## 4. Containment comes before final resolution

Possible containment actions include:

- safe-stop on a protected action;
- deadline escalation;
- preservation of documents and messages;
- temporary funds hold;
- restriction on new allocations;
- suspension of one Capability or Package;
- removal from one Candidate Route Set;
- customer or Workplace notification;
- replacement planning;
- access restriction;
- enhanced review of related engagements.

Containment should be no broader than necessary unless evidence shows wider risk.

```text
one package incident
≠ automatic universal suspension
```

Where the risk is critical, broad temporary restriction may be justified while scope is determined.

## 5. Investigation must be evidence-led

The investigation should reconstruct:

```text
Need version
→ Recommendation and user disposition
→ Allocation and Acceptance
→ Instruction Package
→ communications
→ milestones
→ funds checkpoints
→ Returns and official Evidence
→ discrepancy
```

Relevant participants should be able to provide:

- original messages;
- documents and versions;
- official receipts;
- call notes;
- professional explanations;
- system audit records;
- timeline corrections;
- evidence of external causes.

The review should distinguish:

- provider-caused failure;
- Workplace-caused failure;
- customer-caused failure;
- official-authority event;
- shared responsibility;
- unavoidable uncertainty;
- control-design failure.

## 6. Due process protects network quality

The affected Provider or participant should normally receive:

- a clear description of the issue;
- the evidence it may respond to, subject to confidentiality;
- a reasonable opportunity to correct factual error;
- an opportunity to provide context and evidence;
- the applicable decision standard;
- notice of material outcome;
- a review or appeal path where appropriate.

Due process is not delay for its own sake. It reduces false findings and improves the quality of corrective action.

Immediate protection may still occur before the response period ends.

## 7. Findings must be typed

Possible findings include:

```text
substantiated
partially substantiated
not substantiated
insufficient evidence
external cause
shared responsibility
control failure
resolved through correction
open pending official evidence
```

The finding should state:

- scope;
- evidence relied upon;
- remaining uncertainty;
- affected service and time period;
- corrective obligations;
- Trust impact;
- restriction or reactivation decision;
- review date.

## 8. Remediation is broader than punishment

A useful incident outcome may require:

- correction of the Matter;
- customer communication;
- replacement Provider;
- refund, credit or cost allocation review;
- updated Instruction or Return controls;
- provider training;
- stronger Evidence requirements;
- package restriction;
- re-verification;
- audit of related engagements;
- suspension or exit.

The objective is to reduce harm and recurrence, not merely label a party.

## 9. Incident scope must remain bounded

A serious event may justify broader review, but MGSN should not automatically infer that every office, professional, service and historical engagement is unsafe.

Scope may be limited by:

- responsible professional;
- jurisdiction;
- Service Package;
- office;
- time period;
- communication channel;
- funds beneficiary;
- type of protected action.

Evidence can later expand or narrow that scope.

## 10. Confidentiality and disclosure

Incident information can contain privileged, personal, commercially sensitive or disciplinary data.

Access should follow purpose and role. Public disclosure should not occur merely because an incident exists.

MGSN may need to inform affected participants of operational restrictions without revealing unnecessary confidential detail.

```text
network safety notice
≠ public accusation
```

## 11. AI support and limits

AI may assist with:

- timeline reconstruction;
- version comparison;
- missing-evidence detection;
- recurring-pattern identification;
- severity factors;
- draft incident summaries;
- related-engagement search within authorized data.

AI must not:

- make final misconduct findings;
- assign legal liability;
- determine professional discipline;
- impose sanctions autonomously;
- disclose confidential incident data;
- convert probability into fact.

## 12. Failure modes

### Complaint becomes automatic suspension

A single unsupported allegation removes a Provider from every service.

Correct response: contain supported risk, investigate and preserve bounded scope.

### Deadline risk waits for final blame

The network delays replacement until responsibility is decided.

Correct response: protect the Matter first; decide responsibility separately.

### Provider correction erases the incident

The immediate error is fixed, but the control failure and costs disappear from the record.

Correct response: preserve the incident, correction and learning outcome.

### Commercial importance weakens containment

A high-volume Provider continues receiving work despite active critical risk.

Correct response: apply severity-based controls independent of revenue.

## 13. Product principle

Incident governance must hold two duties at once:

```text
contain harm quickly
+ determine facts fairly
```

A credible network neither ignores serious risk nor punishes on accusation alone.
