# CH26 — Incidents Need Severity, Containment and Due Process

An Incident is not merely a complaint.

It is a governed record of an event that may have harmed, or may still threaten, a customer, Matter, deadline, fund flow, professional obligation, data boundary or network control.

```text
Complaint
≠ Verified Incident

Incident Opened
≠ Responsibility Decided

Protective Action
≠ Final Sanction
```

MGSN must move quickly enough to contain harm and carefully enough not to turn incomplete allegations into permanent judgment.

## 1. What belongs in Incident governance

Examples include:

- missed or endangered deadlines;
- use of the wrong instruction version;
- action without required authority;
- false or contradictory Evidence;
- undisclosed substitution;
- Conflict or Qualification concerns;
- unauthorized data disclosure;
- funds or beneficiary anomalies;
- refusal to return critical documents;
- repeated non-response during an open engagement;
- material misrepresentation of scope or cost.

Not every ordinary correction needs a formal Incident. Incident governance becomes appropriate when the event creates material risk, repeated control failure, unresolved responsibility or a need for network-level containment.

## 2. Intake must preserve allegation and uncertainty

The intake record should identify:

- reporter and affected engagement;
- Provider, Workplace and service context;
- event and discovery dates;
- observed facts and allegations;
- current Evidence;
- immediate deadline or harm;
- affected data, funds or authority;
- known facts and Unknowns;
- confidentiality needs.

```text
Reported Fact
≠ Verified Fact
≠ Final Finding
```

The original allegation should remain distinguishable from later findings.

## 3. Severity measures risk, not blame

Severity should consider:

- reversibility;
- deadline proximity;
- customer harm;
- legal or professional exposure;
- data and funds sensitivity;
- number of affected engagements;
- recurrence;
- evidence reliability;
- whether harm is still continuing.

An illustrative scale may range from a minor observation to critical active harm.

```text
Severity
= Response urgency and containment depth

Severity
≠ Final responsibility finding
```

## 4. Containment comes first

Possible containment actions include:

- Safe Stop on a Protected Action;
- deadline escalation;
- preservation of messages and documents;
- temporary funds hold;
- restriction on new Allocations;
- suspension of one Capability or Package;
- removal from one Candidate Route Set;
- replacement planning;
- access restriction;
- review of related engagements.

Containment should be no broader than necessary unless Evidence shows wider risk.

```text
One Package Incident
≠ Automatic Universal Suspension
```

A critical event may still justify broad temporary restriction while scope is determined.

## 5. Investigation reconstructs the operating chain

A reliable review should reconstruct:

```text
Need Version
→ Recommendation and User Disposition
→ Allocation and Acceptance
→ Instruction Package
→ Communications
→ Milestones
→ Funds Checkpoints
→ Returns and Official Evidence
→ Discrepancy
```

The review should distinguish Provider-caused failure, Workplace or customer error, official-authority events, shared responsibility, unavoidable uncertainty and control-design failure.

This protects both fairness and Product learning.

## 6. Due process improves decisions

The affected Provider or participant should normally receive:

- a clear description of the issue;
- the Evidence available for response, subject to confidentiality;
- an opportunity to correct factual error;
- an opportunity to provide context and new Evidence;
- notice of material outcome;
- review or appeal where appropriate.

Immediate protective action may still occur before the response period ends.

Due process is not delay for its own sake. It reduces false findings and produces better remediation.

## 7. Findings must be typed and scoped

Possible findings include:

```text
Substantiated
Partially Substantiated
Not Substantiated
Insufficient Evidence
External Cause
Shared Responsibility
Control Failure
Resolved through Correction
Open Pending Official Evidence
```

The finding should state:

- scope and affected service;
- Evidence relied upon;
- remaining uncertainty;
- corrective obligations;
- Trust impact;
- restriction or reactivation decision;
- review date.

A serious event may justify broader review, but it should not automatically condemn every office, professional, Package and historical engagement.

## 8. Remediation is broader than punishment

A useful outcome may require:

- correction of the Matter;
- customer communication;
- replacement Provider;
- refund, credit or cost-allocation review;
- stronger Instruction or Return controls;
- Provider training;
- re-verification;
- Package restriction;
- audit of related engagements;
- suspension or exit.

The objective is to reduce harm and recurrence, not merely label a party.

## 9. Confidentiality and commercial neutrality

Incident records may contain privileged, personal, commercially sensitive or disciplinary information.

Access should follow purpose and role. An operational safety notice does not require public accusation.

```text
Network Safety Notice
≠ Public Accusation
```

Commercial importance must not weaken containment. A high-volume Provider with active critical risk should face the same evidence-based protective controls as any other participant.

## 10. Product principle

Incident governance must hold two duties at once:

```text
Contain Harm Quickly
+ Determine Facts Fairly
```

A credible network neither ignores serious risk nor punishes on accusation alone.
