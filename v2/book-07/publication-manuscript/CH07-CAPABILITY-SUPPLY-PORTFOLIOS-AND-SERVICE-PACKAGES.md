# CH07 — Capability Supply Portfolios and Service Packages

One trusted provider is useful.

One provider is not a resilient network.

A professional relationship can be excellent for years and still become unavailable at the wrong moment because of conflict, workload, illness, staffing change, sanctions, price instability, professional restriction or a deadline the provider cannot safely accept.

MGSN therefore manages supply as a portfolio of bounded Capability routes rather than one permanent contact per country.

```text
country contact list
≠ service-specific supply portfolio
```

A portfolio makes alternatives governable. A Service Package makes each alternative understandable and operable.

## 1. Why national coverage is too broad

A network may claim that it “covers” a jurisdiction because it knows one provider there.

That claim says very little.

Trademark and related professional services may differ by:

- ordinary filing;
- classification or specification work;
- office-action response;
- opposition or cancellation;
- recordal;
- renewal or use declaration;
- evidence preparation;
- investigation;
- translation;
- notarization or legalization coordination;
- urgent deadline recovery;
- litigation or hearing representation.

A provider suitable for one service may be unsuitable for another. A firm with excellent routine capacity may have no conflict-free contentious team. A low-cost route may depend on slow batch processing. A specialist may be too expensive for ordinary work but essential for a difficult refusal.

The network must therefore ask:

```text
Which Organization
offers which admitted Capability
for which service context
under which package
with which capacity, evidence and restrictions?
```

Coverage is the existence of usable routes for defined Needs—not the presence of a flag on a map.

## 2. The Capability Supply Portfolio

A Capability Supply Portfolio is the network’s governed set of admitted supply routes for a defined service context.

A portfolio may be organized by:

- jurisdiction and authority;
- service family;
- procedural stage;
- matter complexity;
- language;
- urgency;
- professional qualification;
- customer or applicant constraints;
- data and confidentiality requirements;
- funds and payment constraints;
- evidence and Return requirements.

Each portfolio entry links:

```text
Provider Organization
+ qualified Capability
+ admitted Service Package
+ portfolio role
+ current restrictions
+ review and freshness state
```

The entry does not transfer the provider’s business into MGSN. It is a network-controlled statement that this route may be considered under defined conditions.

## 3. Portfolio roles are contextual

A provider does not need one universal rank.

It may hold different roles for different services:

```text
Recommended Primary
Qualified Alternative
Specialist Route
Urgent-capacity Route
Relationship-preserved Route
Backup Route
Pilot Route
Restricted Route
Suspended Route
```

These roles are contextual and revisable.

The same provider may be:

- primary for routine filings;
- specialist for complex classification;
- backup for urgent work;
- relationship-preserved for one Workplace;
- unavailable for disputes;
- under review for evidence-return quality.

A portfolio role is not a permanent public status. It is an internal network operating decision supported by evidence and policy.

## 4. Multiple routes create resilience, not an auction

MGSN maintains multiple routes to reduce operational fragility.

The benefits include:

- continuity when a primary provider cannot act;
- conflict substitution;
- urgent capacity;
- specialist access;
- language and regional alternatives;
- comparison of scope and evidence;
- controlled testing of new supply;
- replacement after failure;
- reduced dependence on one relationship;
- negotiation against stale or unstable terms.

But resilience does not require open bidding.

```text
multiple routes
≠ lowest-price auction
≠ public provider leaderboard
≠ unrestricted user browsing
```

A professional service route should not win simply because it is cheapest or most visible. The network must consider suitability, authority, scope, evidence, risk, availability and recovery.

A larger candidate pool can increase noise. A better portfolio produces a small number of meaningful alternatives.

## 5. Relationship-preserved supply

Participants should be able to bring established providers into MGSN.

The network may record:

- introducing Organization;
- prior relationship evidence;
- length or start of relationship;
- jurisdictions and services previously used;
- declared preferred-route status;
- disclosure limits;
- attribution period;
- known performance history.

Once independently admitted, the provider may appear as a relationship-preserved route.

That route can receive appropriate weight because continuity may reduce coordination cost and protect a legitimate business relationship.

It cannot override:

- current qualification;
- conflict;
- availability;
- package fit;
- excessive or unexplained cost;
- sanctions or restriction;
- customer interest;
- material performance risk.

```text
relationship preference
≠ routing monopoly
```

The network protects provenance while retaining the ability to recommend a safer alternative.

## 6. Honest gaps are part of portfolio quality

A well-governed portfolio can return no eligible supply.

This may occur because:

- no provider has the required qualification;
- all qualified providers have conflicts;
- evidence has expired;
- the deadline is too short;
- a required package is unavailable;
- data restrictions prevent a lawful route;
- funds cannot be handled safely;
- the service falls outside current network support.

The safe result is:

```text
no eligible supply
→ clarify or narrow the Need
→ operator review
→ external self-managed route
→ specialist recruitment
→ safe decline
```

The network should not convert an unverified contact into a route merely to avoid showing a gap.

False coverage creates more risk than incomplete coverage.

## 7. Capability is not yet an operable offer

A provider may be capable of performing a service without there being a clear commercial and operational offer.

“Trademark filing” may or may not include:

- classification advice;
- specification drafting;
- preliminary search;
- translation;
- local appointment;
- priority claim;
- power-of-attorney review;
- official filing fees;
- additional item or class fees;
- filing report;
- publication monitoring;
- registration-stage work;
- certificate delivery.

Two providers can quote the same service name while promising materially different work.

A Capability answers:

> What can this provider potentially perform?

A Service Package answers:

> What will this route deliver under this version, price basis and set of assumptions?

```text
Capability
≠ Service Package
≠ engagement-specific instruction
```

## 8. Service Package grammar

MGSN needs a common package grammar so different routes can be understood and compared.

An admitted Service Package should identify at least:

- package identity and version;
- Provider Organization;
- relevant Capability;
- jurisdiction and authority;
- service and procedural stage;
- included professional actions;
- excluded actions;
- required inputs;
- customer or applicant assumptions;
- professional fees;
- official fees;
- taxes and disbursements;
- variable, estimated and contingent components;
- timing assumptions;
- deliverables;
- required Evidence;
- expected Return;
- validity period;
- urgency rules;
- dependency and exception rules;
- correction and change-control path;
- applicable restrictions.

This grammar creates comparability without claiming legal uniformity.

```text
common package structure
+ jurisdiction-specific rules
+ provider-specific admitted terms
= comparable managed route
```

## 9. Inclusions and exclusions define responsibility

Exclusions are not secondary fine print.

They establish what the provider and platform have not undertaken.

Common exclusions may involve:

- substantive refusal responses;
- oppositions, hearings or appeals;
- legalization or notarization;
- courier or original-document handling;
- excessive goods or class charges;
- third-party searches;
- translations beyond a stated limit;
- unexpected official requirements;
- registration-stage fees;
- maintenance work after registration;
- emergency work outside standard timing.

A package that hides likely exclusions creates a false price comparison.

The reader should be able to distinguish:

```text
included
excluded
conditional
estimated
externally determined
not yet known
```

Unknown items should remain unknown until resolved. They should not be silently absorbed into a reassuring total.

## 10. Provider delivery and platform-managed layers

A route may combine provider work with platform-managed coordination.

The provider may perform:

- professional analysis;
- local representation;
- drafting;
- filing;
- correspondence;
- evidence preparation;
- local procedural follow-up.

MGSN may manage:

- standardized intake;
- package comparison;
- document completeness checks;
- communication routing;
- milestone monitoring;
- funds coordination where authorized;
- exception management;
- replacement support;
- Return structure;
- network Trust evidence.

These layers must remain distinguishable.

```text
platform-managed coordination
≠ platform performing reserved professional work
```

The package should explain who performs, who coordinates, who reviews and who updates formal state.

## 11. Package versioning protects commitments

Service Packages change.

Reasons include:

- official-fee changes;
- new documentary requirements;
- provider pricing changes;
- currency or tax changes;
- new exclusions;
- revised turnaround;
- changed evidence requirements;
- platform policy changes;
- provider capacity restrictions;
- legal or professional-rule changes.

A package therefore needs a version and effective period.

```text
same package name
≠ same commercial and professional promise
```

The accepted engagement should retain the relevant package version unless a governed change is approved.

Updating a current package must not silently rewrite:

- prior quotes;
- accepted scope;
- provider obligations;
- expected Evidence;
- historical margin;
- refund or dispute analysis.

## 12. From package to engagement-specific terms

An admitted package is a reusable baseline, not the final instruction.

A specific Matter may require:

- unusual goods or services;
- multiple applicants;
- urgent timing;
- extra translation;
- legalization;
- a difficult priority claim;
- special evidence;
- additional professional review;
- a non-standard official fee;
- customer-specific confidentiality.

The route may therefore produce an engagement-specific offer or package application that records the deviations.

```text
admitted package
+ Matter-specific variables
+ confirmed price and conditions
= engagement-ready route
```

The deviations should be explicit. A provider should not rely on a general package name to conceal material exceptions discovered before acceptance.

## 13. Portfolio governance

Portfolio roles and package admission should change through evidence-backed governance.

Inputs may include:

- identity and qualification status;
- response reliability;
- acceptance behavior;
- deadline performance;
- evidence completeness;
- correction frequency;
- dispute history;
- capacity;
- price stability;
- package-scope clarity;
- official-fee evidence;
- replacement outcomes;
- service-specific Trust.

A provider should not be promoted because one operator likes the relationship. Nor should it be demoted because of an unverified complaint.

Material changes should preserve:

- the previous role or package state;
- the evidence considered;
- the decision reason;
- the decision authority;
- the effective date;
- affected open engagements;
- review or appeal path.

## 14. Supply concentration and dependency

A portfolio should expose when the network depends too heavily on one route.

Dependency can arise from:

- one provider holding most volume;
- one person being the only qualified professional;
- one bank or funds path;
- one language team;
- one package supporting a critical service;
- one jurisdiction having no conflict alternative;
- one provider controlling both ordinary and urgent capacity.

Concentration is not automatically unacceptable. Some services genuinely have scarce supply.

But hidden concentration creates false resilience.

The network should know:

```text
where failure would stop service
how quickly another route could be activated
what evidence and data would transfer
which customer commitments are exposed
```

Recruitment should follow these gaps rather than superficial geographic expansion.

## 15. Failure modes

### 15.1 One provider per country

A long-standing provider becomes conflicted two days before a deadline. No alternative has been admitted.

Correct response: disclose the supply gap, trigger emergency operator review and avoid presenting an unverified contact as equivalent replacement.

### 15.2 Cheapest package wins

A lower quote excludes registration fees and certificate return, while a higher quote includes them. The routes are ranked only by headline price.

Correct response: normalize scope and cost layers before recommendation.

### 15.3 Package change rewrites an accepted engagement

The provider raises its fee after acceptance, and the system updates the historical package record.

Correct response: preserve the accepted version and process the increase as a governed change or exception.

### 15.4 Hidden provider/platform responsibility

The user assumes MGSN reviewed legal substance, while the platform performed only document completeness checks.

Correct response: identify the provider and platform layers explicitly.

### 15.5 Relationship preference becomes exclusivity

An existing provider remains selected despite expired evidence and repeated delays because it was introduced by an important participant.

Correct response: preserve provenance but apply qualification, performance and customer-interest gates.

## 16. Product principle

MGSN does not create supply by listing firms.

It creates resilient, operable supply by joining portfolios and packages:

```text
multiple qualified Capability routes
+ contextual portfolio roles
+ admitted and versioned Service Packages
+ explicit scope, cost, evidence and restrictions
= resilient managed supply
```

This structure allows the network to compare without commoditizing, preserve relationships without creating monopolies and admit gaps without inventing coverage.
