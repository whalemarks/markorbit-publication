# CH23 — Correction Is Part of Fulfillment

Professional service work is not accountable merely because an initial deliverable exists.

A filing may contain the wrong address. A translation may be incomplete. A Provider may use an outdated goods list. A certificate may contain an error. An official receipt may not match the instructed applicant. A promised document may never be returned.

These are not peripheral events. They are part of fulfillment.

```text
work delivered
≠ work accepted

error identified
≠ responsibility resolved

correction requested
≠ correction completed
```

## 1. Correction begins from an accepted expectation

A correction claim needs a reference point.

The network should identify:

- accepted Service Package;
- accepted Instruction Package version;
- relevant milestone;
- required Evidence;
- actual Return;
- observed discrepancy;
- impact;
- responsible party or unresolved responsibility;
- deadline and urgency.

Without this baseline, correction can become an argument based only on memory.

## 2. Types of discrepancy

MGSN may distinguish:

```text
Data Discrepancy
Document Discrepancy
Scope Discrepancy
Professional-content Discrepancy
Fee Discrepancy
Deadline Discrepancy
Authority Discrepancy
Evidence Discrepancy
Official-record Discrepancy
Communication Discrepancy
```

The type determines the review, evidence and recovery route.

## 3. Not every change is a Provider error

A correction may be required because:

- the Provider made an avoidable mistake;
- the Originating Workplace sent incorrect data;
- the customer changed instructions;
- an official authority entered data incorrectly;
- a translation ambiguity was not resolvable earlier;
- requirements changed;
- the original state was Unknown;
- multiple parties contributed to the failure.

The system should not assign blame before the evidence supports it.

```text
correction required
≠ Provider fault proven
```

## 4. Correction lifecycle

A governed correction may pass through:

```text
issue detected
→ evidence preserved
→ impact assessed
→ responsibility reviewed
→ correction route proposed
→ authority and cost approved
→ correction performed
→ Evidence returned
→ result validated
→ customer and formal state reconciled
```

Each stage should remain visible.

## 5. Severity and urgency

Corrections may be classified by impact:

- cosmetic or non-substantive;
- operational but recoverable;
- deadline-sensitive;
- rights-sensitive;
- financial;
- professional-authority related;
- customer-relationship critical;
- potentially irreversible.

Urgency should follow the risk, not the volume of complaints.

A misspelled internal note differs from a wrong applicant on an official filing. A missing certificate scan differs from a missed opposition deadline.

## 6. The correction plan

A correction plan should state:

- what is wrong or uncertain;
- source Evidence;
- affected records and milestones;
- proposed action;
- who must act;
- required professional review;
- required customer approval;
- official procedure if any;
- cost and proposed allocation;
- deadline;
- fallback if correction fails;
- communication plan;
- expected corrective Evidence.

The plan should not promise recoverability before the route is verified.

## 7. Free correction, additional work and liability

The existence of additional work does not automatically justify an additional customer charge.

```text
additional work occurred
≠ customer automatically owes more
```

Commercial treatment may depend on:

- whether the original scope was clear;
- who caused the discrepancy;
- whether the Provider used the instructed version;
- whether the error was avoidable;
- whether official fees are refundable;
- whether the Provider accepted correction obligations;
- whether the customer changed scope;
- whether responsibility is shared or unknown.

Possible outcomes include:

```text
Provider correction at no additional professional fee
Customer-funded scope change
Shared-cost correction
Platform-funded service recovery
Official-fee-only charge
Credit or refund
Liability review
Unknown pending investigation
```

MGSN should record the decision basis without pretending to adjudicate legal liability beyond its authority.

## 8. Correction does not erase the original failure

The system should preserve:

- original Return;
- original Evidence;
- discrepancy report;
- correction request;
- correction version;
- final result;
- customer communication;
- cost treatment;
- Trust or governance consequence.

```text
corrected current state
≠ history rewritten
```

This history supports audit, learning and fair evaluation.

## 9. Provider response to correction

A Provider may:

- accept responsibility and correct;
- accept the correction but dispute responsibility;
- request clarification;
- explain that official action is required;
- state that correction is impossible;
- propose refiling or replacement;
- dispute the alleged discrepancy;
- fail to respond.

Silence near a deadline should trigger escalation and replacement readiness.

## 10. Official-record corrections

When the official record is wrong, the correction may require:

- a formal request;
- new evidence;
- a fee;
- a deadline;
- a local professional;
- customer authorization;
- reissuance of a certificate;
- appeal or refiling.

MGSN should distinguish:

```text
Provider corrected its own document
≠ official record corrected
```

A corrected report cannot substitute for an official correction.

## 11. Communication correction

Sometimes the operational error is not the filing but the report.

A Provider may have acted correctly but sent an inaccurate summary. The Workplace may have told the customer that a case was registered when it was only accepted for publication.

Correction then requires:

- accurate replacement communication;
- explanation of the earlier statement;
- impact assessment;
- customer notification;
- preservation of both messages;
- review of future reporting controls.

## 12. Correction and Trust

One error should not automatically destroy Trust.

Trust evidence should consider:

- severity;
- avoidability;
- disclosure speed;
- cooperation;
- correction quality;
- recurrence;
- impact;
- whether the Provider attempted concealment;
- whether the system or Workplace contributed.

A Provider that identifies and corrects an issue promptly may be safer than one with a superficially clean record created by under-reporting.

## 13. AI assistance

AI may assist with:

- comparing instructed and returned versions;
- detecting inconsistent names, dates, classes and addresses;
- classifying discrepancy type;
- drafting correction questions;
- tracking unresolved corrective Evidence;
- identifying repeat patterns.

AI must not:

- assign legal liability;
- decide who pays without authority;
- declare an official remedy available;
- alter approved content;
- send a correction instruction for a protected action without approval;
- erase earlier Evidence.

## 14. Failure scenarios

### 14.1 Wrong applicant address

The filing receipt contains a different order or component from the approved address.

Correct response: compare the approved version, Provider submission and official record; determine whether the difference is harmless, incorrect or requires official correction.

### 14.2 Provider asks the customer to pay for its own version error

The Provider used an outdated specification and requests a new fee.

Correct response: review responsibility and commercial terms before approving any charge.

### 14.3 Correction claimed but not evidenced

The Provider says the office corrected the record but returns no official proof.

Correct response: keep the correction milestone as Evidence pending.

### 14.4 Error discovered after Provider exit

A certificate discrepancy appears after the original Provider leaves the network.

Correct response: preserve the original Provider obligation and prepare a qualified replacement route where necessary.

## 15. Product principle

Correction is a normal, governed fulfillment path.

```text
accepted expectation
→ discrepancy
→ evidence and impact review
→ correction plan
→ authorized corrective action
→ corrective Return
→ validation and reconciliation
```

A network earns trust not by claiming that errors never occur, but by making errors visible, recoverable and attributable.

The next chapter addresses the harder case: when the original Provider cannot or should not continue, and continuity must be preserved through replacement and recovery.
