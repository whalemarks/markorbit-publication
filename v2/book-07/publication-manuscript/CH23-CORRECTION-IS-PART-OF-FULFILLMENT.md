# CH23 — Correction Is Part of Fulfillment

Professional work is not accountable merely because an initial deliverable exists.

A filing may contain the wrong address. A Provider may use an outdated goods list. A certificate may contain an error. A promised document may never be returned. These are not peripheral events. They are part of fulfillment.

```text
work delivered
≠ work accepted

error identified
≠ responsibility resolved

correction requested
≠ correction completed
```

## 1. Correction begins from an accepted expectation

A correction claim needs a reference point:

- accepted Service Package;
- accepted Instruction Package version;
- relevant milestone and required Evidence;
- actual Return;
- observed discrepancy;
- impact, deadline and urgency.

Without that baseline, correction becomes an argument based on memory.

## 2. Discrepancy is not the same as fault

The difference may concern data, documents, scope, professional content, fees, deadlines, authority, Evidence, official records or communication.

A correction may be needed because the Provider made a mistake, the Workplace supplied incorrect data, the customer changed instructions, an authority entered information incorrectly or the original state was genuinely Unknown.

```text
correction required
≠ Provider fault proven
```

The network should preserve Evidence before allocating responsibility.

## 3. The correction lifecycle

```text
issue detected
→ original Evidence preserved
→ impact assessed
→ correction route proposed
→ authority and cost reviewed
→ correction performed
→ corrective Return received
→ result validated
→ customer, funds and formal state reconciled
```

Severity should follow the actual risk. A minor reporting error differs from a wrong applicant on an official filing or a missed opposition deadline.

## 4. The correction plan

A useful plan states:

- what is wrong or uncertain;
- affected versions and milestones;
- proposed corrective action;
- who must act and review;
- customer or professional approval required;
- official procedure, if any;
- cost treatment;
- deadline and fallback;
- expected corrective Evidence.

The plan should not promise that recovery is available before the route is verified.

## 5. Additional work and cost

The existence of more work does not automatically justify another customer charge.

```text
additional work occurred
≠ customer automatically owes more
```

The result may be:

- Provider correction without extra professional fee;
- customer-funded scope change;
- shared-cost correction;
- official-fee-only charge;
- credit or refund;
- liability or dispute review;
- Unknown pending investigation.

MGSN may record the commercial decision and supporting facts without claiming authority to determine legal liability.

## 6. History must remain visible

Correction should improve the current state without erasing the original failure.

The record should preserve the original Return, discrepancy, correction request, corrected version, customer communication, cost treatment and final result.

```text
corrected current state
≠ history rewritten
```

This history supports audit, Trust and network learning.

## 7. Provider response and official correction

A Provider may accept and correct, dispute responsibility, request clarification, state that an official procedure is required, propose refiling or fail to respond.

Silence near a deadline should trigger escalation and replacement readiness.

Where the official record is wrong, a corrected Provider report is not enough.

```text
Provider corrected its own document
≠ official record corrected
```

The route may require a formal request, new Evidence, a fee, customer authority or reissuance by the relevant authority.

## 8. Communication correction

Sometimes the underlying action was correct but the report was wrong. A Workplace may tell the customer that registration is complete when the official notice shows only publication acceptance.

The correction then requires an accurate replacement message, explanation of the earlier statement, customer notification and preservation of both versions.

## 9. Trust follows recovery behavior

One error should not automatically destroy Trust.

Relevant evidence includes severity, avoidability, disclosure speed, cooperation, correction quality, recurrence and whether the issue was concealed.

A Provider that identifies and corrects an issue promptly may be safer than one whose clean record exists only because failures were not reported.

## 10. Product principle

Correction is a normal governed fulfillment path.

```text
accepted expectation
→ discrepancy
→ Evidence and impact review
→ correction plan
→ authorized corrective action
→ corrective Return
→ validation and reconciliation
```

A network earns Trust not by claiming that errors never occur, but by making them visible, attributable and recoverable.
