# Part VII — Trust, Intelligence and Network Governance

## Chapter 63 — Trust is contextual evidence

Provider trust is based on more than volume or popularity.

Relevant evidence includes:

- qualification validity;
- acceptance accuracy;
- deadline performance;
- communication behavior;
- evidence completeness;
- correction rate;
- dispute history;
- customer or Workplace impact;
- verified outcomes;
- performance by service and jurisdiction.

## Chapter 64 — No public star-rating shortcut

A single public score is too crude for professional routing and can be distorted by retaliation, volume gaming or different service complexity.

Trust views should be role-appropriate and explain the dimensions relevant to the current route.

## Chapter 65 — Evidence freshness

Old success does not prove current capacity, qualification, pricing or staff quality. Trust evidence requires recency and service-specific decay.

## Chapter 66 — Capacity and scarcity

The Network should understand:

- capability supply by jurisdiction;
- qualified-provider scarcity;
- single-provider dependency;
- language gaps;
- review bottlenecks;
- seasonal closure;
- demand concentration.

Recruitment follows actual gaps rather than map coverage alone.

## Chapter 67 — Provider dependency risk

A corridor that relies on one firm is fragile even when that firm performs well. MGSN should develop alternate routes and continuity plans for critical services.

## Chapter 68 — Routing fairness

Routing should not permanently concentrate work among incumbents merely because they possess more historic evidence. Controlled probation routes allow qualified new providers to build evidence without exposing high-risk matters prematurely.

## Chapter 69 — Performance review

Provider review distinguishes isolated error, systemic weakness, scope mismatch, communication problem and professional misconduct.

Remedies may include coaching, package correction, enhanced review, reduced scope, suspension or retirement.

## Chapter 70 — Network policy

Policies cover admission, disclosure, communication, rebrokering, conflicts, pricing, evidence, funds, correction, suspension, data use and appeals.

Material policy changes require versioning and notice.

## Chapter 71 — Provider appeal

Providers should be able to challenge material trust findings, suspension or incorrect evidence through a recorded appeal path.

## Chapter 72 — Network learning

Accepted outcomes may improve:

- route eligibility;
- package design;
- instruction checklists;
- deadline controls;
- evidence requirements;
- risk models;
- recruitment priorities.

Private terms, confidential data and professional work remain subject to rights and purpose limits.

## Chapter 73 — AI in routing

AI may normalize quotations, detect missing requirements, recommend routes and identify risk. It must preserve source, confidence and human review where allocation or disclosure is consequential.

## Chapter 74 — Human network governance

Senior network managers and qualified professionals remain necessary for exceptional routes, provider disputes, qualification interpretation and high-risk recovery.
