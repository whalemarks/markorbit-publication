# CH18 — Opportunity as a Potential Value Route

## Opportunity should describe possibility, not pretend commitment

Professional platforms need a way to represent potential value before a customer, provider, asset owner or operating Workplace has committed to a route.

That shared mechanism is Opportunity.

Opportunity answers:

> Through what plausible route could a Need, Capability, asset, audience, capacity or provider supply create value?

It is not the value itself and not the authorization to pursue it.

```text
Opportunity
≠ Need
≠ Customer Instruction
≠ Order
≠ Matter
≠ Engagement
≠ Assignment
```

## Opportunity is broader than sales

A conventional CRM treats Opportunity as a potential sale. MarkOrbit requires a broader but still governed meaning.

Opportunity may arise from:

- a customer Need;
- a public trademark lifecycle event;
- an available trademark asset;
- provider capacity;
- reusable professional Capability;
- content demand;
- an audience with a recurring problem;
- a partnership route;
- a service recovery route.

The potential value may be:

- revenue;
- cost reduction;
- risk reduction;
- customer retention;
- asset liquidity;
- provider utilization;
- content utility;
- data or Knowledge improvement;
- strategic learning.

```text
potential value
≠ permission to commercialize every source record
```

## Opportunity preserves its origin

Every Opportunity should retain the originating context:

- originating Need, asset, Capability, audience, capacity or signal;
- source Workplace or service;
- discovery actor or method;
- Evidence and epistemic status;
- applicable customer and relationship constraints;
- data-use and contact permissions;
- time sensitivity;
- known risks and Unknowns.

This prevents a projected signal from losing its source obligations after entering a commercial funnel.

For example, a Lite maintenance signal derived from public data may support a service Opportunity. It does not automatically create a Customer, authorize direct marketing, or prove the underlying deadline.

## Opportunity is a route hypothesis

An Opportunity should represent a plausible route that still requires validation.

A proposed Opportunity may contain:

- value hypothesis;
- affected actors;
- related Need or asset;
- candidate Product or Capability route;
- jurisdiction and scope;
- expected benefit;
- estimated cost and effort;
- prerequisites;
- professional-authority requirements;
- consent and relationship constraints;
- risk and reversibility;
- expiry or decision window;
- current validation state.

```text
Opportunity identified
≠ route validated
≠ customer interested
≠ commercial terms accepted
```

## Product-local Opportunity profiles

Different Products may define specialized profiles without creating parallel Core roots.

Examples include:

- service Opportunity;
- transaction Opportunity;
- maintenance Opportunity;
- content Opportunity;
- partnership Opportunity;
- provider-capacity Opportunity;
- recovery Opportunity.

Each profile may add local fields and scoring, but should preserve the shared distinction between potential route and accepted commitment.

```text
Transaction Opportunity
= Product-local Opportunity profile

not

Transaction Opportunity
= separate universal Core root by default
```

## Opportunity scoring is not semantic truth

Products may rank Opportunities by:

- urgency;
- expected value;
- fit;
- customer likelihood;
- service readiness;
- provider capacity;
- Evidence quality;
- strategic priority.

The score is a Product-local decision aid.

```text
high score
≠ verified Need
≠ legal priority
≠ customer consent
≠ guaranteed value
```

The scoring model, input version and intended purpose should remain visible. AI-generated likelihood should be treated as inference, not as a customer fact.

## Opportunity and Customer relationship

An Opportunity may refer to:

- an existing Customer;
- a prospect;
- an unidentified public-record owner;
- a partner's protected customer;
- an asset with no current buyer;
- a provider with spare capacity.

These contexts must not be flattened.

```text
Opportunity related to an Organization
≠ Organization is a Customer

Opportunity visible to another Workplace
≠ relationship ownership transferred
```

Relationship Owner, origin, contact permission and disclosure policy should remain explicit throughout routing.

## Opportunity and Engagement

Opportunity represents a possible route. Engagement begins when actors enter a governed collaboration boundary around a defined purpose.

```text
Opportunity
→ actor interest and validation
→ governed Engagement candidate
```

An Opportunity may close without any Engagement. Several Engagements may arise from one Opportunity. An Engagement may also arise directly from an existing Matter or contract without a separate sales Opportunity.

```text
Opportunity accepted internally
≠ external party engaged
```

## Opportunity and Order

An Order is a commercial commitment. Opportunity precedes it.

Before an Order is created, the platform may need:

- Need clarification;
- route comparison;
- eligibility checks;
- professional feasibility review;
- pricing approval;
- provider availability;
- customer decision;
- contracting-party identification.

```text
quote sent
≠ Opportunity won
≠ Order accepted
```

Commercial systems should not create an accepted Order merely because an Opportunity stage changes.

## Opportunity lifecycle

A useful lifecycle may distinguish:

```text
Observed
Qualified for Investigation
Validated
Proposed
Under Discussion
Deferred
Converted to Engagement or Order
Closed — No Route
Closed — Declined
Expired
Superseded
```

`Won` and `Lost` alone are insufficient because the underlying Need or asset may remain active.

Opportunity closure should preserve:

- disposition reason;
- actor and authority;
- related Need status;
- whether contact or follow-up remains permitted;
- reusable learning signal;
- whether a future Opportunity may be created.

## Conflicting opportunities

One Need may support several routes:

- self-service preparation;
- partner-led delivery;
- MarkReg direct delivery;
- provider referral;
- delayed action;
- no safe route.

The platform should compare these without silently choosing the route with the highest platform revenue.

```text
commercially preferable for platform
≠ best route for customer
```

Decision-ready options should expose cost, risk, authority, evidence, timing and trade-offs.

## Asset opportunities

A trademark asset can create a transaction Opportunity, licensing Opportunity, defensive acquisition Opportunity or content Opportunity.

But:

```text
asset listed
≠ title verified
≠ seller authorized
≠ transfer feasible
```

Product-local transaction records must reference identity, asset, Evidence, Relationship and authority semantics rather than convert a Listing into accepted ownership truth.

## Provider and capacity opportunities

Provider capacity may support routing and network growth, but capacity data has scope and freshness limits.

```text
Provider available generally
≠ eligible for this Matter
≠ appointed
≠ accepted the instruction
```

Opportunity can identify a potential route; Book 03 eligibility, Handoff, Assignment or provider appointment controls the actual transition.

## Learning boundary

Opportunity outcomes may improve ranking and route design. They must not rewrite historical Need, customer consent or professional facts.

```text
Opportunity converted
≠ original inference was universally correct

Opportunity declined
≠ Need did not exist
```

Learning signals should preserve Product, audience, source, timing, route and decision context.

## Shared status

Opportunity is an existing shared concept in the reconciled Book 02 architecture. Product specializations should normally remain profiles unless a separate admission analysis proves that a new shared root is necessary.

## Constitutional rule

```text
Opportunity represents a possible route to value.
It does not create demand, consent, commitment,
relationship ownership, execution authority or formal state.
```
